*Discover the true historical reasons why ancient Jews completely avoided Samaria, making the famous story of the Good Samaritan truly revolutionary.*

---

For the modern reader, the phrase "Good Samaritan" has become a cultural idiom. We use it to describe a person who stops to help a stranded motorist change a flat tire, or a stranger who pays for someone else's groceries. We have hospitals, charities, and laws named after this concept. Over the centuries, the edge of the original story has been sanded down by familiarity, leaving us with a pleasant, moralistic tale about simply being kind to those in need.

But in the first-century Middle East, to a Jewish audience sitting at the feet of Jesus of Nazareth, the phrase "Good Samaritan" was a jarring contradiction in terms. It was an oxymoron. To the original listeners, a Samaritan could not be good, and a good person could not be a Samaritan. When Jesus told the story of a man beaten by robbers and ignored by his own religious leaders, only to be saved by a Samaritan, He was not just teaching a lesson on general kindness. He was dropping a cultural bombshell.

To fully grasp the shockwaves this parable sent through its original audience, and to understand why the Gospel of John notes that "Jews have no dealings with Samaritans," we must step out of the modern world and journey back into the ancient past. We must look at the dirt, the geography, the politics, and the blood-soaked history of the Levant. The enmity between Judea and Samaria was not a minor disagreement; it was a deeply entrenched, thousand-year-old family feud fueled by civil war, foreign invasion, religious schism, and mutual desecration.

This is the history of a broken border, and the revolutionary attempt to heal it.

## 1. A Tale of Two Kingdoms: The Great Fracture

To find the root of the hatred between Jews and Samaritans, we have to rewind the clock long before the time of the Roman Empire, back to the golden age of the united monarchy of Israel in the 10th century BC. Under the reigns of King David and his son King Solomon, the twelve tribes of Israel were united as a single, formidable kingdom. The capital was firmly established in Jerusalem, located in the southern territory of Judah, where Solomon built the magnificent First Temple.

However, unity is often fragile. Solomon’s massive building projects and the lavish upkeep of his court came at a heavy cost. He imposed severe taxation and forced labor on the northern tribes, creating a simmering resentment. When Solomon died in 931 BC, his son Rehoboam ascended to the throne. The northern tribes approached the young king and pleaded for tax relief and an easing of their burdens. Ignoring the counsel of his wiser, older advisors, Rehoboam arrogantly promised to tax them even heavier and punish them more severely than his father had.

The result was catastrophic. Ten of the twelve tribes rebelled, seceding from the union. The kingdom was violently ripped in two.

In the south was the Kingdom of Judah (from which we get the term "Jew"), consisting of the tribes of Judah and Benjamin, retaining Jerusalem as its capital and the lineage of David as its kings. In the north was the newly formed Kingdom of Israel, which would eventually establish its capital in the city of Samaria.

The political fracture immediately created a religious crisis. The new northern king, Jeroboam, realized that if his subjects continued to travel south to Jerusalem to worship at the Temple, their loyalties would eventually revert to the House of David. To secure his borders, Jeroboam made a fateful decision: he established two rival worship centers in the north—one at Dan and one at Bethel—and placed golden calves in them, telling his people, "Here are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of Egypt."

From the perspective of the southern Kingdom of Judah, this was the ultimate betrayal. The north had not only committed political treason; they had committed spiritual adultery. The border between north and south became a wall of deep religious suspicion.

## 2. The Assyrian War Machine and the Mixed Multitude

For two centuries, the two sister kingdoms existed side by side, sometimes as uneasy allies, often as bitter enemies. But the geopolitical landscape of the ancient Near East was brutal, and a dark cloud was gathering in the east: the Neo-Assyrian Empire.

The Assyrians were the terror of the ancient world. They did not just conquer nations; they systematically dismantled them. When the Assyrian war machine rolled into a territory, their standard policy was mass deportation. They would tear the native population from their homeland and scatter them across the empire, simultaneously importing pagan peoples from other conquered lands to settle in the newly emptied territory. This cruel strategy severed a conquered people's ties to their land, their history, and their gods, preventing future rebellions.

In 722 BC, the Assyrians besieged and destroyed Samaria, the capital of the northern kingdom. The ten northern tribes of Israel were carried away into exile, eventually becoming known to history as the "Ten Lost Tribes."

In their place, the Assyrian king imported pagans from Babylon, Cuthah, Avva, Hamath, and Sepharvaim. According to the biblical record in 2 Kings 17, these new inhabitants brought their own foreign gods with them. However, they also began to experience attacks by lions, which they interpreted as the anger of the "god of the land." In a highly pragmatic ancient response, the Assyrian king sent one of the exiled Israelite priests back to Samaria to teach the new inhabitants how to worship the God of Israel—Yahweh.

The result was a profound religious and ethnic syncretism. The inhabitants of Samaria began to worship the God of Israel, but they also continued to serve their own pagan deities. Over generations, these imported peoples intermarried with the few Israelites who had been left behind.

To the people of the southern Kingdom of Judah, who had survived the Assyrian invasion, the people of Samaria were no longer their brothers. They were a "mixed multitude"—ethnically impure half-breeds and religiously corrupted idolaters who had polluted the holy land. The term "Samaritan" began to take on a derogatory flavor, synonymous with compromise and contamination.

## 3. The Return from Exile and the Rebuilding Snub

The southern Kingdom of Judah eventually met its own tragic fate. In 586 BC, the Babylonian Empire (which had conquered the Assyrians) destroyed Jerusalem, burned Solomon's Temple to the ground, and carried the Jews into exile in Babylon.

However, unlike the northern tribes, the Jews maintained their distinct identity, their scriptures, and their fierce devotion to Yahweh during their captivity. Seventy years later, the Persians conquered Babylon. In a stunning reversal of ancient foreign policy, the Persian King Cyrus the Great issued a decree allowing the Jews to return to their homeland and rebuild their Temple.

When the returning Jewish exiles, led by figures like Zerubbabel and later Ezra and Nehemiah, arrived back in the ruins of Jerusalem, they were approached by the Samaritans. The Samaritans offered a seemingly olive branch: "Let us help you build, for we worship your God as you do, and we have been sacrificing to him ever since the days of Esarhaddon king of Assyria who brought us here."

To modern ears, this sounds like a wonderful opportunity for reconciliation. But to the returning Jews, who had just spent seventy years in exile specifically because of their ancestors' failure to remain pure and faithful to God alone, compromise was not an option. They viewed the Samaritans' syncretic religion as dangerous. The Jewish leaders delivered a blunt, uncompromising refusal: "You have no part with us in building a house to our God."

The Samaritans were deeply insulted. The rejected olive branch quickly turned into a spear. Historical records and biblical texts detail how the Samaritans actively campaigned against the Jews, hiring counselors to frustrate their building plans, writing letters to the Persian authorities accusing the Jews of plotting rebellion, and even threatening military action against Nehemiah as he rebuilt the walls of Jerusalem.

The rift was now permanent. It was no longer just a historical grievance; it was an active, ongoing political and religious conflict.

## 4. The Rival Temple on Mount Gerizim

As the centuries rolled on, the theological differences between the Jews and the Samaritans solidified. While the Jews recognized all the writings of the prophets and the historical books (what we now call the Old Testament), the Samaritans accepted only the Torah—the first five books of Moses (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy). Furthermore, they used a slightly altered version known as the Samaritan Pentateuch.

The most critical difference in the Samaritan scriptures was a specific commandment regarding where God was to be worshiped. While the Jewish scriptures implicitly and explicitly pointed to Jerusalem (Mount Zion) as the place where God would put His name, the Samaritan Torah claimed that God had chosen Mount Gerizim, a mountain in the heart of Samaritan territory.

To solidify their claim, around the 4th century BC, the Samaritans built their own massive temple on the summit of Mount Gerizim. They established their own priesthood, offered their own sacrifices, and celebrated their own Passover. To the Jews in Jerusalem, this was an unbearable blasphemy—a counterfeit temple directly challenging the authority of the true Temple on Mount Zion.

The tension finally boiled over into outright war during the intertestamental period (the time between the Old and New Testaments). In the 2nd century BC, the Jews fought a successful rebellion against their Greek overlords, establishing an independent Jewish kingdom ruled by the Hasmonean dynasty. In 128 BC, a Jewish high priest and king named John Hyrcanus marched a Judean army north into Samaria.

Hyrcanus did the unthinkable: he attacked Mount Gerizim and burned the Samaritan temple to the ground.

Imagine the psychological and cultural impact of this event. Just a little over a century before Jesus was born, the Jewish army had destroyed the holiest site of the Samaritan people. If there was ever any hope of mending the broken border between the two peoples, the ashes of the Gerizim temple buried it forever. The hatred was now visceral, deeply personal, and seemingly irreversible.

## 5. The Geography of Avoidance

By the first century AD, the era of Jesus and the Roman occupation, the land of Israel was divided into three distinct vertical regions, stacked like a geopolitical layer cake.

To understand the daily reality of this animosity, one must understand the map of the time.

In the north was Galilee, a predominantly Jewish region where Jesus grew up and based his ministry. In the south was Judea, the heartland of Jewish religion and culture, home to Jerusalem and the Temple.

Wedged directly between them was Samaria.

Geographically, the most direct, efficient route for a Galilean Jew traveling south to Jerusalem for the holy festivals (like Passover) was straight through the hill country of Samaria. It was a journey of about three days on foot.

However, because the hatred between the two groups was so intense, strict Jews would refuse to set foot on Samaritan soil. They believed that even the dust of Samaria was ritually unclean. Therefore, Jewish pilgrims traveling from Galilee to Jerusalem would embark on a grueling, multi-day detour. They would walk east, cross the Jordan River into the region of Perea, hike all the way south along the eastern bank of the river through the desert heat, and then cross back over the Jordan near Jericho to make the steep, dangerous ascent up to Jerusalem.

They literally doubled their travel time—turning a three-day walk into a brutal six-day trek—just to avoid making contact with Samaritans. The borders were not defended by walls or razor wire; they were enforced by deeply ingrained religious prejudice and cultural disgust.

## 6. Everyday Animosity in the First Century

The historical records from the first century paint a grim picture of everyday life along these borders. The animosity was mutual and frequently violent.

Jewish rabbinic texts from the era reveal the depth of the contempt. Some rabbis taught that it was forbidden to eat the bread of a Samaritan, stating that eating with them was akin to eating swine's flesh. Samaritans were generally not allowed to convert to Judaism, and their testimony was not accepted in Jewish courts. The Jewish apocryphal book of Sirach (written in the 2nd century BC) bluntly declares: "There are two nations that my soul detests, and the third is not even a people... the foolish people that dwell in Shechem [Samaria]."

When Jewish opponents of Jesus wanted to throw the ultimate, most vile insult at Him to discredit His ministry, they said, "Are we not right in saying that you are a Samaritan and have a demon?" (John 8:48). To be called a Samaritan was the highest form of character assassination.

The Samaritans, for their part, gave as good as they got. They were known to be hostile to Jewish pilgrims who did choose to travel through their lands. According to the first-century Jewish historian Josephus, Samaritans would sometimes light false signal fires on the hills to confuse Jews regarding the timing of the new moon, throwing off the sacred Jewish calendar.

In one particularly horrifying incident recorded by Josephus, during a Passover celebration between 6 and 9 AD, a group of Samaritans snuck into the Temple in Jerusalem under the cover of darkness and scattered human bones throughout the sanctuary. By doing so, they ritually defiled the holiest site in Judaism right before the most sacred festival of the year, rendering the priests unclean and disrupting the entire nation's worship.

This was the toxic environment into which Jesus stepped. A world where Jews and Samaritans viewed each other not just as historical rivals, but as subhuman, spiritually defiled enemies.

## 7. Jesus and the Radical Middle Ground

Understanding this context transforms how we read the life and ministry of Jesus. Jesus did not just ignore the social norms of His day; He actively dismantled them.

In the Gospel of John, chapter 4, we read a simple geographical note that actually carries profound theological weight: "He had to pass through Samaria." While other Jewish rabbis were taking the long, exhausting detour across the Jordan River to avoid contamination, Jesus walked straight across the forbidden border.

He stopped at Jacob's Well in Sychar, deep in Samaritan territory, and did the unthinkable: He asked a Samaritan woman for a drink of water. By asking to drink from her vessel, He was willingly ignoring the strict purity laws that dictated a Jew would become ritually unclean by sharing a cup with a Samaritan. The woman herself was shocked, asking, "How is it that you, a Jew, ask for a drink from me, a woman of Samaria?"

Jesus engaged her not with hostility, but with theological depth and divine grace. He directly addressed the ancient dispute over the rival temples—acknowledging that "salvation is from the Jews," yet declaring that a time was coming when true worshipers would worship the Father neither on Mount Gerizim nor in Jerusalem, but "in spirit and truth." In doing so, He effectively declared that the geographical and ethnic borders that had defined their hatred for a millennium were being abolished by His arrival.

Later, in the Gospel of Luke, Jesus heals ten lepers. Leprosy was a disease that erased social boundaries; in their shared suffering, Jewish and Samaritan lepers banded together in isolation from society. Yet, when all ten were healed, only one turned back to fall at Jesus' feet and thank Him. The Gospel writer makes sure to include a specific detail: "Now he was a Samaritan." Jesus holds this historical enemy up as the ultimate example of faith and gratitude.

## Conclusion: The Revolutionary Power of the Parable

It is only with this heavy, blood-stained history in mind that we can finally return to the Parable of the Good Samaritan.

In Luke 10, a lawyer stands up to test Jesus, asking what he must do to inherit eternal life. Jesus points him to the Law: Love God, and love your neighbor. The lawyer, seeking to find a loophole in the command, asks, "And who is my neighbor?" In the first-century Jewish mindset, a "neighbor" was strictly a fellow Jew, a member of the covenant community. It certainly did not include Romans, Greeks, and absolutely, positively did not include Samaritans.

Jesus answers with a story. A man is going down the treacherous road from Jerusalem to Jericho—a notoriously dangerous path known for bandits. He is stripped, beaten, and left half dead.

Jesus first introduces a Priest, and then a Levite. These were the spiritual elite of Jewish society. The original audience would have fully expected these men to be the heroes of the story. But they pass by on the other side of the road. Why? Perhaps because touching a corpse would render them ritually unclean, unable to perform their temple duties or collect the tithes. They placed their religious purity above the life of a bleeding man.

The audience is waiting for the third man. In Jewish storytelling of the time, the rule of three usually dictated that if a Priest and a Levite failed, the hero would be an ordinary Israelite layman. It would be an anti-clerical story, praising the common Jewish man over the out-of-touch religious elite.

Instead, Jesus says, "But a Samaritan..."

We can imagine the collective gasp of the audience. A Samaritan? The people who desecrated our temple with bones? The half-breed idolaters? The ones whose temple we burned to the ground?

Jesus describes this historical enemy doing what the religious leaders would not. The Samaritan feels compassion. He crosses the road. He touches the bleeding, unclean man. He pours expensive oil and wine on his wounds. He puts the man on his own animal, pays for his lodging out of his own pocket, and promises to return to cover any further debts.

Jesus then asks the lawyer the most devastating question: "Which of these three, do you think, proved to be a neighbor to the man who fell among the robbers?"

The lawyer cannot even bring himself to say the word "Samaritan." He simply mutters, "The one who showed him mercy."

Jesus replies, "You go, and do likewise."

The Parable of the Good Samaritan is not merely a call to civic charity or volunteering at a soup kitchen. It is a profound, demanding theological mandate that cuts to the very heart of the Christian faith. By making a Samaritan the hero of His story, Jesus obliterated the boundaries of human prejudice. He demonstrated that in the Kingdom of God, love cannot be restricted by national borders, historical grievances, ethnic purity, or religious tribalism.

To love one's neighbor, according to Jesus, is to look directly at the person you have been culturally conditioned to hate the most—the person whose history is entirely at odds with yours—and to willingly cross the road to bind up their wounds. It is the realization that God's grace is vast enough to bridge a thousand years of bitter enmity, and that true holiness is not found in avoiding the brokenness of the world, but in stepping into the dirt to heal it.
