From the graceful elliptical paths of planets to the dramatic crashing of ocean tides, the universe is bound together by the invisible hand of gravity. This chapter explores the fundamental forces and physical laws governing celestial mechanics. We trace the monumental leap from Kepler’s geometrical laws of planetary motion to Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation. Building on these foundations, we examine the conservation laws dictating orbital dynamics and the differential pulls that create tidal forces. Finally, we introduce Einstein’s General Relativity, revealing how massive objects warp the fabric of spacetime to guide the cosmic dance.

## 3.1 Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

While the Copernican revolution effectively repositioned the Sun at the center of the known universe, it retained the ancient assumption that celestial bodies must move in perfect circles. To reconcile this heliocentric model with the highly precise observational data compiled by Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler spent years attempting to fit planetary motions to circular paths. He ultimately discarded perfectly circular orbits, realizing that planetary paths are elliptical. This breakthrough led to the formulation of his three laws of planetary motion, which fundamentally transformed celestial mechanics.

### The First Law: The Law of Ellipses

**Kepler's First Law** states that the orbit of every planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci.

An ellipse is a closed planar curve generated by a point moving such that the sum of its distances from two fixed points (the foci) is constant. In the context of the Solar System, the Sun occupies one focus, while the other focus represents an empty point in space.

Mathematically, the geometry of an ellipse is defined by its semi-major axis ($a$) and its semi-minor axis ($b$). The semi-major axis represents half of the longest diameter of the ellipse, while the semi-minor axis represents half of the shortest diameter.

```text
                      ^ y (Minor Axis)
                      |
                . . . | . . .
            .         | b       .
          .           |           .
         .            |            .
        .             |             .
--------+-------------+-------------+--------> x (Major Axis)
 Peri-  .   Sun (F1)  |   Empty (F2).   Ap-
 helion  .     *      |      *     .   helion
          .           |           .
            .         |         .
                . . . | . . .
                      |
        |---- c ------|
        |---------- a --------------|

```

The degree of "flattening" of the ellipse is quantified by its eccentricity ($e$), defined as the ratio of the distance from the center to a focus ($c$) to the semi-major axis ($a$):

$$e = \frac{c}{a}$$

Since $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$, eccentricity can also be expressed as:

$$e = \sqrt{1 - \frac{b^2}{a^2}}$$

For a perfect circle, the two foci perfectly overlap at the center ($c = 0$), resulting in an eccentricity of $e = 0$. As the ellipse becomes more elongated, $e$ approaches 1. Most planets in our Solar System have highly circular orbits with very low eccentricities (e.g., Earth's eccentricity is approximately $0.0167$), which is why planetary orbits appear almost circular to the naked eye.

### The Second Law: The Law of Equal Areas

**Kepler's Second Law** states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.

This law provides a geometrical description of how a planet's orbital velocity changes depending on its distance from the Sun. Because the orbit is elliptical, the distance between the planet and the Sun varies continuously. To sweep out an equivalent area in a given timeframe, the planet must travel faster when it is closer to the Sun and slower when it is further away.

```text
          Areal Velocity Diagram

                . . . . . . 
            .                 .  Area 2
         .       * Sun          \
        .          \             .
        .  Area 1    \            .
        .            /            .
         .         /             .
            .    /            .
                . . . . . . 

```

If the time taken to travel along the arc bordering Area 1 equals the time taken to travel along the arc bordering Area 2, then Area 1 is strictly equal to Area 2.

The closest point to the Sun in a planetary orbit is called the **perihelion**, and the farthest point is the **aphelion**. Consequently, a planet's orbital velocity is at its maximum at perihelion and at its minimum at aphelion. The mathematical formulation of the areal velocity ($dA/dt$) can be expressed as a constant:

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} r^2 \frac{d\theta}{dt} = \text{constant}$$

Where $r$ is the radial distance from the Sun and $\theta$ is the angular position. This constant sweeping of area is a direct consequence of the conservation of angular momentum, a concept that will be formalized when discussing orbital mechanics.

### The Third Law: The Law of Harmonies

**Kepler's Third Law** states that the square of the orbital period of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit.

While the first two laws describe the mechanics of an individual planet's orbit, the Third Law establishes a relationship between the orbits of different planets within the same system. It implies that a planet farther from the Sun not only has a longer orbital path but also travels at a slower average speed.

The proportionality is written as:

$$P^2 \propto a^3$$

Where:

* $P$ is the orbital period (the time it takes to complete one orbit).
* $a$ is the semi-major axis of the orbit.

By defining the unit of time as the Earth year and the unit of distance as the Astronomical Unit (AU)—which is the length of Earth's semi-major axis—the proportionality constant becomes exactly 1 for our Solar System. Therefore, the equation simplifies dramatically to:

$$P^2 = a^3$$

For example, Jupiter has a semi-major axis of approximately $5.20 \text{ AU}$. According to Kepler's Third Law, $a^3 \approx 140.6$. The square root of $140.6$ gives $P \approx 11.86$ years, which precisely matches Jupiter's observed orbital period.

Kepler derived these laws empirically—by finding mathematical patterns that fit observed data—without understanding the underlying physical cause of these motions. It would take nearly another century for a deeper physical framework to emerge, demonstrating that these three laws are natural consequences of a universal attractive force.

## 3.2 Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation

Johannes Kepler’s laws of planetary motion provided a precise geometrical description of *how* the planets moved, but they offered no physical explanation as to *why* they followed these specific paths. The transition from empirical observation to fundamental physical law was achieved by Sir Isaac Newton, who unified the physics of the heavens with the physics of the Earth in his seminal 1687 work, the *Principia Mathematica*.

Newton realized that the same force causing an apple to fall to the ground on Earth is responsible for holding the Moon in its orbit around the Earth, and the planets in their orbits around the Sun. He identified this ubiquitous force as gravity.

### The Formulation of Universal Gravitation

Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation postulates that every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

Mathematically, the magnitude of this attractive force ($F$) is expressed as:

$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:

* $F$ is the magnitude of the gravitational force between the two masses.
* $G$ is the universal gravitational constant, approximately $6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3 \text{ kg}^{-1} \text{ s}^{-2}$.
* $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the respective masses of the two interacting bodies.
* $r$ is the distance between the centers of mass of the two bodies.

Gravitation is always an attractive force and acts along the straight line intersecting the centers of the two masses. In accordance with Newton's Third Law of Motion (for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction), the force body 1 exerts on body 2 is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the force body 2 exerts on body 1.

```text
       Force on m_1                       Force on m_2
       exerted by m_2                     exerted by m_1
            F_12                               F_21
      <------------- [ m_1 ] - - - - - [ m_2 ] ------------->
                         |------ r ------|
                         
                   | F_12 | = | F_21 | = F

```

### The Inverse-Square Law

A critical component of Newton's law is the inverse-square relationship with distance. If the distance $r$ between two celestial bodies is doubled, the gravitational force between them is not halved, but rather reduced to one-fourth of its original value. If the distance is tripled, the force drops to one-ninth.

This geometric property is not unique to gravity; it applies to any physical quantity (like light or radiation) that radiates equally in all directions from a point source in three-dimensional space. As the influence spreads outward, it must cover the surface area of a sphere, and the surface area of a sphere grows in proportion to the square of its radius ($A = 4\pi r^2$).

```text
                        Point Source (Mass)
                                *
                               / \
                              /   \
                             /     \
                            /       \
                           /         \
                 Distance: r         2r
                     Area: A         4A
          Force Intensity: F         F/4

```

### Deriving Kepler from Newton

Newton’s crowning achievement in astrodynamics was demonstrating that Kepler’s three laws are direct mathematical consequences of universal gravitation and his own laws of motion.

For instance, Kepler's Third Law, which originally lacked a physical basis and relied on a specific planetary constant, was expanded by Newton into a universal form applicable to any system of orbiting bodies (such as moons orbiting Jupiter, or binary stars). By equating the gravitational force to the centripetal force required to maintain an orbit, Newton formulated the generalized version of Kepler's Third Law:

$$P^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{G(m_1 + m_2)} a^3$$

Where:

* $P$ is the orbital period.
* $a$ is the semi-major axis of the orbit.
* $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two orbiting bodies.

In the case of our Solar System, the mass of the Sun ($m_1$) is so overwhelmingly large compared to the mass of any planet ($m_2$) that the $m_2$ term effectively becomes negligible. The mass sum $(m_1 + m_2)$ is roughly constant for all planets, which explains why Kepler observed a constant ratio between $P^2$ and $a^3$ without realizing that the mass of the system was the hidden variable governing that ratio.

Newton’s formulation allowed astronomers to do something unprecedented: weigh the universe. By simply observing the orbital period ($P$) and orbital radius ($a$) of a moon, astronomers could use Newton's generalized law to calculate the mass of the host planet, an application of celestial mechanics that remains fundamental to modern astronomy.

## 3.3 Orbital Mechanics and Conservation Laws

The precise trajectories of celestial bodies, spacecraft, and even galaxies are governed by the principles of orbital mechanics, also known as astrodynamics. While Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation describes the instantaneous force between two masses, understanding their long-term motion requires the application of conservation laws. In an isolated two-body system, two fundamental quantities remain constant over time: angular momentum and total mechanical energy.

### Conservation of Angular Momentum

Angular momentum ($L$) is a measure of the rotational motion of a body relative to a central point. For a planet of mass $m$ moving at velocity $v$ at a distance $r$ from the Sun, the magnitude of its angular momentum is given by:

$$L = m v r \sin(\theta)$$

Where $\theta$ is the angle between the velocity vector and the position vector.

According to classical mechanics, angular momentum is conserved unless an external torque is applied to the system. Gravity is a *central force*, meaning it always pulls directly along the line connecting the centers of the two bodies. Because the force is strictly radial, it cannot exert a torque. Therefore, the angular momentum of an orbiting body remains absolutely constant throughout its orbit.

This conservation law provides the physical proof for Kepler’s Second Law (the law of equal areas). For $L$ to remain constant as the distance $r$ changes in an elliptical orbit, the velocity $v$ must change inversely. At perihelion (closest approach, $r_p$) and aphelion (farthest distance, $r_a$), the velocity vector is strictly perpendicular to the position vector ($\sin(90^\circ) = 1$). This allows us to establish a direct relationship between the orbital velocities at these two extreme points:

$$m v_p r_p = m v_a r_a$$

$$v_p r_p = v_a r_a$$

Because $r_p < r_a$, the velocity at perihelion ($v_p$) must be strictly greater than the velocity at aphelion ($v_a$).

### Conservation of Orbital Energy

The total mechanical energy ($E$) of an orbiting body is the sum of its kinetic energy ($K$) and its gravitational potential energy ($U$). In a closed system with no non-conservative forces (like atmospheric drag), the total mechanical energy is conserved.

Kinetic energy depends on the body's velocity:

$$K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2$$

Gravitational potential energy depends on the distance between the two bodies. By convention, potential energy is defined as zero at an infinite distance. Because gravity is an attractive force, work must be done to move a mass *away* from the central body against the gravitational pull. Consequently, the potential energy at any finite distance is negative:

$$U = -\frac{G M m}{r}$$

The total specific mechanical energy (energy per unit mass, $\epsilon = E/m$) is therefore constant at any point in the orbit:

$$\epsilon = \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{G M}{r} = \text{constant}$$

### The Vis-Viva Equation

By combining the conservation of energy and angular momentum with the geometric properties of an ellipse, astrodynamicists derive the *vis-viva equation* (from the Latin for "living force"). This is arguably the most important equation in orbital mechanics, as it allows for the calculation of a body's velocity at any given distance $r$ along an orbit with a semi-major axis $a$:

$$v^2 = G M \left( \frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a} \right)$$

For a perfectly circular orbit, the radius is constant ($r = a$). Substituting this into the vis-viva equation yields the circular orbital velocity formula:

$$v_{circular} = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}$$

### Orbital Trajectories and Conic Sections

The total mechanical energy ($E$) defines the shape of the orbit, mapping directly to the mathematical family of curves known as conic sections.

```text
          y
          |       Hyperbola (E > 0, e > 1)
          |      /
          |    /   Parabola (E = 0, e = 1)
          |   |  / 
          |   | |
          |   | |  Ellipse (E < 0, 0 < e < 1)
          |   | | /
          |   | | |  Circle (E < 0, e = 0)
          |   | | | /
----------+---+-+-++---------- x
          |   * (Focus / Central Mass)
          |   | | | \
          |   | | \  Circle
          |   | |  \
          |   |  \   Ellipse
          |    \   Parabola
          |      \ 
          |       Hyperbola

```

* **Closed (Bound) Orbits ($E < 0$):** If the total energy is negative, the kinetic energy is insufficient to overcome the gravitational potential well. The body is forever bound to the central mass. The resulting trajectory is an **ellipse** (or a **circle**, which is a special case of an ellipse where $E$ is exactly minimized for a given angular momentum).
* **Marginally Bound Orbit ($E = 0$):** If the kinetic energy exactly cancels out the potential energy, the body has precisely enough energy to travel an infinite distance before its velocity drops to zero. This trajectory is a **parabola**. The velocity required to achieve this state is called the escape velocity ($v_{esc}$). By setting the vis-viva equation's semi-major axis $a$ to infinity (making $1/a = 0$), we derive:

$$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2 G M}{r}}$$

* **Open (Unbound) Orbits ($E > 0$):** If the total energy is positive, the kinetic energy exceeds the gravitational potential energy. The body will escape the central mass's gravitational pull entirely and retain a residual velocity (hyperbolic excess velocity) even at an infinite distance. The resulting trajectory is a **hyperbola**. This is the typical trajectory of an interstellar comet passing through our Solar System, or a spacecraft utilizing a gravity assist.

## 3.4 Tides and Tidal Forces

Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation assumes, for the sake of simplicity, that celestial bodies can be treated as point masses where all mass is concentrated at a single, central point. However, in reality, planets and moons are extended, three-dimensional objects. Because gravitational force diminishes with distance, the gravitational pull exerted by a massive body is not uniform across the volume of a nearby secondary body. This gradient in gravitational pull creates differential forces, commonly known as **tidal forces**.

### The Physics of Differential Gravity

Consider two bodies: a large primary mass (like the Earth) and a perturbing mass (like the Moon). The side of the Earth facing the Moon is closer to it than the Earth's center, and the Earth's center is closer to the Moon than the Earth's far side. Consequently, the Moon's gravitational pull is strongest on the near side, average at the center, and weakest on the far side.

```text
Gravitational Pull from the Moon:
Far Side                  Center                  Near Side
  --->                    ----->                  ------->

Tidal Forces (Relative to the Earth's Center):
  <---                      .                       --->
(Pulled away from Moon)   (Net Zero)       (Pulled toward Moon)

```

From the perspective of the Earth's center, the near side is being pulled *away* from the center toward the Moon, while the center is being pulled *away* from the far side. This differential pull stretches the Earth along the axis connecting it to the Moon, creating two distinct tidal bulges: one facing the Moon, and one diametrically opposite.

### Mathematical Formulation of Tidal Force

The magnitude of the tidal force can be derived by finding the difference in gravitational force across the radius of the extended body. Using Newton's law, the gravitational acceleration ($a$) exerted by a mass $M$ at a distance $r$ is $a = \frac{GM}{r^2}$.

To find the difference in acceleration ($\Delta a$) across a small distance $\Delta r$ (the radius of the planet), we take the derivative of the gravitational acceleration with respect to distance:

$$\frac{da}{dr} = -2\frac{GM}{r^3}$$

Ignoring the negative sign (which simply indicates the force is attractive and decreases with distance), the tidal acceleration ($a_{tidal}$) exerted by a perturbing body of mass $M$ on the surface of a planet with radius $R$, orbiting at a distance $d$, is approximated as:

$$a_{tidal} \approx \frac{2 G M R}{d^3}$$

This reveals a fundamental property of tidal mechanics: while gravitational force follows an **inverse-square law** ($\frac{1}{d^2}$), tidal force follows an **inverse-cube law** ($\frac{1}{d^3}$).

This inverse-cube relationship explains why the Moon dominates Earth's tides despite the Sun's immense mass. Although the Sun exerts a gravitational force on Earth that is nearly 180 times stronger than the Moon's, the Sun is roughly 390 times farther away. Cubing that distance diminishes the Sun's tidal influence significantly, making the solar tidal force only about 45% as strong as the lunar tidal force.

### Oceanic Tides on Earth

Because Earth is not a rigid solid, it deforms under these tidal forces. The solid lithosphere bulges slightly (Earth tides), but the most visible effect occurs in the fluid hydrosphere, creating oceanic tides. As the Earth rotates on its axis beneath these relatively stationary tidal bulges, a specific point on the coastline will pass through two high tides and two low tides roughly every 24 hours and 50 minutes (a lunar day).

The amplitude of these tides varies depending on the alignment of the Earth, Moon, and Sun:

* **Spring Tides:** When the Earth, Moon, and Sun form a straight line (syzygy)—occurring during the New Moon and Full Moon phases—the solar and lunar tidal forces constructively interfere. This produces the highest high tides and the lowest low tides.
* **Neap Tides:** When the Moon is at a 90-degree angle to the Sun relative to Earth (quadrature)—occurring during the First and Third Quarter phases—the solar tidal force partially cancels the lunar tidal force, resulting in moderate tides with minimal variation between high and low.

```text
Spring Tide Alignment (Constructive):
[ SUN ] ====================> [ Moon ] ---- [ Earth ]

Neap Tide Alignment (Destructive):
                                [ Moon ]
                                   |
[ SUN ] ====================> [ Earth ]

```

### Tidal Friction and Orbital Evolution

Tidal forces are not merely passive effects; they are active drivers of long-term orbital and rotational evolution. Because the Earth rotates faster (roughly 24 hours) than the Moon revolves around it (roughly 27.3 days), friction between the moving tidal bulges and the ocean floor drags the bulges slightly ahead of the direct Earth-Moon line.

This misalignment creates a gravitational torque. The Moon pulls backward on the leading bulge, which acts as a brake on Earth's rotation, slowing our day by about 1.7 milliseconds per century. Conversely, the leading bulge pulls the Moon forward in its orbit, adding orbital energy and causing the Moon to spiral outward from Earth at a rate of approximately 3.8 centimeters per year.

### Tidal Locking and Heating

This transfer of angular momentum continues until an equilibrium is reached. In the case of the Moon, Earth's massive tidal forces long ago slowed the Moon's rotation until its rotational period perfectly matched its orbital period. This phenomenon, known as **tidal locking** or synchronous rotation, is why the Moon always keeps the same hemisphere facing the Earth.

When a moon is tidally locked but has an eccentric (elliptical) orbit, the distance between the planet and the moon changes constantly. This causes the amplitude of the tidal bulge to swell and subside, and the bulge to wobble slightly across the surface (libration). This continuous flexing generates immense internal friction, a process called **tidal heating**. In the outer Solar System, tidal heating is the primary energy source driving the extreme volcanism on Jupiter's moon Io and maintaining the subsurface liquid water ocean on Europa.

## 3.5 Introduction to General Relativity in Astrodynamics

For over two centuries, Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation reigned supreme in celestial mechanics. It successfully predicted the return of Halley's Comet and led to the triumphant discovery of Neptune based on the gravitational perturbations of Uranus. However, by the late 19th century, high-precision astrometry revealed a glaring anomaly: the orbit of Mercury was not behaving exactly as Newtonian physics dictated.

The major axis of Mercury's highly eccentric orbit was slowly rotating—a phenomenon known as apsidal precession. While Newtonian perturbations from other planets accounted for most of this rotation, there remained an unexplained discrepancy of roughly 43 arcseconds per century. This anomaly was not a measurement error; it was the first crack in the Newtonian paradigm, eventually resolved by Albert Einstein's General Theory of Relativity in 1915.

### The Geometric Nature of Gravity

Newton modeled gravity as an instantaneous, invisible force pulling masses together across a static void. Einstein proposed a radically different framework: space and time are not an inert backdrop, but a dynamic, interwoven four-dimensional fabric known as **spacetime**.

In General Relativity, gravity is not a force at all. Instead, it is the geometric curvature of spacetime caused by the presence of mass and energy. As physicist John Archibald Wheeler elegantly summarized: *"Space-time tells matter how to move; matter tells space-time how to curve."*

An orbiting planet does not feel a "pull" from the Sun. It is simply moving forward in a straight line (a geodesic) through a region of spacetime that has been warped into a “funnel” by the Sun's immense mass.

```text
    Flat Spacetime (Absence of Mass)
      |   |   |   |   |
    --+---+---+---+---+--
      |   |   |   |   |
    --+---+---+---+---+--
      |   |   |   |   |

    Curved Spacetime (Presence of Mass)
      \   |   |   |   /
    ---\--+---+---+--/---
        \ |  ***  | /
    -----++ ***** ++-----  <-- Massive Body (e.g., Sun)
        / |  ***  | \
    ---/--+---+---+--\---
      /   |   |   |   \

```

### The Effective Potential in General Relativity

To understand how General Relativity modifies orbital dynamics, astrodynamicists look at the effective potential energy ($V_{eff}$) of a two-body system. In classical Newtonian mechanics, the effective potential for a particle of mass $m$ and angular momentum $L$ orbiting a central mass $M$ at a distance $r$ is:

$$V_{eff, Newton}(r) = -\frac{G M m}{r} + \frac{L^2}{2 m r^2}$$

The first term is the standard attractive gravitational potential, and the second term is the repulsive "centrifugal" potential arising from angular momentum.

General Relativity introduces a third, highly distance-dependent term to this equation:

$$V_{eff, GR}(r) = -\frac{G M m}{r} + \frac{L^2}{2 m r^2} - \frac{G M L^2}{m c^2 r^3}$$

Where $c$ is the speed of light in a vacuum.

This inverse-cube relativistic correction ($-\frac{G M L^2}{m c^2 r^3}$) is an additional attractive effect that becomes significant only when $r$ is small or velocity is a significant fraction of $c$. For Earth, this term is virtually zero, and Newton's laws are perfectly adequate. But for Mercury, plunging deep into the Sun's gravitational well at perihelion, this extra term slightly steepens the potential well, causing the planet to "overshoot" a perfect closed ellipse, perfectly explaining the 43 arcseconds of precession.

### Relativistic Effects in Modern Astrodynamics

While interplanetary navigation largely relies on Newtonian mechanics, General Relativity is indispensable for high-precision astrodynamics, exotic astrophysical systems, and modern technology.

1. **Gravitational Time Dilation:** Time runs slower in a stronger gravitational field. A clock on the surface of the Earth ticks slightly slower than a clock in high Earth orbit. The satellites of the Global Positioning System (GPS), orbiting at roughly 20,200 km, experience both less gravity than receivers on the ground (speeding up their clocks) and high orbital velocities (slowing down their clocks via Special Relativity). The net effect is that GPS clocks run about 38 microseconds faster per day than ground clocks. If astrodynamicists did not program relativistic corrections into the satellites, the GPS network's navigational accuracy would fail within minutes.
2. **Frame Dragging (Lense-Thirring Effect):** A rotating massive body slowly drags the fabric of spacetime around with it, like a spinning ball submerged in viscous molasses. This means that a satellite orbiting a rotating planet will have its orbital plane slightly rotated over time. This highly subtle effect was definitively measured around Earth by the Gravity Probe B satellite in 2011.
3. **Orbital Decay via Gravitational Waves:** When two massive, dense objects (like neutron stars or black holes) orbit each other tightly, they churn the fabric of spacetime, radiating energy outward in the form of gravitational waves. As the system loses energy, the orbit decays, causing the bodies to spiral inward. This loss of orbital energy was first observed in the Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar system, providing the first indirect evidence for gravitational waves long before their direct detection by LIGO in 2015.

```text
       Gravitational Wave Emission in a Binary System

               (   (   (   ( M1 )   )   )   )
                 \        /   ^   \        /
                   \    /     |     \    /
                     \/  Grav. Waves  \/
                     /\     (Loss of  /\
                   /    \   Energy) /    \
                 /        \   v   /        \
               (   (   (   ( M2 )   )   )   )

```

Through General Relativity, orbital mechanics evolved from the study of objects moving *through* space to the study of objects moving *with* spacetime.
