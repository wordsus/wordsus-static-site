---

If you slide a heavy book across a wooden table, it moves for a short moment, gradually slows down, and eventually comes to a complete halt. If you stop pedaling your bicycle on a flat road, you will eventually coast to a stop. If you kick a soccer ball across a grassy field, it will roll, slow down, and rest. 

For thousands of years, these everyday observations led humanity to a very logical, very intuitive, and completely incorrect conclusion: the natural state of all objects is to be at rest. It seems painfully obvious to anyone living on Earth that if you want something to keep moving, you have to keep pushing it. Without an active pusher, the motion dies out. 

But what if the universe doesn't actually work this way? What if the book, the bicycle, and the soccer ball "want" to keep moving forever, and something invisible is actively robbing them of their speed? 

This is the profound realization that sits at the very heart of modern physics. It is a concept that took humanity millennia to grasp because it requires us to imagine a world entirely unlike the one we experience daily—a world free of friction, free of air resistance, and free of the hidden hands that constantly grab at moving objects. 

This is the story of the Law of Inertia, famously known as Newton's First Law of Motion. It is the fundamental rule that governs why planets continue to orbit the sun, why you get thrown forward in your seat when a car slams on its brakes, and why a spacecraft can drift through the cold void of the cosmos for billions of years without a single drop of fuel. 

To understand why things don't stop, we first have to unlearn everything our daily life has taught us about moving objects. We must take a journey through ancient philosophy, Renaissance thought experiments, mathematical revelations, and the deep vacuum of outer space.

## 1. The Aristotelian Illusion: Why We Thought Rest Was King

To truly appreciate the genius of the Law of Inertia, we must first understand the intellectual wall that humanity was banging its head against for nearly two thousand years. This wall was built by the ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle.

Aristotle was a brilliant observer of nature. Looking around the world, he categorized motion into two distinct types: "natural motion" and "violent motion." 

According to Aristotelian physics, every element in the universe had a natural place it wanted to reach. Heavy things, made of earth and water, wanted to move downward toward the center of the universe (which, conveniently, was the center of the Earth). Light things, like air and fire, wanted to move upward toward the heavens. Once an object reached its natural place, it stopped. Therefore, the "natural state" of all matter was absolute rest.

If you wanted to make an object move in a way that wasn't its natural motion—say, throwing a rock sideways—you had to apply a force. Aristotle called this "violent motion." His rule was simple: no force, no violent motion. When the force stops, the violent motion stops, and the object returns to its natural state of rest.

Aristotle's theory made perfect sense based on human experience. It was so perfectly aligned with everyday common sense that it became the undisputed dogma of science for nearly two millennia. If a cart stopped moving when the horse stopped pulling, Aristotle's theory was validated. 

However, there was a glaring logical loophole in Aristotle's framework that troubled thinkers for centuries: the flight of an arrow. 

When an archer fires an arrow, the bowstring (the source of the force) only touches the arrow for a fraction of a second. Once the arrow leaves the bow, the active force is gone. So, according to Aristotle, the violent motion should immediately cease, and the arrow should fall straight down to the ground to resume its "natural" state. But it doesn't. It keeps flying through the air.

To patch this hole in his theory, Aristotle proposed that as the arrow moves, it pushes air out of the way. This displaced air swirls around to the back of the arrow and pushes it forward. This convoluted idea, sometimes called "antiperistasis," was the best explanation the ancient world had. It was a desperate attempt to hold onto the idea that motion requires a constant pusher.

It would take a genius armed with nothing more than ramps, bronze balls, and an unstoppable imagination to finally shatter the Aristotelian illusion.

## 2. Enter Galileo: The Birth of the Thought Experiment

In the early 17th century, the Italian polymath Galileo Galilei decided to challenge the Aristotelian worldview. Galileo realized that our everyday environment is "noisy." It is filled with hidden interactions like friction and air resistance that mask the true nature of reality. 

Galileo could not build a vacuum chamber, nor could he eliminate friction entirely. But he could minimize them, and more importantly, he could use his mind to extrapolate what would happen if they were completely removed. This was the birth of the scientific "thought experiment."

Galileo began by conducting physical experiments with highly polished bronze balls rolling down highly polished wooden ramps. He noticed a few crucial things:

1. A ball rolling down an inclined plane speeds up.
2. A ball rolling up an inclined plane slows down.

From these simple observations, Galileo made a massive intuitive leap. If rolling down a slope causes an increase in speed, and rolling up a slope causes a decrease in speed, what happens if the ball rolls on a perfectly flat, horizontal surface? 

Galileo reasoned that on a perfectly flat surface, the ball is neither moving down (gaining speed) nor moving up (losing speed). Therefore, its speed should remain perfectly constant. 

He further illustrated this with his famous "double inclined plane" thought experiment. Imagine a U-shaped ramp. If you release a ball from a certain height on the left side, it rolls down, gains speed, crosses the bottom, and rolls up the right side. 

```text
Galileo's Double Inclined Plane Experiment

Start (Height h)                  End (Height h)
   O                                O
    \                              /
     \                            /
      \                          /
       \________________________/
              Path of the ball
```

Galileo observed that the ball will almost reach the exact same height on the right side as it started from on the left side. The slight loss in height was due to the friction between the ball and the ramp. 

Then, Galileo asked a revolutionary question: What if we lower the angle of the second ramp?

```text
Lowering the second ramp

Start (Height h)                                End (Height h)
   O                                                  O
    \                                                /
     \                                              /
      \                                            /
       \__________________________________________/
              Ball must travel further to reach the same height
```

If the second ramp is less steep, the ball has to travel a much longer distance along the ramp to reach its original starting height. The ball "wants" to reach the height it started from, regardless of the distance it has to travel.

Finally, Galileo dropped the ultimate conceptual bombshell. What if the second ramp is completely flat? What if the angle is zero?

```text
The Infinite Flat Plane

Start (Height h)
   O
    \
     \
      \
       \___________________________________________________________ -> Infinity
        Ball travels forever seeking its original height
```

If the second side is perfectly horizontal, the ball will *never* reach its original starting height. Because it can never reach that height, it will never stop rolling. It will continue moving in a straight line, at a constant speed, forever.

With this elegant chain of logic, Galileo overturned 2,000 years of dogma. The natural state of an object is not necessarily rest. An object's natural tendency is to simply *keep doing whatever it is already doing*. If it is resting, it stays resting. If it is moving, it stays moving.

Galileo had discovered the principle of inertia. However, he primarily applied it to horizontal motion on the Earth (which he actually viewed as circular motion around the Earth's center). It would take another genius to formalize this concept into a universal mathematical law.

## 3. Sir Isaac Newton and the Formalization of Inertia

Born in 1642, the very year Galileo died, Isaac Newton was destined to take the baton and carry physics across the finish line of the scientific revolution. In 1687, Newton published his magnum opus, *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy), arguably the most important book in the history of science.

In the *Principia*, Newton laid out three laws of motion that formed the foundation of classical mechanics. The very first of these laws was his formal definition of inertia. 

Translated from the original Latin, Newton's First Law of Motion states:

> *"Every body perseveres in its state of rest, or of uniform motion in a right line, unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed thereon."*

Let us break down this brilliant sentence piece by piece, as it is incredibly dense with meaning.

**"Every body..."**
Newton makes it clear that this is a universal law. It applies to a grain of sand, a bowling ball, a human being, a planet, and a star. There are no exceptions in the macroscopic realm.

**"...perseveres in its state of rest..."**
This is the part Aristotle got right. If you leave a coffee cup on your desk, it won't suddenly start hovering or sliding sideways on its own. It perseveres—it obstinately continues—in its state of rest.

**"...or of uniform motion in a right line..."**
This is Galileo's triumph, expanded. "Uniform motion" means a constant speed. "A right line" means a perfectly straight path. Newton is stating that moving at 50 miles per hour in a perfectly straight line is physically indistinguishable from standing perfectly still. Both are "natural" states that require zero effort to maintain. 

**"...unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed thereon."**
Here is the core mechanism of the universe. An object will not change its speed or its direction on its own. It is lazy. The physical term for this laziness is **inertia**. To overcome this inertia, to make an object speed up, slow down, or turn, you must apply an external force.

Newton didn't just propose a qualitative idea; he provided a framework that could be quantified. He definitively shifted the question of physics. Before Newton, physicists asked, "What keeps this object moving?" After Newton, the question became, "What is causing this object to stop or change direction?"

## 4. Demystifying "Force": The Invisible Hands

Newton's First Law introduces the concept of an "external force," but to truly understand why things don't stop, we need to understand what a force actually is. 

In physics, a force is simply a push or a pull acting upon an object as a result of its interaction with another object. Forces are what cause a change in an object's state of motion. 

When you slide a book across a table, it stops. Newton tells us it stops because a force is compelling it to change its state of uniform motion. What is that force? It is **friction**. 

Friction is the microscopic interaction between the bottom of the book and the surface of the table. Even the smoothest-looking surfaces are actually jagged and mountainous at the microscopic level.

```text
Microscopic View of Surfaces

Book Surface:  \/\/\/\_/\/\/\_/\/\/
                | | | | | | | | | |   <- Surfaces grinding and catching
Table Surface: /\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\
```

When the book slides, these microscopic peaks and valleys crash into each other, snag, and break off. This constant collision creates a backward force that opposes the motion of the book. Friction is the grand deceiver; it is the ever-present force on Earth that convinced Aristotle motion naturally decays.

Another invisible hand is **air resistance** (or drag). As you ride a bicycle, you must physically push the air molecules out of your way. Every single nitrogen and oxygen molecule you crash into exerts a tiny backward force on you. At low speeds, you barely notice it. But stick your hand out the window of a car traveling at 70 miles per hour, and you will feel the massive, invisible wall of air pushing back against you.

**Gravity** is another persistent force. It is the invisible tether pulling everything toward the center of the Earth. It changes the vertical motion of a thrown ball, bending its otherwise straight, eternal path into a parabolic arc that brings it crashing back to the dirt.

If you can find an environment where you can completely strip away friction, air resistance, and gravity, you will witness Newton's First Law in all its unadulterated glory.

## 5. The Mathematics of Zero: Expressing Inertia in LaTeX

While "Physics for Mortals" avoids overly complex mathematics, understanding how physicists write the First Law provides a beautiful perspective on the concept of "doing nothing."

The First Law is essentially a statement about what happens when the net force on an object is exactly zero. A "net force" is the combined total of all the pushes and pulls acting on an object. 

Imagine you and a friend are playing tug-of-war. You pull to the left with a force of 100 Newtons, and your friend pulls to the right with a force of 100 Newtons. Forces are vectors—they have both a magnitude (size) and a direction. Because your forces are equal and opposite, they cancel each other out. 

We can write this as a mathematical summation:

$$ \sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_{\text{you}} + \mathbf{F}_{\text{friend}} = (-100\text{ N}) + (100\text{ N}) = 0 $$

The Greek letter Sigma ($\sum$) means "the sum of." So, $\sum \mathbf{F}$ means "the sum of all forces," or the net force. The bold $\mathbf{F}$ denotes that it is a vector with direction.

Newton's First Law can be elegantly summarized by this simple conditional statement:

$$ \text{If } \sum \mathbf{F} = 0, \text{ then } \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 0 $$

Let's demystify the second half of that equation: $\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}$. 
*   $\mathbf{v}$ stands for velocity. Velocity is an object's speed *and* its direction. 
*   The $d$ simply means "change in." 
*   $t$ stands for time. 

So, $\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}$ literally translates to "the change in velocity over a change in time." You might recognize this concept by its everyday name: **acceleration**. 

Therefore, the equation tells us: If the total of all forces acting on an object is zero ($\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$), then its acceleration must be zero ($\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 0$). 

If an object has an acceleration of zero, it means its velocity is not changing. If it was moving at 10 meters per second to the North, it continues moving at 10 meters per second to the North. If it was sitting still (a velocity of 0 meters per second), it continues to sit still.

This mathematical expression reveals that rest and constant velocity are two sides of the exact same coin: they are both states of zero acceleration, caused by a net force of zero. 

## 6. Inertia in Everyday Life: Seatbelts, Coffee, and Tablecloths

You might think that a law formulated in the 17th century dealing with frictionless vacuums has little to do with your daily routine. In reality, you fight, utilize, and experience the Law of Inertia every single day.

**The Seatbelt's Lifesaving Job**
Imagine you are a passenger in a car traveling at 60 miles per hour. According to the First Law, you are a body in uniform motion. You possess an inertia that "wants" you to keep moving forward at 60 mph. 

Suddenly, a deer jumps into the road, and the driver slams on the brakes. The brakes apply a massive external friction force to the tires, which drastically slows down the car. 

However, the brakes are attached to the car, not to *you*. Unless a force is applied specifically to your body, your body will obey the Law of Inertia and persevere in its state of uniform motion. The car stops, but you keep moving forward at 60 mph. 

This is where the seatbelt comes in. The seatbelt provides the necessary external force to overcome your inertia. It catches your torso, changing your state of motion so that you decelerate along with the car, preventing you from continuing your 60 mph journey straight through the windshield.

**The Commuter's Coffee Spill**
You are standing on a subway train or a bus, holding a full cup of coffee. The vehicle is at rest, and so is the coffee. The bus driver suddenly steps on the gas to accelerate. 

The bus moves forward. Your feet, anchored to the floor by friction, are pulled forward. But the liquid coffee inside your cup has mass, and therefore, it has inertia. It wants to remain at rest. As the cup is jerked forward by your hand, the coffee stays exactly where it was. Relative to the moving cup, the coffee appears to slosh backward, spilling all over your hand. The coffee didn't move backward; the cup simply moved forward, leaving the coffee behind.

When the bus approaches the next stop and hits the brakes, the opposite happens. The coffee is now traveling at 30 mph. When the bus and the cup stop, the liquid's inertia dictates that it keeps moving forward at 30 mph, spilling over the front rim of the cup. 

**The Magician's Tablecloth Trick**
We have all seen the classic magic trick: a table is fully set with heavy plates, crystal glasses, and silverware. The magician grabs the edge of the silky tablecloth and gives it a sharp, violent yank downward. Miraculously, the cloth vanishes, and the dishes remain perfectly undisturbed on the table.

This is not magic; it is a masterclass in inertia. 

The heavy dishes have a large amount of inertia. They want to remain at rest. The magician uses a tablecloth made of a smooth material like silk to minimize friction. By pulling the cloth extremely fast, the force of friction is only applied to the bottom of the dishes for a tiny fraction of a second. This incredibly brief, weak force is simply not enough to overcome the massive inertia of the heavy dishes. They are effectively "lazy," and before they even have a chance to react to the slight tug, the cloth is gone. 

## 7. Mass: The Measure of Inertia

We have established that objects possess an inherent laziness, an obstinate desire to keep doing what they are doing. But do all objects possess the same amount of laziness? 

Clearly not. Imagine standing on a flat, icy pond (our closest earthly approximation to a frictionless surface). If someone slides a tennis ball toward you, you can stop it easily with your foot. You apply a small force, easily overcoming its inertia and changing its state of motion. 

Now imagine someone slides a solid bowling ball toward you at the exact same speed. Stopping it with your foot will require a significantly larger force and might hurt your toes. 

Finally, imagine someone slides a compact car across the ice toward you at that same speed. No matter how hard you push, you will not be able to stop it. Its inertia is too great; it will simply push you out of the way.

What is the difference between the tennis ball, the bowling ball, and the car? They are all moving at the same speed, but they possess different amounts of **mass**. 

In physics, mass is not just how much an object weighs (weight is the effect of gravity on mass). Instead, **mass is the quantitative measure of an object's inertia**. 

The more mass an object contains, the more matter it is made of, and the more resistance it offers to any change in its motion. A boulder is harder to push than a pebble not because the boulder is heavier, but because the boulder contains vastly more inertia. 

This concept is crucial because it links Newton's First Law to his Second Law (which mathematically defines how forces cause acceleration based on mass: $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$). The First Law tells us that an object resists changes in motion; the concept of mass tells us exactly *how much* it resists. 

## 8. The Cosmic Playground: Inertia Unbound

To see the Law of Inertia operate perfectly, without the muddying effects of Earth's atmosphere and terrain, we must look up. Outer space is the ultimate Galilean laboratory. 

Space is essentially a vacuum. It is incredibly empty. There is no air to create drag, and no ground to create friction. 

In the late 1970s, humanity launched the Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 spacecraft on a grand tour of the outer solar system. These probes utilized chemical rockets to blast off Earth, escape its gravitational pull, and set a course toward the gas giants. 

Once the rockets shut down, the probes were in a vacuum. What happened next? Did they slow down and stop because the engines were off? 

According to Aristotle, they should have stopped immediately. According to Newton, they should continue moving at a constant speed in a straight line forever. Newton, of course, was right. 

Voyager 1 is currently hurtling through interstellar space at a staggering speed of roughly 38,000 miles per hour (61,000 kilometers per hour) relative to the sun. Its engines have been cold for decades. It is not burning any fuel to maintain this speed. It is simply coasting on its own inertia. 

```text
The Journey of Voyager

   Earth                      Interstellar Space
     O -> -> -> -> -> -> -> -> -> -> -> -> | -> -> -> -> -> ∞
   Engines off                             Still moving at 38,000 mph
```

Unless Voyager 1 hits an asteroid or enters the gravitational well of another star (both highly improbable given the vast emptiness of space), it will continue to drift through the Milky Way galaxy at 38,000 mph for millions, perhaps billions, of years. It is a man-made monument to the First Law of Motion.

Similarly, inertia explains planetary orbits, though with a twist. The Earth is moving through space at about 67,000 mph. Its inertia makes it want to fly off in a perfectly straight line into deep space. However, the immense gravity of the Sun acts as a constant, perpendicular external force, pulling the Earth inward. 

The resulting motion is a compromise. The Earth's inertia tries to carry it straight away, while the Sun's gravity tries to pull it straight in. The delicate balance between this inertial tendency to fly straight and the gravitational force pulling it sideways results in the continuous, circular(ish) free-fall we call an orbit. If the Sun's gravity were magically switched off tomorrow, the Earth's inertia would instantly take over, and we would shoot off in a straight tangent line into the frozen darkness of space.

## 9. Frames of Reference: The Illusion of Stillness

Newton's First Law introduces a deeply philosophical and counter-intuitive concept about the nature of reality itself: there is no such thing as "absolute rest." 

Consider this scenario: You are sitting perfectly still in a chair in your living room, reading this article. You feel entirely at rest. 

But are you? 

*   Relative to the floor of your house, your speed is 0 mph. You are at rest.
*   However, your house is attached to the Earth, which is spinning on its axis. If you live near the equator, you are currently moving eastward at about 1,000 mph. 
*   Furthermore, the Earth is orbiting the Sun at 67,000 mph. 
*   The Sun itself is orbiting the center of the Milky Way galaxy at a mind-bending 450,000 mph. 

So, are you at rest, or are you moving at half a million miles per hour? 

According to Galilean relativity (which Newton incorporated into his mechanics), both answers are perfectly correct, depending on your **frame of reference**. 

A frame of reference is simply the perspective from which you are measuring motion. The profound realization of the First Law is that the laws of physics work exactly the same in any "inertial" frame of reference. An inertial frame is one that is either at rest or moving at a constant velocity in a straight line. 

Imagine you are in an airplane flying smoothly at 500 mph, with no turbulence, and all the window blinds are closed. You toss a peanut straight up in the air and catch it. 

Does the peanut fly toward the back of the plane at 500 mph because it's no longer attached to your hand? No. 

Before you tossed it, the peanut was already moving forward at 500 mph (its inertia). When you toss it straight up, it retains that forward inertia. It moves up and down relative to your hand, but it continues to move forward at 500 mph parallel to the plane. From your perspective inside the plane, it behaves exactly as if you were sitting perfectly still on the ground. 

Because the plane is moving at a constant velocity, it is a valid inertial frame of reference. Without looking out the window, no mechanical experiment you can perform inside that cabin can tell you whether you are moving at 500 mph or parked at the gate. The feeling of "speed" doesn't exist; you only feel *acceleration* (changes in speed or direction), which occurs during takeoff, landing, or turbulence.

This means that the "uniform motion" part of the First Law is completely relative. "Doing nothing" (rest) and "moving steadily" (constant velocity) are identical physical states viewed from different perspectives. 

## 10. Corner Cases and Common Misconceptions

To master the concept of inertia, we must clear up a few common traps that trip up physics students and enthusiasts alike.

**Misconception 1: Inertia is a force that keeps things moving.**
This is a linguistic trap. We often say "the car's inertia carried it forward." This makes it sound like inertia is a battery or a fuel that powers motion. It is not. Inertia is a *property* of matter, not a force. A moving object does not need a force to keep it moving; it needs an absence of force. Motion is simply its state of being. 

**Misconception 2: Centrifugal Force.**
Have you ever been on a spinning playground merry-go-round and felt a mysterious force trying to throw you outward, off the edge? We commonly call this "centrifugal force." 

In the framework of Newton's First Law, centrifugal force is considered a "fictitious" or "pseudo" force. It doesn't actually exist as a physical push. 

What you are actually experiencing is your own inertia. Because your body has mass, your inertia wants you to travel in a straight line at a constant speed. But the merry-go-round is spinning in a circle. The floor of the merry-go-round uses friction against your shoes to constantly pull you inward, forcing you off your straight-line path and into a circular path. 

That feeling of being "thrown outward" is merely the sensation of your body's desperate attempt to obey Newton's First Law and travel in a straight line, while the ride forcefully drags you inward in a circle. You aren't being pushed out; the world is being pulled in out from under you.

## 11. The Deep Dive: Where Does Inertia Come From?

Newton provided the rules for how inertia behaves, but he didn't explain *why* matter has this property. Why should mass resist a change in motion? Why does the universe care if an object is accelerating?

This is one of the deepest questions in physics, and it borderlines on philosophy. 

In the late 19th century, the Austrian physicist and philosopher Ernst Mach proposed a radical idea that came to be known as **Mach's Principle**. 

Mach argued that inertia is not an inherent, isolated property of an object. Instead, he proposed that an object's inertia is created by its relationship with all the other matter in the universe. 

Imagine a universe that is completely, utterly empty, except for one single bucket of water. If you spin the bucket, does the water creep up the sides to form a concave shape? 

On Earth, when you spin a bucket, the water pushes against the sides due to inertia, and its surface dips in the middle. But in Mach's empty universe, how does the water "know" it is spinning? Spinning relative to what? If there are no stars, no galaxies, and no other matter to provide a background reference frame, motion itself loses its meaning. 

Mach's Principle suggests that if there were no other matter in the universe, an object would not possess inertia. You could push a massive boulder with a feather, and it would instantly accelerate to immense speeds, because there would be no universal background to resist its change in motion. 

Albert Einstein was deeply fascinated by Mach's Principle, and it heavily influenced his development of General Relativity. While modern physics hasn't entirely adopted Mach's view in its purest form, General Relativity does show that the distribution of mass in the universe warps space and time, heavily influencing the geometry in which objects move. 

The quest to find the ultimate microscopic origin of mass (and therefore inertia) eventually led physicists to the Large Hadron Collider and the discovery of the Higgs Boson in 2012. The Higgs field permeates all of space. As fundamental particles move through this invisible field, they interact with it. Some particles interact strongly with the Higgs field, gaining a lot of mass, causing them to move sluggishly and possess high inertia. Other particles, like photons (light), don't interact with it at all, remaining massless and zipping around at the absolute speed limit of the universe. 

So, when you stub your toe on a heavy table and curse its inertia, you are ultimately experiencing your foot interacting with an object whose fundamental particles are wading through an invisible cosmic molasses known as the Higgs field.

## Conclusion: The Profound Beauty of Doing Nothing

The Law of Inertia forces us to redefine what is "normal" in the cosmos. 

For Aristotle, normal was stillness. Action was required for motion. 
For Newton, normal is continuity. Action is only required for change. 

In our everyday lives, obscured by friction, gravity, and air drag, we are blind to the eternal permanence of motion. We live in an environment that actively works to rob objects of their momentum, tricking us into believing that rest is the default state of existence. 

Yet, when we look past the terrestrial noise, we uncover a universe of spectacular elegance. We find a cosmos where effort is not required to travel, where objects coast endlessly through the dark, and where doing absolutely nothing is the key to moving forever. 

Newton's First Law teaches us that an object in motion is an object at peace. It is only when the universe intervenes with a force—a crash, a pull, a scraping friction—that the peace is broken, the trajectory is altered, and the story changes. Until that moment, the object simply is, persevering in its state, gliding effortlessly through space and time.
