At the dawn of the 20th century, the classical laws of physics faced a crisis when applied to the fundamental building blocks of matter. In this chapter, we journey into the microscopic realm to explore the structure and behavior of atoms and their extremely dense nuclei. We trace the evolution of atomic theory from Rutherford's planetary model to the probability clouds of quantum mechanics. Beyond the electron shell, we will probe the nucleus itself, examining the strong nuclear force that binds nucleons together, the massive energies unlocked in nuclear reactions, and the spontaneous, statistical processes governing radioactive decay.

## 30.1 Early Models of the Atom

By the end of the 19th century, it was well established that matter is composed of atoms. However, the internal structure of the atom remained a profound mystery. The discovery of the electron by J.J. Thomson in 1897 fundamentally shifted the scientific consensus: atoms were not indivisible solid spheres, but rather composite structures containing smaller, charged particles. Because macroscopic matter is generally electrically neutral, scientists reasoned that the atom must contain an equal amount of positive and negative charge. The question then became: how are these charges arranged?

### The Thomson "Plum Pudding" Model

In 1904, J.J. Thomson proposed the first formal model of atomic structure, often referred to as the **plum pudding model**. Thomson envisioned the atom as a spherical volume of continuous positive charge (the "pudding") with negatively charged electrons (the "plums") distributed throughout it.

In this model, the electrons were thought to oscillate around their equilibrium positions within the positive jelly. If disturbed, these oscillating electrons would emit electromagnetic radiation. While this provided a rudimentary qualitative explanation for how atoms might emit light, the model was soon tested—and dismantled—by one of the most famous experiments in the history of physics.

### The Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment

In 1911, under the direction of Ernest Rutherford, physicists Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden conducted an experiment to probe the structure of the atom. They bombarded a remarkably thin sheet of gold foil (only a few hundred atoms thick) with alpha ($\alpha$) particles. Alpha particles are massive, positively charged particles emitted by certain radioactive materials (later identified as helium nuclei, carrying a charge of $+2e$).

Based on Thomson's model, the positive charge and mass of the gold atoms were spread out thinly over the entire volume of each atom. Therefore, the electric field generated by this diffuse positive charge would be relatively weak. Rutherford expected the highly energetic, massive $\alpha$-particles to tear through the gold foil like "cannonballs through tissue paper," experiencing at most only minor deflections.

```text
      THOMSON'S EXPECTATION                      RUTHERFORD'S OBSERVATION

      + - + - + - +                             \           |
    ->+ - + - + - +->                            \          | 
      + - + - + - +                               \  +------+------+ 
    ->+ - + - + - +->                              \ |      |      |
                                                     |    (+) nucleus
Alpha particles pass straight                   <----/      |      |
through diffuse positive charge.                 ---/-------+------+--->
                                                   / Most pass straight,
                                                  /  some strongly deflect.

```

The actual results were astonishing:

1. **Most** $\alpha$-particles passed straight through the foil with zero or negligible deflection.
2. **Some** $\alpha$-particles were deflected at significant angles.
3. **A very small fraction** (about 1 in 8,000) was deflected by more than $90^\circ$, essentially bouncing straight back toward the source.

Rutherford famously remarked on this phenomenon: *"It was quite the most incredible event that has ever happened to me in my life. It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."*

### The Rutherford Planetary Model

To explain these results, Rutherford proposed a new model of the atom in 1911. He concluded that the Thomson model was entirely incorrect. Instead, the atom's positive charge and nearly all of its mass must be concentrated in an incredibly small, dense central region, which he called the **nucleus**.

Rutherford's conclusions established the **planetary model** of the atom:

* The nucleus sits at the center, containing all the positive charge and >99.9% of the atom's mass.
* The electrons orbit the nucleus at vast distances, held in continuous motion by the electrostatic attraction (Coulomb force) between the negative electrons and the positive nucleus.
* Because the nucleus is infinitesimally small compared to the overall orbital radius of the electrons, **the atom is almost entirely empty space**.

#### Estimating the Size of the Nucleus

We can use the principle of conservation of mechanical energy (introduced in Chapter 6) and electric potential energy (Chapter 19) to estimate the maximum possible radius of a gold nucleus. Consider an $\alpha$-particle of mass $m$ and initial velocity $v$ fired directly at a gold nucleus of charge $+Ze$ (where $Z=79$ is the atomic number of gold).

As the $\alpha$-particle approaches the nucleus, the repulsive Coulomb force slows it down. Its kinetic energy is entirely converted into electric potential energy at the point of closest approach, $r_{\text{min}}$, where the particle momentarily stops before reversing direction.

Equating initial kinetic energy to the electric potential energy at $r_{\text{min}}$:

$$ \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = k_e \frac{(2e)(Ze)}{r_{\text{min}}} $$

Solving for the distance of closest approach:

$$ r_{\text{min}} = \frac{4 k_e Z e^2}{mv^2} $$

Using typical $\alpha$-particle energies ($\approx 5 \text{ to } 7 \text{ MeV}$), Rutherford calculated $r_{\text{min}}$ to be on the order of $10^{-14} \text{ m}$. Since the overall radius of an atom is approximately $10^{-10} \text{ m}$, the nucleus is roughly 10,000 to 100,000 times smaller than the atom itself.

### The Collapse of the Planetary Model

While Rutherford's planetary model perfectly explained the scattering data, it suffered from a fatal theoretical flaw when subjected to the laws of classical electromagnetism (Maxwell's equations, covered in Chapter 25).

In the planetary model, electrons move in circular orbits around the nucleus. According to kinematics (Chapter 3), any object moving in a circle is continuously accelerating toward the center (centripetal acceleration). However, classical electromagnetism dictates that **an accelerating electric charge must radiate electromagnetic waves**.

If an orbiting electron continuously radiates energy in the form of electromagnetic waves, its total mechanical energy must decrease. As it loses energy, the electron should spiral rapidly inward, eventually crashing into the nucleus. Calculations based on classical physics predict that such an atomic collapse would occur in a fraction of a nanosecond ($\approx 10^{-11} \text{ s}$).

Furthermore, as the electron spirals inward, its orbital frequency would increase continuously, which means it should emit a continuous spectrum of light. This directly contradicted experimental evidence showing that atoms are stable and emit light only at discrete, quantized frequencies (line spectra).

Classical physics had reached a dead end at the atomic scale. A radically new framework was required to resolve the paradox of atomic stability, setting the stage for the quantum revolution.

## 30.2 The Bohr Model of the Hydrogen Atom

To resolve the catastrophic collapse predicted by applying classical physics to Rutherford's planetary model, Danish physicist Niels Bohr proposed a radical new model in 1913. Bohr realized that classical mechanics and electromagnetism were insufficient to describe the atomic realm. By incorporating the emerging quantum ideas of Max Planck and Albert Einstein, Bohr constructed a semi-classical model that successfully explained the stability of the hydrogen atom and its discrete emission spectrum.

Because hydrogen is the simplest atom—consisting of a single proton and a single electron—it served as the perfect testing ground for Bohr's hypothesis.

### Bohr's Postulates

Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom was built upon three fundamental postulates that boldly contradicted classical physics:

1. **Stationary States:** The electron orbits the nucleus in specific, allowed circular paths called *stationary states*. While in one of these discrete orbits, the electron does not radiate electromagnetic energy, despite its centripetal acceleration. Therefore, the atom remains entirely stable.
2. **Quantization of Angular Momentum:** Only certain orbital radii are allowed. The allowed orbits are those for which the electron's orbital angular momentum, $L$, is an integer multiple of Planck's reduced constant, $\hbar$ (where $\hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi}$).

$$ L = m_e v r = n\hbar $$

Here, $m_e$ is the electron mass, $v$ is its orbital speed, $r$ is the orbital radius, and $n$ is an integer ($n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$) known as the **principal quantum number**.
3. **Radiative Transitions:** An electron can "jump" between these stationary states. The atom emits a photon when an electron drops from a higher energy state ($E_i$) to a lower energy state ($E_f$). Conversely, the atom absorbs a photon to jump from a lower state to a higher state. The energy of the photon matches the energy difference between the states, conforming to Einstein's photoelectric equation:

$$ E_i - E_f = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} $$

### Derivation of Orbital Radii and Energy Levels

Using classical mechanics combined with his quantization postulate, Bohr derived expressions for the allowed radii and energies of the hydrogen atom.

First, consider the classical forces. The centripetal force required to keep the electron in a circular orbit is provided entirely by the electrostatic (Coulomb) attraction between the positive proton and the negative electron:

$$ \frac{m_e v^2}{r} = k_e \frac{e^2}{r^2} $$

where $k_e$ is Coulomb's constant and $e$ is the elementary charge.

We can solve Bohr's angular momentum postulate for velocity: $v = \frac{n\hbar}{m_e r}$. Substituting this into the force equation gives:

$$ \frac{m_e}{r} \left( \frac{n\hbar}{m_e r} \right)^2 = k_e \frac{e^2}{r^2} $$

Solving for the radius $r$ yields the allowed orbital radii, $r_n$:

$$ r_n = \left( \frac{\hbar^2}{m_e k_e e^2} \right) n^2 $$

For the lowest energy state ($n = 1$), this radius is a fundamental physical constant known as the **Bohr radius**, denoted $a_0$:

$$ a_0 = \frac{\hbar^2}{m_e k_e e^2} \approx 0.529 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m} = 0.0529 \text{ nm} $$

Thus, the allowed radii scale with the square of the principal quantum number:

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0 $$

Next, we evaluate the total mechanical energy of the electron, which is the sum of its kinetic energy ($K$) and electric potential energy ($U$). From the force equation, we know that $m_e v^2 = k_e \frac{e^2}{r}$, so:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2} m_e v^2 = \frac{k_e e^2}{2r} $$

$$ U = -k_e \frac{e^2}{r} $$

$$ E = K + U = \frac{k_e e^2}{2r} - k_e \frac{e^2}{r} = -\frac{k_e e^2}{2r} $$

Substituting the quantized radius $r_n$ into this energy equation yields the allowed energy levels, $E_n$:

$$ E_n = -\left( \frac{m_e k_e^2 e^4}{2\hbar^2} \right) \frac{1}{n^2} $$

Evaluating the constants in the parentheses gives a value of $13.6 \text{ eV}$. Therefore, the energy levels of the hydrogen atom are described by:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2} $$

The lowest energy level ($n = 1$) is $E_1 = -13.6 \text{ eV}$ and is called the **ground state**. The negative sign indicates that the electron is bound to the nucleus; energy must be supplied to free the electron (to reach $E = 0$ at $r = \infty$).

### Energy Level Diagrams and Spectral Lines

Bohr's model brilliantly explained the discrete emission spectrum of hydrogen, which had been empirically described by the Rydberg formula decades earlier but remained a theoretical mystery.

When hydrogen gas is excited (e.g., by an electrical discharge), electrons are bumped up to higher energy levels ($n > 1$). As they cascade back down to lower levels, they emit photons.

Different sets of transitions correspond to specific spectral series:

* **Lyman Series:** Transitions from higher levels down to $n=1$. These energetic jumps produce high-frequency photons in the ultraviolet region.
* **Balmer Series:** Transitions down to $n=2$. These jumps produce photons in the visible light spectrum (e.g., the distinct red, blue-green, and violet lines of hydrogen).
* **Paschen Series:** Transitions down to $n=3$. These produce photons in the infrared region.

Using Bohr's energy equation and his transition postulate ($hf = E_i - E_f$), the wavelength $\lambda$ of an emitted photon can be written as:

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{E_i - E_f}{hc} = \left( \frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{hc} \right) \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right) $$

The collection of constants $\left(\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{hc}\right)$ evaluates exactly to the Rydberg constant ($R_H \approx 1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$), proving that Bohr's theoretical model perfectly recovered the empirical observations of 19th-century spectroscopists.

### Limitations of the Bohr Model

Despite its monumental success with hydrogen, the Bohr model was a transitional theory with significant limitations:

1. **Multi-Electron Atoms:** The model fails to accurately predict the energy levels or spectral lines of any atom with more than one electron (like helium or lithium). It only works for hydrogenic (one-electron) ions, such as $\text{He}^+$ and $\text{Li}^{2+}$.
2. **Spectral Line Intensity:** The model cannot explain why certain spectral lines are brighter (more probable) than others.
3. **Fine Structure:** High-resolution spectroscopy revealed that some single spectral lines were actually pairs of very closely spaced lines. The Bohr model could not account for this "fine structure" (which was later explained by electron spin).
4. **Violation of Uncertainty:** The model specifies exact, simultaneous values for the electron's position (radius) and momentum, which fundamentally violates the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle (Section 29.6).

To address these shortcomings, physicists had to abandon the idea of definitive circular orbits entirely, paving the way for the full, wave-mechanical treatment of the atom: Quantum Mechanics.

## 30.3 Quantum Mechanics of the Hydrogen Atom

The Bohr model was a crucial stepping stone, but its reliance on classical circular orbits ultimately failed to capture the true nature of the electron. In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger formulated a comprehensive theory of quantum mechanics based on wave-particle duality. Instead of treating the electron as a particle moving in a deterministic path, Schrödinger's approach modeled the electron as a three-dimensional standing wave surrounding the nucleus.

To describe the hydrogen atom quantum mechanically, one must solve the **Schrödinger equation** in three dimensions. The electric potential energy function governing the electron-proton interaction is the same Coulomb potential used in the Bohr model:

$$ U(r) = -k_e \frac{e^2}{r} $$

Because this potential depends only on the radial distance $r$ from the nucleus (a central force), the Schrödinger equation is best solved using spherical polar coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$. The solutions to this differential equation are mathematically complex wave functions, denoted by $\psi(r, \theta, \phi)$.

In quantum mechanics, the wave function itself does not have a direct physical meaning, but its absolute square, $|\psi|^2$, represents the **probability density**—the probability per unit volume of finding the electron at a specific location in space. This completely replaces the concept of a definitive orbit with a "cloud" of probability.

### The Quantum Numbers

Solving the 3D Schrödinger equation naturally yields a set of three integers that act as boundary conditions for the electron's wave function. These are the **quantum numbers**. A fourth quantum number, arising from relativistic effects and experimental observation, is also required to fully describe the electron's state.

#### 1. The Principal Quantum Number ($n$)

The principal quantum number determines the total energy of the atom and the overall size of the electron cloud. It is analogous to the $n$ used in the Bohr model.

* **Allowed values:** $n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$
* **Energy:** The energy levels of the hydrogen atom depend almost entirely on $n$ and match Bohr's derivation:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2} $$

All states with the same principal quantum number are said to form an electron **shell**.

#### 2. The Orbital Quantum Number ($l$)

This number determines the magnitude of the electron's orbital angular momentum, $L$, and defines the shape of the electron cloud.

* **Allowed values:** $l = 0, 1, 2, \dots, (n - 1)$
* **Angular Momentum Magnitude:** Unlike Bohr's prediction ($L = n\hbar$), quantum mechanics dictates:

$$ L = \sqrt{l(l + 1)}\hbar $$

States with different values of $l$ are traditionally designated by letters—a relic from early spectroscopy:

* $l = 0$ is an **s state** (spherical cloud)
* $l = 1$ is a **p state** (dumbbell-shaped cloud)
* $l = 2$ is a **d state** (clover-shaped cloud)
* $l = 3$ is an **f state**

States with the same $n$ and $l$ form a **subshell** (e.g., the 2p subshell corresponds to $n=2$ and $l=1$).

#### 3. The Magnetic Quantum Number ($m_l$)

The magnetic quantum number specifies the orientation of the electron's orbital angular momentum vector in space. This phenomenon is known as **space quantization**. If an external magnetic field is applied (typically defined along the $z$-axis), the angular momentum vector can only point in specific directions such that its $z$-component, $L_z$, is quantized.

* **Allowed values:** $m_l = -l, -(l - 1), \dots, 0, \dots, +(l - 1), +l$
* **$z$-component of Angular Momentum:**

$$ L_z = m_l \hbar $$

For example, if $l=1$ (a p state), $m_l$ can be $-1, 0,$ or $+1$. This means the dumbbell-shaped probability cloud can orient itself in three distinct ways along the three spatial axes ($p_x, p_y, p_z$).

#### 4. The Spin Magnetic Quantum Number ($m_s$)

In 1925, the Stern-Gerlach experiment demonstrated that electrons possess an intrinsic form of angular momentum, completely independent of their orbital motion. This is called **spin**. It is a purely quantum mechanical property with no classical analog (the electron is not physically spinning like a top).

* **Allowed values:** $m_s = +\frac{1}{2}$ ("spin up") or $m_s = -\frac{1}{2}$ ("spin down")
* **$z$-component of Spin Angular Momentum:**

$$ S_z = m_s \hbar $$

### Degeneracy

A complete quantum state of a hydrogen electron is defined by the set of four quantum numbers: $(n, l, m_l, m_s)$.

Because the energy of the hydrogen atom depends only on $n$ ($E_n = -13.6 \text{ eV}/n^2$), multiple distinct states can share the exact same energy. This condition is called **degeneracy**. For example, the $n=2$ energy level contains an $s$ subshell ($l=0$, one orientation) and a $p$ subshell ($l=1$, three orientations). Accounting for the two possible spin states for each, there are 8 entirely different quantum states that all have an energy of $-3.40 \text{ eV}$.

### Probability Clouds and the Radial Probability Density

The concept of atomic orbitals replaces Bohr's planetary orbits. We can visualize the ground state of hydrogen ($n=1, l=0, m_l=0$) as a spherically symmetric cloud of negative charge density surrounding the nucleus.

To determine where the electron is most likely to be found, physicists use the **radial probability density function**, $P(r)$. This function represents the probability of finding the electron within a thin spherical shell of radius $r$ and thickness $dr$.

For the 1s ground state, the radial probability function is:

$$ P(r) = \frac{4}{a_0^3} r^2 e^{-2r/a_0} $$

where $a_0$ is the Bohr radius ($0.0529 \text{ nm}$).

```text
       Radial Probability Density P(r) for the 1s State

      |     *     *
      |   *         *

      |*                   *
      |*                      * *

      +---|-----------------------------------* * *-----> r

```

Notice a profound connection between the two models: calculating the maximum of this probability curve (taking the derivative of $P(r)$ with respect to $r$ and setting it to zero) reveals that the curve peaks exactly at $r = a_0$.

This means that while the electron could theoretically be found anywhere from $r=0$ to $r=\infty$, **the most probable distance** of the electron from the nucleus in the ground state of hydrogen is precisely the Bohr radius. Quantum mechanics fundamentally altered our view of atomic reality, substituting strict orbits for probabilities, yet perfectly reproduced the successful mathematical predictions of the older models.

## 30.4 Properties of Nuclei and Binding Energy

While quantum mechanics successfully mapped the behavior of the electron cloud, the nucleus remained relatively unexplored. The nucleus contains more than 99.9% of an atom's mass, compressed into a volume billions of times smaller than the atom itself. Understanding the properties of the nucleus requires examining the particles it contains and the fundamental forces that hold them together.

### Nucleons and Nuclear Notation

The nucleus is composed of two types of particles, collectively called **nucleons**:

1. **Protons:** Positively charged particles ($+e$) with a mass of $m_p \approx 1.6726 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$.
2. **Neutrons:** Electrically neutral particles with a mass of $m_n \approx 1.6749 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$, slightly more massive than a proton.

The composition of any nucleus is described by three integers:

* **Atomic Number ($Z$):** The number of protons. This defines the chemical element.
* **Neutron Number ($N$):** The number of neutrons.
* **Mass Number ($A$):** The total number of nucleons in the nucleus.

$$ A = Z + N $$

A specific nuclear species, or **nuclide**, is represented using the standard chemical symbol for the element ($\text{X}$), with the mass number as a superscript and the atomic number as a subscript:

$$ {}^A_Z\text{X} $$

For example, the most common form of carbon has 6 protons and 6 neutrons, written as ${}^{12}_{\phantom{0}6}\text{C}$.

Nuclei that have the same atomic number ($Z$) but different neutron numbers ($N$) are called **isotopes**. Because chemical properties are dictated by the electron cloud (which depends strictly on $Z$), isotopes of a given element are chemically identical, even though their nuclear properties (like mass and stability) can differ drastically. For example, hydrogen has three isotopes:

* ${}^1_1\text{H}$: Normal Hydrogen (1 proton, 0 neutrons)
* ${}^2_1\text{H}$: Deuterium (1 proton, 1 neutron)
* ${}^3_1\text{H}$: Tritium (1 proton, 2 neutrons)

### Size and Density of the Nucleus

High-energy electron scattering experiments have revealed that nuclei are roughly spherical. The radius $r$ of a nucleus scales with the total number of nucleons $A$ according to the empirical formula:

$$ r = r_0 A^{1/3} $$

where $r_0$ is a constant approximately equal to **1.2 fm** (femtometers, where $1 \text{ fm} = 10^{-15} \text{ m}$).

This simple relationship implies something profound about nuclear material. If we assume the nucleus is a sphere, its volume $V$ is:

$$ V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 = \frac{4}{3} \pi (r_0 A^{1/3})^3 = \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r_0^3 \right) A $$

Because the volume is directly proportional to the mass number $A$, the volume *per nucleon* is constant. This means that all nuclei, from light carbon to heavy uranium, have roughly the same density. This **nuclear density** is incomprehensibly large—approximately $2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3$. A macroscopic object with this density, the size of a marble, would weigh hundreds of millions of tons.

### The Strong Nuclear Force

If the nucleus is packed densely with positively charged protons, it should explode instantaneously due to the massive electrostatic (Coulomb) repulsion between them. The fact that stable nuclei exist proves the existence of another fundamental force: the **strong nuclear force**.

The strong force is the underlying "glue" of the universe, characterized by several key properties:

1. **It is strictly attractive at short ranges:** It overcomes the Coulomb repulsion between protons to hold the nucleus together.
2. **It is charge-independent:** The strong force acts equally between two protons, two neutrons, or a proton and a neutron.
3. **It is extremely short-ranged:** The strong force is dominant at distances around **1 fm**, but it drops to zero extremely rapidly beyond **2 to 3 fm**. Unlike gravity and electromagnetism, it does not exert influence over macroscopic distances.
4. **It becomes repulsive at incredibly short distances:** If nucleons are pushed closer than about **0.5 fm**, the strong force violently repels them. This "hard core" prevents the nucleus from collapsing in on itself and explains the constant density of nuclear matter.

### Mass Defect and Binding Energy

If you were to place two protons and two neutrons on a theoretical scale, and then place a helium-4 nucleus (${}^4_2\text{He}$) on another, you would find a discrepancy: the fully assembled helium nucleus is less massive than the sum of its isolated parts.

Where did the missing mass go? It was converted into energy, governed by Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$.

When individual nucleons are brought together by the strong force to form a nucleus, a tremendous amount of energy is released. Conversely, to tear a stable nucleus apart into individual protons and neutrons, you must supply that exact same amount of energy. This required energy is called the **binding energy** ($BE$).

The difference in mass between the separated nucleons and the bound nucleus is the **mass defect** ($\Delta m$):

$$ \Delta m = [Z m_p + N m_n] - M_{\text{nucleus}} $$

The total binding energy of the nucleus is simply the mass defect multiplied by the speed of light squared:

$$ BE = \Delta m \cdot c^2 $$

In nuclear physics, it is much more convenient to measure mass in **atomic mass units (u)** rather than kilograms. One atomic mass unit is defined as exactly $1/12$ the mass of a neutral carbon-12 atom. The conversion factors are:

* **1 u** = $1.660539 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$
* **1 u** corresponds to an energy equivalent of **931.5 MeV** ($1 \text{ MeV} = 10^6 \text{ eV}$).

Using atomic mass units, the binding energy formula becomes extremely practical for calculations:

$$ BE = (\Delta m \text{ in u}) \times 931.5 \text{ MeV/u} $$

### Binding Energy per Nucleon

While the total binding energy tells us how much energy is holding a specific nucleus together, it is not the best measure of nuclear stability. Heavy nuclei naturally have higher total binding energies simply because they have more nucleons.

To compare the relative stability of different nuclei, physicists use the **binding energy per nucleon** ($BE / A$). This value indicates how tightly bound, on average, each individual nucleon is within a specific nucleus.

Plotting the $BE / A$ against the mass number $A$ yields one of the most important graphs in all of physics:

**Key Features of the Curve:**

1. **The Peak at Iron:** The curve rises steeply for light nuclei, reaches a maximum around **8.8 MeV/nucleon** near mass number $A = 56$ (isotopes of iron and nickel), and then gradually decreases for heavier nuclei. Iron-56 is one of the most tightly bound and stable nuclei in the universe.
2. **Implications for Nuclear Fission:** Heavy nuclei (like uranium, $A \approx 238$) have a lower binding energy per nucleon (about 7.6 MeV) than mid-mass nuclei. If a massive nucleus splits into two lighter, mid-mass nuclei, the nucleons transition to a more tightly bound state. The difference in binding energy is released as massive amounts of kinetic energy and radiation. This is **nuclear fission**.
3. **Implications for Nuclear Fusion:** Light nuclei (like isotopes of hydrogen) have very low binding energies per nucleon. If two light nuclei are forced together to form a heavier nucleus, the nucleons drop into a much deeper potential energy well. This process, which powers the sun and other stars, is **nuclear fusion**. Gram for gram, fusion releases significantly more energy than fission because the slope of the curve is much steeper at the low-$A$ end than at the high-$A$ end.

## 30.5 Radioactivity and Decay Processes

As we saw in the previous section, the strong nuclear force is responsible for holding the nucleus together against the immense electrostatic repulsion of the protons. However, not all combinations of protons and neutrons are stable. When a nucleus is unstable—perhaps because it is too massive or has an unbalanced ratio of neutrons to protons—it will eventually undergo a spontaneous transformation to reach a more stable, lower-energy state. This spontaneous emission of particles or electromagnetic radiation is called **radioactivity**.

Discovered accidentally by Henri Becquerel in 1896 and extensively studied by Marie and Pierre Curie, radioactivity is a purely quantum mechanical, statistical process. We cannot predict exactly *when* a specific nucleus will decay, but we can predict the behavior of a large ensemble of nuclei with extreme precision.

There are three primary mechanisms by which an unstable nucleus (the **parent nucleus**) decays into a more stable one (the **daughter nucleus**): alpha ($\alpha$), beta ($\beta$), and gamma ($\gamma$) decay.

### Alpha ($\alpha$) Decay

In alpha decay, an unstable, heavy nucleus emits an alpha particle, which is a tightly bound package of two protons and two neutrons—identical to a helium-4 nucleus (${}^4_2\text{He}$).

Because the $\alpha$-particle carries away two protons and two neutrons, the daughter nucleus has a mass number $A$ that is 4 less than the parent, and an atomic number $Z$ that is 2 less:

$$ {}^A_Z\text{X} \longrightarrow {}^{A-4}_{Z-2}\text{Y} + {}^4_2\text{He} $$

For this process to occur spontaneously, energy must be released. This energy, called the **disintegration energy** or **$Q$-value**, comes from the conversion of mass into energy. It is calculated using the mass defect between the initial state and the final state:

$$ Q = (M_X - M_Y - M_\alpha)c^2 $$

If $Q > 0$, the decay can happen spontaneously. The $Q$-value appears as the kinetic energy shared by the emitted $\alpha$-particle and the recoiling daughter nucleus. Because the daughter nucleus is typically much more massive than the $\alpha$-particle, conservation of momentum dictates that the $\alpha$-particle carries away the vast majority of this kinetic energy.

A famous example is the decay of Uranium-238 into Thorium-234:

$$ {}^{238}_{\phantom{0}92}\text{U} \longrightarrow {}^{234}_{\phantom{0}90}\text{Th} + {}^4_2\text{He} $$

### Beta ($\beta$) Decay

Beta decay occurs when a nucleus has too many or too few neutrons relative to protons to be stable. There are two main types of beta decay: $\beta^-$ and $\beta^+$.

**1. Beta-Minus ($\beta^-$) Decay:**
An overly neutron-rich nucleus transforms a neutron into a proton, emitting an electron ($e^-$) and an uncharged, nearly massless particle called an **electron antineutrino** ($\bar{\nu}_e$). The atomic number increases by 1, while the mass number remains unchanged:

$$ {}^A_Z\text{X} \longrightarrow {}^{\phantom{Z+}A}_{Z+1}\text{Y} + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e $$

Example (Carbon-14 dating relies on this decay):

$$ {}^{14}_{\phantom{0}6}\text{C} \longrightarrow {}^{14}_{\phantom{0}7}\text{N} + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e $$

**2. Beta-Plus ($\beta^+$) Decay:**
An overly proton-rich nucleus transforms a proton into a neutron, emitting a positron ($e^+$, the antimatter counterpart to the electron) and an **electron neutrino** ($\nu_e$). The atomic number decreases by 1:

$$ {}^A_Z\text{X} \longrightarrow {}^{\phantom{Z-}A}_{Z-1}\text{Y} + e^+ + \nu_e $$

*Note on the Neutrino:* When $\beta$-decay was first observed, electrons were emitted with a continuous spectrum of kinetic energies, rather than a single distinct energy. This appeared to violate the conservation of energy and momentum. In 1930, Wolfgang Pauli proposed a "desperate remedy": an undetected, neutral, practically massless particle was carrying away the missing energy and momentum. This particle was the neutrino, which was finally observed experimentally in 1956.

### Gamma ($\gamma$) Decay

After a nucleus undergoes $\alpha$ or $\beta$ decay, the resulting daughter nucleus is often left in an excited, high-energy state (much like an electron in a hydrogen atom can be in an excited state). To return to its lowest energy state (ground state), the nucleus emits a high-energy photon, known as a **gamma ray** ($\gamma$).

An excited nucleus is denoted with an asterisk ($*$). Because a photon carries no mass and no charge, $\gamma$-decay does not change $A$ or $Z$:

$$ {}^A_Z\text{X}^* \longrightarrow {}^A_Z\text{X} + \gamma $$

Gamma rays are incredibly energetic (typically in the MeV range) and are highly penetrating, requiring thick shielding, such as lead or concrete, to be stopped.

### The Radioactive Decay Law

Radioactive decay is a statistical process. We define the **decay constant** ($\lambda$) as the probability per unit time that a specific nucleus will decay. If we have a sample containing $N$ radioactive nuclei, the rate at which they decay, $\frac{dN}{dt}$, is directly proportional to the number of nuclei present:

$$ \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N $$

The negative sign indicates that $N$ is decreasing over time. Solving this simple differential equation yields the **exponential decay law**:

$$ N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

where $N_0$ is the initial number of radioactive nuclei at $t = 0$.

The decay rate itself is called the **activity** ($R$) of the sample. Activity is simply the magnitude of $\frac{dN}{dt}$:

$$ R = \left| \frac{dN}{dt} \right| = \lambda N = \lambda N_0 e^{-\lambda t} = R_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

The SI unit of activity is the **becquerel (Bq)**, where $1 \text{ Bq} = 1 \text{ decay/second}$. Another common, older unit is the **curie (Ci)**, defined originally by the activity of 1 gram of radium ($1 \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}$).

#### Half-Life

A more intuitive way to express the lifespan of a radioactive isotope is its **half-life** ($T_{1/2}$). The half-life is the time required for exactly half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.

```text
    Number of Remaining Nuclei (N)
       |
   N_0 | * 
       |   *
 N_0/2 |-----* (1st half-life: 50% remains)
       |      *
 N_0/4 |------- * (2nd half-life: 25% remains)
       |         * 
 N_0/8 |---------- * (3rd half-life: 12.5% remains)
       |             *    
       |                   *        *
       +---|------|---------|--------------- Time (t)
           T_1/2  2T_1/2    3T_1/2

```

We can find the mathematical relationship between the half-life and the decay constant by setting $N(t) = \frac{N_0}{2}$ at $t = T_{1/2}$:

$$ \frac{N_0}{2} = N_0 e^{-\lambda T_{1/2}} $$

$$ \frac{1}{2} = e^{-\lambda T_{1/2}} $$

$$ \ln(2) = \lambda T_{1/2} $$

Solving for $T_{1/2}$:

$$ T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda} $$

Half-lives vary enormously depending on the isotope. For example, Uranium-238 has a half-life of 4.5 billion years (roughly the age of the Earth), Carbon-14 has a half-life of 5,730 years, and Francium-223 has a half-life of just 22 minutes.

## Chapter Summary

**Chapter 30: Atomic and Nuclear Physics**

* **30.1 Early Models of the Atom:** The Thomson "plum pudding" model was disproved by the Rutherford gold foil experiment. Rutherford demonstrated that atoms consist of a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space. Classical physics, however, falsely predicted that orbiting electrons in this planetary model would radiate energy and immediately collapse into the nucleus.
* **30.2 The Bohr Model of the Hydrogen Atom:** Niels Bohr saved atomic stability by postulating that electrons orbit in discrete, quantized "stationary states" defined by $L = n\hbar$. Atoms emit or absorb photons only when an electron jumps between these states ($hf = \Delta E$). This model accurately predicted the hydrogen emission spectrum but failed for multi-electron atoms.
* **30.3 Quantum Mechanics of the Hydrogen Atom:** Schrödinger's wave mechanics replaced Bohr's definitive orbits with probability clouds (orbitals). An electron's state is completely described by four quantum numbers: principal ($n$), orbital ($l$), magnetic ($m_l$), and spin ($m_s$).
* **30.4 Properties of Nuclei and Binding Energy:** Nuclei are tightly packed spheres of protons and neutrons (nucleons) held together by the short-range, incredibly powerful strong nuclear force. The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its parts; this mass defect corresponds to the nuclear binding energy ($BE = \Delta m c^2$). The binding energy per nucleon peaks around Iron-56, explaining the energy release in both nuclear fission (heavy nuclei splitting) and nuclear fusion (light nuclei combining).
* **30.5 Radioactivity and Decay Processes:** Unstable nuclei spontaneously decay to lower energy states via Alpha ($\alpha$) decay (emitting a He nucleus), Beta ($\beta$) decay (emitting an electron/positron and a neutrino), or Gamma ($\gamma$) decay (emitting a high-energy photon). Radioactive decay is a statistical process governed by the exponential decay law $N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$, where the rate of decay is characterized by the decay constant $\lambda$ or the half-life $T_{1/2}$.
