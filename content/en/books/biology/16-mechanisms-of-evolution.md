Before Darwin and Wallace proposed natural selection, species were viewed as immutable. This chapter explores the paradigm shift that united biology. We examine the conceptual foundations and empirical evidence for evolution across the fossil record, comparative anatomy, and molecular biology. Transitioning from individuals to populations, we introduce microevolution to track how allele frequencies shift over generations. Finally, we establish the Hardy-Weinberg baseline to analyze how forces like mutation, genetic drift, gene flow, and natural selection dynamically shape the vast diversity of life on Earth.

## 16.1 Darwin, Wallace, and the Theory of Natural Selection

The publication of Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* in 1859 marks a profound paradigm shift in biological thought. Before this period, the prevailing Western worldview held that species were immutable—created in their current forms and completely unchanging. While earlier philosophers and naturalists, notably Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, proposed that life evolves as environments change, the proposed mechanisms (such as the inheritance of acquired physical characteristics) lacked empirical support. The breakthrough came when two British naturalists, Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace, independently conceived of a coherent, observable mechanism for evolution: **natural selection**.

### The Conceptual Foundations of Natural Selection

Darwin’s formulation of natural selection was heavily influenced by disciplines outside of traditional biology, particularly geology and economics.

**1. Uniformitarianism (Charles Lyell):**
During his five-year voyage on the *HMS Beagle* (1831–1836), Darwin read Charles Lyell’s *Principles of Geology*. Lyell argued that the geological processes shaping the Earth today (like erosion and volcanic activity) are the same processes that operated in the past, operating at the same gradual rate. This concept, known as uniformitarianism, implied two critical things for Darwin: the Earth must be ancient—vastly older than the few thousand years traditionally believed—and slow, subtle processes acting over immense periods can produce substantial biological changes.

**2. The Principle of Population (Thomas Malthus):**
In 1838, Darwin read an essay by the English economist Thomas Malthus. Malthus observed that human populations have the potential to grow exponentially, while food supplies increase only arithmetically. This leads to an inevitable point of crisis, resulting in famine, disease, and competition.

In population ecology, this unconstrained exponential growth is often expressed using the differential equation for population growth:

$$ \frac{dN}{dt} = rN $$

where $N$ is the population size, $t$ is time, and $r$ is the intrinsic rate of increase. Integrating this yields the exponential growth model:

$$ N(t) = N_0 e^{rt} $$

Darwin recognized that this mathematical reality applies universally to all species. Because organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support, a continuous "struggle for existence" occurs.

### Wallace’s Parallel Journey

While Darwin spent decades meticulously compiling evidence from his *Beagle* voyage, particularly analyzing the endemic finches and tortoises of the Galápagos Islands, Alfred Russel Wallace was conducting fieldwork in the Amazon River basin and later the Malay Archipelago.

Stricken with a fever in Indonesia in 1858, Wallace had an epiphany regarding the survival of the fittest that perfectly mirrored Darwin’s unpublished theory. Wallace sent a manuscript outlining his ideas directly to Darwin. To ensure fair credit, excerpts from Wallace's essay and Darwin’s earlier unpublished sketches were presented together to the Linnean Society of London in July 1858. This event spurred Darwin to finalize and publish his comprehensive theory the following year.

### The Logic of Natural Selection

Natural selection is not a random process; it is the non-random survival and reproductive success of individuals based on their phenotypic traits. The theory is built upon two observable facts and two logical inferences:

* **Observation 1: Overproduction and Struggle for Existence.** All species have such high reproductive potential that their population size would increase exponentially if all individuals born reproduced successfully. Yet, populations tend to remain stable in size, and environmental resources are limited.
* **Observation 2: Heritable Variation.** Individuals within a population vary extensively in their characteristics; no two individuals are exactly alike. Much of this variation is heritable (passed from parent to offspring).
* **Inference 1: Unequal Reproductive Success.** Individuals whose inherited traits give them a higher probability of surviving and reproducing in a given environment tend to leave more offspring than other individuals.
* **Inference 2: Descent with Modification.** This unequal ability of individuals to survive and reproduce will lead to the gradual accumulation of favorable traits in the population over generations.

The logical flow of this mechanism can be visualized as follows:

```text
[ Overproduction of Offspring ]  +  [ Heritable Phenotypic Variation ]
               |                                     |
               v                                     v
     [ Resource Limitation ]             [ Environmental Pressures ]
               |                                     |
               +------------------+------------------+
                                  |
                                  v
                     [ THE STRUGGLE FOR EXISTENCE ]
                                  |
                                  v
                  [ UNEQUAL REPRODUCTIVE SUCCESS ]
                       (Natural Selection)
                                  |
                                  v
                [ ACCUMULATION OF FAVORABLE TRAITS ]
                    (Descent with Modification)

```

### Key Clarifications and Common Misconceptions

To fully grasp the mechanism of natural selection, several fundamental rules must be explicitly understood:

1. **Populations Evolve, Not Individuals:** Natural selection acts on the traits of *individuals* (they either survive and reproduce, or they do not), but the evolutionary impact is only measurable as changes in the genetic composition of a *population* over time. An individual organism cannot evolve during its lifespan.
2. **Selection Acts on Existing Variation:** Natural selection cannot create new traits on demand. It acts only as an editing mechanism, increasing or decreasing the frequency of heritable variations that already exist in the population due to random mutations.
3. **Evolution is Not Goal-Oriented:** Natural selection does not strive toward perfection or complexity. A trait that is favorable in one environment may be detrimental if the environment changes. Fitness is strictly contextual and tied to the immediate, local environment.

### Lamarck vs. Darwin-Wallace

To contrast the pre-Darwinian paradigm with the modern view, consider how both theories explain the elongation of the giraffe's neck:

| Feature | Lamarckian Evolution (Discarded) | Darwinian Evolution (Accepted) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mechanism** | Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics | Natural Selection |
| **Process** | Ancestors stretched their necks to reach high leaves. The physical act of stretching permanently lengthened the neck, and this longer neck was passed to offspring. | The ancestral population contained individuals with varying neck lengths. Those with naturally longer necks could reach more food, survived better, and left more offspring. |
| **Direction** | Directed by the organism's inner "need" or effort. | Driven by environmental pressures acting on random genetic variations. |

Through the mechanism of natural selection, Darwin and Wallace provided a testable, observable explanation for the adaptation of organisms to their environments and the vast diversity of life on Earth.

## 16.2 Anatomical, Molecular, and Fossil Evidence for Evolution

The theory of evolution is supported by a robust and diverse array of empirical data drawn from multiple independent scientific disciplines. When distinct lines of inquiry—such as paleontology, comparative anatomy, and molecular biology—converge on the same conclusion, the scientific validity of that conclusion is significantly strengthened. This consilience of evidence provides the foundational proof for descent with modification.

### The Fossil Record

Fossils are the preserved remains or traces of organisms from the distant past, typically found in sedimentary rock. The fossil record provides a chronological archive of evolutionary history, demonstrating that the vast majority of species that once lived are now extinct and that extant species represent a fraction of Earth's historical biodiversity.

**Stratigraphy and Superposition**
Sedimentary rocks form in layers called strata. According to the law of superposition, younger strata are deposited on top of older ones. This provides a relative timeline of life on Earth.

```text
       SURFACE 
=====================  [Stratum A: 10 MYA]  --> Abundant mammalian & modern avian fossils
---------------------  
=====================  [Stratum B: 50 MYA]  --> Extinct reptilian lineages, early mammals
---------------------
=====================  [Stratum C: 200 MYA] --> Giant ferns, early dinosaurs
---------------------
=====================  [Stratum D: 400 MYA] --> Jawless fishes, trilobites
       BEDROCK

```

**Transitional Fossils**
A common misconception is that the fossil record is entirely fragmented. In reality, paleontologists have discovered numerous transitional fossils that display intermediate traits between ancestral groups and their derived descendants. A classic example is *Tiktaalik roseae*, which exhibits both fish-like characteristics (gills, scales) and tetrapod-like characteristics (a neck, functional wrist bones), bridging the evolutionary gap between lobe-finned fishes and early amphibians. Another prominent example is *Archaeopteryx*, which possesses both non-avian dinosaur features (teeth, a long bony tail) and avian features (flight feathers).

**Absolute Dating**
While stratigraphy provides relative ages, radiometric dating allows scientists to determine the absolute age of rocks and the fossils within them. This technique relies on the constant decay rate of radioactive isotopes. The amount of a radioactive parent isotope remaining after time $t$ is calculated using the exponential decay formula:

$$ N(t) = N_0 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{T_{1/2}}} $$

where $N_0$ is the initial quantity of the isotope, $N(t)$ is the quantity remaining, and $T_{1/2}$ is the known half-life of the isotope.

### Anatomical Evidence

Comparative anatomy reveals profound structural similarities among highly diverse species, indicating a shared ancestry.

**Homologous Structures**
Homologous structures are anatomical features in different species that share an underlying structural plan due to common descent, even if their current functions differ drastically. The classic example is the pentadactyl (five-digit) forelimb found across all tetrapod vertebrates.

```text
Ancestral Tetrapod Limb Plan:
[Humerus] ---> [Radius & Ulna] ---> [Carpals] ---> [Metacarpals & Phalanges]
 (Upper)          (Lower)            (Wrist)             (Digits)

Evolutionary Divergence:
* Human Arm:      Adapted for grasping and tool use.
* Bat Wing:       Phalanges highly elongated to support a membranous wing.
* Whale Flipper:  Bones shortened and thickened for steering in water.
* Horse Leg:      Digits reduced (walking on a single modified digit) for speed.

```

Despite these divergent functions (flying, swimming, running), the skeletal architecture remains fundamentally identical, which makes sense only in the light of inherited modification from a common ancestor.

**Vestigial Structures**
Some homologous structures lose their original function over evolutionary time due to changing selective pressures. These remnants are known as vestigial structures. Examples include the pelvic bones in modern whales and snakes (remnants of walking ancestors), the human appendix (a reduced cecum once used for digesting tough plant material), and the *arrector pili* muscles in humans that cause "goosebumps" (useful in hairy mammals for insulation and intimidation, but useless in modern humans).

**Convergent Evolution and Analogous Structures**
It is crucial to distinguish homology from analogy. Analogous structures arise through convergent evolution, where unrelated organisms independently evolve similar traits as adaptations to similar environmental challenges. For instance, the streamlined bodies of sharks (fish), penguins (birds), and dolphins (mammals) are remarkably similar. However, they are not closely related; their body shapes evolved independently because hydrodynamic efficiency is highly advantageous in aquatic environments. Analogous structures share function, but not an underlying anatomical history.

### Molecular and Biochemical Evidence

The most compelling modern evidence for evolution comes from the molecular level. If all life on Earth shares a common ancestor, we should expect to find fundamental biochemical similarities across all domains of life.

**The Universal Genetic Code**
All known living organisms use DNA and RNA to store and transmit genetic information. Furthermore, the genetic code—the specific triplet codons that translate to specific amino acids—is nearly universal. A bacterial cell can read a human gene and synthesize the correct human protein. This universality strongly implies that the code was established in an early common ancestor and passed down to all subsequent branches of the tree of life.

**Highly Conserved Proteins**
Certain essential proteins, such as Cytochrome c (an enzyme vital for the electron transport chain in cellular respiration), are found in almost all aerobic organisms. By comparing the amino acid sequences of Cytochrome c across different species, scientists can measure evolutionary distance.

* The Cytochrome c sequence in humans and chimpanzees is 100% identical.
* Between humans and dogs, there is a divergence of about 11 amino acids.
* Between humans and yeast, the divergence is over 50 amino acids.

These molecular differences act as a "molecular clock." Because mutations accumulate at a relatively steady rate over long periods, the degree of genetic or protein divergence between two species closely correlates with the time elapsed since they last shared a common ancestor, a finding perfectly corroborated by the fossil record.

## 16.3 Microevolution and Population Genetics

While Darwin and Wallace successfully articulated the macroscopic patterns of evolution and the driving mechanism of natural selection, they lacked a functional understanding of inheritance. It was not until the mid-20th century, during the "Modern Synthesis," that biologists successfully integrated Darwinian evolution with Mendelian genetics. This synthesis shifted the analytical focus from individual organisms to whole populations, giving rise to the field of population genetics.

At this level, evolution is defined as **microevolution**: a change in allele frequencies within a population over successive generations.

### The Population as the Unit of Evolution

A common misconception is that individual organisms evolve during their lifetimes. However, evolutionary change is only measurable at the population level. To understand microevolution, we must define its structural components:

* **Population:** A localized group of individuals belonging to the same species that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
* **Gene Pool:** The comprehensive collection of all alleles for all loci in all individuals of a population. If every individual in a population is homozygous for a particular allele, that allele is said to be *fixed* in the gene pool.
* **Allele Frequency:** The proportion of a specific allele out of the total number of alleles at a given locus within the gene pool.

### The Substrate for Evolution: Genetic Variation

Microevolution cannot occur without genetic variation. Natural selection, genetic drift, and gene flow all require a diverse gene pool upon which to act. This variation originates from two primary sources: mutation and sexual reproduction.

**1. Mutation and Chromosomal Alterations**
Mutations are the ultimate source of new alleles. As discussed in Chapter 14, point mutations can alter the sequence of a gene, potentially modifying the structure and function of the resulting protein.

Beyond point mutations, large-scale chromosomal changes significantly impact genetic variation. The duplication of genes—often caused by errors in meiosis such as unequal crossing over—provides a crucial source of evolutionary novelty. The original gene can continue its essential function, while the duplicated copy is free to accumulate mutations over generations, potentially acquiring a entirely new function.

**2. Sexual Reproduction**
In organisms that reproduce sexually, most of the genetic variation observed in a population results from the unique combination of alleles that each individual receives from its parents. As detailed in Chapter 10, three mechanisms shuffle existing alleles into fresh combinations every generation:

* **Crossing Over:** The physical exchange of homologous chromosome segments during Prophase I of meiosis.
* **Independent Assortment:** The random distribution of maternal and paternal chromosomes into gametes during Metaphase I.
* **Random Fertilization:** The unpredictable union of one specific sperm with one specific egg.

### Quantifying the Gene Pool

To determine if a population is evolving, biologists measure the frequencies of alleles and genotypes within the gene pool across generations.

Consider a population of diploid organisms with a single gene locus that has two alleles: a dominant allele ($A$) and a recessive allele ($a$). By convention, population geneticists use specific variables to represent allele frequencies:

* $p$ represents the frequency of the dominant allele ($A$).
* $q$ represents the frequency of the recessive allele ($a$).

Because there are only two alleles for this locus in the population, their combined frequencies must equal 100% of the alleles present. Therefore, we can express this relationship mathematically as:

$$ p + q = 1 $$

By counting the number of individuals with each genotype, we can calculate the exact allele frequencies in the gene pool.

```text
[ POPULATION GENETICS CALCULATION EXAMPLE ]

Scenario: A population of 100 wildflowers.
Locus: Flower color. Red (A) is dominant to White (a).
Total Alleles: 100 diploid plants * 2 alleles/plant = 200 total alleles.

1. Genotype Census:
   * 40 plants are Homozygous Dominant (AA)
   * 40 plants are Heterozygous (Aa)
   * 20 plants are Homozygous Recessive (aa)

2. Counting the Alleles:
   * 'A' alleles from (AA) plants:  40 * 2 = 80
   * 'A' alleles from (Aa) plants:  40 * 1 = 40
     Total 'A' alleles = 120

   * 'a' alleles from (aa) plants:  20 * 2 = 40
   * 'a' alleles from (Aa) plants:  40 * 1 = 40
     Total 'a' alleles = 80

3. Calculating Frequencies (p and q):
   * p = (Total 'A') / (Total Alleles) = 120 / 200 = 0.60
   * q = (Total 'a') / (Total Alleles) =  80 / 200 = 0.40

4. Verification:
   * p + q = 0.60 + 0.40 = 1.00

```

In this specific generation, the frequency of the $A$ allele is 60%, and the frequency of the $a$ allele is 40%. If we measure this same population ten generations later and find that $p = 0.75$ and $q = 0.25$, we can definitively state that microevolution has occurred, because the allele frequencies in the gene pool have changed over time. The mechanisms driving these changes will be explored in the subsequent sections.

## 16.4 The Hardy-Weinberg Principle and Null Models

To determine if a population is evolving, biologists must first establish a baseline representing a population that is *not* evolving. In experimental science, a null hypothesis states that there is no significant difference or active process occurring between variables. In population genetics, the **Hardy-Weinberg principle** serves as this crucial null model.

Developed independently in 1908 by English mathematician G. H. Hardy and German physician Wilhelm Weinberg, this principle states that the frequencies of alleles and genotypes in a population will remain constant from generation to generation, provided that only Mendelian segregation and recombination of alleles are at work. Such a population is said to be in **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium**.

### The Mathematics of Hardy-Weinberg

The principle relies on two fundamental equations that describe the relationship between allele frequencies and genotype frequencies in a diploid population.

Consider a single gene locus with two alleles: a dominant allele ($A$) and a recessive allele ($a$). As established in the previous section, the frequency of the dominant allele is represented by $p$, and the frequency of the recessive allele is represented by $q$.

The first equation represents the **allele frequencies** in the gene pool:

$$ p + q = 1 $$

If the population is mating randomly, we can predict the frequencies of the resulting genotypes in the next generation by treating the gene pool as a massive Punnett square. The probability of an offspring inheriting two dominant alleles ($A$ from the sperm and $A$ from the egg) is the product of their independent probabilities: $p \times p = p^2$.

Expanding this logic to all possible fertilizations yields a binomial expansion: $(p + q)^2 = 1$. This provides the second equation, which represents the **genotype frequencies**:

$$ p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1 $$

Each term in this equation corresponds to a specific genotype within the population:

* $p^2$: The expected frequency of the homozygous dominant genotype ($AA$).
* $2pq$: The expected frequency of the heterozygous genotype ($Aa$). The factor of 2 accounts for the two possible ways a heterozygote can be formed (an $A$ sperm fertilizing an $a$ egg, or an $a$ sperm fertilizing an $A$ egg).
* $q^2$: The expected frequency of the homozygous recessive genotype ($aa$).

```text
[ HARDY-WEINBERG GENE POOL CROSS ]

          Sperm in Gene Pool (Frequencies)
                 (p) A         (q) a
              ---------------------------
            |             |             |
      (p) A |   (p²) AA   |   (pq) Aa   |
Ova         |             |             |
in          |-------------|-------------|
Gene        |             |             |
Pool  (q) a |   (pq) Aa   |   (q²) aa   |
            |             |             |
              ---------------------------

```

### The Five Conditions for Equilibrium

For a population to maintain Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium—meaning its allele and genotype frequencies do not change over time—it must satisfy five restrictive conditions:

1. **No Mutations:** The gene pool cannot be modified by the addition, deletion, or alteration of alleles. Mutations introduce new genetic variation, immediately altering $p$ and $q$.
2. **Random Mating:** Individuals must pair by chance, not by preference for specific phenotypes or geographical proximity. Inbreeding or assortative mating alters genotype frequencies (though not necessarily allele frequencies on their own).
3. **No Gene Flow:** No individuals (or their gametes) can migrate into or out of the population. Gene flow transfers alleles between populations, altering existing frequencies.
4. **Extremely Large Population Size:** The population must be large enough that random chance events do not significantly alter allele frequencies. In small populations, allele frequencies fluctuate by chance, a process known as genetic drift.
5. **No Natural Selection:** All individuals, regardless of their genotype, must have equal probabilities of surviving and reproducing. If specific traits confer an advantage, those alleles will disproportionately increase in the next generation.

### Applying the Null Model in Evolutionary Biology

In real, natural populations, these five conditions are rarely, if ever, met simultaneously. Evolution is almost always occurring to some degree. The true value of the Hardy-Weinberg principle is not in describing nature perfectly, but in providing a mathematical baseline to detect when and how evolutionary forces are acting.

Biologists use the Hardy-Weinberg equations to calculate the *expected* genotype frequencies of a population assuming it is not evolving. They then compare these expected values to the *observed* genotype frequencies in the wild.

If the observed frequencies significantly deviate from the expected $p^2$, $2pq$, and $q^2$ values, the population is not in equilibrium. This deviation proves that microevolution is occurring and prompts researchers to investigate which of the five conditions is being violated (e.g., investigating if a particular disease is acting as a selective pressure, or if a physical barrier is restricting gene flow).

## 16.5 Evolutionary Forces: Genetic Drift, Gene Flow, and Mutation

While natural selection is the most famous mechanism of evolution, it is not the only one. Natural selection is unique because it is the only mechanism that consistently leads to *adaptive* evolution—traits that enhance survival and reproduction. However, microevolution is defined simply as a change in allele frequencies over generations. Three other evolutionary forces can drastically alter allele frequencies within a gene pool: mutation, genetic drift, and gene flow. These forces are largely random and do not inherently produce adaptations, yet they are fundamental to the evolutionary history of all species.

### Mutation: The Ultimate Source of Variation

As established in the Hardy-Weinberg model, a population will only remain in equilibrium if no mutations occur. In reality, mutations are inevitable. A mutation is any change in the nucleotide sequence of an organism's DNA.

Because mutations are random, they do not arise out of an organism's "need" to adapt to a changing environment. The vast majority of point mutations occurring in non-coding regions are neutral, conferring no selective advantage or disadvantage. Mutations that alter protein function are often deleterious, reducing the organism's fitness. However, on rare occasions, a mutation results in a novel allele that improves an organism's ability to survive and reproduce.

While mutation is the ultimate source of all genetic variation, its mutation rate is generally low in plants and animals (approximately one mutation in every $10^5$ to $10^6$ genes per generation). Therefore, mutation alone does not rapidly drive microevolution in eukaryotes. In contrast, for organisms with rapid generation times, such as bacteria and viruses, mutations can generate significant genetic variation very quickly, leading to rapid evolution (such as antibiotic resistance).

### Genetic Drift: Evolution by Chance

Genetic drift is a process in which chance events cause allele frequencies to fluctuate unpredictably from one generation to the next. The effects of genetic drift are most pronounced in small populations.

To understand drift, consider the statistical concept of sampling error. If you flip a coin 1,000 times, you will likely get very close to 500 heads and 500 tails (a 50% frequency). However, if you flip a coin only 10 times, you might easily get 7 heads and 3 tails (a 70% frequency) purely by chance. In population genetics, the smaller the population size ($N$), the more likely it is that the allele frequencies of the offspring generation will deviate from the parent generation due to random mating events and unpredictable survival.

Over time, genetic drift tends to reduce genetic variation through the random loss of alleles. An allele may eventually reach a frequency of 0% (lost) or 100% (fixed).

Two specific circumstances can result in a small population size, magnifying the impact of genetic drift:

**1. The Bottleneck Effect**
A sudden, severe change in the environment (such as a fire, flood, or widespread disease) can drastically reduce the size of a population. The few surviving individuals constitute a random genetic sample of the original population.

```text
[ THE BOTTLENECK EFFECT ]

Original Population (High Diversity)
  ( p=0.33, q=0.33, r=0.33 )
      A  b  C  A  c  B
      b  C  a  B  A  c
           |
           |  <-- Catastrophic Event (e.g., Earthquake)
          \|/     Only a few random individuals survive.
           V
    [ Bottleneck ]
        A  a  A
           |
           v
Surviving Population (Low Diversity)
  ( p=0.66, q=0.33, r=0.00 )
      A  a  A  A  a  A
      A  A  a  A  A  A

```

Even if the population eventually recovers in size, its gene pool will reflect the allele frequencies of the bottleneck survivors, not the original population. The allele 'C'/'c' ($r$) in the diagram above was lost entirely by chance.

**2. The Founder Effect**
When a few individuals become isolated from a larger population and establish a new colony, the founder effect occurs. The gene pool of the new population is dictated entirely by the alleles present in the original "founders." For example, if a small group of birds is blown off course to a remote island, the genetic makeup of the resulting island population will likely differ significantly from the mainland population, simply due to the random assortment of alleles the founding birds happened to carry.

### Gene Flow: Migration and Allele Transfer

Gene flow is the transfer of alleles into or out of a population due to the movement of fertile individuals or their gametes (such as pollen carried by wind or insects).

```text
[ GENE FLOW BETWEEN POPULATIONS ]

Population 1 (Mainland)                   Population 2 (Island)
Mostly Allele 'A' (p = 0.9)               Mostly Allele 'a' (p = 0.1)
[ A  A  A  A  a ]                         [ a  a  A  a  a ]
[ A  A  A  A  A ] ------ Migration -----> [ a  a  a  a  a ]
[ A  A  a  A  A ]                         [ a  a  a  a  a ]

```

Gene flow tends to reduce the genetic differences between interacting populations. If gene flow is extensive enough, it can merge neighboring populations into a single population with a common gene pool.

Gene flow can also have a profound effect on a population's adaptation to its local environment. It can introduce advantageous new alleles into a population, but it can also continuously introduce maladaptive alleles that counteract the localized effects of natural selection. For example, if a specific plant allele is advantageous in a copper-heavy soil environment but disadvantageous in normal soil, continuous cross-pollination from the normal-soil plants will prevent the copper-tolerant population from becoming completely fixed for the advantageous allele.

**Chapter 16: Mechanisms of Evolution**

* **16.1** Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace proposed natural selection as the mechanism for evolution. It asserts that populations overproduce offspring, variations exist, and individuals with traits best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those traits on (descent with modification).
* **16.2** Evolution is supported by multiple lines of evidence: the fossil record (showing transitional forms and historical timelines), comparative anatomy (homologous and vestigial structures), and molecular biology (the universal genetic code and conserved protein sequences).

* **16.4** The Hardy-Weinberg principle provides a mathematical null model for an unchanging population ($p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$). A population is only in equilibrium if there are no mutations, random mating, no gene flow, large population size, and no natural selection.
