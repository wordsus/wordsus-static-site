Plants are dynamic organisms perfectly adapted to a dual existence. Roots mine the soil for water and minerals, while shoots reach upward to capture sunlight and carbon dioxide. This chapter explores the remarkable architecture that makes this lifestyle possible. We will examine the fundamental tissue systems—dermal, vascular, and ground—that build all plant organs. From the microscopic anatomy of leaves to the processes of primary and secondary growth that allow a tiny seedling to become a towering tree, you will discover the elegant structural solutions plants possess to survive and thrive across the biosphere.

## 19.1 Plant Tissue Systems: Dermal, Vascular, and Ground

Just as animal bodies are organized into distinct tissues and organs, a plant body is hierarchically organized. A plant’s organs—such as roots, stems, and leaves (which will be detailed in Section 19.2)—are constructed from three fundamental **tissue systems**: the dermal, vascular, and ground tissue systems. Each tissue system spans the entire plant body, connecting all organs and providing functional continuity.

A tissue system consists of one or more tissues organized into a functional unit. Some plant tissues are simple, composed of a single basic cell type, while others are complex, composed of multiple interacting cell types. To understand these systems, we must explore their structure, placement, and primary roles.

### The Dermal Tissue System

The **dermal tissue system** serves as the plant's outer protective covering. It is the plant's first line of defense against physical damage, pathogens, and environmental stressors.

* **Epidermis:** In non-woody plants (and the young parts of woody plants), the dermal tissue consists of a single layer of tightly packed cells called the epidermis.
* **Cuticle:** The epidermal cells of leaves and most stems secrete a waxy coating called the cuticle. This vital adaptation helps prevent excessive water ($H_2O$) loss in terrestrial environments.
* **Periderm:** In woody plants undergoing secondary growth, older regions of stems and roots replace the epidermis with a tougher, thicker dermal tissue known as the periderm (commonly recognized as part of bark).

The dermal tissue also contains specialized cells tailored to specific organ functions. In roots, **root hairs** are extensions of epidermal cells that significantly increase the surface area for water and mineral absorption. In leaves, specialized epidermal cells called **guard cells** regulate the opening and closing of stomata, pores that allow for the exchange of $CO_2$ and $O_2$ while controlling evaporative water loss. Hair-like outgrowths called **trichomes** can protect against herbivores by secreting toxic compounds or provide shade to reduce solar radiation.

### The Vascular Tissue System

The **vascular tissue system** facilitates the long-distance transport of materials between the root and shoot systems and provides mechanical support. The vascular tissue of a root or stem is collectively called the **stele**, though its arrangement varies depending on the organ and the plant group (e.g., a solid central cylinder in roots versus distinct vascular bundles in stems).

The vascular system consists of two complex tissues: xylem and phloem.

#### Xylem

The **xylem** conducts water and dissolved minerals upward from the roots into the shoots. Xylem tissue consists primarily of two types of water-conducting cells: **tracheids** and **vessel elements**.

Both of these cell types are highly specialized for transport: they are tubular, elongated, and crucially, they are *dead* at functional maturity. Before dying, they deposit highly lignified secondary cell walls, leaving behind a hollow, rigid pipeline. Tracheids are long, thin cells with tapered ends, while vessel elements are generally wider, shorter, and align end-to-end to form microscopic pipes called vessels.

#### Phloem

The **phloem** transports sugars (primarily sucrose), the products of photosynthesis, from where they are made (usually the leaves) to where they are needed or stored (such as growing tissues, developing flowers, or roots).

Unlike xylem, the sugar-conducting cells of the phloem are *alive* at functional maturity. In angiosperms (flowering plants), these are called **sieve-tube elements**. Because these cells lack a nucleus, ribosomes, and a distinct vacuole to leave a clear channel for sap flow, they rely on alongside **companion cells**, which contain these organelles and help regulate the metabolism and loading of sugars into the sieve-tube elements.

### The Ground Tissue System

Tissues that are neither dermal nor vascular are classified as the **ground tissue system**. Ground tissue is not merely "filler"; it is responsible for most of the plant's metabolic functions, including photosynthesis, storage, and short-distance transport.

The organization of ground tissue often depends on its relationship to the vascular tissue:

* **Pith:** Ground tissue that is internal to the vascular tissue.
* **Cortex:** Ground tissue that is external to the vascular tissue.

The ground tissue system is primarily composed of three basic cell types, which are distinguished by their cell wall structures and their vitality at maturity:

1. **Parenchyma Cells:** These are the most abundant and versatile plant cells. They possess thin, flexible primary cell walls and generally lack secondary walls. Parenchyma cells are alive at maturity and perform most metabolic functions, such as synthesizing and storing organic products (e.g., chloroplasts in leaves, amyloplasts for starch storage in roots).
2. **Collenchyma Cells:** Grouped in strands or cylinders, collenchyma cells help support young parts of the plant shoot. They have thicker primary cell walls than parenchyma cells, particularly at the corners, but lack secondary walls. They are alive at maturity and provide flexible support without restraining growth.
3. **Sclerenchyma Cells:** These cells function primarily as structural support elements. They possess thick secondary cell walls heavily reinforced with **lignin**, a rigid polymer. Because they are often dead at functional maturity, sclerenchyma cells provide an inflexible, strong scaffold in regions of the plant that have stopped growing in length.

## 19.2 The Structure and Function of Roots, Stems, and Leaves

The vascular plant body is functionally divided into two major systems: a subterranean **root system** and an aerial **shoot system** (consisting of stems and leaves). These systems are highly interdependent. Roots are starved of the organic sugars produced by photosynthesis, while the shoot system relies entirely on the roots for the absorption of water ($H_2O$) and essential minerals. The structural organization of these organs reflects their evolutionary adaptation to the contrasting environments of soil and air.

### The Root System

A root is an organ that anchors a vascular plant in the soil, absorbs minerals and water, and often stores carbohydrates.

Most eudicots and gymnosperms have a **taproot system**, consisting of one main vertical root (the taproot) that develops from the embryonic root. The taproot gives rise to lateral (branch) roots. This system penetrates deeply, well-adapted to deep soils where groundwater is not near the surface. In contrast, most monocots (such as grasses) have a **fibrous root system**, a dense mat of slender roots emerging from the stem. Fibrous roots do not penetrate deeply, making them excellent at preventing soil erosion and capturing surface water.

The absorption of water and minerals occurs primarily near the tips of elongating roots, where vast numbers of **root hairs** (extensions of epidermal cells, introduced in Section 19.1) emerge and massively increase the surface area.

Longitudinally, root growth occurs in three distinct zones behind the protective **root cap**, which secretes a polysaccharide slime to lubricate the root's passage through abrasive soil.

### The Stem

A stem is a plant organ bearing leaves and buds. Its primary function is to elongate and orient the shoot in a way that maximizes photosynthesis by the leaves. Stems also serve as the central conduit for the vascular tissues linking roots and leaves.

The stem consists of an alternating system of **nodes**, the points at which leaves are attached, and **internodes**, the stem segments between nodes. In the angle formed by each leaf and the stem is an **axillary bud**, which has the potential to form a lateral shoot (or branch). Most of the growth of a young shoot, however, is concentrated near the shoot tip, which consists of an **apical bud**.

The presence of an intact apical bud partially inhibits the growth of axillary buds, a phenomenon called **apical dominance**. If an animal eats the apical bud, or if the plant is pruned, the axillary buds break dormancy, giving rise to lateral branches.

While primary stems are meant for support and transport, many plants possess modified stems adapted for other functions:

* **Rhizomes:** Horizontal underground stems that allow plants to spread.
* **Stolons:** Horizontal above-ground stems ("runners," like in strawberry plants) that enable asexual reproduction.
* **Tubers:** Enlarged ends of rhizomes or stolons specialized for storing food (e.g., the standard potato).

### The Leaf

The leaf is the primary photosynthetic organ of most vascular plants. While leaves vary extensively in form, they generally consist of a flattened **blade** and a stalk called the **petiole**, which joins the leaf to the stem at a node. Monocots and eudicots differ in the arrangement of veins (the vascular tissue of leaves): most monocots have parallel major veins, whereas eudicots generally have a branched, netted network of veins.

To perform photosynthesis efficiently, the internal structure of a leaf is highly organized into specific tissue layers.

1. **Epidermis and Cuticle:** The upper and lower surfaces are covered by the epidermis and a waxy cuticle that prevents dehydration. The lower epidermis is typically punctuated by **stomata**, pores flanked by guard cells that regulate the intake of $CO_2$ and the release of $O_2$ and $H_2O$ vapor.
2. **Mesophyll:** The ground tissue of the leaf, sandwiched between the upper and lower epidermis, is called the mesophyll. In many eudicots, the mesophyll is divided into two layers:

* **Palisade mesophyll:** Located just beneath the upper epidermis, consisting of tightly packed, column-like cells laden with chloroplasts to maximize light absorption.
* **Spongy mesophyll:** Located below the palisade layer, consisting of loosely arranged cells with labyrinthine air spaces to facilitate the rapid diffusion of gases ($CO_2$ and $O_2$) toward and away from the palisade cells.

1. **Veins (Vascular Bundles):** The vascular tissue is continuous with the vascular tissue of the stem. Veins branch extensively throughout the mesophyll, ensuring that no photosynthetic cell is far from xylem (which delivers water and minerals) or phloem (which carries away the synthesized sugars). The veins also function as a structural skeleton, reinforcing the shape of the leaf blade.

## 19.3 Primary Growth: Lengthening of the Plant Body

Unlike most animals, which exhibit determinate growth (ceasing to grow after reaching a certain size or age), most plants exhibit **indeterminate growth**. This means a plant can grow continuously throughout its lifespan. This remarkable capacity is made possible by **meristems**—localized regions of perpetually undifferentiated, actively dividing cells that function much like stem cells in animals.

When meristematic cells divide, some daughter cells remain in the meristem to generate more cells (initials), while others are displaced and begin to specialize (derivatives). The meristems responsible for increasing the length of the plant are called **apical meristems**, located at the tips of roots and shoots, as well as in the axillary buds of shoots. The elongation of the plant body produced by apical meristems is called **primary growth**.

### The Three Primary Meristems

As cells are displaced from the apical meristem, they organize into three distinct **primary meristems**. These transitional tissues are partially differentiated but still dividing, and they are directly responsible for producing the three mature tissue systems introduced in Section 19.1:

1. **Protoderm:** The outermost primary meristem, which differentiates into the epidermis (dermal tissue system).
2. **Ground Meristem:** Located deep to the protoderm, this gives rise to the cortex and pith (ground tissue system), consisting mostly of parenchyma cells.
3. **Procambium:** The innermost primary meristem, which develops into the primary xylem and primary phloem (vascular tissue system).

### Primary Growth in Roots

The root apical meristem is shielded by the protective root cap as it pushes through the soil. As explored in Section 19.2, primary growth occurs in overlapping zones (Division, Elongation, Differentiation) immediately behind the root tip.

As the cells in the zone of differentiation mature, the three primary meristems complete their development into a highly organized internal structure. In most eudicot roots, the vascular tissue forms a solid central cylinder called the vascular cylinder (or stele).

* **Endodermis:** The innermost layer of the cortex is a cylinder one cell thick called the endodermis. It serves as a selective barrier, regulating the passage of minerals from the soil into the vascular cylinder.
* **Pericycle:** Just inside the endodermis is the pericycle, the outermost cell layer in the vascular cylinder. Lateral roots arise destructively from the pericycle, pushing their way outward through the cortex and epidermis to establish connection with the main root's vascular system.

### Primary Growth in Shoots

The **shoot apical meristem (SAM)** is a dome-shaped mass of dividing cells at the very tip of a growing shoot. Unlike roots, shoots do not have a protective cap; instead, the delicate SAM is protected by tightly folded young leaves.

Leaves develop from **leaf primordia**, which are small, finger-like projections of tissue that emerge along the flanks of the apical dome. As these primordia grow, they envelop and shield the meristem. Axillary buds (which can later develop into branches) form from islands of meristematic cells left behind at the base of the leaf primordia.

Primary growth in stems primarily involves the elongation of internodes (the spaces between leaves). As the stem grows, the procambium develops into vascular bundles. The arrangement of these bundles differs significantly between major plant groups:

```text
       EUDICOT STEM                  MONOCOT STEM
       (Vascular Bundles in a Ring)  (Vascular Bundles Scattered)
       ===========================   ===========================
       [   Epidermis             ]   [   Epidermis             ]

             (Xylem inner,                 (Monkey-face shape,
              Phloem outer)                 xylem & phloem)

```

* **Eudicots:** The vascular bundles are typically arranged in a distinct ring near the perimeter of the stem. This ring separates the ground tissue into an outer cortex and an inner pith.
* **Monocots:** The vascular bundles are scattered throughout the ground tissue. Because there is no distinct ring, the ground tissue is not delineated into a separate cortex and pith.

In both roots and stems, primary growth builds the fundamental architecture of the plant. For herbaceous (non-woody) plants, primary growth produces almost the entire plant body. However, for woody plants, primary growth is merely the scaffolding upon which secondary growth will build.

## 19.4 Secondary Growth: Increasing the Girth of Woody Plants

While primary growth allows roots to push through the soil and shoots to reach for sunlight, many plants require additional structural support and transport capacity as they grow taller. **Secondary growth** is the increase in the thickness or girth of the plant body. It is characteristic of gymnosperms and many eudicots, but is exceedingly rare in monocots.

Secondary growth is driven by **lateral meristems**—cylinders of dividing cells that extend along the length of roots and stems. There are two lateral meristems responsible for this thickening: the **vascular cambium**, which adds secondary vascular tissue, and the **cork cambium**, which replaces the epidermis with a thicker, tougher covering.

### The Vascular Cambium and Wood

The vascular cambium is a continuous cylinder of meristematic cells, often only one cell thick. In a typical eudicot stem, it develops from the undifferentiated parenchyma cells situated between the primary xylem and primary phloem (the procambium) and the cells in the ground tissue between the vascular bundles.

As the cells of the vascular cambium divide, they increase the cambium's circumference and add layers of tissue to both its interior and exterior:

* **Secondary Xylem:** Cells produced to the *inside* of the vascular cambium differentiate into secondary xylem. As year after year of secondary xylem accumulates, it forms what we common know as **wood**.
* **Secondary Phloem:** Cells produced to the *outside* of the vascular cambium differentiate into secondary phloem. Unlike wood, secondary phloem does not accumulate extensively over time; the older layers are crushed and sloughed off as the stem expands.

#### Growth Rings and Wood Anatomy

In temperate regions where distinct growing seasons occur, the vascular cambium exhibits varying levels of activity. During the spring, when water is abundant, the cambium produces **early wood** (or spring wood) composed of large, thin-walled vessel elements and tracheids to maximize water delivery to new leaves. During the summer, when water is less plentiful, it produces **late wood** (or summer wood) consisting of smaller, thicker-walled cells that provide more physical support.

This annual alternation between the low-density early wood and the high-density late wood creates visible **growth rings**. By counting these rings, dendrochronologists can determine the age of a tree, and by analyzing their varying thicknesses, they can reconstruct past climate conditions.

As a tree ages, the older layers of secondary xylem near the center of the stem no longer transport water. These inner layers, called **heartwood**, become clogged with resins and gums, which darken the wood and make it highly resistant to decay, serving purely as a structural pillar. The newer, outer layers of secondary xylem that still actively transport xylem sap are called **sapwood**.

### The Cork Cambium and Bark

As the vascular cambium rapidly expands the stem's diameter, the original epidermis and cortex are stretched, ruptured, and eventually shed. To prevent the plant from drying out and succumbing to pathogens, a new protective layer must be generated.

The **cork cambium** arises from the outer cortex initially (and later from secondary phloem as the stem continues to expand). It produces **cork cells** to its exterior. As cork cells mature, they deposit a thick, waxy, hydrophobic polymer called **suberin** in their cell walls before dying. This creates a highly impermeable barrier. Together, the cork cambium and the cork cells it produces make up the **periderm**.

Because the periderm is heavily suberized, it blocks the exchange of gases necessary for the living cells in the interior of the stem. To solve this, the periderm is punctuated by **lenticels**—small, raised, spongy areas where cork cells are loosely arranged, allowing oxygen to diffuse inward for cellular respiration.

#### What is Bark?

In botanical terms, **bark** refers to all tissues external to the vascular cambium. Therefore, bark is not a single tissue but a composite of two distinct zones:

1. **Inner Bark:** The active, living secondary phloem.
2. **Outer Bark:** All the periderm layers (cork cambium and dead cork cells).

When a tree is "girdled" (a strip of bark is removed entirely around the trunk), the plant dies not because it loses water, but because the secondary phloem is severed, starving the root system of the sugars produced by the leaves.

## Chapter Summary

A plant's body is hierarchically organized to optimize its survival in a dual environment of soil and air.

* **19.1:** All plant organs are composed of three continuous tissue systems: the protective **dermal tissue**, the transporting **vascular tissue** (xylem and phloem), and the metabolically active **ground tissue**.
* **19.2:** The vascular plant consists of a root system for anchorage and resource acquisition, and a shoot system (stems and leaves) adapted for light capture, gas exchange, and photosynthesis.
* **19.3:** **Primary growth** is driven by apical meristems at the tips of roots and shoots, resulting in the lengthening of the plant body and the formation of primary tissues.
* **19.4:** **Secondary growth** is driven by lateral meristems (the vascular cambium and cork cambium), resulting in the thickening of woody plants. The vascular cambium generates wood (secondary xylem) and inner bark (secondary phloem), while the cork cambium generates the protective outer bark (periderm).
