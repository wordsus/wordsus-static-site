In Chapter 5, we explored the quantum mechanical model of the atom. Now, we will see how electron configurations—particularly those of valence electrons—dictate the macroscopic properties of elements. The periodic table is not just a reference chart; it is a profound tool reflecting the underlying rhythm of atomic structure. We will explore how atomic size, ionization energy, and electron affinity vary systematically across periods and down groups. These recurring trends are fundamentally governed by the delicate interplay between effective nuclear charge and the shielding effect of electron-electron repulsions.

## 6.1 Historical Development of the Periodic Table

As the 19th century progressed, the number of known elements grew rapidly. Chemists recognized the need for a systematic way to organize this expanding array of chemical knowledge. The periodic table we use today did not emerge overnight; rather, it is the result of decades of qualitative observations, quantitative measurements, and theoretical breakthroughs. While Chapter 2 introduced the modern layout of the periodic table, understanding its historical development provides critical context for how we relate macroscopic chemical properties to underlying atomic structure.

### Early Attempts at Classification: Triads and Octaves

The earliest attempts to classify elements relied on finding patterns in their physical and chemical properties and relating these properties to their relative atomic masses.

In 1829, the German chemist **Johann Döbereiner** observed that certain groups of three elements with similar chemical properties exhibited a distinct quantitative relationship. He called these groups **triads**. In a triad, the atomic mass of the middle element was approximately the average of the atomic masses of the other two.

**Example of Döbereiner’s Halogen Triad:**

* Chlorine (Cl): Atomic mass $\approx 35.5$
* Iodine (I): Atomic mass $\approx 126.9$
* Bromine (Br): Average mass $\approx \frac{35.5 + 126.9}{2} = 81.2$ (Actual atomic mass of Br is $79.9$)

While Döbereiner's triads highlighted an intriguing pattern, they were limited to only a few known elements and could not serve as a universal classification system.

In 1864, the English chemist **John Newlands** proposed a broader system. When he arranged the known elements in order of increasing atomic mass, he noticed that properties seemed to repeat every eighth element, much like the octaves on a musical scale. He formulated the **Law of Octaves**. However, Newlands’ system failed for elements beyond calcium, and his musical analogy was largely ridiculed by the scientific community at the time.

### Mendeleev, Meyer, and the Periodic Law

The major breakthrough in organizing the elements occurred in 1869 when Russian chemist **Dmitri Mendeleev** and German chemist **Lothar Meyer** independently published remarkably similar periodic tables. Both scientists arranged the elements in order of increasing atomic mass and noted that chemical and physical properties recurred periodically.

Mendeleev is generally given more credit than Meyer because he advanced the system in two profound ways:

1. **Correcting Atomic Masses:** Mendeleev altered the order of a few elements, defying the strict sequence of atomic masses, to ensure they fell into columns with elements of similar properties. For example, he placed tellurium (atomic mass 127.6) before iodine (atomic mass 126.9) because iodine’s chemistry clearly aligned with the halogens (fluorine, chlorine, bromine).
2. **Predicting Undiscovered Elements:** Mendeleev boldly left blank spaces in his table where the pattern broke down, predicting that these gaps corresponded to elements that had not yet been discovered.

He even predicted the chemical and physical properties of these missing elements based on their position. One of his most famous predictions was for an element he called "eka-silicon," which was to sit below silicon in his table. When **germanium (Ge)** was discovered in 1886, its properties matched Mendeleev’s predictions with astonishing accuracy.

| Property | Predicted for Eka-Silicon (1871) | Observed for Germanium (Discovered 1886) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Atomic Mass | $72$ | $72.6$ |
| Density | $5.5 \text{ g/cm}^3$ | $5.47 \text{ g/cm}^3$ |
| Melting Point | High | $938\text{°C}$ |
| Formula of Oxide | $EsO_2$ | $GeO_2$ |
| Density of Oxide | $4.7 \text{ g/cm}^3$ | $4.70 \text{ g/cm}^3$ |

### Moseley and the Modern Periodic Law

While Mendeleev’s table was highly successful, the inversions of atomic mass (like the Te/I anomaly) remained unexplained. The underlying reason for periodicity was not understood until the early 20th century, following the discovery of subatomic particles.

In 1913, the English physicist **Henry Moseley** examined the X-ray emission spectra of various elements. When elements were bombarded with high-energy electrons, they emitted X-rays of characteristic frequencies. Moseley discovered a direct mathematical relationship between the square root of the X-ray frequency ($\nu$) and a fundamental property of the atom, which he identified as the atomic number ($Z$). This relationship is expressed as:

$$ \sqrt{\nu} = a(Z - b) $$

where $a$ and $b$ are constants for a given spectral line.

Moseley established that the atomic number is not merely an arbitrary index, but a physically meaningful quantity representing the number of protons in the nucleus (and thus the number of electrons in a neutral atom).

When the elements were rearranged by increasing **atomic number** rather than atomic mass, the anomalies in Mendeleev’s table completely disappeared. Tellurium ($Z = 52$) naturally precedes iodine ($Z = 53$), even though it has a higher atomic mass due to the isotopic abundances of its naturally occurring sample.

This discovery led to the **Modern Periodic Law**: *The physical and chemical properties of the elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.*

```text
The Evolution of the Periodic Concept:
Atomic Mass (Mendeleev/Meyer)  -------->  Atomic Number (Moseley)
[Observation of Trends]                   [Explanation via Atomic Structure]

```

By organizing the table according to atomic number, the structural foundation of the periodic table aligns perfectly with the quantum mechanical model of the atom (discussed in Chapter 5). The periodic repetition of properties is a direct consequence of the periodic repetition of electron configurations, particularly in the outermost, or valence, shells.

## 6.2 Shielding and Effective Nuclear Charge

To understand the macroscopic periodic trends of the elements—such as their size, reactivity, and ionization energy—we must first examine the electrostatic forces operating at the atomic level. In a hydrogen atom, the single electron experiences the full, unmitigated attractive force of the $+1$ nucleus. However, in any atom with more than one electron (a many-electron atom), the situation is significantly more complex due to electron-electron repulsions.

### The Concept of Shielding

In a many-electron atom, each electron is simultaneously attracted to the positively charged nucleus and repelled by the other negatively charged electrons. The inner electrons, which reside in lower energy levels closer to the nucleus, act as a "screen" that partially obscures the outer (valence) electrons from the full positive charge of the nucleus. This phenomenon is known as **shielding** or the **screening effect**.

```text
               (+) Nucleus (Charge = Z)
                |
                |--- Strong attraction
                |
          (e-) (e-) Core Electrons 
                |
                |--- Electron-electron repulsion (Shielding)
                |
              (e-)  Valence Electron 
                    (Feels a reduced charge, Z_eff)

```

Because of this electron-electron repulsion, a valence electron does not feel the full actual nuclear charge ($Z$). Instead, it experiences a reduced net attractive force. The actual nuclear charge mitigated by the shielding effect of other electrons is called the **effective nuclear charge**, denoted by $Z_{\text{eff}}$.

The relationship between the actual nuclear charge, the shielding effect, and the effective nuclear charge is expressed by the fundamental equation:

$$Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - S$$

Where:

* **$Z_{\text{eff}}$** is the effective nuclear charge.
* **$Z$** is the actual nuclear charge (which equals the atomic number, or the total number of protons).
* **$S$** is the shielding constant (or screening constant), representing the average number of electrons that effectively shield the nucleus from the electron in question.

### Estimating the Shielding Constant ($S$)

Core electrons are highly effective at shielding valence electrons because their orbitals penetrate closer to the nucleus. Conversely, electrons in the same valence shell are very poor at shielding one another because they are at roughly the same average distance from the nucleus and are spread out in different orbitals.

Therefore, as a simple first-order approximation, we can estimate the shielding constant $S$ by assuming that only the core electrons contribute to shielding, and they do so perfectly (each core electron contributes exactly $1$ to the value of $S$). Electrons in the valence shell are assumed to contribute $0$ to $S$.

$$S \approx \text{Number of core electrons}$$

**Example Calculation: Sodium (Na)**

* Electron configuration of Na: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^1$ or $[\text{Ne}] 3s^1$
* Actual nuclear charge ($Z$): $11$ (since sodium has $11$ protons)
* Core electrons ($S$): $10$ (the $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6$ electrons)
* $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx 11 - 10 = +1$

The single $3s$ valence electron in sodium experiences an effective nuclear charge of approximately $+1$, rather than the full $+11$ of the nucleus.

### Periodic Trends in Effective Nuclear Charge

Understanding how $Z_{\text{eff}}$ changes across the periodic table is the master key to predicting almost all other atomic properties.

**1. Across a Period (Left to Right)**
As we move from left to right across a given period, the atomic number ($Z$) increases by one for each successive element. This means one proton is added to the nucleus and one electron is added to the valence shell. Because valence electrons do not effectively shield one another, the shielding constant ($S$) remains relatively constant (equal to the number of core electrons).

Since $Z$ is increasing while $S$ remains constant, **$Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases significantly from left to right across a period.**

*Table 6.1: Approximate $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for Period 2 Elements*

| Element | $Z$ (Protons) | Electron Configuration | Core e⁻ ($S$) | Approximate $Z_{\text{eff}}$ ($Z - S$) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Lithium (Li) | $3$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^1$ | $2$ | $+1$ |
| Beryllium (Be) | $4$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2$ | $2$ | $+2$ |
| Boron (B) | $5$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^1$ | $2$ | $+3$ |
| Carbon (C) | $6$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^2$ | $2$ | $+4$ |
| Nitrogen (N) | $7$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^3$ | $2$ | $+5$ |
| Oxygen (O) | $8$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^4$ | $2$ | $+6$ |
| Fluorine (F) | $9$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^5$ | $2$ | $+7$ |
| Neon (Ne) | $10$ | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^6$ | $2$ | $+8$ |

As the effective nuclear charge increases, the nucleus exerts a stronger pull on the valence electrons, drawing them closer.

**2. Down a Group (Top to Bottom)**
As we descend a group, both the nuclear charge ($Z$) and the number of core electrons ($S$) increase dramatically. Using our simple approximation ($S = \text{core electrons}$), $Z_{\text{eff}}$ would appear to remain perfectly constant down a group (e.g., $+1$ for all alkali metals).

However, in reality, more advanced calculations (such as Slater's Rules) reveal that core electrons do not shield *perfectly*. As a result, **$Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases slightly down a group.** Despite this slight increase in nuclear pull, the overriding factor down a group is that electrons are being added to shells with higher principal quantum numbers ($n$). These higher-energy shells are significantly further from the nucleus, meaning the valence electrons are physically further away and less tightly bound, even with the slight increase in $Z_{\text{eff}}$.

## 6.3 Trends in Atomic and Ionic Radii

Because the electron cloud of an atom does not have a rigid, well-defined boundary, measuring the "size" of an atom is not as straightforward as measuring a macroscopic object. Instead, chemists define atomic size based on the distances between nuclei when atoms are bonded together or packed in a solid.

The most common metric used is the **bonding atomic radius** (often called the covalent radius for nonmetals). It is defined as one-half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms joined by a single chemical bond. For example, the distance between the two iodine nuclei in an $I_2$ molecule is $2.66 \text{ \AA}$ (angstroms), so the atomic radius of iodine is defined as $1.33 \text{ \AA}$.

### Periodic Trends in Atomic Radii

The size of an atom is determined by two opposing forces: the inward pull of the nucleus on the electrons and the outward push of electron-electron repulsions. By applying the concepts of principal quantum shells ($n$) and effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) discussed in Section 6.2, we can predict two distinct periodic trends for atomic radii.

**1. Trend Down a Group: Atomic Radius Increases**
As we move from top to bottom down a group in the periodic table, the atomic radius increases. With each successive step down the group, electrons are added to a new principal quantum shell with a higher value of $n$. These higher-energy orbitals extend further from the nucleus. While the effective nuclear charge does increase slightly down a group, the physical expansion of the higher principal quantum shells completely dominates this effect, resulting in a larger overall atomic volume.

**2. Trend Across a Period: Atomic Radius Decreases**
As we move from left to right across a period, the principal quantum number ($n$) of the valence shell remains the same. However, as established in the previous section, the effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) increases steadily due to the addition of protons without corresponding effective shielding from valence electrons. This increasing nuclear pull draws the valence electron cloud closer to the nucleus, causing the atomic radius to systematically decrease.

### Trends in Ionic Radii

When an atom gains or loses electrons to become an ion, its radius changes dramatically. The principles governing these changes are grounded in electron-electron repulsions and effective nuclear charge.

**Cations are Always Smaller than their Parent Atoms**
When a neutral atom loses one or more electrons to form a cation, the electron-electron repulsions within the valence shell decrease. Because there are fewer electrons shielding each other, the remaining electrons experience a greater effective nuclear charge and are pulled tighter to the nucleus. Furthermore, main-group metals often lose all their valence electrons when forming cations, completely emptying the outermost principal quantum shell, which results in a massive decrease in size.

* *Example:* The radius of a neutral sodium atom (Na) is $1.86 \text{ \AA}$, while the radius of the sodium cation ($Na^+$) is only $0.99 \text{ \AA}$.

**Anions are Always Larger than their Parent Atoms**
When a nonmetal atom gains one or more electrons to form an anion, the nuclear charge remains constant, but the number of electrons increases. This added negative charge increases the electron-electron repulsions within the valence shell, causing the electron cloud to spread out and expand. The effective nuclear charge per electron decreases.

* *Example:* The radius of a neutral chlorine atom (Cl) is $0.99 \text{ \AA}$, whereas the chloride anion ($Cl^-$) has a radius of $1.81 \text{ \AA}$.

### Isoelectronic Series

An **isoelectronic series** is a group of ions or atoms that possess the exact same number of electrons, and therefore the same electron configuration. Comparing the radii of species within an isoelectronic series provides a perfect demonstration of the effect of nuclear charge on atomic size.

Consider the following series of ions, all of which contain $10$ electrons and have the electron configuration of neon: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6$.

| Species | Protons ($Z$) | Electrons | Electron Configuration | Ionic Radius (\AA) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $O^{2-}$ | $8$ | $10$ | $[\text{Ne}]$ | $1.40$ |
| $F^-$ | $9$ | $10$ | $[\text{Ne}]$ | $1.33$ |
| $Na^+$ | $11$ | $10$ | $[\text{Ne}]$ | $0.99$ |
| $Mg^{2+}$ | $12$ | $10$ | $[\text{Ne}]$ | $0.72$ |
| $Al^{3+}$ | $13$ | $10$ | $[\text{Ne}]$ | $0.53$ |

Because the number of electrons (and therefore the shielding effect) is identical across the entire series, the size of the ion is determined solely by the actual nuclear charge ($Z$). As the nuclear charge increases from $+8$ in oxygen to $+13$ in aluminum, the identical cloud of $10$ electrons experiences an increasingly powerful attractive force. Consequently, the radius decreases steadily as the atomic number increases within an isoelectronic series.

## 6.4 Trends in Ionization Energy

The chemical reactivity of an element is heavily dictated by how tightly it holds onto its electrons. **Ionization energy (IE)** is defined as the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of an isolated gaseous atom or ion.

Because electrons are electrostatically attracted to the positively charged nucleus, removing an electron always requires an input of energy. Therefore, ionization processes are always endothermic, and ionization energies are always positive values (typically expressed in $\text{kJ/mol}$).

We can define successive ionization energies depending on which electron is being removed:

* **First Ionization Energy ($I_1$):** The energy required to remove the first electron from a neutral atom.

$$ \text{X}(g) \rightarrow \text{X}^+(g) + e^- \quad \Delta E = I_1 $$

* **Second Ionization Energy ($I_2$):** The energy required to remove the second electron (from a $+1$ ion).

$$ \text{X}^+(g) \rightarrow \text{X}^{2+}(g) + e^- \quad \Delta E = I_2 $$

### Successive Ionization Energies

For any given element, the successive ionization energies always increase: $I_1 < I_2 < I_3$, and so on. Once the first electron is removed, the remaining electrons experience less electron-electron repulsion. The effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) acting on the remaining electrons increases, pulling them closer to the nucleus and making the next electron harder to remove.

A critical feature of successive ionization energies is the massive jump in energy required when removing a **core electron**. Valence electrons are shielded and relatively far from the nucleus, making them comparatively easy to remove. Core electrons, however, are in lower principal quantum shells ($n$), are much closer to the nucleus, and experience a nearly unshielded nuclear charge.

*Table 6.2: Successive Ionization Energies ($\text{kJ/mol}$) for Period 3 Elements*

| Element | $I_1$ | $I_2$ | $I_3$ | $I_4$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Na** ($[\text{Ne}]3s^1$) | **$496$** | $4560$ (core) | $6910$ | $9540$ |
| **Mg** ($[\text{Ne}]3s^2$) | $738$ | **$1450$** | $7730$ (core) | $10500$ |
| **Al** ($[\text{Ne}]3s^2 3p^1$) | $578$ | $1820$ | **$2750$** | $11600$ (core) |

Notice the bolded line separating the valence electrons from the core electrons. For sodium, the second electron must be removed from the stable, noble-gas core ($[\text{Ne}]$). This requires nearly ten times more energy than removing the valence electron, explaining why sodium forms $\text{Na}^+$ ions but never $\text{Na}^{2+}$ under normal chemical conditions.

### Periodic Trends in First Ionization Energy ($I_1$)

The trends for first ionization energy closely mirror those of atomic radius (Section 6.3) but in reverse. The closer an electron is to the nucleus, and the higher the effective nuclear charge it experiences, the more energy is required to remove it.

**1. Trend Down a Group: Ionization Energy Decreases**
As we move down a group, the valence electrons are located in higher principal quantum shells ($n$). The atomic radius increases significantly. Because the valence electron is physically further from the positive nucleus, the electrostatic force of attraction is weaker. Consequently, it requires less energy to remove the outermost electron as you go down a group.

* *Example:* $I_1$ for Lithium ($\text{Li}$) is $520 \text{ kJ/mol}$, while $I_1$ for Cesium ($\text{Cs}$) is only $376 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

**2. Trend Across a Period: Ionization Energy Generally Increases**
As we move from left to right across a period, electrons are added to the same principal quantum shell, but the effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) increases. The atom's radius shrinks, and the valence electrons are pulled closer to the increasingly positive nucleus. This stronger electrostatic attraction means more energy is required to remove an electron.

* *Example:* $I_1$ for Lithium ($\text{Li}$) is $520 \text{ kJ/mol}$, while $I_1$ for Neon ($\text{Ne}$) is $2080 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

### Exceptions to the Periodic Trend

While the general trend across a period is an increase in ionization energy, a close examination reveals a "saw-tooth" pattern with two distinct irregularities occurring between Groups 2/13 and Groups 15/16.

**Exception 1: Group 2 to Group 13 (e.g., Be to B)**
We would expect Boron ($Z=5$) to have a higher $I_1$ than Beryllium ($Z=4$) due to its higher nuclear charge. However, $I_1(\text{Be}) = 899 \text{ kJ/mol}$ and $I_1(\text{B}) = 801 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

* **The Reason:** Beryllium's valence electrons are in the $2s$ subshell ($[\text{He}]2s^2$). Boron's outermost electron is in the $2p$ subshell ($[\text{He}]2s^2 2p^1$). The $2p$ subshell is higher in energy than the $2s$ subshell. Furthermore, the $2s$ electrons effectively shield the $2p$ electron. Because the $2p$ electron in Boron is slightly further from the nucleus and well-shielded by the $2s$ electrons, it requires less energy to remove than a $2s$ electron in Beryllium.

**Exception 2: Group 15 to Group 16 (e.g., N to O)**
Similarly, Oxygen ($Z=8$) has a lower $I_1$ than Nitrogen ($Z=7$). $I_1(\text{N}) = 1402 \text{ kJ/mol}$ and $I_1(\text{O}) = 1314 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

* **The Reason:** This anomaly is explained by electron-electron repulsions within the orbitals. Nitrogen has a half-filled $2p$ subshell, with one electron in each of the three $p$ orbitals (according to Hund's Rule):
$\text{N}: [\text{He}] \ 2s^2 \ 2p_x^1 \ 2p_y^1 \ 2p_z^1$
Oxygen contains one additional electron, which must pair up with another electron in one of the $2p$ orbitals:
$\text{O}: [\text{He}] \ 2s^2 \ 2p_x^2 \ 2p_y^1 \ 2p_z^1$
The two electrons sharing the $2p_x$ orbital repel each other more strongly than electrons in separate orbitals. This increased electrostatic repulsion makes it easier to remove one of those paired electrons in Oxygen, resulting in a dip in ionization energy.

## 6.5 Trends in Electron Affinity

While ionization energy measures the energy required to *remove* an electron from an atom, **electron affinity (EA)** measures the energy change that occurs when an electron is *added* to a gaseous atom. It represents the likelihood of a neutral atom gaining an electron to form an anion.

The general equation for the first electron affinity is:

$$ \text{X}(g) + e^- \rightarrow \text{X}^-(g) \quad \Delta E = \text{EA} $$

Because ionization energy (removing an electron) must overcome the electrostatic attraction of the nucleus, it is always an endothermic process ($\Delta E > 0$). In contrast, an electron approaching a neutral atom experiences an attraction to the nucleus, provided the nucleus is not completely shielded by the existing electrons. Therefore, adding an electron is often an exothermic process, meaning energy is released.

By standard thermodynamic convention, if energy is released, the value of $\Delta E$ is negative. The more negative the electron affinity, the greater the tendency of the atom to accept an electron and the more stable the resulting anion.

*Note: Some texts define electron affinity as a positive number when energy is released. In this text, we will treat electron affinity strictly as an energy change ($\Delta E$), where negative values indicate an exothermic process.*

### Periodic Trends in Electron Affinity

The trends for electron affinity are fundamentally driven by the same factors as ionization energy and atomic radius—specifically, effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) and the distance of the valence shell from the nucleus. However, the trends for electron affinity are notably less regular than those for ionization energy.

**1. Trend Across a Period: Generally Becomes More Negative**
As we move from left to right across a period, the effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) increases, and the atomic radius decreases. The incoming electron is pulled closer to the nucleus and experiences a stronger electrostatic attraction. Consequently, more energy is released, and the electron affinity becomes more negative.

* Halogens (Group 17) have the most negative electron affinities because adding one electron completely fills their valence $p$ subshell, resulting in a highly stable, noble-gas electron configuration.

**2. Trend Down a Group: Generally Becomes Less Negative (with Notable Exceptions)**
As we descend a group, the principal quantum number ($n$) increases. The added electron must be placed in a larger orbital further from the nucleus. Because the distance between the nucleus and the incoming electron is greater, the attractive force is weaker, and less energy is generally released.

* This trend is quite weak and fraught with exceptions, largely due to variations in electron-electron repulsions in different-sized orbitals.

### Notable Exceptions to the Trends

The irregularities in electron affinity trends provide profound insights into quantum mechanical orbital structure and electron-electron repulsions.

**Exception 1: Filled and Half-Filled Subshells (Groups 2, 15, and 18)**
Just as we saw irregularities in ionization energy for elements with stable orbital configurations, we see parallel anomalies in electron affinity.

* **Group 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals):** Elements like Beryllium ($[\text{He}]2s^2$) have a filled $s$ subshell. Any added electron must go into the higher-energy $p$ subshell ($[\text{He}]2s^2 2p^1$). The $2p$ orbital is significantly shielded by the $2s$ electrons and is higher in energy, making the addition of an electron endothermic ($\Delta E > 0$).
* **Group 15 (Pnictogens):** Elements like Nitrogen have a exactly half-filled $p$ subshell.

```text
Nitrogen (N):  [He]  (↑↓)   (↑ )(↑ )(↑ )
                      2s     2px 2py 2pz

```

Adding an electron requires placing it into an already occupied $p$ orbital, forcing two electrons to share the same region of space. The resulting electron-electron repulsion counteracts the attraction of the nucleus. Thus, the electron affinity of Nitrogen is actually positive ($\Delta E > 0$), and phosphorus has a much less negative EA than expected.

* **Group 18 (Noble Gases):** Noble gases have completely filled valence shells ($ns^2 np^6$). An added electron would have to be placed into an entirely new, higher-energy principal quantum shell ($n+1$). This is highly unfavorable, resulting in large positive electron affinities.

**Exception 2: The Period 2 vs. Period 3 Anomaly (e.g., F vs. Cl)**
Based on the group trend, one would expect Fluorine (the top halogen) to have the most negative electron affinity of all elements. However, Chlorine actually holds this title.

| Element | Electron Configuration | Radius (\AA) | Electron Affinity ($\text{kJ/mol}$) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fluorine (F) | $[\text{He}] 2s^2 2p^5$ | $0.71$ | $-328$ |
| Chlorine (Cl) | $[\text{Ne}] 3s^2 3p^5$ | $0.99$ | **$-349$** |
| Bromine (Br) | $[\text{Ar}] 4s^2 3d^{10} 4p^5$ | $1.14$ | $-325$ |
| Iodine (I) | $[\text{Kr}] 5s^2 4d^{10} 5p^5$ | $1.33$ | $-295$ |

**The Reason:** Fluorine is an extremely small atom. Its seven valence electrons are crowded into a very compact $n=2$ shell. When an eighth electron is added, it experiences intense electron-electron repulsion from the highly concentrated negative charge already present in that small volume. This repulsion significantly reduces the net amount of energy released.

Chlorine, on the other hand, is larger. Its seven valence electrons are spread out in the more diffuse $n=3$ shell. The electron-electron repulsions are much lower, allowing the high effective nuclear charge to pull in the extra electron more favorably. This same anomaly occurs between Oxygen ($-141 \text{ kJ/mol}$) and Sulfur ($-200 \text{ kJ/mol}$).

### Second Electron Affinities

While some atoms readily gain one electron to form $-1$ ions (like halogens), others, like oxygen, gain two electrons to form $-2$ ions (the oxide ion, $\text{O}^{2-}$).

The *first* electron affinity of oxygen is exothermic:

$$ \text{O}(g) + e^- \rightarrow \text{O}^-(g) \quad \Delta E_1 = -141 \text{ kJ/mol} $$

However, the *second* electron affinity is always highly endothermic:

$$ \text{O}^-(g) + e^- \rightarrow \text{O}^{2-}(g) \quad \Delta E_2 = +744 \text{ kJ/mol} $$

Adding a negatively charged electron to an already negatively charged ion requires overcoming immense electrostatic repulsion. Therefore, all second (and higher) electron affinities require a substantial input of energy. The formation of stable $-2$ or $-3$ ions in nature (such as in the ionic compound $\text{MgO}$) is not driven by electron affinity, but rather by the massive stabilization energy released when these highly charged ions pack together into a solid crystal lattice, a concept we will explore further in Chapter 7.

## 6.6 Chemical Properties of Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

The broad categorization of elements into metals, nonmetals, and metalloids is one of the most fundamental subdivisions of the periodic table. The macroscopic chemical and physical properties of these three categories are direct macroscopic manifestations of the atomic properties we have discussed in previous sections—namely, effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$), atomic radius, ionization energy, and electron affinity.

The concept of **metallic character** refers to the extent to which an element exhibits the physical and chemical properties of metals. Metallic character generally increases as we move from right to left across a period and from top to bottom down a group.


### Metals

Metals comprise the vast majority of the periodic table, occupying the left side and the center (the transition metals, lanthanides, and actinides).

**Atomic Properties:** Metals are characterized by large atomic radii and low ionization energies. Their valence electrons are relatively loosely bound, meaning they can be removed with relatively little energy.

**Chemical Behavior:** Because of their low ionization energies, metals tend to undergo oxidation (loss of electrons) during chemical reactions to form positive ions (cations).

* Alkali metals (Group 1) easily lose one electron to form $+1$ ions, taking on a stable noble-gas electron configuration.
* Alkaline earth metals (Group 2) lose two electrons to form $+2$ ions.

The reactivity of metals generally correlates with their ionization energies. For example, within the alkali metals, reactivity violently increases as you move down the group (from Lithium to Cesium) because the outermost electron is further from the nucleus and progressively easier to remove.

**Compounds:** Compounds formed between metals and nonmetals tend to be ionic. When metals react with oxygen, they form **metal oxides**. A defining chemical characteristic of most metal oxides is that they are **basic oxides**. Those that are soluble in water react to form metal hydroxides (basic solutions).

$$ \text{Na}_2\text{O}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}(aq) $$

$$ \text{CaO}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{Ca(OH)}_2(aq) $$

Metal oxides that are insoluble in water will still typically react with acids to form a salt and water, a classic neutralization reaction.

### Nonmetals

Nonmetals are found in the upper right quadrant of the periodic table (along with Hydrogen, which is a nonmetal positioned in Group 1).

**Atomic Properties:** Nonmetals are characterized by small atomic radii, high effective nuclear charges, high ionization energies, and highly negative (exothermic) electron affinities. They hold onto their own electrons very tightly and have a strong tendency to attract additional electrons.

**Chemical Behavior:** Because of their high electron affinities, nonmetals tend to undergo reduction (gain of electrons) when reacting with metals, forming negative ions (anions). When reacting with other nonmetals, rather than transferring electrons completely, they typically share electrons to form covalent bonds (a topic explored deeply in Chapter 7).

**Compounds:** Compounds composed entirely of nonmetals are typically molecular substances. When nonmetals react with oxygen, they form **nonmetal oxides**. Unlike metal oxides, most nonmetal oxides are **acidic oxides**. When they dissolve in water, they react to form acids.

$$ \text{CO}_2(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3(aq) $$

$$ \text{SO}_3(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) $$

Like metal oxides, even insoluble nonmetal oxides (or those that do not react directly with water) will generally react with bases to form salts and water.

### Metalloids

The metalloids (or semimetals) are found along the stair-step line that divides the metals from the nonmetals. The commonly recognized metalloids are boron (B), silicon (Si), germanium (Ge), arsenic (As), antimony (Sb), and tellurium (Te).

**Atomic Properties:** Metalloids have intermediate effective nuclear charges, atomic radii, ionization energies, and electron affinities.

**Chemical Behavior:** The properties of metalloids fall between those of metals and nonmetals. They may exhibit some properties characteristic of metals and others characteristic of nonmetals, or their behavior may be entirely intermediate. For instance, a metalloid might be physically shiny like a metal but brittle like a nonmetal.

Chemically, they often form covalent bonds rather than ionic bonds, behaving somewhat more like nonmetals in their compounds. Several metalloids, most notably silicon and germanium, are electrical **semiconductors**. Unlike metals, which are excellent conductors of electricity, semiconductors only conduct electricity under certain conditions (such as increased temperature or when doped with impurities). This unique property makes metalloids indispensable in the modern electronics and solid-state computing industries.

## Chapter Summary

* **Development of the Periodic Table:** The modern periodic table evolved from early attempts to group elements by atomic mass (such as by Mendeleev and Meyer) to the modern arrangement based on increasing **atomic number**, as established by Moseley. The **Periodic Law** states that the properties of the elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
* **Effective Nuclear Charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$):** The net attractive force experienced by an electron from the positive nucleus is its effective nuclear charge. It is calculated by subtracting the shielding effect of inner-core electrons from the actual nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - S$). $Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases significantly across a period and slightly down a group.
* **Atomic and Ionic Radii:** Atomic radius increases down a group (due to increasing principal quantum number, $n$) and decreases across a period (due to increasing $Z_{\text{eff}}$). Cations are always smaller than their parent atoms, while anions are always larger. Within an isoelectronic series, radius decreases as nuclear charge increases.
* **Ionization Energy (IE):** The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom. IE generally decreases down a group and increases across a period. Successive ionization energies always increase, with a massive jump in energy required to remove a core electron.
* **Electron Affinity (EA):** The energy change associated with adding an electron to a gaseous atom. EA generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) across a period. Halogens have the most negative electron affinities, while noble gases have positive electron affinities.
* **Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids:**
* **Metals** have low ionization energies, tend to form cations, and produce basic oxides.
* **Nonmetals** have highly negative electron affinities, tend to form anions or covalent bonds, and produce acidic oxides.
* **Metalloids** have intermediate properties and are fundamentally important as semiconductors. Metallic character increases towards the bottom left of the periodic table.
