Chemistry is not merely about describing what matter *is*; it is fundamentally about how matter *changes*. This chapter bridges the microscopic world of atoms with the macroscopic world of laboratory measurement. We will delve into stoichiometry, the mathematical language of chemistry. By mastering the mole concept, you will learn to balance chemical equations, deduce chemical formulas from experimental mass data, and precisely calculate the quantities of reactants consumed and products yielded in reactions. These foundational tools are essential for predicting and quantifying chemical behavior in the laboratory.

## 3.1 Balancing Chemical Equations

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances are converted into new substances with different physical and chemical properties. To concisely describe these transformations, chemists use **chemical equations**.

A chemical equation represents a chemical reaction using the chemical formulas of the participating substances. The starting substances, known as **reactants**, are written on the left side of the equation. The substances formed during the reaction, known as **products**, are written on the right side. An arrow ($\rightarrow$) points from the reactants to the products, representing the phrase "yields" or "reacts to produce."

### The Law of Conservation of Mass

When writing a chemical equation, we must adhere to a fundamental principle of nature established by Antoine Lavoisier in the late 18th century: the **Law of Conservation of Mass**. This law states that in an ordinary chemical reaction, matter is neither created nor destroyed.

In the context of Chapter 2, where we established the particulate nature of matter, this means that atoms are merely rearranged during a chemical reaction. No atoms are lost, and no new atoms are formed. Consequently, a valid chemical equation must be **balanced**, meaning it must contain the exact same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the arrow.

### Anatomy of a Chemical Equation

Consider the reaction where hydrogen gas reacts with oxygen gas to form liquid water. We can write the initial, unbalanced skeletal equation as:

$$ \text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

Let's break down the components of this equation:

* **Chemical Formulas:** $\text{H}_2$, $\text{O}_2$, and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ identify the specific substances involved. The subscript numbers indicate how many atoms of each element are present in a single molecule.
* **Physical States:** The letters in parentheses indicate the physical state of each substance at the time of the reaction. The most common state symbols are:
* $(g)$ for gas
* $(l)$ for liquid
* $(s)$ for solid
* $(aq)$ for aqueous (dissolved in water)

If we count the atoms in our skeletal equation, we immediately see a problem:

| Element | Reactants (Left Side) | Products (Right Side) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **H** | 2 | 2 |
| **O** | 2 | 1 |

We have two oxygen atoms on the reactant side but only one on the product side. This violates the Law of Conservation of Mass. To fix this, we must balance the equation.

### Subscripts vs. Coefficients

To balance an equation, we place whole numbers called **coefficients** in front of the chemical formulas. A coefficient multiplies the entire chemical formula that immediately follows it.

> **Crucial Rule:** When balancing a chemical equation, you may **only** change the coefficients. You must **never** change the subscripts within a chemical formula.

Changing a subscript changes the actual identity of the substance. For example, if you tried to balance the water equation by changing $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ to $\text{H}_2\text{O}_2$, you are no longer describing the formation of water; you are describing the formation of hydrogen peroxide, a completely different compound with very different chemical properties.

### Steps for Balancing by Inspection

Most chemical equations you will encounter at this stage can be balanced by a systematic trial-and-error process known as balancing by inspection. Follow these steps:

1. **Write the skeletal equation:** Ensure all reactant and product formulas are written correctly.
2. **Count the atoms:** Tally the number of atoms of each element on both sides of the equation.
3. **Add coefficients to balance elements:** Start with the most complex molecule. Balance one element at a time by placing coefficients in front of formulas.

* *Pro Tip 1:* Leave elements that appear as pure, uncombined substances (like $\text{O}_2$, $\text{H}_2$, or solid $\text{Fe}$) for last.
* *Pro Tip 2:* If polyatomic ions appear unchanged on both sides of the equation, treat them as a single unit rather than counting their individual atoms.

1. **Verify your work:** Recount the atoms of each element to ensure both sides are equal.
2. **Simplify if necessary:** Coefficients should be in the lowest possible whole-number ratio. If you balance an equation and end up with $2\text{H}_2 + 2\text{Cl}_2 \rightarrow 4\text{HCl}$, divide all coefficients by 2 to get the simplest form: $\text{H}_2 + \text{Cl}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{HCl}$.

#### Returning to the Water Equation

Let's apply these rules to our water formation reaction:

$$ \text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

1. **Balance Oxygen:** We have 2 oxygen atoms on the left and 1 on the right. Place a coefficient of 2 in front of water.

$$ \text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

1. **Balance Hydrogen:** Now we have 4 hydrogen atoms on the right (2 molecules $\times$ 2 atoms/molecule) and only 2 on the left. Place a coefficient of 2 in front of the hydrogen gas.

$$ 2\text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

1. **Verify:**

* Left: 4 H, 2 O
* Right: 4 H, 2 O

The equation is balanced and in its simplest whole-number ratio.

### Worked Examples

**Example 1: The Combustion of Methane**
Methane ($\text{CH}_4$) gas reacts with oxygen gas to produce carbon dioxide gas and water vapor. Write the balanced equation.

**Step 1:** Write the skeletal equation.

$$ \text{CH}_4(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{CO}_2(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

**Step 2:** Count atoms.

* Reactants: 1 C, 4 H, 2 O
* Products: 1 C, 2 H, 3 O (2 from $\text{CO}_2$, 1 from $\text{H}_2\text{O}$)

**Step 3:** Balance. Carbon is already balanced. Let's balance Hydrogen by placing a 2 in front of $\text{H}_2\text{O}$.

$$ \text{CH}_4(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

Now recount oxygen. We have 2 O atoms in $\text{CO}_2$ and 2 O atoms in $2\text{H}_2\text{O}$, for a total of 4 O atoms on the right. We only have 2 on the left. Place a 2 in front of $\text{O}_2$.

$$ \text{CH}_4(g) + 2\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

**Step 4:** Verify.

* Reactants: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O
* Products: 1 C, 4 H, 4 O. The equation is balanced.

**Example 2: Reaction with Polyatomic Ions**
Aqueous solutions of sodium phosphate and calcium chloride react to form solid calcium phosphate and aqueous sodium chloride.

**Step 1:** Write the skeletal equation (relying on nomenclature rules from Chapter 2).

$$ \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4(aq) + \text{CaCl}_2(aq) \rightarrow \text{Ca}_3(\text{PO}_4)_2(s) + \text{NaCl}(aq) $$

**Step 2:** Notice that the phosphate ion ($\text{PO}_4^{3-}$) remains intact on both sides. We will treat it as a single unit.

* Reactants: 3 Na, 1 $\text{PO}_4$, 1 Ca, 2 Cl
* Products: 1 Na, 2 $\text{PO}_4$, 3 Ca, 1 Cl

**Step 3:** Balance the complex parts first. There are 3 Ca on the right, so we need 3 $\text{CaCl}_2$ on the left.

$$ \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4(aq) + 3\text{CaCl}_2(aq) \rightarrow \text{Ca}_3(\text{PO}_4)_2(s) + \text{NaCl}(aq) $$

There are 2 $\text{PO}_4$ groups on the right, so we need 2 $\text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4$ on the left.

$$ 2\text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4(aq) + 3\text{CaCl}_2(aq) \rightarrow \text{Ca}_3(\text{PO}_4)_2(s) + \text{NaCl}(aq) $$

Now check the remaining elements. On the left, we have $2 \times 3 = 6$ Na atoms and $3 \times 2 = 6$ Cl atoms. To balance this, place a 6 in front of $\text{NaCl}$.

$$ 2\text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4(aq) + 3\text{CaCl}_2(aq) \rightarrow \text{Ca}_3(\text{PO}_4)_2(s) + 6\text{NaCl}(aq) $$

**Step 4:** Verify.

* Reactants: 6 Na, 2 $\text{PO}_4$, 3 Ca, 6 Cl
* Products: 3 Ca, 2 $\text{PO}_4$, 6 Na, 6 Cl. The equation is balanced.

## 3.2 Simple Patterns of Chemical Reactivity

The sheer number of possible chemical reactions is staggering. However, by observing the reactants and products of many processes, chemists have identified fundamental patterns of reactivity. Recognizing these patterns allows us to predict the products of a reaction when we know the reactants. In this section, we will examine three of the most common and simple types of chemical reactions: combination, decomposition, and combustion.

### Combination (Synthesis) Reactions

In a **combination reaction** (also known as a synthesis reaction), two or more substances react to form one single product. The reactants can be elements or compounds, but the product is always a single compound.

We can represent the general pattern of a combination reaction as follows:

```text
  Reactant 1      Reactant 2          Product
    [ A ]     +     [ B ]      ➞      [ AB ]

```

$$ A + B \rightarrow C $$

#### Reactions of Elements

The most straightforward combination reactions occur when two elements react to form a compound. For example, when solid magnesium metal burns in the presence of oxygen gas, a bright white flame is produced alongside a white powdery product, magnesium oxide:

$$ 2\text{Mg}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{MgO}(s) $$

Another classic example is the violent reaction between an alkali metal and a halogen gas to form an ionic salt. When sodium metal is exposed to chlorine gas, sodium chloride (table salt) is synthesized:

$$ 2\text{Na}(s) + \text{Cl}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{NaCl}(s) $$

#### Reactions Involving Compounds

Combination reactions can also involve compounds acting as reactants. For instance, when gaseous hydrogen chloride and gaseous ammonia come into contact, they undergo a combination reaction to form a white cloud of solid ammonium chloride:

$$ \text{NH}_3(g) + \text{HCl}(g) \rightarrow \text{NH}_4\text{Cl}(s) $$

### Decomposition Reactions

A **decomposition reaction** is essentially the reverse of a combination reaction. In this process, a single reactant breaks down into two or more simpler substances. These reactions typically require an input of energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity to break the chemical bonds within the reactant.

The general pattern of a decomposition reaction is:

```text
   Reactant           Product 1      Product 2
    [ AB ]      ➞       [ A ]    +     [ B ]

```

$$ C \rightarrow A + B $$

#### Thermal Decomposition

Many compounds decompose when heated. A highly industrially relevant example is the decomposition of calcium carbonate (limestone). When heated vigorously (indicated by the $\Delta$ symbol over the reaction arrow), it breaks down into solid calcium oxide (quicklime) and carbon dioxide gas:

$$ \text{CaCO}_3(s) \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{CaO}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g) $$

Metal azides also decompose upon impact or rapid heating. The decomposition of sodium azide is the explosive reaction used to rapidly inflate automobile airbags:

$$ 2\text{NaN}_3(s) \rightarrow 2\text{Na}(s) + 3\text{N}_2(g) $$

#### Electrolytic Decomposition

Passing an electrical current through certain compounds can also induce decomposition, a process known as electrolysis. For example, liquid water can be separated into its constituent elemental gases, hydrogen and oxygen:

$$ 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \xrightarrow{\text{electricity}} 2\text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) $$

### Combustion Reactions

**Combustion reactions** are rapid reactions that produce a flame. Most combustion reactions we observe in everyday life involve oxygen gas ($\text{O}_2$) from the air acting as a reactant.

#### Combustion of Hydrocarbons

The most common combustion reactions involve the burning of hydrocarbons (compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen). When a hydrocarbon undergoes *complete* combustion in the presence of sufficient oxygen, the products are always carbon dioxide gas and water vapor, along with the release of significant thermal energy.

Consider the combustion of propane ($\text{C}_3\text{H}_8$), a gas commonly used in outdoor grills and heating systems:

$$ \text{C}_3\text{H}_8(g) + 5\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 3\text{CO}_2(g) + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

#### Combustion of Oxygen-Containing Organic Compounds

Organic compounds that contain oxygen—such as alcohols or sugars—also undergo combustion to produce carbon dioxide and water. For example, the combustion of liquid methanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$) proceeds as follows:

$$ 2\text{CH}_3\text{OH}(l) + 3\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_2(g) + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

> **Important Note:** If the supply of oxygen is limited during a combustion reaction, *incomplete* combustion occurs. Incomplete combustion yields carbon monoxide ($\text{CO}$), a highly toxic and invisible gas, or fine solid particles of carbon ($\text{C}$, soot) in addition to, or instead of, carbon dioxide.

#### Combustion of Metals

It is important to note that the term "combustion" is not strictly limited to organic compounds. As seen in the combination reaction of magnesium and oxygen earlier, metals can also burn (combust) in oxygen. Therefore, the reaction:

$$ 2\text{Mg}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{MgO}(s) $$

can be correctly classified as *both* a combination reaction and a combustion reaction.

## 3.3 Formula Weights and Molecular Weights

In Chapter 2, we established that the mass of a single atom is measured in atomic mass units (amu) and can be found on the periodic table. When atoms combine to form compounds, the mass of the resulting chemical unit is simply the sum of the masses of its constituent atoms.

The **formula weight (FW)** of a substance is defined as the sum of the atomic weights of the atoms in its chemical formula. If the chemical formula happens to be the chemical symbol of an individual element, such as $\text{Na}$, the formula weight equals the atomic weight of that element. If the formula is that of a compound, the formula weight is found by multiplying the atomic weight of each element by its subscript in the formula and adding the results.

### Calculating Formula Weight

To determine the formula weight, we tally the number of atoms of each element present in the chemical formula, multiply by their respective atomic weights, and sum the total.

Consider sulfuric acid, $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$. To find its formula weight, we sum the atomic weights of two hydrogen atoms, one sulfur atom, and four oxygen atoms:

| Element | Number of Atoms | &nbsp; | Atomic Weight | &nbsp; | Total Mass Contribution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Hydrogen (H)** | 2 | $\times$ | 1.01 amu | = | 2.02 amu |
| **Sulfur (S)** | 1 | $\times$ | 32.06 amu | = | 32.06 amu |
| **Oxygen (O)** | 4 | $\times$ | 16.00 amu | = | 64.00 amu |
| &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; |
| **Formula Weight** | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | &nbsp; | **98.08 amu** |

For compounds containing polyatomic ions in parentheses, remember to multiply the subscript outside the parentheses by the subscript of every element inside the parentheses. For example, in calcium nitrate, $\text{Ca}(\text{NO}_3)_2$, there is 1 calcium atom, 2 nitrogen atoms, and 6 oxygen atoms.

### Formula Weight vs. Molecular Weight

The terms *formula weight* and *molecular weight* are closely related but hold an important distinction based on the type of bonding in the compound.

If the chemical formula represents a true molecule—a discrete, independently existing entity formed by covalent bonds (which we will explore deeply in Part III)—the formula weight is specifically called the **molecular weight (MW)**. For example, water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) and glucose ($\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$) exist as molecules. Therefore, it is perfectly correct to say the molecular weight of water is 18.02 amu.

However, ionic compounds, such as sodium chloride ($\text{NaCl}$), do not exist as independent molecules. Instead, they form vast, three-dimensional crystalline lattices consisting of alternating positive and negative ions. As established in Chapter 2, the formula $\text{NaCl}$ is an *empirical formula*, representing only the simplest whole-number ratio of ions in the crystal.

```text
    Na+  Cl-  Na+  Cl-
    Cl-  Na+  Cl-  Na+  (Simplified 2D representation 
    Na+  Cl-  Na+  Cl-   of an ionic crystal lattice)

```

Because there is no distinct "molecule" of $\text{NaCl}$, it is technically incorrect to refer to its "molecular weight." Instead, we must use the broader term **formula weight**. The formula weight of $\text{NaCl}$ is the mass of one sodium ion plus the mass of one chloride ion (approximately 58.44 amu).

> **Note:** The mass of an electron is so exceptionally small compared to protons and neutrons that the loss or gain of electrons to form ions has a negligible effect on mass. Therefore, the atomic weight of a neutral sodium atom is effectively identical to the mass of a sodium ion ($\text{Na}^+$) for all standard chemical calculations.

### Percentage Composition from Chemical Formulas

Chemists often need to know the elemental composition of a compound—that is, the mass percentage of each element making up the substance. This is known as the **percentage composition** by mass.

We can calculate the percentage composition of any element in a compound directly from its chemical formula using the following equation:

$$ \% \text{ Mass of Element} = \left( \frac{n \times \text{Atomic Weight of Element}}{\text{Formula Weight of Compound}} \right) \times 100\% $$

Where $n$ represents the number of atoms of that specific element in the chemical formula.

#### Example Calculation: Percentage Composition

Let us calculate the percentage composition of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen in ethanol ($\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}$).

**Step 1: Calculate the formula weight of ethanol.**
Ethanol contains 2 carbon atoms, 6 hydrogen atoms (5 + 1), and 1 oxygen atom.

* Mass from C: $2 \times 12.01 \text{ amu} = 24.02 \text{ amu}$
* Mass from H: $6 \times 1.01 \text{ amu} = 6.06 \text{ amu}$
* Mass from O: $1 \times 16.00 \text{ amu} = 16.00 \text{ amu}$
* **Formula Weight = 46.08 amu**

**Step 2: Apply the percentage composition equation for each element.**

For Carbon:

$$ \% \text{ C} = \left( \frac{24.02 \text{ amu}}{46.08 \text{ amu}} \right) \times 100\% = 52.13\% $$

For Hydrogen:

$$ \% \text{ H} = \left( \frac{6.06 \text{ amu}}{46.08 \text{ amu}} \right) \times 100\% = 13.15\% $$

For Oxygen:

$$ \% \text{ O} = \left( \frac{16.00 \text{ amu}}{46.08 \text{ amu}} \right) \times 100\% = 34.72\% $$

**Step 3: Verify the results.**
As a simple check, the sum of all individual mass percentages should equal 100%.
$52.13\% + 13.15\% + 34.72\% = 100.00\%$. The calculation is confirmed.

Understanding percentage composition is a critical step backward; just as a chemical formula dictates the mass percentages of its elements, knowing the mass percentages of an unknown substance allows a chemist to experimentally deduce its chemical formula, a process we will explore in Section 3.5.

## 3.4 Avogadro's Number and the Mole Concept

Even the smallest speck of matter visible to the naked eye contains a staggering, unimaginably large number of atoms. Because atoms and molecules are so minuscule, it is entirely impractical for chemists to count them individually when measuring out reactants for a chemical reaction. Instead, chemists need a bridge—a way to connect the microscopic world of individual atoms (measured in atomic mass units) to the macroscopic world of laboratory balances (measured in grams). This bridge is provided by the mole concept.

### The Mole: A Chemist's Dozen

In everyday life, we frequently use counting units to group items. We buy eggs by the **dozen** (12 items), paper by the **ream** (500 items), and pencils by the **gross** (144 items). A dozen always means 12, regardless of whether we are talking about eggs, donuts, or cars.

In chemistry, the standard unit for counting exceedingly large numbers of particles is the **mole** (abbreviated as **mol**).

A mole is defined as the amount of a substance that contains exactly $6.02214076 \times 10^{23}$ elementary entities (which can be atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, or any other specified particle). In practical calculations, this value is rounded to four significant figures:

$$ 1 \text{ mole} = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ particles} $$

This extraordinary value is known as **Avogadro's number** ($N_A$), named in honor of the 19th-century Italian scientist Amedeo Avogadro. To grasp the sheer magnitude of this number, consider this: if you had a mole of marbles, they would cover the entire surface of the Earth to a depth of several miles. Yet, because atoms are infinitesimally small, a mole of water molecules ($6.022 \times 10^{23}$ molecules of $\text{H}_2\text{O}$) fits easily into a small sip—about 18 milliliters.

### Molar Mass: Bridging the Micro and Macro

The true power of the mole lies in its relationship to mass. The mole is rigorously defined so that the mass of one mole of a substance in grams is numerically equal to the formula weight of that substance in atomic mass units (amu).

The mass in grams of exactly one mole of a substance is called its **molar mass**. The units of molar mass are grams per mole ($\text{g/mol}$).

Let's look at how this beautiful symmetry connects our calculations:

* A single carbon-12 atom has a mass of exactly $12.00 \text{ amu}$.
* Therefore, one mole of carbon-12 atoms ($6.022 \times 10^{23}$ atoms) has a mass of exactly $12.00 \text{ g}$. The molar mass of carbon is $12.00 \text{ g/mol}$.
* A single molecule of water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) has a molecular weight of $18.02 \text{ amu}$.
* Therefore, one mole of water has a mass of $18.02 \text{ g}$. The molar mass of water is $18.02 \text{ g/mol}$.

By determining the formula weight of any substance (as detailed in Section 3.3), you immediately know its molar mass.

### Interconverting Masses, Moles, and Particles

In the laboratory, we cannot measure moles directly; we measure mass using a balance. However, chemical equations (Section 3.1) are fundamentally written in terms of moles of reacting particles. Therefore, fluent interconversion between mass, moles, and the number of particles is one of the most critical skills in chemistry.

We achieve this using dimensional analysis (Section 1.6), relying on two primary conversion factors:

1. **Molar Mass:** Converts between Mass (grams) and Moles.
2. **Avogadro's Number:** Converts between Moles and Number of Particles.

The following schematic illustrates the pathway for these conversions:

```text
               [ Mass of Substance (grams) ]
                            |
                            |  Divide by Molar Mass (g/mol)
                            |  Multiply by Molar Mass (g/mol)
                            v
                    [ Amount (moles) ]
                            |
                            |  Multiply by Avogadro's No. (particles/mol)
                            |  Divide by Avogadro's No. (particles/mol)
                            v
       [ Number of Particles (atoms, molecules, ions) ]

```

Notice that you cannot convert directly from mass to number of particles; you must always use the mole as your intermediary stepping stone.

### Worked Examples

**Example 1: Converting Mass to Moles**
Ascorbic acid, commonly known as Vitamin C, has the chemical formula $\text{C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6$. If a daily vitamin tablet contains $500.0 \text{ mg}$ ($0.5000 \text{ g}$) of Vitamin C, how many moles of ascorbic acid does it contain?

**Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of $\text{C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6$.**

* C: $6 \times 12.01 \text{ g/mol} = 72.06 \text{ g/mol}$
* H: $8 \times 1.01 \text{ g/mol} = 8.08 \text{ g/mol}$
* O: $6 \times 16.00 \text{ g/mol} = 96.00 \text{ g/mol}$
* *Total Molar Mass* = $176.14 \text{ g/mol}$

**Step 2: Use dimensional analysis to convert grams to moles.**
We use the molar mass as a conversion factor, ensuring grams cancel out and moles remain.

$$ 0.5000 \text{ g C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6}{176.14 \text{ g C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6} \right) = 0.002839 \text{ mol C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6 $$

$$ = 2.839 \times 10^{-3} \text{ mol C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6 $$

**Example 2: Converting Mass to Number of Particles (Two-Step)**
A copper penny minted before 1982 consists almost entirely of pure copper ($\text{Cu}$) and has a mass of $3.10 \text{ g}$. How many individual copper atoms are in this penny?

**Step 1: Find the molar mass of copper.**
Looking at the periodic table, the atomic weight of Cu is $63.55 \text{ amu}$, meaning its molar mass is $63.55 \text{ g/mol}$.

**Step 2: Convert mass to moles.**

$$ 3.10 \text{ g Cu} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol Cu}}{63.55 \text{ g Cu}} \right) = 0.04878 \text{ mol Cu} $$

**Step 3: Convert moles to atoms using Avogadro's number.**

$$ 0.04878 \text{ mol Cu} \times \left( \frac{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms Cu}}{1 \text{ mol Cu}} \right) = 2.94 \times 10^{22} \text{ atoms Cu} $$

We can also write this elegantly as a single continuous calculation:

$$ 3.10 \text{ g Cu} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol Cu}}{63.55 \text{ g Cu}} \right) \times \left( \frac{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms Cu}}{1 \text{ mol Cu}} \right) = 2.94 \times 10^{22} \text{ atoms Cu} $$

**Example 3: Extracting Atoms from Molecules**
How many oxygen atoms are present in $10.0 \text{ g}$ of carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$)?

*Reasoning:* We must first find the moles of $\text{CO}_2$ molecules, then use Avogadro's number to find the number of $\text{CO}_2$ molecules, and finally recognize that each molecule contains exactly two oxygen atoms.

* Molar mass of $\text{CO}_2 = 12.01 + 2(16.00) = 44.01 \text{ g/mol}$.

$$ 10.0 \text{ g CO}_2 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol CO}_2}{44.01 \text{ g CO}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ molecules CO}_2}{1 \text{ mol CO}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{2 \text{ atoms O}}{1 \text{ molecule CO}_2} \right) $$

$$ = 2.74 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms O} $$

## 3.5 Determining Empirical and Molecular Formulas

In Section 3.3, we learned how to calculate the mass percentage of elements in a compound from its chemical formula. In the laboratory, chemists frequently must perform the reverse process: using experimental mass data to deduce the chemical formula of an unknown substance.

Because chemical formulas are built upon the ratios of *atoms* (and therefore *moles*), our primary goal when analyzing mass data is to convert grams into moles.

### Calculating Empirical Formulas

Recall that an **empirical formula** expresses the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. If we know the mass percentage of each element in a substance, we can determine its empirical formula using a straightforward, four-step process:

```text
  [ Mass % ]  ---1--->  [ Mass (g) ]  ---2--->  [ Moles ]  ---3--->  [ Mole Ratio ]  ---4--->  [ Empirical Formula ]

```

1. **Assume a 100 g sample:** This makes the math effortless. If a compound is 40.0% carbon by mass, a 100 g sample will contain exactly 40.0 g of carbon.
2. **Convert grams to moles:** Use the molar mass of each element (from the periodic table) to convert the mass of each element into moles.
3. **Determine the simplest mole ratio:** Divide the number of moles of each element by the *smallest* number of moles calculated in Step 2. This forces the smallest value to become exactly 1.
4. **Convert to whole numbers (if necessary):** The numbers obtained in Step 3 are the subscripts for the empirical formula. If any subscript is a fraction (like 1.5 or 1.33), multiply *all* subscripts by a small whole number to convert them to integers (e.g., multiply by 2 to clear a .5, or by 3 to clear a .33).

#### Worked Example: Empirical Formula from Percentage Composition

Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) cures scurvy and may help prevent the common cold. It is composed of 40.92% carbon ($\text{C}$), 4.58% hydrogen ($\text{H}$), and 54.50% oxygen ($\text{O}$) by mass. Determine its empirical formula.

**Step 1: Assume 100 g.**

* Mass of C = $40.92 \text{ g}$
* Mass of H = $4.58 \text{ g}$
* Mass of O = $54.50 \text{ g}$

**Step 2: Convert to moles.**

$$ 40.92 \text{ g C} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol C}}{12.01 \text{ g C}} \right) = 3.407 \text{ mol C} $$

$$ 4.58 \text{ g H} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol H}}{1.008 \text{ g H}} \right) = 4.54 \text{ mol H} $$

$$ 54.50 \text{ g O} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol O}}{16.00 \text{ g O}} \right) = 3.406 \text{ mol O} $$

**Step 3: Divide by the smallest number of moles (3.406).**

* $\text{C}: \frac{3.407}{3.406} = 1.00$
* $\text{H}: \frac{4.54}{3.406} = 1.33$
* $\text{O}: \frac{3.406}{3.406} = 1.00$

**Step 4: Convert to whole numbers.**
The ratio of C:H:O is $1 : 1.33 : 1$. We cannot have $1.33$ atoms in a formula. Recognizing that $0.33$ is roughly $\frac{1}{3}$, we multiply all subscripts by $3$ to achieve whole numbers.

* $\text{C}: 1 \times 3 = 3$
* $\text{H}: 1.33 \times 3 = 4$
* $\text{O}: 1 \times 3 = 3$

The empirical formula for ascorbic acid is **$\text{C}_3\text{H}_4\text{O}_3$**.

### Calculating Molecular Formulas

The empirical formula gives only the *ratio* of atoms. For molecular compounds, we often want the **molecular formula**, which provides the *actual* number of atoms in a single molecule.

The subscripts of a molecular formula are always a whole-number multiple of the subscripts in the empirical formula. To find this multiplier, we must know the compound's experimentally determined molecular weight (molar mass) and compare it to the weight of the empirical formula.

$$ \text{Whole-Number Multiple} = \frac{\text{Molecular Weight}}{\text{Empirical Formula Weight}} $$

#### Worked Example: Molecular Formula from Empirical Data

Let us continue with ascorbic acid. Through independent experiments, scientists determined that the molecular weight of ascorbic acid is approximately $176 \text{ amu}$. What is its molecular formula?

**Step 1: Calculate the empirical formula weight.**
Using the empirical formula we just found ($\text{C}_3\text{H}_4\text{O}_3$):

$$ (3 \times 12.01) + (4 \times 1.008) + (3 \times 16.00) = 36.03 + 4.032 + 48.00 = 88.06 \text{ amu} $$

**Step 2: Determine the whole-number multiple.**

$$ \text{Multiple} = \frac{176 \text{ amu}}{88.06 \text{ amu}} = 1.998 \approx 2 $$

**Step 3: Multiply the empirical formula by the multiple.**
Multiply the subscripts of $\text{C}_3\text{H}_4\text{O}_3$ by $2$:

$$ \text{Molecular Formula} = \text{C}_6\text{H}_8\text{O}_6 $$

### Combustion Analysis

How do chemists determine the mass percentage of elements in the first place? For compounds containing primarily carbon and hydrogen (hydrocarbons and many organic compounds), a common experimental technique is **combustion analysis**.

In a combustion train, a weighed sample of the unknown compound is burned vigorously in a furnace with an excess of oxygen gas. As we learned in Section 3.2, all carbon in the sample is converted to carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$) gas, and all hydrogen is converted to water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) vapor.

```text
                      [ Furnace ]           [ H2O Absorber ]        [ CO2 Absorber ]
 Excess O2(g) ➔   | Sample Burns |  ➔  | Absorbs H2O only |  ➔  | Absorbs CO2 only |  ➔  Excess Gas Exits

```

The combustion gases pass through two specialized absorption chambers. The first contains a chemical that absorbs only water; the second absorbs only carbon dioxide. By weighing these absorbers before and after the reaction, the chemist determines the exact mass of $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ and $\text{CO}_2$ produced.

Because of the Law of Conservation of Mass:

* All the carbon atoms in the $\text{CO}_2$ came directly from the original sample.
* All the hydrogen atoms in the $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ came directly from the original sample.

#### Worked Example: Combustion Analysis Calculation

A $0.255 \text{ g}$ sample of isopropyl alcohol, a compound containing only C, H, and O, undergoes combustion analysis. The reaction produces $0.561 \text{ g}$ of $\text{CO}_2$ and $0.306 \text{ g}$ of $\text{H}_2\text{O}$. Determine the empirical formula.

**Step 1: Calculate the mass of Carbon.**
Find the moles of $\text{CO}_2$, then moles of C, then mass of C.

$$ 0.561 \text{ g CO}_2 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol CO}_2}{44.01 \text{ g CO}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol C}}{1 \text{ mol CO}_2} \right) = 0.01275 \text{ mol C} $$

$$ 0.01275 \text{ mol C} \times 12.01 \text{ g/mol} = 0.153 \text{ g C} $$

**Step 2: Calculate the mass of Hydrogen.**
Find the moles of $\text{H}_2\text{O}$, then moles of H (note the 2:1 ratio), then mass of H.

$$ 0.306 \text{ g H}_2\text{O} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol H}_2\text{O}}{18.02 \text{ g H}_2\text{O}} \right) \times \left( \frac{2 \text{ mol H}}{1 \text{ mol H}_2\text{O}} \right) = 0.03396 \text{ mol H} $$

$$ 0.03396 \text{ mol H} \times 1.008 \text{ g/mol} = 0.0342 \text{ g H} $$

**Step 3: Calculate the mass of Oxygen (if present).**
The oxygen in the products came from *both* the sample and the added $\text{O}_2$ gas, so we cannot calculate it directly from the products. Instead, we subtract the mass of C and H from the total starting mass of the sample.

$$ \text{Mass of O} = \text{Total Mass} - (\text{Mass of C} + \text{Mass of H}) $$

$$ \text{Mass of O} = 0.255 \text{ g} - (0.153 \text{ g} + 0.0342 \text{ g}) = 0.0678 \text{ g O} $$

Now, convert the mass of O to moles:

$$ 0.0678 \text{ g O} \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol O}}{16.00 \text{ g O}} \right) = 0.00424 \text{ mol O} $$

**Step 4: Determine the simplest mole ratio.**
Divide by the smallest number of moles ($0.00424$):

* $\text{C}: \frac{0.01275}{0.00424} = 3.01 \approx 3$
* $\text{H}: \frac{0.03396}{0.00424} = 8.01 \approx 8$
* $\text{O}: \frac{0.00424}{0.00424} = 1.00$

The empirical formula for isopropyl alcohol is **$\text{C}_3\text{H}_8\text{O}$**.

## 3.6 Quantitative Information from Balanced Equations

The coefficients in a balanced chemical equation provide the exact ratio in which substances react and are produced. This area of study—calculating the quantities of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction—is known as **stoichiometry** (from the Greek words *stoicheion*, meaning "element," and *metron*, meaning "measure").

In Section 3.1, we saw that equations can be read at the microscopic level:

$$ 2\text{H}_2(g) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

This states that 2 molecules of hydrogen react with 1 molecule of oxygen to yield 2 molecules of water. However, because chemical reactions in the laboratory involve massive numbers of molecules, it is far more practical to read the coefficients as quantities of **moles**.

Thus, the balanced equation also states: **2 moles** of hydrogen gas react with **1 mole** of oxygen gas to yield **2 moles** of liquid water.

### Stoichiometric Equivalent Ratios

The stoichiometric coefficients allow us to write conversion factors, called **stoichiometric ratios** or **mole ratios**, that relate the amount of any two substances in a reaction. For the formation of water, we can establish several equivalencies:

* 2 mol $\text{H}_2 \simeq$ 1 mol $\text{O}_2$
* 2 mol $\text{H}_2 \simeq$ 2 mol $\text{H}_2\text{O}$
* 1 mol $\text{O}_2 \simeq$ 2 mol $\text{H}_2\text{O}$

The symbol $\simeq$ means "is stoichiometrically equivalent to." These relationships allow us to convert from moles of one substance to moles of another.

### The Stoichiometric Pathway

In a typical laboratory scenario, you measure substances by mass (grams), not by moles. Therefore, a complete stoichiometric calculation usually requires a three-step bridge to convert the mass of a known substance (Substance A) into the mass of an unknown substance (Substance B).

```text
[ Mass of A ]                          [ Mass of B ]
      |                                      ^
      | 1. Use Molar Mass of A               | 3. Use Molar Mass of B
      v                                      |
[ Moles of A ] ---------------------> [ Moles of B ]
               2. Use Mole Ratio from
                  Balanced Equation

```

**The Three-Step Process:**

1. **Convert grams of A to moles of A:** Divide the given mass of Substance A by its molar mass.
2. **Convert moles of A to moles of B:** Multiply the moles of A by the stoichiometric mole ratio (moles of B / moles of A) derived from the balanced chemical equation.
3. **Convert moles of B to grams of B:** Multiply the moles of B by the molar mass of Substance B.

### Worked Examples

**Example 1: Calculating the Mass of a Product**
Butane ($\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$) is the liquid fuel found in standard lighters. When it burns in the presence of oxygen, it undergoes combustion. Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$) produced when $1.00 \text{ g}$ of butane undergoes complete combustion.

**Step 1: Write and balance the chemical equation.**
As established in Section 3.2, combustion of a hydrocarbon yields $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$.

$$ 2\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}(l) + 13\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 8\text{CO}_2(g) + 10\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

**Step 2: Determine necessary molar masses.**

* Molar mass of $\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$: $(4 \times 12.01) + (10 \times 1.008) = 58.12 \text{ g/mol}$
* Molar mass of $\text{CO}_2$: $12.01 + (2 \times 16.00) = 44.01 \text{ g/mol}$

**Step 3: Apply the stoichiometric pathway.**
We are starting with $1.00 \text{ g}$ of $\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$ (Substance A) and want to find the mass of $\text{CO}_2$ (Substance B). We will string the three conversion steps into a single dimensional analysis calculation.

$$ 1.00 \text{ g C}_4\text{H}_{10} \times \underbrace{ \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol C}_4\text{H}_{10}}{58.12 \text{ g C}_4\text{H}_{10}} \right) }_{\text{Step 1: Mass} \rightarrow \text{Moles}} \times \underbrace{ \left( \frac{8 \text{ mol CO}_2}{2 \text{ mol C}_4\text{H}_{10}} \right) }_{\text{Step 2: Mole Ratio}} \times \underbrace{ \left( \frac{44.01 \text{ g CO}_2}{1 \text{ mol CO}_2} \right) }_{\text{Step 3: Moles} \rightarrow \text{Mass}} $$

$$ = \left( \frac{1.00}{58.12} \right) \times \left( \frac{8}{2} \right) \times 44.01 \text{ g CO}_2 = 3.03 \text{ g CO}_2 $$

Burning $1.00 \text{ g}$ of butane yields $3.03 \text{ g}$ of carbon dioxide. (Notice how the units cancel at each step, leaving only grams of $\text{CO}_2$ at the end).

**Example 2: Calculating the Mass of a Required Reactant**
Solid lithium hydroxide ($\text{LiOH}$) is used in spacecraft and submarines to remove carbon dioxide from the air, preventing toxic buildup. The reaction produces solid lithium carbonate and liquid water:

$$ 2\text{LiOH}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{Li}_2\text{CO}_3(s) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

How many grams of solid lithium hydroxide are required to completely absorb $1.00 \text{ kg}$ ($1000 \text{ g}$) of exhaled $\text{CO}_2$?

**Step 1: Check the equation and determine molar masses.**
The equation is already balanced.

* Molar mass of $\text{CO}_2 = 44.01 \text{ g/mol}$
* Molar mass of $\text{LiOH} = 6.94 + 16.00 + 1.008 = 23.95 \text{ g/mol}$

**Step 2: Apply the stoichiometric pathway.**
We start with $1000 \text{ g}$ of $\text{CO}_2$ and need to find the mass of $\text{LiOH}$.

$$ 1000 \text{ g CO}_2 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol CO}_2}{44.01 \text{ g CO}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{2 \text{ mol LiOH}}{1 \text{ mol CO}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{23.95 \text{ g LiOH}}{1 \text{ mol LiOH}} \right) $$

$$ = \left( \frac{1000}{44.01} \right) \times 2 \times 23.95 \text{ g LiOH} = 1088 \text{ g LiOH} $$

It requires approximately $1.09 \times 10^3 \text{ g}$ (or $1.09 \text{ kg}$) of lithium hydroxide to scrub $1.00 \text{ kg}$ of carbon dioxide from the cabin air.

## 3.7 Limiting Reactants and Theoretical Yield

In the stoichiometric calculations performed in Section 3.6, we assumed that we had exactly the right amount of every reactant required for the reaction to proceed perfectly to completion. In reality, chemists rarely mix reactants in their exact stoichiometric ratios. Usually, one reactant is supplied in abundance to ensure that the more expensive or important reactant is completely consumed.

When reactants are not mixed in precise stoichiometric ratios, one reactant will run out before the others. This completely halts the reaction, dictating the maximum amount of product that can be formed.

### The Sandwich Analogy

To understand this concept, consider a non-chemical example: assembling a simple cheese sandwich. The "recipe" (or balanced equation) is:

```text
  2 Slices of Bread  +  1 Slice of Cheese  ➞  1 Cheese Sandwich
        [B] [B]      +         [C]         ➞       [B-C-B]

```

Suppose you look in your kitchen and find you have 10 slices of bread and 3 slices of cheese. How many sandwiches can you make?

* From 10 slices of bread, you *could* make 5 sandwiches (assuming infinite cheese).
* From 3 slices of cheese, you *could* make 3 sandwiches (assuming infinite bread).

You can only make **3 sandwiches**. As soon as the third sandwich is assembled, you run out of cheese. The process stops. You will have 4 slices of bread left over.

In chemical terms:

* The **cheese** is the **limiting reactant**. It is the reactant that is entirely consumed and limits the amount of product formed.
* The **bread** is the **excess reactant**. It is the reactant that is not completely consumed; some remains unreacted when the process stops.

### Identifying the Limiting Reactant Mathematically

In a chemistry laboratory, you cannot simply look at the mass of two reactants and immediately know which is limiting, because atoms and molecules have different masses and react in different molar ratios. You must convert the starting amounts to moles and evaluate them against the balanced equation.

The most foolproof method for determining the limiting reactant is to calculate the amount of product that *could* be formed from each reactant individually.

**The Step-by-Step Method:**

1. Take the given mass of Reactant A and calculate the mass (or moles) of a specific product it would form if it reacted completely.
2. Take the given mass of Reactant B and calculate the mass (or moles) of that *same* product it would form if it reacted completely.
3. Compare the results. The reactant that yields the **smaller amount** of product is your limiting reactant. The smaller calculated amount of product is the absolute maximum that can be generated.

### Theoretical, Actual, and Percent Yield

The maximum amount of product that can be calculated from the limiting reactant is called the **theoretical yield**. It is the amount of product formed if the reaction goes to 100% completion with no errors, no side reactions, and no product lost during experimental transfers. It is a calculated, "perfect world" value.

In practice, we almost never achieve the theoretical yield. Reactions may not go to completion, side reactions might produce unwanted byproducts, or some product might stick to the filter paper or glassware during purification. The quantity of product actually gathered and weighed in the laboratory is the **actual yield**.

To evaluate the efficiency of a reaction, chemists calculate the **percent yield**, which compares the actual yield to the theoretical yield:

$$ \text{Percent Yield} = \left( \frac{\text{Actual Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} \right) \times 100\% $$

Percent yield cannot be greater than 100% (doing so would violate the Law of Conservation of Mass). A yield over 100% usually indicates an error, such as the product still being wet with solvent or containing impurities.

### Comprehensive Worked Example

**The Synthesis of Ammonia**
Ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$) is a vital industrial chemical used primarily in fertilizers. It is synthesized directly from nitrogen and hydrogen gases via the Haber process:

$$ \text{N}_2(g) + 3\text{H}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{NH}_3(g) $$

A chemist mixes $25.0 \text{ g}$ of $\text{N}_2$ with $5.00 \text{ g}$ of $\text{H}_2$.

1. Which is the limiting reactant?
2. What is the theoretical yield of ammonia in grams?
3. If the reaction is carried out and $18.5 \text{ g}$ of ammonia is actually collected, what is the percent yield?

**Part 1 & 2: Finding Limiting Reactant and Theoretical Yield**

We will calculate the mass of $\text{NH}_3$ that can be produced from each reactant.

* *Determine Molar Masses:*
* $\text{N}_2 = 2 \times 14.01 = 28.02 \text{ g/mol}$
* $\text{H}_2 = 2 \times 1.008 = 2.016 \text{ g/mol}$
* $\text{NH}_3 = 14.01 + (3 \times 1.008) = 17.03 \text{ g/mol}$

* *Calculate yield from Nitrogen ($\text{N}_2$):*

$$ 25.0 \text{ g N}_2 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol N}_2}{28.02 \text{ g N}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{2 \text{ mol NH}_3}{1 \text{ mol N}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{17.03 \text{ g NH}_3}{1 \text{ mol NH}_3} \right) = 30.4 \text{ g NH}_3 $$

* *Calculate yield from Hydrogen ($\text{H}_2$):*

$$ 5.00 \text{ g H}_2 \times \left( \frac{1 \text{ mol H}_2}{2.016 \text{ g H}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{2 \text{ mol NH}_3}{3 \text{ mol H}_2} \right) \times \left( \frac{17.03 \text{ g NH}_3}{1 \text{ mol NH}_3} \right) = 28.2 \text{ g NH}_3 $$

* *Compare and Conclude:*
Hydrogen produces the smaller amount of product ($28.2 \text{ g}$ vs. $30.4 \text{ g}$). Therefore:

1. **Hydrogen ($\text{H}_2$) is the limiting reactant.** (Nitrogen is in excess).
2. **The theoretical yield is $28.2 \text{ g}$ of $\text{NH}_3$.**

**Part 3: Calculating Percent Yield**

We know the actual yield is $18.5 \text{ g}$ and our calculated theoretical yield is $28.2 \text{ g}$.

$$ \text{Percent Yield} = \left( \frac{18.5 \text{ g}}{28.2 \text{ g}} \right) \times 100\% = 65.6\% $$

The reaction was 65.6% efficient in converting the limiting reactant into the desired product.

## Chapter Summary

* **Chemical Equations:** Reactions are concisely represented by chemical equations, which must be balanced to obey the Law of Conservation of Mass. Coefficients dictate the quantitative ratios of reactants and products, while subscripts dictate chemical identity.
* **Patterns of Reactivity:** Many reactions fall into predictable categories, such as combination (synthesis), decomposition, and combustion. Recognizing these patterns allows chemists to predict reaction products.
* **Formula Weights:** The formula weight (or molecular weight) of a substance is the sum of the atomic weights of all atoms in its chemical formula, giving the mass of a single chemical unit in atomic mass units (amu).
* **The Mole Concept:** The mole is the chemist's standard counting unit, defined as $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's number). The mass of one mole of a substance in grams (molar mass) is numerically equal to its formula weight in amu.
* **Empirical and Molecular Formulas:** Experimental mass percentage data can be used to determine a compound's empirical formula (the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms). If the molecular weight is known, the true molecular formula can also be deduced.
* **Stoichiometry:** The coefficients in a balanced equation act as mole ratios, allowing us to calculate the mass of products formed from a given mass of reactants, or vice versa, using dimensional analysis.
* **Limiting Reactants and Yield:** In non-stoichiometric mixtures, the limiting reactant is the one that is completely consumed first, determining the theoretical yield (maximum possible product). The efficiency of a reaction is evaluated using percent yield, comparing the experimental actual yield to the theoretical yield.
