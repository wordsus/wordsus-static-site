In this chapter, we expand our perspective to the ecosystem, examining the interplay of organisms and their environment. We will trace how energy flows unidirectionally through trophic levels and how essential elements like carbon, nitrogen, and water are endlessly recycled. By understanding the biological production that sustains Earth's diverse biomes, we gain vital insight into our planet's life-support systems. Finally, we confront human-driven threats to global biodiversity and examine the vital conservation strategies, restoration ecology, and sustainable practices required to preserve the biosphere for future generations.

## 25.1 Energy Flow and Trophic Efficiency in Ecosystems

An ecosystem consists of all the organisms living in a community, as well as the abiotic factors with which they interact. While Chapter 24 focused on how species interact with one another, ecosystem ecology expands this view to track the transformation of energy and matter. Unlike chemical elements, which continuously cycle within ecosystems (a topic we will explore in Section 25.2), **energy flows through an ecosystem**. It enters as light, is converted to chemical energy by autotrophs, passes to heterotrophs in the organic compounds of food, and is ultimately dissipated as heat.

### Thermodynamics in Ecosystems

The flow of energy through ecosystems is governed by the laws of thermodynamics (previously introduced in Chapter 6):

1. **The First Law of Thermodynamics (Conservation of Energy):** Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. In an ecosystem, the total amount of energy stored in organic molecules plus the amounts reflected and dissipated as heat must equal the total solar energy intercepted by the plant.
2. **The Second Law of Thermodynamics (Entropy):** Every exchange of energy increases the entropy of the universe. In biological systems, energy conversions are inefficient; some energy is always lost as heat. Consequently, an ecosystem requires a constant influx of energy from an external source (usually the sun) to maintain its organization.

### Trophic Structure and Energy Routing

Energy and nutrients pass from primary producers to primary consumers, then to higher-level consumers. This feeding hierarchy is known as the ecosystem's **trophic structure**.

* **Primary Producers:** The trophic level that supports all others consists of autotrophs. Most are photosynthetic organisms (plants, algae, and cyanobacteria) that use light energy to synthesize sugars and other organic compounds.
* **Primary Consumers:** Herbivores that eat plants and other primary producers.
* **Secondary Consumers:** Carnivores that eat herbivores.
* **Tertiary & Quaternary Consumers:** Carnivores that eat other carnivores.
* **Detritivores (Decomposers):** Consumers that get their energy from **detritus**—nonliving organic material such as the remains of dead organisms, feces, and fallen leaves. Prokaryotes and fungi are the primary detritivores, secreting enzymes that digest organic material extracellularly.

```text
[Solar Energy]
      │
      ▼
[Primary Producers] ───────┐
      │                    │
      ▼ (Chemical Energy)  │ (Dead organic matter)
[Primary Consumers] ───────┼──────► [Detritivores]

      ▼ (Chemical Energy)  │              │
[Secondary Consumers] ─────┘              │
                                          ▼

```

### Secondary Production and Production Efficiency

When a caterpillar feeds on a plant leaf, only about a sixth of the energy in the leaf is used for **secondary production**—the amount of chemical energy in consumers' food that is converted to their own new biomass during a given period.

To understand where the rest of the energy goes, we must examine the organism's energy budget. The energy ingested is either:

1. Eliminated in feces (not assimilated).
2. Used for cellular respiration (assimilated, but lost as heat).
3. Used for growth and reproduction (assimilated and stored as biomass).

Only the energy stored as biomass is available to the next trophic level. We can measure the efficiency of this specific organism-level process using the **production efficiency** formula:

$$Production\ Efficiency = \frac{Net\ Secondary\ Production}{Assimilation\ of\ Primary\ Production} \times 100\%$$

* **Net Secondary Production:** The energy stored in biomass represented by growth and reproduction.
* **Assimilation:** Consists of the total energy taken in, minus the energy lost in feces (i.e., used for respiration + used for secondary production).

> **Note on Efficiency Variations:** Production efficiencies vary significantly among taxa. Birds and mammals, being endothermic, have highly inefficient production efficiencies (in the range of 1% to 3%) because they use so much energy to maintain a constant, high body temperature. Fishes (ectotherms) have production efficiencies around 10%, while insects and microorganisms can reach efficiencies of 40% or more.

### Trophic Efficiency and the "10% Rule"

While production efficiency looks at an individual or species, **trophic efficiency** is the percentage of production transferred from one *entire* trophic level to the next.

$$TE = \frac{P_n}{P_{n-1}} \times 100\%$$

*(Where $TE$ is Trophic Efficiency, $P_n$ is the net production of trophic level $n$, and $P_{n-1}$ is the net production of the preceding trophic level).*

Trophic efficiencies must always be less than production efficiencies because they take into account not only the energy lost through respiration and feces but also the fact that not all organic production at one trophic level is consumed by the next.

Trophic efficiencies generally range from 5% to 20%, depending on the ecosystem type. A widely used approximation in ecology is the **10% rule**: on average, only about 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is incorporated into the biomass of the next.

### Ecological Pyramids

The compounding loss of energy across trophic levels can be visualized using ecological pyramids.

**1. Pyramid of Net Production (Energy Pyramid):**
This pyramid represents the loss of energy with each transfer in a food chain. The width of each tier is proportional to the net production, expressed in Joules (J), of each trophic level. Because of the laws of thermodynamics, energy pyramids are *always* bottom-heavy.

```text
            [Tertiary Consumers]  10 J
           [Secondary Consumers]  100 J
          [Primary Consumers   ]  1,000 J
         [Primary Producers    ]  10,000 J
        [Sunlight: 1,000,000 J ]

```

**2. Pyramid of Biomass:**
In a biomass pyramid, each tier represents the standing crop (the total dry mass of all organisms) in one trophic level. Most biomass pyramids are upright, narrowing sharply from primary producers to top-level carnivores.

However, some aquatic ecosystems have **inverted biomass pyramids**. In open-ocean ecosystems, phytoplankton (primary producers) grow, reproduce, and are consumed rapidly by zooplankton. They never develop a large standing crop, yet their high turnover rate supports a zooplankton biomass that is physically larger than their own at any given moment. Despite the inverted biomass, the *energy* pyramid for this aquatic system remains strictly upright, maintaining the fundamental laws of thermodynamics.

## 25.2 Biogeochemical Cycles: Water, Carbon, Nitrogen, and Phosphorus

While energy flows through ecosystems and is ultimately dissipated as heat, chemical elements are limited and must be continually recycled. Life on Earth depends on the recycling of essential chemical elements. Because nutrient circuits involve both biotic (living) and abiotic (nonliving) components of ecosystems, they are called **biogeochemical cycles**.

A general model of nutrient cycling tracks elements as they move between four distinct reservoirs, defined by two characteristics: whether the materials are organic or inorganic, and whether the materials are directly available for organisms to use.

### 1. The Water Cycle

Water is essential to all organisms, and its availability heavily influences the rates of primary production and decomposition in terrestrial ecosystems.

* **Biological Importance:** Water makes up the majority of the mass of living cells, acts as the universal solvent for biochemical reactions, and is the primary participant in osmoregulation.
* **Forms Available to Life:** Liquid water is the primary form used, though some organisms can harvest water vapor.
* **Major Reservoirs:** The oceans contain about 97% of the biosphere's water. Approximately 2% is bound in glaciers and polar ice caps, and the remaining 1% is in lakes, rivers, and groundwater, with a negligible amount in the atmosphere.
* **Key Processes:** The main processes driving the water cycle are evaporation of liquid water by solar energy, condensation of water vapor into clouds, and precipitation. **Transpiration** by terrestrial plants also moves large volumes of water into the atmosphere. Surface and groundwater flow returns water to the oceans, completing the cycle.

### 2. The Carbon Cycle

Carbon forms the framework of the organic molecules essential to all organisms.

* **Biological Importance:** The ability of carbon to form four covalent bonds allows for the immense diversity of biological macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids) discussed in Chapter 3.
* **Forms Available to Life:** Photosynthetic organisms utilize $CO_2$ during the Calvin cycle to convert inorganic carbon into organic forms that are used by consumers.
* **Major Reservoirs:** Fossil fuels, soils, the sediments of aquatic ecosystems, the oceans (as dissolved carbon compounds), plant and animal biomass, and the atmosphere. The largest reservoir is sedimentary rocks such as limestone, though this carbon turns over very slowly.
* **Key Processes:** Photosynthesis by plants and phytoplankton removes substantial amounts of atmospheric $CO_2$ each year. This quantity is approximately equaled by $CO_2$ added to the atmosphere through cellular respiration by producers and consumers. Over geologic time, volcanoes are a substantial source of $CO_2$. In modern times, the burning of fossil fuels and wood is adding significant amounts of additional $CO_2$ to the atmosphere.

### 3. The Nitrogen Cycle

Nitrogen is a crucial building block for life, but its cycling is complex because it relies heavily on diverse groups of bacteria to transform it into biologically useful forms.

* **Biological Importance:** Nitrogen is a component of amino acids, proteins, and nucleic acids. It is often a limiting plant nutrient in both terrestrial and marine ecosystems.
* **Forms Available to Life:** Plants can assimilate two inorganic forms of nitrogen: ammonium ($NH_4^+$) and nitrate ($NO_3^-$), as well as some organic forms like amino acids. Various bacteria can use all of these, plus nitrite ($NO_2^-$). Animals can only utilize organic forms of nitrogen obtained through their diet.
* **Major Reservoirs:** The main reservoir of nitrogen is the atmosphere, which is 78% free nitrogen gas ($N_2$). The other reservoirs are soils and the sediments of lakes, rivers, and oceans; surface water and groundwater; and the biomass of living organisms.
* **Key Processes:**
* **Nitrogen Fixation:** The conversion of $N_2$ to forms that can be used to synthesize organic nitrogen compounds. Certain bacteria, lightning, and volcanic activity fix nitrogen naturally.
* **Nitrification:** The oxidation of ammonia or ammonium to nitrite ($NO_2^-$) followed by the oxidation of the nitrite to nitrate ($NO_3^-$), carried out by nitrifying bacteria.
* **Assimilation:** The uptake and incorporation of nitrogen compounds into plant or animal biomass.
* **Ammonification:** The decomposition of organic nitrogen back into ammonium ($NH_4^+$) by decomposers.
* **Denitrification:** The reduction of nitrate ($NO_3^-$) to nitrogen gas ($N_2$) by denitrifying bacteria, returning nitrogen to the atmosphere.

```text
[Atmospheric N2] ──────────────────────────────────────────┐
       │                                                   │
       │ (Nitrogen Fixation by bacteria/roots)             │
       ▼                                                   │
[Ammonium (NH4+)] ◄─────── (Ammonification) ─────── [Organic N in Plants/Animals]
       │                                                   ▲

                                               ▼

```

### 4. The Phosphorus Cycle

Unlike carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen, phosphorus does not have a significant atmospheric component. It is a sedimentary cycle.

* **Biological Importance:** Phosphorus is a major constituent of nucleic acids, phospholipids, and ATP and other energy-storing molecules. As a mineral constituent of bones and teeth, it is also highly important to vertebrates.
* **Forms Available to Life:** The most biologically important inorganic form of phosphorus is phosphate ($PO_4^{3-}$), which plants absorb and use in the synthesis of organic compounds.
* **Major Reservoirs:** The largest accumulations of phosphorus are in sedimentary rocks of marine origin. There are also large quantities in soil, in the oceans (in dissolved form), and in organisms.
* **Key Processes:** Weathering of rocks gradually adds phosphate to soil; some leaches into groundwater and surface water and may eventually reach the sea. Phosphate taken up by producers and incorporated into biological molecules may be eaten by consumers. Phosphorus is returned to soil or water by either decomposition of biomass or excretion by consumers. Because there are no significant phosphorus-containing gases, only small amounts of phosphorus move through the atmosphere, usually in the form of dust and sea spray.

## 25.3 Primary and Secondary Production in Terrestrial and Aquatic Biomes

In most ecosystems, the amount of light energy converted to chemical energy—in the form of organic compounds—by autotrophs during a given time period is the system's **primary production**. This foundational process dictates the total energy budget for the entire ecosystem, setting the upper limit for the biomass of all higher trophic levels.

### Gross and Net Primary Production

To accurately track energy pathways, ecologists distinguish between total energy assimilated and the energy actually available to consumers.

Total primary production in an ecosystem is known as **Gross Primary Production (GPP)**. It is the total amount of light energy that is converted into chemical energy by photosynthesis per unit time.

However, not all of this energy is stored as organic material. Plants and other autotrophs must use some of these molecules as fuel for their own cellular respiration. **Net Primary Production (NPP)** is equal to gross primary production minus the energy used by the primary producers for their autotrophic respiration ($R_a$):

$$NPP = GPP - R_a$$

NPP represents the storage of chemical energy that will be available to consumers in the ecosystem. On average, NPP is about one-half of GPP.

While NPP focuses solely on autotrophs, ecologists also measure **Net Ecosystem Production (NEP)**, a measure of the total biomass accumulation during a given period. NEP is gross primary production minus the total respiration of *all* organisms in the system ($R_T$), including primary producers, consumers, and decomposers:

$$NEP = GPP - R_T$$

NEP is a vital metric because it determines whether an ecosystem is gaining or losing carbon over time. If NEP is greater than zero, the ecosystem is a carbon sink; if it is less than zero, it is a carbon source.

### Primary Production in Aquatic Biomes

In marine and freshwater ecosystems, both light and nutrients control primary production.

**1. Light Limitation:**
Solar radiation drives photosynthesis, but the depth of light penetration profoundly affects primary production in the photic zone of an ocean or lake. About half of the solar radiation is absorbed in the first 15 meters of clear water. Even in "clear" water, only 5% to 10% of the radiation may reach a depth of 75 meters.

**2. Nutrient Limitation:**
More than light, nutrients limit primary production across most of the world's oceans and lakes. A **limiting nutrient** is the element that must be added for production to increase. In aquatic ecosystems, nitrogen and phosphorus are the most common limiting nutrients.

The nutrient density of the open ocean is typically very low because phytoplankton constantly consume nutrients in the photic zone, and when they die, they sink, carrying nutrients to the dark benthic zone. However, in regions where **upwelling** occurs—deep, nutrient-rich waters circulating to the ocean surface—primary production is exceptionally high.

In freshwater lakes, the addition of limiting nutrients (often phosphorus from agricultural runoff or sewage) can cause explosive increases in the biomass of primary producers, a process called **eutrophication**. When these algal blooms die, detritivores consume all the available oxygen during decomposition, frequently leading to the suffocation of fishes and other aerobic organisms.

### Primary Production in Terrestrial Biomes

At regional and global scales, climate factors—specifically temperature and moisture—are the primary drivers of primary production in terrestrial ecosystems.

Contrast the highly productive tropical rain forests, which are warm and wet year-round, with the low-productivity ecosystems of deserts (hot and dry) or arctic tundras (cold and dry). A useful predictor of terrestrial NPP is **actual evapotranspiration**, the total amount of water transpired by plants and evaporated from a landscape annually. Evapotranspiration increases with the temperature and amount of solar energy available to drive evaporation and transpiration.

On a more local scale, mineral nutrients in the soil can limit terrestrial primary production. As in aquatic systems, nitrogen and phosphorus are the most common limiting nutrients. Phosphorus limitations are particularly common in older soils where phosphate molecules have been leached away by water over thousands of years, such as in many tropical ecosystems. To overcome these limitations, plants have evolved various adaptations, including mutualisms with nitrogen-fixing bacteria and mycorrhizal fungi.

By examining the average NPP and the total surface area covered by different biomes, we can understand their relative contributions to the Earth's total primary production.

### Secondary Production at the Biome Level

As established in Section 25.1, secondary production is the amount of chemical energy in consumers' food that is converted to their own new biomass.

The relationship between primary and secondary production varies drastically between terrestrial and aquatic biomes. In terrestrial ecosystems, herbivory typically consumes a relatively small fraction of NPP (often around 10%), as much of the plant biomass consists of indigestible structural compounds like lignin and cellulose. Consequently, the majority of terrestrial energy flow is routed through the detrital food web.

Conversely, in many aquatic biomes, a much larger fraction of NPP is consumed by herbivores (such as zooplankton). Because aquatic primary producers like phytoplankton lack the extensive structural support tissues of terrestrial plants, they are highly digestible. This leads to higher rates of energy transfer to secondary consumers, supporting the rapid nutrient turnover and inverted biomass pyramids occasionally observed in these ecosystems.

## 25.4 Major Threats to Global Biodiversity

Extinction is a natural phenomenon that has occurred throughout Earth's history. However, the current rate of extinction is estimated to be 100 to 1,000 times higher than the typical background rate seen in the fossil record. This rapid decline in global biodiversity is driven almost entirely by human activities. Ecologists generally categorize the primary threats to biodiversity into four major types: habitat loss, introduced species, overharvesting, and global change.

### 1. Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

Human alteration of habitat is the single greatest threat to biodiversity throughout the biosphere. It is brought about by agriculture, urban development, forestry, mining, and pollution. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) implicates the destruction of physical habitat in the decline of more than 70% of the species that have become extinct, endangered, vulnerable, or rare in the last few hundred years.

When no alternative habitat is available or a species is unable to move, habitat loss invariably leads to population decline or extinction. Even when a habitat is not completely destroyed, it is often broken into smaller pieces—a process called **habitat fragmentation**.

Fragmentation leads to a phenomenon known as the **edge effect**. The boundaries (or edges) of a habitat experience different abiotic conditions—such as more sunlight, less moisture, greater temperature fluctuations, and higher exposure to predators—than the interior. When a habitat is fragmented, the proportion of edge habitat increases drastically relative to the interior habitat, harming species that require deep, undisturbed ecosystems to survive.

### 2. Introduced Species

**Introduced species** (also called non-native, exotic, or invasive species) are those that humans move intentionally or accidentally from the species' native locations to new geographic regions.

In their native habitats, populations of these species are held in check by natural predators, parasites, and pathogens. Free from these limitations in their new environments, transplanted species often spread rapidly. They can completely disrupt their adopted community by outcompeting native organisms for resources or preying upon them.

* **Accidental Introduction:** The brown tree snake was accidentally introduced to the island of Guam via military cargo after World War II. Because the native birds and lizards had not evolved with snake predators, they lacked defensive behaviors. The snake caused the extinction of several native species on the island.
* **Intentional Introduction:** Kudzu, a fast-growing vine native to Japan, was introduced to the southeastern United States to help control soil erosion. It has since taken over vast tracts of landscape, smothering native plants and trees under a dense, unbroken canopy.

### 3. Overharvesting

**Overharvesting** (or overexploitation) refers to the human harvesting of wild plants or animals at rates exceeding the ability of populations of those species to rebound naturally.

Species with restricted habitats, such as those living on small islands, are highly vulnerable to overharvesting. Also exceptionally vulnerable are large organisms with low reproductive rates, such as elephants, rhinoceroses, and whales.

The decline of commercially important marine fishes provides a stark example of overharvesting in modern times. For instance, the western Atlantic cod fishery collapsed in the 1990s due to decades of unsustainable harvesting facilitated by highly efficient modern fishing technology. Because access to the open ocean is notoriously difficult to regulate, common resources are subject to an economic pressure known as the "tragedy of the commons," where individuals overexploit a shared resource for short-term gain, leading to its long-term depletion.

### 4. Global Change

The fourth major threat to biodiversity is **global change**, which alters the fabric of Earth's ecosystems at regional and global scales. This category includes alterations in climate, atmospheric chemistry, and broad ecological systems that reduce the capacity of Earth to sustain life.

* **Pollution and Acid Precipitation:** A prominent historical example of global change is acid precipitation (rain, snow, or fog with a pH less than 5.2). The burning of wood and fossil fuels releases sulfur oxides and nitrogen oxides, which react with water in the atmosphere to produce sulfuric and nitric acids. This dramatically lowered the pH of lakes and streams, severely impacting aquatic life.
* **Climate Change:** Today, the most pressing facet of global change is anthropogenic (human-caused) climate change. The rapid warming of the planet, driven by the accumulation of greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide and methane, is shifting habitats and altering weather patterns faster than many species can adapt. Polar bears, which rely on sea ice to hunt, and coral reefs, which suffer from massive bleaching events caused by warming and acidifying oceans, are highly visible victims of these sweeping ecological changes.

## 25.5 Conservation Strategies, Restoration Ecology, and Sustainable Development

Conservation biology is a multidisciplinary science that integrates ecology, physiology, molecular biology, genetics, and evolutionary biology to conserve biological diversity at all levels. Given the severe global threats discussed in Section 25.4, ecologists apply various diagnostic and therapeutic strategies to protect declining populations, preserve landscapes, and restore degraded ecosystems.

### Population and Species Conservation

Biologists who focus on conservation at the population and species levels generally utilize two main approaches, depending on the specific circumstances of the endangered group.

**1. The Small-Population Approach**
This approach studies the processes that can cause very small populations to finally become extinct. When a population drops below a certain size, it becomes highly vulnerable to inbreeding and genetic drift. These forces can draw the population down an **extinction vortex**, a downward spiral of population decline unique to small populations.

**Plain Text Diagram: The Extinction Vortex**

```text
          [Small Population]
                  │
                  ▼
   [Inbreeding and Genetic Drift]
                  │
                  ▼
   [Loss of Genetic Variability]
                  │
                  ▼
[Lower Reproduction & Higher Mortality]
                  │
                  ▼
         [Smaller Population] ──────► (Cycle repeats toward Extinction)

```

To prevent this, ecologists determine the **Minimum Viable Population (MVP)**, the minimal population size at which a species is able to sustain its numbers and survive. However, total population size is often a misleading metric, as not all individuals successfully breed. To account for this, ecologists calculate the **effective population size ($N_e$)**, which represents the breeding potential of the population:

$$N_e = \frac{4 N_f N_m}{N_f + N_m}$$

*(Where $N_f$ and $N_m$ are the number of females and the number of males that successfully breed).*

**2. The Declining-Population Approach**
Rather than focusing solely on absolute numbers, this approach focuses on threatened and endangered populations that show a downward trend, even if the population is currently far above its MVP. The declining-population approach emphasizes environmental factors that caused a population decline in the first place and requires step-by-step ecological evaluation to reverse those specific causes.

### Landscape and Regional Conservation

While saving individual species is crucial, modern conservation biology increasingly focuses on sustaining the biodiversity of entire communities, ecosystems, and landscapes.

* **Biodiversity Hotspots:** A biodiversity hotspot is a relatively small area with an exceptional concentration of endemic species (species found nowhere else in the world) and a large number of endangered and threatened species. While these areas represent a tiny fraction of Earth's surface, preserving them yields the highest conservation return on investment.
* **Movement Corridors:** Habitat fragmentation isolates populations. A movement corridor is a narrow strip or series of small clumps of habitat connecting otherwise isolated patches. Corridors are essential for promoting dispersal, reducing inbreeding, and allowing species to migrate in response to climate change.
* **Zoned Reserves:** The zoned reserve model recognizes that conservation efforts often involve working in landscapes that are largely human-dominated. A zoned reserve includes relatively undisturbed areas surrounded by habitat that has been modified for human economic use (buffer zones). The challenge is to regulate human activities in the buffer zones so they do not devastate the protected core.

### Restoration Ecology

While conservation biology seeks to preserve existing ecosystems, **restoration ecology** seeks to initiate or speed up the recovery of degraded ecosystems. The foundational assumption of restoration ecology is that most environmental damage is at least partially reversible. Two key strategies are employed in ecosystem restoration:

**1. Bioremediation**
Bioremediation is the use of organisms—usually prokaryotes, fungi, or plants—to detoxify polluted ecosystems. Some plants are adapted to soils containing heavy metals and can accumulate high concentrations of toxic elements like zinc, nickel, or lead in their tissues. Restoration ecologists can plant these species in polluted mining sites and then harvest the plants to remove the metals from the ecosystem.

**2. Biological Augmentation**
In contrast to bioremediation, which *removes* harmful substances, biological augmentation uses organisms to *add* essential materials to a degraded ecosystem. For example, planting nitrogen-fixing plants like lupines can rapidly increase the nitrogen concentration in soils damaged by mining or intensive agriculture, paving the way for other plant species to return.

### Sustainable Development

The most ambitious goal of ecological science is to help steer humanity toward **sustainable development**—economic development that meets the needs of people today without limiting the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

Achieving sustainable development is not merely a scientific challenge; it requires connecting life sciences with social sciences, economics, and humanities. It requires reassessing our ecological footprint, transitioning to renewable energy, implementing sustainable agriculture and fisheries, and reducing resource consumption. By understanding the intricate energy flows, nutrient cycles, and interdependent relationships that characterize Earth's ecosystems, humanity can make informed decisions to preserve the biosphere upon which all life depends.

## Chapter Summary

* **Ecosystem Dynamics:** Ecosystems involve two fundamental processes: energy flow and chemical cycling. Energy enters as light, flows through trophic levels via chemical energy in organic compounds, and exits as heat.
* **Trophic Efficiency:** Only a fraction of energy (typically about 10%) is transferred from one trophic level to the next, severely limiting the number of top predators an ecosystem can support and creating upright energy pyramids.
* **Biogeochemical Cycles:** Chemical elements are limited and must be recycled between biotic and abiotic reservoirs. The water, carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus cycles are driven by a combination of biological, geological, and chemical processes.
* **Production:** Gross Primary Production (GPP) is the total light energy converted to chemical energy. Net Primary Production (NPP) is the energy available to consumers. Production is often limited by light and nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus) in aquatic biomes, and by temperature and moisture in terrestrial biomes.
* **Biodiversity Threats:** Human activities are causing an extinction crisis primarily through habitat loss and fragmentation, the introduction of invasive species, overharvesting, and global climate change.
* **Conservation and Restoration:** Ecologists work to preserve biodiversity through targeted population approaches and broader landscape strategies like identifying hotspots and establishing zoned reserves. Restoration ecology employs bioremediation and biological augmentation to repair degraded environments, all contributing to the ultimate goal of global sustainable development.
