Every living organism depends on the remarkable ability of cells to reproduce. In this chapter, we explore the precise choreography that makes life's continuity possible. We begin by examining how the massive eukaryotic genome is efficiently packaged into manageable chromosomes. Next, we trace the stages of the cell cycle, detailing how a cell duplicates its DNA and physically divides through mitosis and cytokinesis. Finally, we uncover the sophisticated molecular control systems that regulate this biological clock and explore the devastating consequences—such as cancer—that arise when these critical cellular safeguards fail.

## 9.1 Chromosome Structure and Genomic Organization

A defining characteristic of life is the ability of organisms to reproduce their kind. In every cell division, whether an amoeba dividing to form two new individuals or a human embryo developing from a single fertilized egg, a cell must reliably duplicate and distribute its genetic material. Before exploring the mechanics of cell division, it is essential to understand how this genetic information is organized, structured, and packaged within the cell.

### The Genome: The Cell's Genetic Endowment

A cell’s complete endowment of DNA is called its **genome**. The organization of the genome differs significantly between the two broad categories of cells:

* **Prokaryotic Genomes:** In organisms such as bacteria and archaea, the genome is typically composed of a single, circular DNA molecule residing in the nucleoid region. While these genomes are highly folded to fit inside the cell, they are relatively simple in their organization.
* **Eukaryotic Genomes:** In plants, animals, and fungi, the genome consists of multiple distinct, linear DNA molecules localized within the nucleus. The sheer volume of DNA in eukaryotes is staggering; a typical human cell contains roughly 2 meters of DNA.

To manage this massive amount of information and ensure accurate distribution during cell division, eukaryotic DNA is partitioned into manageable units called **chromosomes** (from the Greek *chroma*, color, and *soma*, body, named for their ability to take up certain dyes used in microscopy).

### Chromatin and the Challenge of Packaging

If you were to stretch out the DNA from a single human cell, it would be over 250,000 times longer than the diameter of the nucleus that houses it. To solve this spatial problem, eukaryotic chromosomes do not exist as naked DNA. Instead, they consist of **chromatin**, a complex of DNA and associated proteins.

These proteins, primarily **histones**, are responsible for the first level of DNA packing in chromatin. Histones are small proteins with a high proportion of positively charged amino acids (like lysine and arginine), which bind tightly to the negatively charged sugar-phosphate backbone of DNA.

### The Hierarchy of Chromosomal Compaction

The folding of chromatin is not random; it is a highly structured, multi-level hierarchy that compacts the DNA enough to fit into the nucleus while still allowing the cellular machinery access to genes when needed.

**1. Nucleosomes ("Beads on a String")**
The most basic unit of DNA packing is the **nucleosome**. In electron micrographs, unfolded chromatin resembles beads on a string. Each "bead" is a nucleosome, consisting of DNA wound twice around a core of eight histone proteins (two each of histones H2A, H2B, H3, and H4). The "string" connecting the beads is called linker DNA. The amino end (N-terminus) of each histone extends outward from the nucleosome and plays a critical role in regulating gene expression.

**2. The 30-nm Fiber**
The next level of packing involves interactions between the histone tails of one nucleosome, the linker DNA, and the nucleosomes adjacent to it. A fifth type of histone, called H1, binds to the DNA near the nucleosome and causes the 10-nm fiber to coil or fold into a thicker, roughly 30-nm thick fiber.

**3. Looped Domains (300-nm Fiber)**
The 30-nm fiber forms loops called looped domains attached to a chromosome scaffold made of non-histone proteins. This scaffold is rich in one particular type of enzyme—topoisomerase—which helps manage the physical stress of coiled DNA.

**4. The Metaphase Chromosome**
In a dividing cell, the looped domains themselves coil and fold, further compacting the chromatin to produce the characteristic metaphase chromosome seen in karyotypes. At this stage, the width of a single sister chromatid is approximately 700 nm.

### Chromosome Anatomy Prior to Division

When a cell is not dividing, and even as it replicates its DNA in preparation for division, each chromosome is in the form of a long, thin chromatin fiber. However, after DNA replication and just before the cell physically divides, the chromatin condenses intensely.

Following replication, each chromosome consists of two identical **sister chromatids**. These chromatids are exact copies of the original DNA molecule, joined all along their lengths by protein complexes called **cohesins** (a connection known as sister chromatid cohesion).

Each sister chromatid has a **centromere**, a specialized region where the chromatid is attached most closely to its sister chromatid. The sequence of DNA at the centromere is highly repetitive. The centromere divides the chromosome into two "arms": the shorter arm is designated the *p* arm (for *petit*), and the longer arm is designated the *q* arm. The physical ends of the linear chromosomes are protected by specialized, repetitive DNA sequences called **telomeres**, which prevent genetic degradation.

### Euchromatin vs. Heterochromatin

Chromatin structure is highly dynamic. During interphase (the period when the cell is not dividing), the chromatin is generally uncoiled enough to allow enzymes to transcribe the DNA. This less compacted, transcriptionally active chromatin is called **euchromatin** ("true chromatin").

However, even during interphase, certain regions of the genome remain highly condensed. This tightly packed DNA is called **heterochromatin**. Centromeres and telomeres exist largely as heterochromatin, as do regions of the chromosome where genes are entirely inactive. Because of its dense compaction, heterochromatin is largely inaccessible to the cellular machinery responsible for transcribing genetic information. The cell can dynamically shift regions of DNA between euchromatin and heterochromatin to turn genes on and off, a fundamental mechanism of cellular regulation.

## 9.2 The Phases of the Eukaryotic Cell Cycle

The life of a cell, from the moment it is first formed from a dividing parent cell until its own division into two daughter cells, is known as the **cell cycle**. While the structural organization of the genome ensures that genetic information is packaged efficiently, the cell cycle provides the precise chronological framework required to duplicate that information and distribute it flawlessly.

In eukaryotic cells, this cycle is broadly divided into two main phases: **Interphase**, where the cell grows and duplicates its DNA, and the **Mitotic (M) phase**, where the cell physically divides.

### Interphase: Preparation and Synthesis

Interphase is often misunderstood as a "resting" phase because the cell is not actively dividing. In reality, it is a period of intense metabolic activity and growth. For a typical dividing mammalian cell, interphase accounts for about 90% of the total cell cycle duration. Interphase is subdivided into three distinct sub-phases: $G_1$, $S$, and $G_2$.

#### The $G_1$ Phase (First Gap)

During the $G_1$ phase, the cell grows physically larger, copies its organelles, and synthesizes the molecular building blocks it will need in later steps. The term "gap" is a historical misnomer; early microscopists saw nothing happening between cell division and DNA replication, but biochemically, this is a highly active period of protein synthesis and cellular accumulation. A cell in $G_1$ performs its standard functional role within the tissue (e.g., a stomach cell secreting digestive enzymes).

#### The $S$ Phase (Synthesis)

The $S$ phase is strictly dedicated to the replication of the genome. During this time, the cell synthesizes a complete copy of the DNA in its nucleus.

Crucially, while the sheer amount of DNA doubles, the *number* of chromosomes remains the same. Each chromosome, which entered $S$ phase as a single, linear DNA molecule, exits $S$ phase consisting of two identical sister chromatids physically bound together. Additionally, an important microtubule-organizing structure called the **centrosome** is duplicated during the $S$ phase. These two centrosomes will be essential during the M phase to separate the DNA.

#### The $G_2$ Phase (Second Gap)

During the $G_2$ phase, the cell grows further, synthesizes additional proteins and organelles, and begins to reorganize its contents in preparation for mitosis. $G_2$ serves as a critical checkpoint period where the cell inspects its newly replicated DNA for errors. Once $G_2$ is complete, interphase ends, and the cell is ready to enter the dividing phase.

### The Mitotic (M) Phase: Division

The Mitotic phase, or M phase, is the shortest part of the cell cycle. During this phase, the cell reorganizes its duplicated components and divides them equally to create two identical daughter cells. The M phase consists of two overlapping processes:

1. **Mitosis:** The division of the genetic material in the nucleus.
2. **Cytokinesis:** The division of the cytoplasm, organelles, and cell membrane into two distinct cells.

*(Note: The specific mechanics and stages of Mitosis—Prophase, Prometaphase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase—will be detailed in Section 9.3).*

### The $G_0$ Phase: Exiting the Cycle

Not all cells continuously divide. Cells can exit the cell cycle and enter a non-dividing state called the **$G_0$ phase**.

Some cells, like highly specialized nerve cells and mature muscle cells in humans, remain in $G_0$ permanently and never divide again. Other cells, such as liver cells, enter $G_0$ temporarily but can be "called back" into the cell cycle by specific external signals, such as growth factors released in response to an injury.

### Tracking DNA Content and Chromosome Number

To truly understand the cell cycle, it is helpful to track both the ploidy (chromosome number, denoted by $n$) and the total DNA mass (denoted by $C$). For a typical somatic, diploid eukaryotic cell, the baseline state is $2n$ (two sets of chromosomes) and $2C$ (the baseline amount of DNA).

The following changes occur throughout the cycle:

* **$G_1$ Phase:** The cell is diploid ($2n$) and has a baseline DNA content of $2C$.
* **$S$ Phase:** Chromosome number remains constant ($2n$), but as DNA replicates, the DNA content continuously increases: $2C \rightarrow 4C$.
* **$G_2$ Phase:** The cell remains $2n$, but the DNA content is now firmly $4C$ because every chromosome consists of two sister chromatids.
* **M Phase (Mitosis):** Sister chromatids separate. During anaphase, the transient chromosome number technically doubles to $4n$ (since chromatids are now individual chromosomes), but the cell still contains $4C$ DNA.
* **M Phase (Cytokinesis):** The cell splits. Each new daughter cell returns to the baseline state of $2n$ and $2C$.

## 9.3 Mitosis and Cytokinesis

The physical division of a eukaryotic cell occurs during the M phase of the cell cycle. This phase is conventionally divided into two distinct, but overlapping processes: **mitosis** (the division of the nucleus) and **cytokinesis** (the division of the cytoplasm). Mitosis is a continuous, dynamic process, but for the sake of study, biologists have divided it into five observable stages: prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase.

### The Mitotic Spindle

The events of mitosis depend heavily on the **mitotic spindle**, a macromolecular machine made of microtubules and associated proteins. While the cell is in interphase, the cell's cytoskeleton partially disassembles to provide the building blocks (tubulin subunits) for the spindle.

Spindle assembly begins at the **centrosome**, a subcellular region that functions as the microtubule-organizing center. During interphase, the single centrosome replicates. As mitosis begins, the two centrosomes move apart, and microtubules grow out from them, forming a star-like radial array called an **aster** around each centrosome. The spindle eventually includes the centrosomes, the spindle microtubules, and the asters.

### The Five Stages of Mitosis

**1. Prophase**
During prophase, the loosely coiled chromatin fibers condense tightly into discrete, observable chromosomes. Because DNA replication has already occurred, each chromosome appears as two identical sister chromatids joined together at their centromeres and along their arms by cohesin proteins. The nucleoli (sites of ribosome synthesis) disappear. In the cytoplasm, the mitotic spindle begins to form, pushing the two centrosomes away from each other toward opposite ends (poles) of the cell.

**2. Prometaphase**
The nuclear envelope fragments and breaks apart, allowing the spindle microtubules to invade the nuclear area. Each sister chromatid develops a **kinetochore**, a specialized protein structure situated at the centromere. Some of the spindle microtubules—called kinetochore microtubules—attach to these kinetochores. The chromosomes are jerked back and forth as microtubules from opposite poles engage in a molecular "tug-of-war." Meanwhile, non-kinetochore microtubules overlap with those from the opposite pole, contributing to cell elongation.

**3. Metaphase**
Metaphase is typically the longest stage of mitosis. The centrosomes are now securely positioned at opposite poles of the cell. The tug-of-war ends in a dynamic equilibrium, and the chromosomes convene at the **metaphase plate**, an imaginary plane equidistant between the two spindle poles. For each chromosome, the kinetochores of the sister chromatids are attached to kinetochore microtubules coming from opposite poles.

**4. Anaphase**
Anaphase is the shortest stage of mitosis, often lasting only a few minutes. It begins suddenly when the cohesin proteins holding the sister chromatids together are cleaved by an enzyme called **separase**. The chromatids instantly become fully independent chromosomes. The kinetochore microtubules shorten by depolymerizing at their kinetochore ends, pulling the daughter chromosomes centromere-first toward opposite poles. Simultaneously, the non-kinetochore microtubules push against each other, elongating the cell. By the end of anaphase, the two ends of the cell have equivalent—and complete—collections of chromosomes.

**5. Telophase**
Two daughter nuclei begin to form in the cell. Nuclear envelopes are constructed from the fragments of the parent cell's nuclear envelope and other portions of the endomembrane system. Nucleoli reappear, and the chromosomes begin to decondense back into their looser chromatin state. Any remaining spindle microtubules are depolymerized. Mitosis—the equal division of one nucleus into two genetically identical nuclei—is complete.

### Cytokinesis: Dividing the Cytoplasm

While mitosis ensures the genetic material is equally divided, cytokinesis is responsible for dividing the rest of the cell contents. This process usually begins during late anaphase or telophase, so the two daughter cells appear shortly after the end of mitosis. The mechanism of cytokinesis differs fundamentally between animal and plant cells due to the presence of the rigid plant cell wall.

#### Cytokinesis in Animal Cells (Cleavage)

In animal cells, cytokinesis occurs by a process known as **cleavage**. The first sign of cleavage is the appearance of a **cleavage furrow**, a shallow groove in the cell surface near the old metaphase plate. On the cytoplasmic side of the furrow, a contractile ring of actin microfilaments forms, interacting with myosin proteins. This ring contracts, acting much like a drawstring on a bag, pinching the cell in two until the parent cell is pinched entirely in half, producing two completely separate cells.

#### Cytokinesis in Plant Cells (Cell Plate Formation)

Plant cells have stiff cell walls and cannot be simply pinched in half. Instead, during telophase, vesicles derived from the Golgi apparatus move along microtubules to the middle of the cell. There, the vesicles coalesce to produce a **cell plate**. Cell wall materials carried in the vesicles collect inside the cell plate as it grows. The cell plate enlarges outward until its surrounding membrane fuses with the plasma membrane along the perimeter of the cell. The contents of the cell plate form a new cell wall between the daughter cells.


At the conclusion of mitosis and cytokinesis, the single original parent cell has successfully yielded two distinct daughter cells. Assuming no mutations occurred during the $S$ phase, these daughter cells possess identical genomes, ready to enter the $G_1$ phase of their own cellular life cycle.

## 9.4 Molecular Regulation of the Cell Cycle

The timing and rate of cell division in different parts of a plant or animal are crucial to normal growth, development, and maintenance. Skin cells divide frequently throughout life, whereas liver cells maintain the ability to divide but keep it in reserve until a need arises, such as repairing a wound. Some of the most specialized cells, such as fully formed nerve and muscle cells, do not divide at all in a mature human. These differences in cell cycle rhythm are driven by a sophisticated molecular control system operating at the biochemical level.

### The Cell Cycle Control System and Checkpoints

The sequential events of the cell cycle are directed by a distinct **cell cycle control system**, a cyclically operating set of molecules in the cell that both triggers and coordinates key events. This system is analogous to the control device of an automatic washing machine; it proceeds on its own, driven by a built-in clock, but is also subject to internal and external adjustments.

Crucial to this regulation are **checkpoints**—specific control points where stop and go-ahead signals can regulate the cycle. Animal cells generally have built-in stop signals that halt the cell cycle at checkpoints until overridden by a go-ahead signal.

* **The $G_1$ Checkpoint:** For many cells, the $G_1$ checkpoint—dubbed the "restriction point" in mammalian cells—is the most important. If a cell receives a go-ahead signal at the $G_1$ checkpoint, it will usually complete the $S$, $G_2$, and M phases and divide. If it does not receive a go-ahead signal, it exits the cycle, switching into the nondividing state called the $G_0$ phase.
* **The $G_2$ Checkpoint:** This checkpoint prevents cells from entering mitosis when DNA is damaged or replication is incomplete, providing an opportunity for repair.
* **The M Checkpoint:** Operating during metaphase, this internal checkpoint ensures that anaphase does not begin until all chromosomes are properly attached to the spindle fibers. Only when all kinetochores are anchored to kinetochore microtubules does the appropriate regulatory protein complex become activated, triggering the cleavage of cohesins (via the enzyme separase) and allowing sister chromatids to separate.

### The Molecular Clock: Cyclins and Cyclin-Dependent Kinases

At the heart of the cell cycle control system are rhythmic fluctuations in the abundance and activity of specific protein kinases. **Kinases** are enzymes that activate or inactivate other proteins by phosphorylating them (transferring a phosphate group from ATP to the target protein).

While many of the kinases that drive the cell cycle are present at a constant concentration in the growing cell, they exist in an inactive form. To be active, such a kinase must be attached to a specific regulatory protein called a **cyclin**, named because its concentration cyclically fluctuates in the cell. Because of this requirement, these kinases are known as **cyclin-dependent kinases**, or **Cdks**. The activity of a Cdk rises and falls with changes in the concentration of its cyclin partner.

#### MPF: The Maturation-Promoting Factor

The first cyclin-Cdk complex discovered was **MPF** (maturation-promoting factor, or M-phase-promoting factor). MPF triggers the cell's passage past the $G_2$ checkpoint into M phase.

1. **Synthesis:** Cyclin synthesis begins in late S phase and continues through $G_2$. Because cyclin is protected from degradation during this stage, it accumulates.
2. **Binding:** Cyclin combines with Cdk, producing active MPF. When enough MPF molecules accumulate, the cell passes the $G_2$ checkpoint and begins mitosis.
3. **Action:** MPF promotes mitosis by phosphorylating various proteins. For example, it phosphorylates proteins of the nuclear lamina, promoting fragmentation of the nuclear envelope during prometaphase.
4. **Degradation:** During anaphase, the cyclin component of MPF is degraded, terminating M phase. The cell enters $G_1$, and the Cdk remains intact but inactive, awaiting the next wave of cyclin synthesis.

### External Signals: Growth Factors and Physical Constraints

While the intrinsic cyclin-Cdk clockwork dictates the rhythm of the cell cycle, external signals provide crucial "override" instructions, tying cell division to the needs of the whole organism.

* **Growth Factors:** Most mammalian cells divide in culture only if the growth medium includes specific growth factors—proteins released by certain cells that stimulate other cells to divide. For example, **platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)** is required for the division of cultured fibroblasts (a type of connective tissue cell). Fibroblasts have PDGF receptors on their plasma membranes. The binding of PDGF molecules to these receptors triggers a signal transduction pathway that allows the cells to pass the $G_1$ checkpoint and divide. In the body, PDGF is released by blood platelets in the vicinity of an injury, promoting massive fibroblast division to help heal the wound.
* **Density-Dependent Inhibition:** Cultured cells normally divide until they form a single layer on the inner surface of the culture container, at which point they stop dividing. This phenomenon, called **density-dependent inhibition**, occurs when cell-surface proteins on adjacent cells bind to each other, sending a growth-inhibiting signal to both cells, preventing them from moving forward in the cell cycle.
* **Anchorage Dependence:** To divide, most animal cells must be attached to a substratum, such as the extracellular matrix of a tissue or the bottom of a culture flask. This requirement is known as **anchorage dependence**. Anchorage is signaled to the cell cycle control system via pathways involving plasma membrane proteins and elements of the cytoskeleton linked to them.

Together, these internal checkpoints and external regulatory mechanisms ensure that a cell divides only when it is developmentally beneficial and physically safe to do so. When these regulatory mechanisms fail, the result is unregulated cell proliferation, leading to the devastating consequences explored in the next section.

## 9.5 Cancer: Out-of-Control Cell Division

The cell cycle control system is a marvel of evolutionary engineering, ensuring that tissues grow, repair, and maintain themselves with extraordinary precision. However, this system is not infallible. When the molecular brakes and accelerators of the cell cycle fail, the result is the unregulated, explosive cellular proliferation known as cancer.

Cancer cells do not heed the normal signals that regulate the cell cycle. They do not stop dividing when growth factors are depleted. If they do stop dividing, they do so at random points in the cycle, rather than at the standard checkpoints. Most crucially, cancer cells can go on dividing indefinitely in culture if given a continual supply of nutrients; in essence, they are "immortal." By contrast, nearly all normal, non-cancerous mammalian cells divide only 20 to 50 times before they stop, age, and die.

### The Genetic Basis of Cancer

Cancer is fundamentally a disease of the genome, though not usually an inherited one. It arises from an accumulation of genetic mutations in a single somatic cell. These mutations typically affect two broad categories of genes:

* **Proto-oncogenes:** These are normal genes that code for proteins promoting cell growth and division (the "accelerators"). When a proto-oncogene mutates, it can become an **oncogene**, a cancer-causing gene that is hyperactive or resistant to degradation, driving the cell cycle forward continuously.
* **Tumor Suppressor Genes:** These genes code for proteins that prevent uncontrolled cell growth (the "brakes"). Their products might repair damaged DNA, control the adhesion of cells to each other, or inhibit the cell cycle if DNA damage is too severe to fix. A mutation that permanently deactivates a tumor suppressor gene removes a crucial layer of cellular defense.

One of the most famous tumor suppressor genes is *p53*, often called the "guardian of the genome." When DNA is damaged (e.g., by UV radiation), the p53 protein halts the cell cycle at the $G_1$ checkpoint, allowing time for repair. If the damage is irreparable, p53 triggers apoptosis (programmed cell death). Mutations in the *p53* gene are found in more than 50% of all human cancers, illustrating how critical checkpoint enforcement is to preventing unregulated growth.

### Tumor Formation and Progression

The journey from a normal cell to a cancerous one is called **transformation**. Because the immune system usually recognizes transformed cells as abnormal, they are typically destroyed. However, if a transformed cell evades destruction, it may proliferate to form a **tumor**, a mass of abnormal cells within otherwise normal tissue.


If the abnormal cells remain localized at their original site because they lack the genetic or cellular changes necessary to survive elsewhere, the lump is called a **benign tumor**. Most benign tumors do not cause serious problems and can be removed via surgery.

In contrast, a **malignant tumor** includes cells whose genetic and cellular changes enable them to spread to new tissues and impair the functions of one or more organs. An individual with a malignant tumor is said to have cancer.

### Metastasis

Malignant tumors are defined by their ability to invade surrounding tissues. As a malignant tumor grows, its cells may secrete signaling molecules that cause blood vessels to grow toward the tumor (a process called angiogenesis), supplying it with oxygen and nutrients.

Cancer cells also lose their anchorage dependence and density-dependent inhibition. They detach from neighboring cells and the extracellular matrix, allowing them to enter the newly formed blood vessels or the lymphatic system. This spread of cancer cells to locations distant from their original site is called **metastasis**. Once lodged in a new tissue, these cells may proliferate and form secondary tumors.

### Cancer Treatment and the Cell Cycle

Understanding the cell cycle is crucial for developing cancer treatments. Because cancer cells are defined by their rapid and continuous division, many therapies are designed to target actively dividing cells:

* **Radiation Therapy:** Localized tumors are often treated with high-energy radiation. This damages DNA heavily. While normal cells can repair this damage, cancer cells—which have often lost their DNA repair mechanisms (like p53)—cannot, leading to cell death.
* **Chemotherapy:** For metastatic tumors, chemotherapy is used. Chemotherapeutic drugs are toxic to actively dividing cells and are administered through the circulatory system. For example, the drug Taxol prevents the depolymerization of microtubules, freezing the mitotic spindle in place and actively stopping cells from proceeding past metaphase.

The side effects of chemotherapy—such as nausea, hair loss, and immune suppression—occur because these drugs also affect normal cells that divide frequently, such as those lining the intestine, hair follicle cells, and blood-forming cells in the bone marrow. The ongoing quest in cancer research is to develop targeted therapies that distinguish between normal and cancerous cells, exploiting the specific genetic mutations of a patient's unique tumor.

* **9.2 The Phases of the Eukaryotic Cell Cycle:** The cell cycle is the life history of a dividing cell, consisting of Interphase ($G_1$, $S$, $G_2$) and the Mitotic (M) phase. The genome is replicated during the $S$ phase, while actual physical division occurs in the M phase. Cells that cease dividing exit the cycle into a resting $G_0$ phase.
* **9.3 Mitosis and Cytokinesis:** Mitosis is the division of the nucleus, progressing through prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase. It relies on the microtubule-based mitotic spindle to separate sister chromatids equally. Cytokinesis follows, dividing the cytoplasm via a cleavage furrow in animal cells or a cell plate in plant cells.
* **9.4 Molecular Regulation of the Cell Cycle:** A molecular control system dictates the timing of the cell cycle using checkpoints ($G_1$, $G_2$, M). Cyclin-dependent kinases (Cdks) and cyclins drive the biochemical clock, while external signals like growth factors, density-dependent inhibition, and anchorage dependence integrate cellular division with the organism's needs.
* **9.5 Cancer: Out-of-Control Cell Division:** Cancer results from the failure of cell cycle regulation due to mutations in proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes. Transformed cells bypass checkpoints and proliferate into tumors. Malignant tumors can invade neighboring tissues and metastasize throughout the body, necessitating treatments that specifically target the mechanisms of cell division.
