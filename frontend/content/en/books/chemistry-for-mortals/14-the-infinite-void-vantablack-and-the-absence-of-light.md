## 1. The Illusion of Black: A History of Pigments

To understand how extraordinary Vantablack is, we must first understand what "black" has meant for the majority of human history. When we look at a red apple, we are seeing the surface of the apple absorbing all wavelengths of visible light except for red, which it reflects back into our retinas. When we see something white, it is reflecting nearly all visible wavelengths.

Therefore, intuitively, we define black as the *absorption* of all visible light.

For millennia, humans have sought to create the perfect black pigment for art, clothing, and writing. Early humans used charcoal and soot—essentially impure forms of carbon. The ancient Greeks and Romans used "bone black," created by charring animal bones. During the Renaissance, artists used "lamp black," collected from the soot of oil lamps.

However, none of these materials were truly, perfectly black. Even the darkest black paints you can buy at a hardware store or an art supply shop are essentially just very dark gray. They absorb a lot of light, but they still reflect between $2\%$ and $5\%$ of the photons that hit them.

When millions of photons are bouncing off a surface, reflecting even $2\%$ is more than enough for the human eye—an incredibly sensitive optical instrument—to detect variations in the surface texture. You can see the brushstrokes on a black canvas; you can see the wrinkles in a black shirt.

Standard pigments work on a molecular level. The molecules in black paint have electronic structures that allow them to absorb the energy of incoming visible light photons. When a photon hits a pigment molecule, its energy is transferred to an electron, exciting it to a higher energy state. This energy is then quickly released as microscopic amounts of heat. But because these molecules are randomly arranged and packed into a binder (like oil or acrylic), the surface is relatively smooth and reflective on a microscopic scale. Light grazes the surface and bounces off before it even has a chance to interact with the pigment molecules.

To create a true black, chemists realized they had to stop thinking about *pigments* and start thinking about *structures*.

## 2. The Physics of Sight and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

To appreciate the trap that Vantablack sets, we must understand the prey it catches: light.

Light is an electromagnetic wave, and the "visible light" we see is just a tiny sliver of the broader electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from wavelengths of about $380 \text{ nm}$ (violet) to $700 \text{ nm}$ (red).

The energy of a single particle of light, a photon, is dictated by the Planck-Einstein relation:

$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Where:

* $E$ is the energy of the photon
* $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$)
* $c$ is the speed of light ($3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$)
* $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light

When light strikes a surface, three things can happen:

1. **Reflection:** The photon bounces off.
2. **Transmission:** The photon passes completely through (like through glass).
3. **Absorption:** The photon's energy is transferred to the material.

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. Therefore, the total energy of the incident light ($E_{incident}$) must equal the sum of the reflected, transmitted, and absorbed energy:

$$E_{incident} = E_{reflected} + E_{transmitted} + E_{absorbed}$$

For an opaque material like a painted wall or a piece of metal, transmission is zero. The goal of an ultra-black material is to drive $E_{reflected}$ as close to absolutely zero as physically possible, forcing $E_{incident}$ to entirely become $E_{absorbed}$.

When $E_{absorbed}$ absorbs the photon, that optical energy is converted into kinetic energy at the atomic level—which we experience as heat ($\Delta Q$). This is why wearing a black shirt on a sunny day makes you hotter than wearing a white shirt.

To drive reflection down to near zero, scientists needed a material that was not just chemically absorbent, but physically built to prevent bouncing. They found their answer in one of the most versatile elements in the universe.

## 3. The Carbon Atom: The Architect of the Void

Carbon is the foundation of all known life, largely due to its remarkable ability to form stable bonds with itself and other elements. Carbon has an atomic number of 6, meaning it has 4 valence electrons available for bonding.

Depending on how these carbon atoms arrange themselves, they create materials with wildly different properties—these different forms are called *allotropes*.

* When each carbon atom bonds to four others in a rigid, 3D tetrahedral lattice ($sp^3$ hybridization), you get a **diamond**, the hardest natural substance, which is highly transparent.
* When each carbon atom bonds to three others in flat, 2D hexagonal sheets ($sp^2$ hybridization), you get **graphite**, the soft, dark grey material in your pencil.

But what happens if you take a single, one-atom-thick layer of graphite—known as **graphene**—and roll it up into a seamless cylinder?

You get a **Carbon Nanotube (CNT)**.

```text
    A flat sheet of Graphene (Hexagonal Lattice)
    
      / \   / \   / \
    |   | |   | |   |
     \ /   \ /   \ /
      |     |     |
      
    Rolled into a Carbon Nanotube (CNT)
    
       _______ 
     /         \  <-- Diameter: ~1 to 50 nanometers
    |           |     Wall thickness: 1 atom
    |           |
    |           |
    |           |
     \ _______ /

```

Carbon nanotubes are miracles of modern chemistry. Because of the incredible strength of the carbon-carbon $sp^2$ double bonds, CNTs are conceptually the strongest and stiffest materials yet discovered in terms of tensile strength and elastic modulus. They conduct electricity better than copper and transmit heat better than diamond.

But for our purposes, their most important property is their size and their chemical makeup. A carbon nanotube has a diameter of just a few nanometers. To put that in perspective, a single strand of human hair is about 80,000 to 100,000 nanometers wide. A single carbon nanotube is tens of thousands of times thinner than a human hair.

Because they are made of carbon—the same element that makes soot and charcoal dark—they naturally have light-absorbing properties. But a single carbon nanotube lying on its side isn't enough to create absolute blackness. The magic happens when you stand them up.

## 4. Vantablack Defined: Vertically Aligned NanoTube Arrays

The name "Vantablack" sounds like a futuristic, sci-fi paint, but it is actually a very literal, scientific acronym. It stands for:

**V**ertically

**A**ligned

**N**ano**T**ube

**A**rrays

Developed originally by researchers at the National Physical Laboratory in the UK and later commercialized by Surrey NanoSystems in 2014, Vantablack is not a paint, a pigment, or a fabric. It is a highly specialized *coating*.

Imagine a forest. If you fly over a dense pine forest in a helicopter, looking straight down, it appears incredibly dark. This is because the sunlight enters the canopy, hits a branch, bounces down to another branch, hits the trunk, and bounces down to the soil. With every bounce, a portion of the light is absorbed by the trees. By the time the light reaches the forest floor, almost none of it has the correct angle or energy to bounce all the way back up to your eyes in the helicopter. The forest has trapped the light.

Vantablack is a microscopic forest.

It consists of billions of carbon nanotubes packed tightly together and standing perfectly vertical, like trees. They are so densely packed that a surface area of 1 square centimeter contains roughly 1,000 million (one billion) nanotubes.

Yet, astonishingly, Vantablack is composed of 99.96% empty space. The nanotubes are so infinitesimally thin that the vast majority of the coating is just the void of air between the tubes. This ratio of physical matter to empty space is the secret to its optical power.

## 5. The Trap: How Light Enters and Never Leaves

When a photon of visible light (with a wavelength of 380 to 700 nanometers) approaches a surface coated in Vantablack, it does not encounter a solid wall. Because the coating is 99.96% empty space, and the nanotubes are much smaller than the wavelength of the light itself, the photon effectively doesn't "see" a solid surface to bounce off.

The refractive index of Vantablack is almost identical to that of the air around it. In optics, reflection occurs when light passes from a medium with one refractive index (like air) into a medium with a different refractive index (like glass or paint). Because Vantablack is mostly air, the light enters the nanotube "forest" seamlessly, without a surface reflection.

Once inside the forest, the trap is sprung.

```text
Incoming Light Ray (Photon)
         |
         |  (Enters the CNT forest seamlessly)
         V
    |    |    |    |    |   <-- Tops of Carbon Nanotubes
    |    \ /  |    |    |
    |     X   |    |    |   <-- Photon bounces between tubes
    |    / \  |    |    |
    |   |   \ |    |    |
    |   |    \|    |    |
    |   |     X    |    |   <-- More bounces
    |   |    /|    |    |
    |   |   | \    |    |
    |   |   |  \   |    |
    |   |   |   V  |    |   <-- Photon absorbed as heat
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
    Solid Substrate (e.g., Aluminum)

```

The photon strikes the side of a carbon nanotube. Carbon is an excellent absorber of electromagnetic radiation. The nanotube absorbs a fraction of the photon's energy. The photon bounces off, striking a neighboring nanotube, losing more energy. It bounces again, and again, deflected deeper and deeper into the microscopic forest.

By the time the photon has bounced a few dozen times, its optical energy has been completely depleted, converted entirely into a microscopic amount of thermal energy ($\Delta Q$).

The result? The original Vantablack absorbs **99.965%** of visible light. For every 10,000 photons that enter the material, only 3 or 4 manage to escape back out.

If you point a high-powered red laser pointer directly at a surface coated in Vantablack, the red dot simply vanishes. The laser's intense, concentrated light is entirely swallowed by the carbon nanotube array.

## 6. The Alchemist’s Oven: The Chemistry of Synthesis

You cannot simply mix carbon nanotubes into a bucket of water and paint them onto a wall to create Vantablack. If you brush them on, the tubes will lay flat, point in random directions, and clump together. The "forest" must be carefully grown, atom by atom, directly onto the surface of the object you wish to coat.

This is achieved through a complex chemical process known as **Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)**.

Here is how the chemistry of creating the void works:

**Step 1: Catalyst Preparation**
The substrate (the object to be coated, usually made of aluminum or another metal) is placed in a vacuum chamber. A microscopic layer of a catalyst is deposited onto the surface. This catalyst usually consists of nanoscale particles of a transition metal, like iron ($Fe$), cobalt ($Co$), or nickel ($Ni$). These tiny metal droplets will act as the "seeds" from which the carbon "trees" will grow.

**Step 2: The Carbon Feedstock**
The chamber is sealed and heated. A hydrocarbon gas—such as ethylene ($C_2H_4$) or methane ($CH_4$)—is pumped into the chamber.

**Step 3: Thermal Cracking and Deposition**
At high temperatures (historically around $400^\circ\text{C}$ to $450^\circ\text{C}$ for Vantablack), the thermal energy causes the hydrocarbon gas molecules to break apart (crack) when they come into contact with the metal catalyst particles.

Let's look at a simplified chemical breakdown of methane over an iron catalyst:

$$CH_4(g) \xrightarrow{Fe, \Delta} C(s) + 2H_2(g)$$

The hydrogen gas ($H_2$) floats away and is exhausted from the chamber. The solid carbon ($C$) is left behind.

**Step 4: Vertical Growth**
The metal catalyst absorbs the solid carbon atoms until it becomes supersaturated. At this point, the carbon begins to precipitate out of the metal seed. Because the catalyst particles are packed so tightly together on the surface, the precipitating carbon has nowhere to go but *up*.

The carbon atoms arrange themselves into their highly stable $sp^2$ hybridized hexagonal lattice, continuously pushing upward from the base, forming perfect, vertically aligned tubes. The forest grows simultaneously, millimeter by microscopic millimeter, until the desired height is reached.

```text
|  Gas Inlet: Ethylene (C2H4)           |
|     |                                 |
|     V       (High Heat ~400°C)        |
|    o o o     o o o     o o o          | <-- Carbon atoms precipitating
|    | | |     | | |     | | |          | <-- Growing Nanotubes
|    | | |     | | |     | | |          |
|   (Fe/Co)   (Fe/Co)   (Fe/Co)         | <-- Metal Catalyst "Seeds"
|=======================================| <-- Aluminum Substrate
|_______________________________________|

```

The resulting coating is incredibly delicate to the touch. Because it is mostly empty air, touching Vantablack feels like touching nothing at all, but the mechanical pressure of a human finger will easily crush the microscopic forest, ruining the vertical alignment and causing the material to instantly lose its ultra-black properties.

## 7. The Deception of Depth: Hacking the Human Brain

The chemical and physical properties of Vantablack result in a staggering psychological and neurological effect.

The human visual system evolved under the illumination of the sun. Our brains construct a 3D model of the world around us using a variety of cues. One of the most important cues is "shape from shading." When we look at a sphere, the side facing the light source is bright, and the light smoothly drops off into a shadow on the opposite side. Our brain uses this gradient to say, "This object is round."

When an object is coated in Vantablack, this visual cue is entirely eradicated.

Because 99.965% of the light is absorbed regardless of the angle at which it hits the object, there are no highlights. There are no shadows. A crease in a foil coated in Vantablack absorbs exactly as much light as the flat surface next to it.

When you look at a Vantablack-coated sphere, your eyes receive exactly the same number of photons (zero) from the center of the sphere as they do from the curving edges. To your brain, the object ceases to have volume. It renders as a perfectly flat, two-dimensional black circle cut out of the background.

This creates a cognitive dissonance. Your binocular vision (having two eyes) tells you an object is situated in space in front of you, but the lack of shading tells your brain the object has no depth. Observers who have seen Vantablack in person often describe it as unsettling, dizzying, or akin to looking into a bottomless pit. It is an aesthetic experience of pure nothingness.

## 8. The Practical Void: Engineering and Space Exploration

While the visual effect is stunning, Surrey NanoSystems did not develop Vantablack to play tricks on the human eye. They developed it to solve serious problems in optical engineering and aerospace chemistry.

**Stray Light in Telescopes**
When astronomers use optical or infrared telescopes to look at distant, incredibly faint galaxies, they face a massive problem: "stray light." Light from nearby stars, the sun, or even the Earth can enter the telescope tube and bounce off the internal walls. Even if the inside of the telescope is painted flat black, that $2\%$ to $5\%$ reflection is enough to completely wash out the faint light from a distant galaxy, acting like glare on a windshield.

By coating the internal baffles of a telescope with Vantablack, stray light is entirely absorbed. This increases the signal-to-noise ratio of the telescope exponentially, allowing us to see further and clearer into the depths of the universe than ever before. It is quite poetic: we must use the darkest material on Earth to see the faintest lights in the cosmos.

**Star Trackers**
Satellites navigate in space using "star trackers"—highly sensitive cameras that look at star constellations to determine the satellite's orientation. If sunlight hits the inside of the star tracker's lens hood, it blinds the sensor. Vantablack prevents this blinding, allowing satellites to maintain their orientation even when facing relatively close to the sun.

**Infrared Sensors**
Vantablack is not just black in the visible spectrum. Its chemical structure allows it to absorb radiation across a massive swath of the electromagnetic spectrum, from ultraviolet light all the way deep into the infrared and microwave bands. This makes it an ideal coating for calibration targets for thermal imaging cameras and infrared sensors used in military and meteorological applications.

## 9. The Art World "Color Wars"

Because of its profound visual impact, it was inevitable that the art world would take notice of Vantablack. What happened next, however, sparked one of the most bizarre and highly publicized controversies in modern art history.

In 2016, the acclaimed British-Indian sculptor Sir Anish Kapoor (famous for creating the reflective "Cloud Gate" bean in Chicago) secured the *exclusive* artistic rights to Vantablack. Surrey NanoSystems granted Kapoor's studio a monopoly on using the material in fine art.

The broader artistic community was outraged. Colors and pigments had historically been accessible to anyone. The idea that a specific shade of black—the *ultimate* black—could be monopolized by a single artist was seen as an affront to the spirit of creation.

This sparked a retaliatory campaign by another British artist, Stuart Semple. Infuriated by Kapoor's exclusivity deal, Semple worked with chemists and paint manufacturers to create "the world's pinkest pink," an incredibly vibrant fluorescent powder. He made it available for purchase to anyone in the world—*except* Anish Kapoor. To buy it, customers had to sign a legal declaration stating they were not Kapoor and were not associated with him.

Semple didn't stop there. He set out to democratize the void. Working with acrylics and specialized super-absorbent mattifying agents, Semple released a series of paints called "Black 2.0" and eventually "Black 3.0."

While Semple's paints are not made of carbon nanotubes and do not trap light via a microscopic forest (they rely on traditional acrylic pigment chemistry, which makes them safe to touch and easy to paint), Black 3.0 manages to absorb up to 98% to 99% of visible light. It is arguably the blackest *paint* in the world, even if it cannot match the 99.965% absorption of the Vantablack *coating*.

The "Color Wars" highlighted a fascinating intersection between advanced materials chemistry and human expression, posing the question: who owns the absence of light?

## 10. The Unending Quest for Absolute Black

Science, however, never rests. Vantablack held the title for the darkest material in the world for a few years, but the chemical principles behind it left room for improvement.

In 2019, engineers at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) accidentally created a material even darker. They were experimenting with ways to grow carbon nanotubes on aluminum foil that had been treated with chlorine salts to remove its oxide layer, hoping to improve thermal and electrical conductivity.

When they grew the CNT forest on this specially treated aluminum, they noticed it looked exceptionally dark. When they measured its optical properties, they were stunned.

The MIT material absorbed **99.995%** of incoming light, from every angle.

If Vantablack reflects a tiny fraction of a percent of light, the MIT material reflects ten times less than that. To demonstrate its power, the researchers took a brilliantly sparkling, $16.78$-carat yellow diamond (a material defined by its ability to reflect and refract light) and coated it in their new CNT material. The sparkling gem instantly vanished, becoming a flat, featureless black void.

The exact chemistry and geometry that make the MIT material slightly darker than Vantablack are still being studied, but it relies on the exact same fundamental principles: leveraging the carbon atom's incredible ability to form nanoscale structures that physically trap photons and convert their energy into heat.

Will we ever reach 100% absorption?

In practical, terrestrial terms, probably not. Achieving a literal 100% absorption would require a material that perfectly matches the impedance of the surrounding vacuum or air, and has infinite depth to guarantee no photon ever bounces back. In physics, an object that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation is known as a "Black Body." Perfect black bodies are theoretical constructs used in thermodynamics; they do not exist in the physical world. A supermassive black hole is the closest natural phenomenon to a perfect black body, as its immense gravity prevents even light from escaping.

Therefore, creating ultra-black materials is essentially the quest to engineer a black hole in a chemistry lab.

Vantablack and its successors represent a triumph of human ingenuity. It is a profound reminder that what we see is not necessarily what *is*. Our eyes are just biological sensors, calibrated to a specific, narrow band of reality. When we alter the chemistry of a surface just enough, that reality falls away, leaving only an infinite void.

To look at Vantablack is not just to look at a triumph of chemistry. It is to look directly at the absence of information. It is the closest we can come, while standing safely on Earth, to staring into the deep, unending dark of deep space.
