Water is the essential medium for countless chemical processes. In this chapter, we explore the dynamic chemistry of aqueous solutions. We first examine how substances dissolve to form electrolytes and nonelectrolytes. Next, we investigate three major classes of aqueous reactions: precipitation (forming insoluble solids), acid-base neutralizations (transferring protons), and oxidation-reduction (exchanging electrons). Finally, we apply the concept of molarity to quantify these reactions using solution stoichiometry and laboratory titrations, connecting observable volumes to precise molecular interactions.

## 4.1 General Properties of Aqueous Solutions

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. In any solution, the substance present in the greatest quantity is usually called the **solvent**, while the other substances dissolved within it are called **solutes**. When water is the solvent, the resulting mixture is known as an **aqueous solution**. Because water is abundant and an excellent solvent for a wide variety of substances, aqueous solutions play a central role in both natural processes and laboratory chemistry.

To understand the behavior of substances in aqueous solutions, we must examine what happens at the particulate level when a solute dissolves in water and how this process affects the physical properties of the resulting mixture.

### Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

One of the most striking properties of some aqueous solutions is their ability to conduct electricity. Pure water is an extremely poor conductor of electricity. However, when certain substances are dissolved in it, the resulting solution becomes highly conductive.

* An **electrolyte** is a substance whose aqueous solution contains ions, enabling it to conduct electricity.
* A **nonelectrolyte** is a substance that does not form ions in solution, and therefore, its aqueous solution does not conduct electricity.

When sodium chloride ($\text{NaCl}$) dissolves in water, the solid lattice breaks apart into individual sodium ($\text{Na}^+$) and chloride ($\text{Cl}^-$) ions. The presence of these freely moving charged particles is what allows the electrical current to flow. In contrast, when sucrose ($\text{C}_{12}\text{H}_{22}\text{O}_{11}$) dissolves in water, the solid breaks apart into individual, neutral sucrose molecules. Because no charged particles are generated, the solution remains nonconductive.

### The Solvation Process

Water is a polar molecule. The oxygen atom bears a partial negative charge ($\delta^-$), while the hydrogen atoms bear partial positive charges ($\delta^+$). When an ionic compound is introduced to water, the electrostatic forces between the water molecules and the ions overcome the ionic bonds holding the solid lattice together.

As an ion separates from the solid, it becomes immediately surrounded by water molecules. This process is called **solvation** (or **hydration**, specifically when water is the solvent).

The orientation of the water molecules depends on the charge of the ion:

* **Cations** (positive ions) are surrounded by water molecules with the partial negative oxygen atoms pointing toward the ion.
* **Anions** (negative ions) are surrounded by water molecules with the partial positive hydrogen atoms pointing toward the ion.

```text
       Hydration of a Cation (Na⁺)             Hydration of an Anion (Cl⁻)
            
               H       H                                  
                \     /                                 
                  O (δ-)                               O (δ-) -- H (δ+) 
                  |                                   /                  
      (δ-) O ---- Na⁺ ---- O (δ-)          H (δ+) -- Cl⁻ -- H (δ+)       
          /                |                         /                   
         H                 H             (δ-) O -- H (δ+)                
                                         /                               
                                        H                                

```

This hydration shell stabilizes the ions in solution and prevents them from recombining. We denote the hydrated state of an ion by appending $(aq)$ to its chemical symbol:

$$ \text{NaCl}(s) \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) $$

Molecular compounds that act as nonelectrolytes, such as sucrose or methanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$), also undergo hydration. However, instead of separating into ions, the intact, neutral molecules are surrounded by water molecules, usually facilitated by intermolecular forces such as hydrogen bonding.

$$ \text{CH}_3\text{OH}(l) \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{CH}_3\text{OH}(aq) $$

### Strong and Weak Electrolytes

Electrolytes are further classified based on the extent to which they dissociate into ions in solution.

**Strong Electrolytes**
Strong electrolytes are solutes that exist in solution almost completely as ions. Essentially all water-soluble ionic compounds (like $\text{NaCl}$) and a few molecular compounds (like strong acids, e.g., $\text{HCl}$) are strong electrolytes. When a strong electrolyte dissolves, the dissociation is considered complete, which we represent with a single-headed arrow in the chemical equation:

$$ \text{HCl}(g) \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) $$

**Weak Electrolytes**
Weak electrolytes are solutes that exist in solution mostly in the form of neutral molecules, with only a small fraction dissociating into ions. For example, acetic acid ($\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$) is highly soluble in water, but it is a weak electrolyte. Most of the dissolved solute remains as intact $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$ molecules.

To represent the partial dissociation of a weak electrolyte, we use a double arrow ($\rightleftharpoons$). This indicates a state of **chemical equilibrium**, where the forward reaction (molecules breaking into ions) and the reverse reaction (ions recombining into molecules) occur at the same rate:

$$ \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}(aq) \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-(aq) $$

```text
Summary of Solute Types in Aqueous Solution:

| Solute Type        | Predominant Species in Solution | Conductivity | Example         |
|--------------------|---------------------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Strong Electrolyte | Ions                            | High         | NaCl, HCl       |
| Weak Electrolyte   | Molecules (and some ions)       | Low          | CH₃COOH, NH₃    |
| Nonelectrolyte     | Molecules                       | None         | Sucrose, C₂H₅OH |

```

Understanding the distinction between strong electrolytes, weak electrolytes, and nonelectrolytes is essential, as this dictates the exact species present in a mixture and ultimately determines how substances will interact in the aqueous reactions explored in subsequent sections.

## 4.2 Precipitation Reactions and Solubility Rules

A common and visually striking type of reaction in aqueous solutions occurs when two clear solutions are mixed and an insoluble solid forms. This solid is known as a **precipitate**, and the reaction that produces it is called a **precipitation reaction**.

Precipitation reactions occur when the attraction between oppositely charged ions in the solution is so strong that they overcome the forces of hydration, bonding together to form an insoluble ionic lattice.

### Solubility and Soluble vs. Insoluble

To predict whether a precipitate will form when two solutions are mixed, we must understand the concept of solubility. The **solubility** of a substance is the maximum amount of that substance that can dissolve in a given quantity of solvent at a specific temperature.

In the context of aqueous reactions, we broadly categorize substances into two groups:

* **Soluble:** A compound that dissolves readily in water to form a solution (typically defined as having a solubility greater than $0.01 \text{ mol/L}$ at room temperature).
* **Insoluble:** A compound that does not dissolve significantly in water. While all ionic compounds dissolve to some very minute extent, we consider a substance insoluble if its solubility is extraordinarily low.

### The Solubility Rules

Chemists rely on experimental observations to predict solubility. These observations have been summarized into a set of general guidelines known as the **solubility rules** for common ionic compounds in water.

**Table 4.1: Solubility Rules for Common Ionic Compounds in Water**

| Soluble Compounds Contain: | Important Exceptions (Insoluble if combined with:) |
| --- | --- |
| Alkali metal ions ($\text{Li}^+, \text{Na}^+, \text{K}^+, \text{Rb}^+, \text{Cs}^+$) | None |
| Ammonium ion ($\text{NH}_4^+$) | None |
| Nitrates ($\text{NO}_3^-$), Acetates ($\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-$) | None |
| Chlorides ($\text{Cl}^-$), Bromides ($\text{Br}^-$), Iodides ($\text{I}^-$) | $\text{Ag}^+, \text{Hg}_2^{2+}, \text{Pb}^{2+}$ |
| Sulfates ($\text{SO}_4^{2-}$) | $\text{Sr}^{2+}, \text{Ba}^{2+}, \text{Hg}_2^{2+}, \text{Pb}^{2+}$ |

| Insoluble Compounds Contain: | Important Exceptions (Soluble if combined with:) |
| --- | --- |
| Carbonates ($\text{CO}_3^{2-}$), Phosphates ($\text{PO}_4^{3-}$) | Alkali metal ions, $\text{NH}_4^+$ |
| Hydroxides ($\text{OH}^-$) | Alkali metal ions, $\text{NH}_4^+, \text{Ca}^{2+}, \text{Sr}^{2+}, \text{Ba}^{2+}$ |
| Sulfides ($\text{S}^{2-}$) | Alkali metal ions, $\text{NH}_4^+, \text{Ca}^{2+}, \text{Sr}^{2+}, \text{Ba}^{2+}$ |

### Predicting Precipitation: Metathesis Reactions

Precipitation reactions are often **metathesis reactions** (also called double-displacement or exchange reactions). In these reactions, the cations and anions of two different compounds appear to exchange partners to form two new compounds. The general pattern is:

$$ \text{AX} + \text{BY} \rightarrow \text{AY} + \text{BX} $$

*(Where A and B are cations, and X and Y are anions).*

To predict whether a metathesis reaction will result in a precipitate, follow these steps:

1. Identify the ions present in the reactant solutions.
2. Swap the cations and anions to determine the chemical formulas of the potential products. Ensure the new formulas are electrically neutral.
3. Consult the solubility rules to see if either of the potential products is insoluble.
4. If a product is insoluble, a precipitation reaction occurs. If both products are soluble, no reaction occurs, and the ions simply remain mixed in solution.

### Writing Chemical Equations for Aqueous Reactions

When representing aqueous reactions, chemists use three different types of equations to highlight different aspects of the chemical change. Consider the reaction between aqueous lead(II) nitrate and aqueous potassium iodide.

**1. The Molecular Equation**
The molecular equation writes the complete, neutral chemical formulas of all reactants and products, as if they were intact molecules in solution.

$$ \text{Pb(NO}_3)_2(aq) + 2\text{KI}(aq) \rightarrow \text{PbI}_2(s) + 2\text{KNO}_3(aq) $$

**2. The Complete Ionic Equation**
Because strong electrolytes (like $\text{Pb(NO}_3)_2$ and $\text{KI}$) dissociate completely in water, a more accurate representation shows all soluble strong electrolytes as individual, hydrated ions. The precipitate, $\text{PbI}_2(s)$, remains written as a solid unit.

$$ \text{Pb}^{2+}(aq) + 2\text{NO}_3^-(aq) + 2\text{K}^+(aq) + 2\text{I}^-(aq) \rightarrow \text{PbI}_2(s) + 2\text{K}^+(aq) + 2\text{NO}_3^-(aq) $$

```text
       Visualizing the Exchange of Ions
       
       Reactants in Solution:    [ Pb²⁺ + 2 NO₃⁻ ]  +  [ 2 K⁺ + 2 I⁻ ]
                                            \_________/
                                            /         \
       Products after Mixing:    [ PbI₂ ](s)           [ 2 K⁺ + 2 NO₃⁻ ](aq)
                               (Solid Precipitate)      (Remain dissolved)

```

**3. The Net Ionic Equation**
Notice in the complete ionic equation that the potassium ions ($\text{K}^+$) and nitrate ions ($\text{NO}_3^-$) appear in exactly the same form on both sides of the arrow. These are **spectator ions**—they are present in the solution but do not participate directly in the chemical reaction.

If we cancel out the spectator ions, we are left with the **net ionic equation**, which shows only the species that actually undergo a chemical change:

$$ \text{Pb}^{2+}(aq) + 2\text{I}^-(aq) \rightarrow \text{PbI}_2(s) $$

The net ionic equation strips away the unnecessary clutter, clearly demonstrating that the core chemical event is the combination of lead(II) ions and iodide ions to form solid lead(II) iodide.

## 4.3 Acids, Bases, and Neutralization Reactions

Acids and bases are among the most common and important chemical compounds. They are found in biological systems, industrial processes, and everyday household products. In aqueous solutions, their behavior is governed by the generation and interaction of specific ions.

### Acids: Proton Donors

In the late 19th century, the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius defined **acids** as substances that ionize in aqueous solution to form hydrogen ions ($\text{H}^+$). Because a hydrogen atom consists of simply one proton and one electron, a hydrogen ion ($\text{H}^+$) is nothing more than a bare proton. For this reason, acids are often referred to as **proton donors**.

When an acid dissolves in water, the highly reactive $\text{H}^+$ ion does not exist in isolation. Instead, it strongly interacts with the nonbonding electron pairs on a water molecule to form a **hydronium ion** ($\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$):

$$ \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{O}^+(aq) $$

```text
       Formation of the Hydronium Ion
       
                       _                                _
         +           /   \            +               /   \
        H     +     |  O  |    ---------->           |  O  |
      Proton         \   /                            \   /
                     /   \                            / | \
                    H     H                          H  H  H
                    
      (H⁺)          (Water, H₂O)               (Hydronium, H₃O⁺)

```

Chemists often use $\text{H}^+(aq)$ and $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+(aq)$ interchangeably to represent the hydrated proton in solution.

Acids can be classified by the number of acidic protons they can yield per molecule:

* **Monoprotic acids** yield one $\text{H}^+$ per molecule (e.g., hydrochloric acid, $\text{HCl}$, and nitric acid, $\text{HNO}_3$).
* **Diprotic acids** yield two $\text{H}^+$ ions per molecule (e.g., sulfuric acid, $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$). The ionization of polyprotic acids occurs in distinct steps.

### Bases: Proton Acceptors

Arrhenius defined **bases** as substances that react with, or accept, $\text{H}^+$ ions. In aqueous solutions, bases produce hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$).

Ionic hydroxides, such as sodium hydroxide ($\text{NaOH}$) and potassium hydroxide ($\text{KOH}$), dissociate directly in water to release $\text{OH}^-$ ions:

$$ \text{NaOH}(s) \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) $$

Other substances, such as ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$), do not contain hydroxide ions in their chemical formulas but are still bases. They generate $\text{OH}^-$ by extracting a proton from a water molecule:

$$ \text{NH}_3(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) $$

### Strong vs. Weak Acids and Bases

Just as we categorized electrolytes in Section 4.1, acids and bases are classified as "strong" or "weak" depending on their degree of ionization in water.

* **Strong acids and strong bases** are strong electrolytes. They completely dissociate into ions in aqueous solution.
* **Weak acids and weak bases** are weak electrolytes. They only partially ionize, establishing a state of chemical equilibrium between the intact molecules and the generated ions.

Because their behavior fundamentally dictates the chemistry of the solution, it is essential to memorize the common strong acids and strong bases.

**Table 4.2: Common Strong Acids and Strong Bases**

| Strong Acids | Strong Bases |
| --- | --- |
| Hydrochloric acid ($\text{HCl}$) | Group 1A metal hydroxides (e.g., $\text{LiOH}, \text{NaOH}, \text{KOH}$) |
| Hydrobromic acid ($\text{HBr}$) | Heavy Group 2A metal hydroxides ($\text{Ca(OH)}_2, \text{Sr(OH)}_2, \text{Ba(OH)}_2$) |
| Hydroiodic acid ($\text{HI}$) | &nbsp; |
| Nitric acid ($\text{HNO}_3$) | &nbsp; |
| Sulfuric acid ($\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$)* | &nbsp; |
| Chloric acid ($\text{HClO}_3$) | &nbsp; |
| Perchloric acid ($\text{HClO}_4$) | &nbsp; |

*(Note: For $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$, only the first ionization step is strong).*

Any acid or base not listed in this table can generally be assumed to be weak. For example, acetic acid ($\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$) and hydrofluoric acid ($\text{HF}$) are weak acids, while ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$) is a weak base.

### Neutralization Reactions

When an acidic solution and a basic solution are mixed, a **neutralization reaction** occurs. The products of a neutralization reaction between an acid and a metal hydroxide are typically water and a **salt**. (In chemistry, the term "salt" refers to any ionic compound whose cation comes from a base and whose anion comes from an acid).

Consider the reaction between hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide:

**Molecular Equation:**

$$ \text{HCl}(aq) + \text{NaOH}(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{NaCl}(aq) $$

Because $\text{HCl}$, $\text{NaOH}$, and $\text{NaCl}$ are all strong electrolytes, we can separate them into their constituent ions to write the **complete ionic equation**:

$$ \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) + \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) $$

Notice that $\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{Cl}^-$ are spectator ions. Canceling them out yields the **net ionic equation**:

$$ \text{H}^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

This simple net ionic equation summarizes the fundamental nature of *any* neutralization reaction between a strong acid and a strong base: the transfer of a proton from the acid to the hydroxide ion of the base to form liquid water.

### Gas-Forming Neutralization Reactions

Some neutralization reactions yield products that are not stable in aqueous solution and subsequently break down to form a gas. The most common examples involve carbonates ($\text{CO}_3^{2-}$), bicarbonates ($\text{HCO}_3^-$), and sulfides ($\text{S}^{2-}$).

When an acid reacts with a carbonate or bicarbonate, the initial metathesis reaction forms carbonic acid ($\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$). Carbonic acid is unstable and immediately decomposes into liquid water and carbon dioxide gas:

**Step 1 (Exchange):**

$$ 2\text{HCl}(aq) + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3(aq) + 2\text{NaCl}(aq) $$

**Step 2 (Decomposition):**

$$ \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{CO}_2(g) $$

**Overall Molecular Equation:**

$$ 2\text{HCl}(aq) + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\text{NaCl}(aq) $$

Similarly, reactions between acids and metal sulfides produce hydrogen sulfide gas ($\text{H}_2\text{S}$), a substance infamous for its "rotten egg" odor:

$$ 2\text{HCl}(aq) + \text{Na}_2\text{S}(aq) \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{S}(g) + 2\text{NaCl}(aq) $$

Recognizing these gas-forming patterns is crucial for predicting the outcomes of reactions involving acids and basic anions.

## 4.4 Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions

In the previous sections, we examined reactions where ions exchange partners (precipitation) or transfer protons (acid-base neutralization). However, there is a third major class of aqueous reactions driven by the transfer of fundamentally different subatomic particles: electrons. Reactions in which electrons are transferred from one reactant to another are called **oxidation-reduction reactions**, or **redox reactions**.

Corrosion of metals, the metabolism of food in our bodies, and the generation of electricity in batteries are all everyday examples of redox processes.

### Oxidation and Reduction

Historically, "oxidation" referred specifically to the combination of an element with oxygen. Today, the definition is much broader. In modern chemistry, these terms refer strictly to the movement of electrons:

* **Oxidation** is the *loss* of electrons by a substance. When an atom, ion, or molecule loses electrons, it becomes more positively charged.
* **Reduction** is the *gain* of electrons by a substance. When a substance gains electrons, its charge becomes more negative (it is "reduced" mathematically).

Because electrons are conserved in chemical reactions, oxidation and reduction must always occur together. If one substance loses electrons, another substance must immediately accept them.

```text
       The Electron Transfer Process
       
       Reactant A                     Reactant B
     (Electron donor)               (Electron acceptor)
            |                               |
            |----------( e⁻ transfer )----->|
            |                               |
       Becomes Oxidized                 Becomes Reduced
     (Charge increases)               (Charge decreases)

```

A helpful mnemonic device to remember these definitions is **OIL RIG**: **O**xidation **I**s **L**oss, **R**eduction **I**s **G**ain (of electrons).

### Identifying Redox Reactions: Oxidation States

To keep track of electrons during a reaction and determine if a redox process has occurred, chemists use the concept of **oxidation numbers** (or oxidation states). An oxidation number is the actual or hypothetical charge an atom would have if all its bonds were completely ionic.

While Chapter 18 will cover the comprehensive rules for assigning oxidation states and balancing complex redox equations, you can identify many fundamental redox reactions by looking for elements changing from their pure, uncombined state to a compound, or vice versa. Any element in its standard elemental form (such as $\text{Na}(s)$, $\text{O}_2(g)$, or $\text{Cl}_2(g)$) has an oxidation number of zero. Monatomic ions (like $\text{Na}^+$ or $\text{Cl}^-$) have oxidation numbers equal to their ionic charge.

Consider the reaction between sodium metal and chlorine gas to form sodium chloride:

$$ 2\text{Na}(s) + \text{Cl}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{NaCl}(s) $$

* Sodium changes from $\text{Na}$ (charge of $0$) to $\text{Na}^+$ (charge of $+1$). It has lost electrons; it is **oxidized**.
* Chlorine changes from $\text{Cl}_2$ (charge of $0$) to $\text{Cl}^-$ (charge of $-1$). It has gained electrons; it is **reduced**.

### Oxidizing and Reducing Agents

We often describe the reactants in a redox reaction by the effect they have on one another:

* An **oxidizing agent** (or oxidant) is the substance that makes oxidation possible by accepting electrons. Because it gains electrons, the oxidizing agent is itself *reduced*.
* A **reducing agent** (or reductant) is the substance that provides electrons, making reduction possible. Because it loses electrons, the reducing agent is itself *oxidized*.

In the formation of $\text{NaCl}$ above, $\text{Na}(s)$ is the reducing agent, and $\text{Cl}_2(g)$ is the oxidizing agent.

### Metals and Aqueous Solutions: Single Displacement Reactions

Many important redox reactions in aqueous solutions involve a solid metal reacting with the dissolved ions of another metal or with an acid. These are often called **single displacement** or **single replacement** reactions.

For example, if you place a strip of solid zinc into an aqueous solution of hydrochloric acid, bubbles of hydrogen gas immediately form, and the zinc strip dissolves:

**Molecular Equation:**

$$ \text{Zn}(s) + 2\text{HCl}(aq) \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) $$

**Net Ionic Equation:**

$$ \text{Zn}(s) + 2\text{H}^+(aq) \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) $$

By writing the **half-reactions**, we can clearly see the electron transfer:

* **Oxidation Half-Reaction:** $\text{Zn}(s) \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) + 2\text{e}^-$
* **Reduction Half-Reaction:** $2\text{H}^+(aq) + 2\text{e}^- \rightarrow \text{H}_2(g)$

Solid zinc is oxidized by the hydrogen ions. Consequently, zinc acts as the reducing agent, and the aqueous hydrogen ion acts as the oxidizing agent.

### The Activity Series

Not all metals undergo single displacement reactions with the same ease. For instance, while zinc reacts vigorously with acids to produce hydrogen gas, copper metal placed in hydrochloric acid will simply sit there, inert.

Chemists have organized metals into an **activity series**, a list arranged in order of decreasing ease of oxidation. Metals at the top of the list are "active metals"—they lose electrons readily and are excellent reducing agents. Metals at the bottom are "noble metals"—they resist oxidation and are relatively unreactive.

```text
       The Activity Series of Common Metals
       
       [Most easily oxidized; Strongest reducing agents]
          Lithium     (Li)  --- React with cold water
          Potassium   (K)    |  to form H₂(g)
          Barium      (Ba)   |
          Calcium     (Ca)  ---
          Sodium      (Na)   
          Magnesium   (Mg)  --- React with steam
          Aluminum    (Al)   |  to form H₂(g)
          Zinc        (Zn)   |
          Chromium    (Cr)   |
          Iron        (Fe)  ---
          Nickel      (Ni)  --- React with acids
          Tin         (Sn)   |  to form H₂(g)
          Lead        (Pb)  ---
       -------------------------------------------------
          Hydrogen    (H₂)  --- Reference Point
       -------------------------------------------------
          Copper      (Cu)  --- Unreactive with simple
          Silver      (Ag)   |  aqueous acids
          Mercury     (Hg)   |
          Platinum    (Pt)   |
          Gold        (Au)  ---
       [Least easily oxidized; Weakest reducing agents]

```

**Using the Activity Series:**
The fundamental rule of the activity series is that *any metal on the list can be oxidized by the ions of elements below it*.

For example, because zinc is above copper on the list, solid zinc will displace copper(II) ions from solution:

$$ \text{Zn}(s) + \text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) + \text{Cu}(s) $$

In this reaction, the blue color of the $\text{Cu}^{2+}$ solution will gradually fade, and solid copper metal will plate out onto the disintegrating zinc strip. Conversely, if you place a strip of solid copper into a solution containing $\text{Zn}^{2+}$ ions, no reaction will occur, because copper is below zinc in the activity series and cannot transfer its electrons to the zinc ions.

## 4.5 Molarity and Concentration of Solutions

When we dissolve a solute in a solvent, the resulting solution can vary widely in its composition. A solution with a relatively small amount of dissolved solute is described qualitatively as **dilute**, while one with a large amount of dissolved solute is **concentrated**. However, for scientific and quantitative work, these relative terms are insufficient. We must be able to express the exact amount of solute dissolved in a specific volume of solution. This measurement is called **concentration**.

While there are several ways to express concentration (such as mass percentage or molality), the unit most widely used by chemists in aqueous solution stoichiometry is **molarity**.

### The Concept of Molarity

**Molarity** (symbolized by $M$) is defined as the number of moles of solute per liter of solution.

$$ \text{Molarity} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution in liters}} $$

Or, expressed as an algebraic formula:

$$ M = \frac{n}{V} $$

*(Where $n$ represents moles of solute and $V$ represents the volume of the solution in liters).*

A 1.0 molar solution of sodium chloride (written as **1.0 M NaCl**) contains exactly 1.0 mole of $\text{NaCl}$ dissolved in exactly 1.0 liter of total solution.

It is crucial to note that molarity is based on the *total volume of the solution*, not the volume of the solvent used. Because the solute itself occupies space, adding 1.0 mole of a solid to 1.0 liter of water would result in a final volume slightly greater than 1.0 liter, making the molarity slightly less than 1.0 M.

To prepare a solution of a precise molarity in the laboratory, chemists use a specialized piece of glassware called a volumetric flask.

```text
       Preparation of a Standard Solution Using a Volumetric Flask
       
       Step 1           Step 2           Step 3             Step 4
       Solute           Add Water        Swirl to           Fill to Mark
       Added            & Dissolve       Mix                with Water
       
        ||               ||               ||                 ||  <-- Calibration
       /  \             /  \             /  \               /--\     Mark
      |    |           |    |           |    |             |::::|
      |    |           |~~~~|           |~~~~|             |::::|
      | •• |           | •• |           |    |             |::::|
      \____/           \____/           \____/             \____/

```

### Calculating Molarity

Molarity serves as a conversion factor bridging the macroscopic world of laboratory measurements (volume of solutions) and the microscopic world of chemical equations (moles of particles).

If you dissolve **29.22 g** of $\text{NaCl}$ in enough water to make **500.0 mL** of solution, you can calculate the molarity in two steps:

1. **Convert the mass of solute to moles:** Using the molar mass of $\text{NaCl}$ (**58.44 g/mol**).

$$ 29.22 \text{ g NaCl} \times \frac{1 \text{ mol NaCl}}{58.44 \text{ g NaCl}} = 0.5000 \text{ mol NaCl} $$

1. **Convert the volume to liters and divide:**

$$ 500.0 \text{ mL} = 0.5000 \text{ L} $$

$$ M = \frac{0.5000 \text{ mol}}{0.5000 \text{ L}} = 1.000 \text{ M} $$

### Ion Concentrations in Electrolyte Solutions

When dealing with strong electrolytes, it is often necessary to know the concentration of the individual ions in the solution. Because strong electrolytes dissociate completely, the concentration of the resulting ions depends on the stoichiometry of the chemical formula.

Consider a **1.0 M** solution of sodium sulfate ($\text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4$). When this compound dissolves, it completely dissociates according to the following equation:

$$ \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow 2\text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{SO}_4^{2-}(aq) $$

For every one mole of $\text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4$ that dissolves, two moles of $\text{Na}^+$ ions and one mole of $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$ ions are produced. Therefore, in a **1.0 M** $\text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4$ solution:

* The concentration of sodium ions, $[\text{Na}^+]$, is **2.0 M**.
* The concentration of sulfate ions, $[\text{SO}_4^{2-}]$, is **1.0 M**.

*(Note: Square brackets around a chemical formula, such as $[\text{Na}^+]$, are the standard chemical notation for "molarity of").*

### Dilution

In the laboratory, it is common practice to purchase or prepare highly concentrated solutions, known as **stock solutions**, and then carefully add water to achieve a desired, lower concentration. This process is called **dilution**.

When a solution is diluted, solvent is added, but the number of moles of solute remains absolutely constant.

```text
       The Principle of Dilution
       
       Concentrated Stock Solution        Dilute Final Solution
       (High Molarity, M₁)                (Low Molarity, M₂)
              ___                                ___
             |   |                              |   |
             |___|  + Solvent (Water)           |___|
             | • |  ---------------->           |   |
             |• •|  (Moles of solute            | • |
             |_•_|   remain constant)           |• •|
                                                |_•_|
          Volume = V₁                        Volume = V₂

```

Because the moles of solute before dilution must equal the moles of solute after dilution, and because $n = M \times V$, we can establish a very useful mathematical relationship:

$$ M_1V_1 = M_2V_2 $$

*(Where $M_1$ and $V_1$ are the molarity and volume of the initial concentrated solution, and $M_2$ and $V_2$ are the molarity and volume of the final diluted solution).*

**Example Application:**
If you need to prepare **250.0 mL** of a **0.100 M** $\text{HCl}$ solution using a standard **12.0 M** $\text{HCl}$ stock solution, you can use the dilution equation to find out exactly how much stock solution ($V_1$) to measure out:

$$ (12.0 \text{ M})(V_1) = (0.100 \text{ M})(250.0 \text{ mL}) $$

$$ V_1 = \frac{(0.100 \text{ M})(250.0 \text{ mL})}{12.0 \text{ M}} $$

$$ V_1 = 2.08 \text{ mL} $$

To prepare the solution, you would carefully measure **2.08 mL** of the 12.0 M stock solution into a 250 mL volumetric flask, and then add water until the total volume reaches the 250.0 mL calibration mark.

## 4.6 Solution Stoichiometry and Chemical Titrations

In Chapter 3, we explored stoichiometry—the quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction. We learned to convert the mass of a substance into moles using its molar mass, which then allowed us to use the balanced chemical equation to predict the amounts of other substances involved.

However, many reactions do not occur between solid powders; as we have seen throughout this chapter, they take place in aqueous solutions. For dissolved substances, we rarely measure mass directly. Instead, we measure the volume of the solution. By combining the concept of stoichiometry with **molarity** (introduced in Section 4.5), we can perform **solution stoichiometry** calculations.

### The Solution Stoichiometry Pathway

The core principle of stoichiometry remains unchanged: chemical equations relate *moles* of reactants to *moles* of products. When working with solutions, molarity serves as the conversion factor between the measurable volume of a solution and the number of moles of the dissolved solute.

Recall the molarity equation:

$$ M = \frac{\text{moles}}{\text{Volume (L)}} $$

Rearranging this equation gives us the primary tool for solution stoichiometry:

$$ \text{moles} = M \times V $$

To solve a typical solution stoichiometry problem, you follow a logical pathway that mirrors mass-based stoichiometry, simply swapping out molar mass for molarity:

```text
       The Solution Stoichiometry Pathway
       
       Volume of              Moles of               Moles of               Volume of
       Substance A ---------> Substance A ---------> Substance B ---------> Substance B
                     Use                    Use                    Use
                  Molarity of            Stoichiometric         Molarity of
                  Solution A              Coefficients           Solution B

```

### Chemical Titrations

One of the most important applications of solution stoichiometry in the laboratory is a technique called **titration**. A titration is a highly precise analytical procedure used to determine the unknown concentration of a dissolved substance.

In a typical titration, a solution of a known concentration, called the **standard solution** (or **titrant**), is carefully added to a specific volume of a solution with an unknown concentration, called the **analyte**.

```text
       Standard Titration Setup
       
             | |  <-- Buret (Graduated glass tube)
             | |      Contains the Titrant (known Molarity)
             | |
            /   \
           |-----| <-- Stopcock (Valve to control the flow drop-by-drop)
            \___/
              |
              :   <-- Drops of titrant falling into the flask
             ___
            /   \
           /     \ <-- Erlenmeyer Flask
          |_______|    Contains the Analyte (unknown Molarity) + Indicator

```

**The Equivalence Point and End Point**
The goal of a titration is to add just enough standard solution to completely react with all of the analyte present. The exact point at which stoichiometrically equivalent quantities of reacting substances have been combined is called the **equivalence point**.

Because the reactants and products in many titrations (like acid-base neutralizations) are colorless, there is usually no obvious visual cue that the equivalence point has been reached. To solve this, chemists add a small amount of an **indicator** to the analyte flask. An indicator is a dye that changes color depending on the chemical environment (such as pH). The point in the titration where the indicator actually changes color is called the **endpoint**. Ideally, the indicator is chosen so that its endpoint matches the reaction's equivalence point as closely as possible.

### Performing a Titration Calculation

Titration calculations are simply applied solution stoichiometry. Let us examine a typical acid-base titration.

**Example Problem:**
A student wants to determine the concentration of a sample of hydrochloric acid ($\text{HCl}$). The student places exactly $25.0 \text{ mL}$ of the unknown $\text{HCl}$ solution into a flask and adds a few drops of phenolphthalein indicator. The student then titrates the sample with a standard $0.150 \text{ M}$ solution of sodium hydroxide ($\text{NaOH}$). The indicator turns faintly pink after exactly $32.4 \text{ mL}$ of the $\text{NaOH}$ solution has been added. What is the molarity of the original $\text{HCl}$ solution?

**Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.**
First, we must identify the reaction and the stoichiometric ratio.

$$ \text{HCl}(aq) + \text{NaOH}(aq) \rightarrow \text{NaCl}(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$

The balanced equation shows a $1:1$ molar ratio between $\text{HCl}$ and $\text{NaOH}$.

**Step 2: Calculate the moles of the standard solution (Titrant).**
Convert the volume of $\text{NaOH}$ to liters, and use its given molarity to find moles.

$$ V_{\text{NaOH}} = 32.4 \text{ mL} = 0.0324 \text{ L} $$

$$ \text{Moles of NaOH} = 0.150 \text{ mol/L} \times 0.0324 \text{ L} = 0.00486 \text{ mol NaOH} $$

**Step 3: Use stoichiometry to find the moles of the analyte.**
Using the $1:1$ ratio from the balanced equation, determine the moles of $\text{HCl}$ that must have been present in the flask.

$$ 0.00486 \text{ mol NaOH} \times \frac{1 \text{ mol HCl}}{1 \text{ mol NaOH}} = 0.00486 \text{ mol HCl} $$

**Step 4: Calculate the molarity of the unknown solution.**
We now know the moles of $\text{HCl}$ and the original volume of the $\text{HCl}$ sample ($25.0 \text{ mL}$, or $0.0250 \text{ L}$). We can divide to find the molarity.

$$ M_{\text{HCl}} = \frac{0.00486 \text{ mol}}{0.0250 \text{ L}} = 0.194 \text{ M} $$

The molarity of the unknown hydrochloric acid solution is **$0.194 \text{ M}$**.

While acid-base neutralizations are the most common titrations in introductory chemistry, this exact same mathematical framework can be applied to precipitation reactions and redox reactions, provided the balanced chemical equation is known.

## Chapter Summary

* **Aqueous Solutions:** Water is an excellent solvent due to its polarity, hydrating ions and polar molecules. Solutes that dissociate into ions conduct electricity and are called **electrolytes** (strong or weak). **Nonelectrolytes** dissolve as neutral molecules.
* **Precipitation Reactions:** These metathesis (exchange) reactions occur when aqueous ions combine to form an insoluble solid, called a **precipitate**. Whether a precipitate forms can be predicted using standard **solubility rules**. These reactions are best represented by **net ionic equations**, which exclude non-participating spectator ions.
* **Acids and Bases:** Arrhenius defined acids as proton ($\text{H}^+$) donors and bases as hydroxide ($\text{OH}^-$) producers. Strong acids and bases dissociate completely, while weak ones establish equilibrium. The reaction between an acid and a base is a **neutralization reaction**, typically producing water and a salt.
* **Redox Reactions:** Oxidation-reduction reactions involve the transfer of electrons. **Oxidation** is the loss of electrons; **reduction** is the gain of electrons. Single displacement reactions between metals and acids/salts can be predicted using the **activity series**, which ranks metals by their ease of oxidation.
* **Solution Concentration:** **Molarity** ($M$) is the standard unit of chemical concentration, defined as moles of solute per liter of solution ($M = n/V$). Solutions can be diluted according to the relationship $M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$.
* **Solution Stoichiometry and Titration:** Molarity acts as the conversion factor between solution volume and moles. This is practically applied in **titrations**, a laboratory technique where a standard solution of known concentration is used to deduce the precise concentration of an unknown analyte through a controlled reaction monitored by an indicator.
