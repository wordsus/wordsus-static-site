Previously, our study focused on the behavior of electrons, where atomic nuclei remain unchanged during reactions. In this chapter, we shift to the heart of the atom: the nucleus. Nuclear chemistry explores how unstable nuclei undergo spontaneous changes, emitting particles and immense energy to achieve stability. We will examine radioactive decay, the rules for balancing nuclear equations, the kinetics of radiometric dating, and the principles powering both fission reactors and the stars. Finally, we will explore the profound ways ionizing radiation interacts with living tissue, balancing its medical applications against its biological hazards.

## 19.1 Radioactivity and Nuclear Equations

Throughout the previous chapters, our exploration of chemical reactivity has focused entirely on the behavior of electrons. In ordinary chemical reactions, atoms share or transfer electrons to achieve stable configurations, but the identities of the atoms themselves remain unchanged. Nuclear chemistry, however, examines changes that occur within the nucleus of the atom.

When a nucleus is unstable, it undergoes a spontaneous change to achieve a more stable state. This process is known as **radioactivity** or radioactive decay, and it involves the emission of particles, electromagnetic radiation, or both. Because these processes alter the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus, radioactive decay often transforms one element into an entirely different element.

### Reviewing Nuclide Notation

To describe nuclear processes, we must accurately represent the composition of the nucleus. Recall from Chapter 2 that a specific nucleus with a defined number of protons and neutrons is called a **nuclide**. Nuclides are represented using the standard notation:

$$_{Z}^{A}\text{X}$$

* **$\text{X}$** is the chemical symbol of the element.
* **$A$** is the **mass number** (the sum of protons and neutrons).
* **$Z$** is the **atomic number** (the number of protons, which defines the element's identity and nuclear charge).

In nuclear equations, this notation is expanded to include subatomic particles. The subscript $Z$ represents the electrical charge of the particle, while the superscript $A$ represents its mass relative to an atomic mass unit (amu).

### Common Particles in Nuclear Reactions

Several particles are routinely encountered in radioactive decay. Understanding their properties and notations is essential for writing and balancing nuclear equations.

| Particle | Representation | Mass Number ($A$) | Charge ($Z$) | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Alpha particle** | $_{2}^{4}\text{He}$ or $\alpha$ | 4 | +2 | A helium nucleus. |
| **Beta particle** | $_{-1}^{0}\text{e}$ or $\beta^{-}$ | 0 | -1 | A high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus. |
| **Positron** | $_{+1}^{0}\text{e}$ or $\beta^{+}$ | 0 | +1 | The antimatter counterpart to the electron. |
| **Proton** | $_{1}^{1}\text{H}$ or $\text{p}$ | 1 | +1 | The nucleus of a hydrogen-1 atom. |
| **Neutron** | $_{0}^{1}\text{n}$ | 1 | 0 | A neutral subatomic particle. |
| **Gamma ray** | $_{0}^{0}\gamma$ | 0 | 0 | High-energy electromagnetic radiation. |

### Types of Radioactive Decay

Unstable nuclei undergo different types of decay depending on their specific neutron-to-proton ratio. Each type of decay alters the nucleus in a distinct way.

#### 1. Alpha ($\alpha$) Decay

Alpha decay involves the emission of an alpha particle, which consists of two protons and two neutrons. Because an alpha particle is identical to a helium-4 nucleus, it is represented as $_{2}^{4}\text{He}$. Alpha emission typically occurs in heavy, unstable nuclei (usually those with $Z > 83$).

For example, the decay of uranium-238 into thorium-234 occurs via alpha emission:

$$_{92}^{238}\text{U} \rightarrow \ _{90}^{234}\text{Th} + \ _{2}^{4}\text{He}$$

Notice that the emission of an alpha particle decreases the mass number of the parent nuclide by 4 and the atomic number by 2.

#### 2. Beta ($\beta$) Decay

Beta decay (specifically, beta-minus decay) occurs when a nucleus has an excess of neutrons. To achieve stability, a neutron within the nucleus is converted into a proton and a high-speed electron, which is immediately ejected. The ejected electron is the beta particle ($_{-1}^{0}\text{e}$).

The decay of carbon-14 is a classic example:

$$_{6}^{14}\text{C} \rightarrow \ _{7}^{14}\text{N} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e}$$

In beta decay, the mass number remains unchanged (since a neutron is swapped for a proton of essentially equal mass), but the atomic number increases by 1, yielding a new element.

#### 3. Positron Emission

Positron emission is the inverse of beta decay and occurs when a nucleus has an excess of protons. A proton is converted into a neutron and a positron ($_{+1}^{0}\text{e}$), which is ejected. A positron has the same mass as an electron but carries a positive charge.

An example is the decay of carbon-11:

$$_{6}^{11}\text{C} \rightarrow \ _{5}^{11}\text{B} + \ _{+1}^{0}\text{e}$$

Here, the mass number is unchanged, but the atomic number decreases by 1.

#### 4. Electron Capture

Electron capture is another mechanism by which a proton-rich nucleus achieves stability. Instead of emitting a positron, the nucleus captures an inner-shell electron from the surrounding electron cloud. The captured electron combines with a proton to form a neutron.

For example, rubidium-81 undergoes electron capture to become krypton-81:

$$_{37}^{81}\text{Rb} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e} \rightarrow \ _{36}^{81}\text{Kr}$$

The net effect on the atomic and mass numbers is identical to positron emission (mass number remains constant, atomic number decreases by 1), but the particle is on the reactant side of the equation.

#### 5. Gamma ($\gamma$) Emission

Gamma rays are high-energy photons ($_{0}^{0}\gamma$). They do not possess mass or charge. Gamma emission generally accompanies other types of radioactive decay. When a nucleus emits an alpha or beta particle, it is often left in an excited, high-energy state. It drops to a lower, more stable energy state by releasing a gamma ray.

Sometimes, a nucleus exists in an excited state for a measurable amount of time before emitting a gamma ray. Such a state is called *metastable* and is denoted by the letter "m" next to the mass number. The diagnostic medical isotope technetium-99m decays primarily by gamma emission:

$$_{43}^{99m}\text{Tc} \rightarrow \ _{43}^{99}\text{Tc} + \ _{0}^{0}\gamma$$

### Balancing Nuclear Equations

Unlike chemical equations, which are balanced by ensuring the same number of atoms of each element exist on both sides of the arrow, nuclear equations are balanced by conserving nuclear particles.

A nuclear equation is balanced when two conditions are met:

1. **Conservation of Mass Number:** The sum of the superscripts (mass numbers, $A$) on the reactant side must equal the sum of the superscripts on the product side.
2. **Conservation of Atomic Number:** The sum of the subscripts (atomic numbers/charges, $Z$) on the reactant side must equal the sum of the subscripts on the product side.

**Example: Identifying an Unknown Product**
Suppose you need to identify the missing nuclide produced when polonium-210 undergoes alpha decay. We start by writing the incomplete equation:

$$_{84}^{210}\text{Po} \rightarrow \ _{Z}^{A}\text{X} + \ _{2}^{4}\text{He}$$

Applying the conservation rules allows us to solve for $A$ and $Z$:

* **Mass number ($A$):** $210 = A + 4 \implies A = 206$
* **Atomic number ($Z$):** $84 = Z + 2 \implies Z = 82$

Looking at the periodic table, the element with atomic number 82 is lead (Pb). Therefore, the unknown product $\text{X}$ is lead-206, and the balanced equation is:

$$_{84}^{210}\text{Po} \rightarrow \ _{82}^{206}\text{Pb} + \ _{2}^{4}\text{He}$$

## 19.2 Patterns of Nuclear Stability

In the previous section, we observed how unstable nuclei undergo radioactive decay to become more stable. But what exactly makes a nucleus stable or unstable in the first place? The answer lies in the delicate balance between the fundamental forces acting within the nucleus and the specific ratio of neutrons to protons.

The nucleus is a tightly packed cluster of protons and neutrons (collectively called nucleons). Protons are positively charged, and because like charges repel, there is a tremendous electrostatic force of repulsion trying to tear the nucleus apart. Counteracting this is the **strong nuclear force**, an incredibly powerful, short-range attractive force that exists between all nucleons (proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron). For a nucleus to be stable, the attractive strong nuclear force must overcome the repulsive electrostatic force.

Neutrons play a crucial role in this balance. Because they possess no electrical charge, they contribute to the strong nuclear force without adding to the electrostatic repulsion. They act as a sort of "nuclear glue" holding the atomic nucleus together.

### The Neutron-to-Proton Ratio ($n/p$) and the Band of Stability

The primary factor determining the stability of a nucleus is its **neutron-to-proton ratio ($n/p$)**. By plotting the number of neutrons ($N$) against the number of protons ($Z$) for all known stable nuclides, a distinct pattern emerges, known as the **band of stability** (or belt of stability).

Analyzing this graph reveals several key trends:

1. **Light Elements ($Z \le 20$):** For elements with low atomic numbers (up to calcium), the stable isotopes generally have a neutron-to-proton ratio very close to 1. For example, carbon-12 ($_{6}^{12}\text{C}$) has 6 protons and 6 neutrons ($n/p = 1.0$), and oxygen-16 ($_{8}^{16}\text{O}$) has 8 protons and 8 neutrons ($n/p = 1.0$).
2. **Intermediate and Heavy Elements ($20 < Z \le 83$):** As the number of protons increases, the electrostatic repulsion grows rapidly. More neutrons are required to buffer this repulsion. Consequently, the band of stability curves upward, deviating from the $N = Z$ line. The stable $n/p$ ratio steadily increases, reaching approximately 1.5 for the heaviest stable elements. For instance, bismuth-209 ($_{83}^{209}\text{Bi}$) has 83 protons and 126 neutrons, giving an $n/p$ ratio of about 1.52.
3. **Maximum Stable Nucleus ($Z = 83$):** There are no completely stable nuclei with an atomic number greater than 83. All isotopes of elements beyond bismuth (like polonium, uranium, and plutonium) are radioactive. The nucleus simply becomes too large for the short-range strong nuclear force to hold it together against the repulsion of so many protons.

### Predicting Modes of Radioactive Decay

The band of stability allows us to predict the likely decay mode of an unstable nucleus. A nuclide will typically undergo the type of decay that brings its $n/p$ ratio closer to the stable band.

* **Above the Band (High $n/p$ Ratio):** Nuclei located above the band have too many neutrons. To achieve stability, a neutron must be converted into a proton. This lowers the $n/p$ ratio. As discussed in Section 19.1, this process is **beta ($\beta$) emission**.

$$\text{Neutron} \rightarrow \text{Proton} + \text{Beta Particle}$$

$$_{0}^{1}\text{n} \rightarrow \ _{1}^{1}\text{p} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e}$$

* **Below the Band (Low $n/p$ Ratio):** Nuclei located below the band have too many protons relative to neutrons. They need to convert a proton into a neutron to increase their $n/p$ ratio. This is accomplished either by **positron emission** or **electron capture**. Both processes have the same net effect on the nucleus.

$$\text{Proton} \rightarrow \text{Neutron} + \text{Positron}$$

$$_{1}^{1}\text{p} \rightarrow \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} + \ _{+1}^{0}\text{e}$$

* **Heavy Nuclei ($Z > 83$):** Elements beyond bismuth are fundamentally too massive. They need to reduce both their proton and neutron counts significantly. They accomplish this primarily through **alpha ($\alpha$) decay**, which removes two protons and two neutrons simultaneously, effectively decreasing the overall mass and shifting the nucleus down toward the band of stability.

### "Magic Numbers" and the Shell Model of the Nucleus

Just as electrons exist in quantized energy levels (shells) outside the nucleus, evidence suggests that protons and neutrons exist in analogous nuclear shells within the nucleus. In atomic structure, the noble gases possess completely filled electron shells (usually 2, 10, 18, 36, 54, or 86 electrons), resulting in exceptional chemical stability.

Similarly, nuclei containing specific numbers of protons or neutrons exhibit exceptional nuclear stability. These are known as **magic numbers**:

* **Magic numbers for protons or neutrons:** 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126

Nuclei that have a magic number of *both* protons and neutrons are called "double magic" and are highly stable. A prime example is lead-208 ($_{82}^{208}\text{Pb}$), which has 82 protons (a magic number) and 126 neutrons (a magic number). It is the heaviest known highly stable isotope and is the endpoint of several natural radioactive decay chains. Helium-4 ($_{2}^{4}\text{He}$, the alpha particle) has 2 protons and 2 neutrons, explaining why it is such a uniquely stable package to be ejected during radioactive decay.

### The Even-Odd Rule

Another fascinating empirical observation regarding nuclear stability relates to whether the number of protons and neutrons is even or odd. Nucleons tend to pair up (proton with proton, neutron with neutron), which lowers the overall energy of the nucleus and increases stability.

| Protons ($Z$) | Neutrons ($N$) | Number of Stable Isotopes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Even | Even | ~ 157 |
| Even | Odd | ~ 53 |
| Odd | Even | ~ 50 |
| Odd | Odd | ~ 5 |

As the table shows, the vast majority of stable isotopes have an even number of both protons and neutrons. Conversely, "odd-odd" nuclei are inherently unstable. Out of all the elements, there are only a handful of stable odd-odd nuclides (such as $_{1}^{2}\text{H}$, $_{3}^{6}\text{Li}$, $_{5}^{10}\text{B}$, and $_{7}^{14}\text{N}$). This stark contrast further reinforces the paired-nucleon model of nuclear stability.

## 19.3 Nuclear Transmutations and Particle Accelerators

The radioactive decay processes described in Sections 19.1 and 19.2 occur spontaneously in nature. However, nuclear reactions can also be induced artificially. By bombarding a nucleus with other nuclei or subatomic particles, we can force a change in the nuclear structure, converting one element into another. This process is called **nuclear transmutation**.

### The First Artificial Transmutation

The first successful artificial transmutation was performed by Ernest Rutherford in 1919. He discovered that bombarding nitrogen gas with alpha particles (emitted by the radioactive decay of radium) produced oxygen and protons. The balanced nuclear equation for this transmutation is:

$$_{7}^{14}\text{N} + \ _{2}^{4}\text{He} \rightarrow \ _{8}^{17}\text{O} + \ _{1}^{1}\text{H}$$

Because nuclear transmutations are heavily utilized in nuclear chemistry and physics, a shorthand notation is often employed to describe them. This notation follows the general format:

$$\text{Target Nucleus (Bombarding Particle, Ejected Particle) Product Nucleus}$$

Using this shorthand, Rutherford’s historical experiment is written as:

$$^{14}\text{N}(\alpha, p)^{17}\text{O}$$

Here, $\alpha$ represents the bombarding alpha particle ($_{2}^{4}\text{He}$) and $p$ represents the ejected proton ($_{1}^{1}\text{H}$).

### The Coulomb Barrier and Neutrons

For a nuclear transmutation to occur between a nucleus and a charged particle (like an alpha particle or a proton), the bombarding particle must physically collide with the target nucleus. However, the target nucleus is positively charged, and so are alpha particles and protons. As the projectile approaches the target, it experiences a massive electrostatic repulsion known as the **Coulomb barrier**.

To overcome this barrier, the bombarding particle must be moving at incredibly high speeds, possessing massive kinetic energy.

In contrast, neutrons are electrically neutral. They do not experience electrostatic repulsion as they approach a nucleus. Consequently, neutrons do not need high kinetic energies to penetrate a nucleus and induce a transmutation. **Neutron capture** is a highly effective way to create heavier isotopes, which often subsequently undergo beta decay to form new elements.

For example, bombarding cobalt-59 with a neutron produces radioactive cobalt-60, an isotope widely used in cancer radiotherapy:

$$_{27}^{59}\text{Co} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} \rightarrow \ _{27}^{60}\text{Co}$$

### Particle Accelerators

Because positively charged particles must overcome the Coulomb barrier, they cannot simply be "thrown" at a target. They must be artificially accelerated to extreme velocities using devices called **particle accelerators** (often referred to colloquially as "atom smashers"). These devices use strong magnetic and electric fields to accelerate charged particles to speeds approaching the speed of light.

#### 1. Linear Accelerators (Linacs)

A linear accelerator utilizes a series of hollow metallic tubes of increasing length arranged in a straight line. The tubes are connected to an alternating high-voltage source.

```text
       [-]      [+]          [-]              [+]
Ion    +-+      +--+         +---+            +----+
Source | |----->|  |-------->|   |----------->|    |---> Target
(+ )-->+-+      +--+         +---+            +----+
      Tube 1   Tube 2       Tube 3           Tube 4
       
       <--------- Alternating AC Voltage --------->

```

As a positively charged particle leaves the ion source, it is attracted to the first tube, which is temporarily negatively charged. Once the particle enters the hollow tube, it experiences no electric field and "drifts" at a constant speed (hence, they are called drift tubes). While it is inside, the alternating voltage flips. As the particle exits the first tube, the first tube is now positive (repelling the particle forward) and the second tube is negative (attracting it).

This push-pull acceleration occurs in the gaps between every tube. Because the particle is moving faster as it progresses, each successive tube must be longer so that the particle spends the exact same amount of time in each one, staying perfectly in sync with the alternating voltage. The Stanford Linear Accelerator Center (SLAC) is a famous example, stretching over 3 kilometers long.

#### 2. Cyclotrons

While Linacs require vast stretches of land, a **cyclotron** achieves particle acceleration in a much smaller footprint by forcing the particles into a spiral path.

A cyclotron consists of two hollow, D-shaped metal electrodes (called "dees") situated between the poles of a powerful electromagnet.

```text
                Top Magnetic Pole (North)
               _______________________
              /           |           \
             /    Dee 1   |   Dee 2    \
            | (-)      ___|___      (+) |
            |         /   |   \         |
            |        / \__|__  \        | <-- Spiral path of 
            |       |     |  |  |       |     accelerating particle
            |        \____|__ /         |
            |             |             |
             \            |            /
              \___________|___________/
                Bottom Magnetic Pole (South)
                
            <--- High-Frequency AC Oscillator --->

```

The charged particles are introduced at the center of the dees. The powerful magnetic field perpendicular to the dees forces the particles to travel in a circular path. Just like in the Linac, an alternating voltage is applied to the dees. Every time the particle crosses the gap between the two dees, the voltage accelerates it. As its velocity increases, the radius of its circular path also increases, creating an expanding spiral. When the particle reaches the outer edge of the cyclotron, it is deflected toward the target at immense speeds.

### Synthesis of Transuranium Elements

Prior to the 1940s, uranium (atomic number 92) was the heaviest known element. Elements with atomic numbers greater than 92 are called **transuranium elements**. None of these elements occur naturally in observable quantities; they must be synthesized artificially through nuclear transmutation.

The first transuranium element, neptunium ($Z=93$), was synthesized in 1940 by bombarding uranium-238 with neutrons. The initial neutron capture creates uranium-239, which is unstable and undergoes beta decay to form neptunium-239:

1. **Neutron capture:**
$$_{92}^{238}\text{U} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} \rightarrow \ _{92}^{239}\text{U}$$

2. **Beta decay:**
$$_{92}^{239}\text{U} \rightarrow \ _{93}^{239}\text{Np} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e}$$

Neptunium-239 is also radioactive and subsequently decays via beta emission to form plutonium-239 ($Z=94$):

$$_{93}^{239}\text{Np} \rightarrow \ _{94}^{239}\text{Pu} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e}$$

To create even heavier transuranium elements, scientists use particle accelerators to bombard heavy targets with positive ions ranging from alpha particles to much larger nuclei. For example, curium ($Z=96$) was first produced by bombarding plutonium-239 with alpha particles:

$$_{94}^{239}\text{Pu} + \ _{2}^{4}\text{He} \rightarrow \ _{96}^{242}\text{Cm} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n}$$

Superheavy elements (those with $Z > 100$) require the bombardment of heavy targets with substantial ion projectiles. For instance, roentgenium ($Z=111$) was synthesized by bombarding bismuth-209 with nickel-64 nuclei:

$$_{83}^{209}\text{Bi} + \ _{28}^{64}\text{Ni} \rightarrow \ _{111}^{272}\text{Rg} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n}$$

Through the continuous advancement of particle accelerator technology, scientists have successfully expanded the periodic table, exploring the theoretical limits of nuclear mass and stability.

## 19.4 Rates of Radioactive Decay and Radiometric Dating

In Chapter 13, we learned that chemical reaction rates are significantly influenced by external factors such as temperature, pressure, concentration, and the presence of catalysts. Radioactive decay, however, is a strictly nuclear process. The rate at which an unstable nuclide decays is completely unaffected by its physical state, its chemical environment (such as the compound it is part of), or external temperature and pressure. The nucleus is insulated from these outside influences by its electron cloud.

Because radioactive decay is a spontaneous, statistically predictable process occurring within individual atoms, it invariably follows **first-order kinetics**.

### The Kinetics of Radioactive Decay

Since radioactive decay is a first-order process, the rate of decay—often called the **activity** of the sample—is directly proportional to the number of radioactive nuclei present at any given time.

$$\text{Rate} = -\frac{\Delta N}{\Delta t} = kN$$

Where:

* $N$ is the number of radioactive nuclei at time $t$.
* $k$ is the **decay constant** (analogous to the rate constant in chemical kinetics), which is specific to a particular nuclide.

Using calculus, this rate law is integrated to yield the **integrated rate law for radioactive decay**, exactly matching the first-order integrated rate law from Chapter 13:

$$\ln\left(\frac{N_t}{N_0}\right) = -kt$$

Because the mass of a pure radioactive sample or its measurable activity is directly proportional to the number of nuclei, this equation can be written in several practical forms:

$$\ln\left(\frac{\text{Mass}_t}{\text{Mass}_0}\right) = -kt \quad \text{or} \quad \ln\left(\frac{\text{Activity}_t}{\text{Activity}_0}\right) = -kt$$

### Half-Life ($t_{1/2}$)

A defining characteristic of first-order kinetics is that the **half-life** ($t_{1/2}$)—the time required for exactly half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay—is constant and independent of the initial amount.

Substituting $N_t = \frac{1}{2}N_0$ into the integrated rate law yields the relationship between half-life and the decay constant:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \approx \frac{0.693}{k}$$

Each radioactive isotope has a unique, unalterable half-life. These can range from fractions of a microsecond (e.g., polonium-212, $t_{1/2} = 3 \times 10^{-7}$ seconds) to billions of years (e.g., uranium-238, $t_{1/2} = 4.5 \times 10^9$ years).

The constant nature of a half-life means that after one half-life, 50% of the original sample remains. After two half-lives, 25% ($\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2}$) remains. After three half-lives, 12.5% remains, and so forth.

### Radiometric Dating

Because the decay rate of a specific isotope is constant and impervious to environmental changes, radioactive isotopes act as internal, natural clocks. By measuring the ratio of the parent isotope (the decaying nuclide) to the daughter isotope (the product of the decay), or by comparing current activity to historical activity, scientists can determine the age of various objects. This technique is known as **radiometric dating**.

#### Radiocarbon Dating

Radiocarbon dating is an immensely valuable technique used by archaeologists and historians to date organic materials (items that were once alive, such as wood, bone, or cloth). It relies on the isotope carbon-14 ($_{6}^{14}\text{C}$).

Carbon-14 is continuously produced in the upper atmosphere when high-energy cosmic rays strike nitrogen atoms:

$$_{7}^{14}\text{N} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} \rightarrow \ _{6}^{14}\text{C} + \ _{1}^{1}\text{H}$$

This newly formed carbon-14 reacts with oxygen to form radioactive carbon dioxide ($^{14}\text{CO}_2$), which mixes uniformly with stable $^{12}\text{CO}_2$ in the atmosphere. Plants absorb this $\text{CO}_2$ during photosynthesis, and animals consume the plants. Consequently, all living organisms maintain a constant ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12 that matches the atmospheric ratio.

However, when an organism dies, it stops ingesting new carbon. The stable carbon-12 remains intact, but the carbon-14 begins to steadily decay back into nitrogen-14 via beta emission:

$$_{6}^{14}\text{C} \rightarrow \ _{7}^{14}\text{N} + \ _{-1}^{0}\text{e}$$

The half-life of carbon-14 is **5,715 years**. By measuring the remaining carbon-14 activity in a dead organic sample and comparing it to the activity of living tissue today, we can determine $t$ using the integrated rate law.

*Limitation:* Radiocarbon dating is highly accurate but is limited to objects that are roughly 50,000 years old or younger. Beyond 50,000 years (nearly 9 half-lives), the remaining carbon-14 becomes too miniscule to measure accurately.

#### Dating Older Geological Materials

To determine the age of much older objects, such as dinosaur fossils, meteorites, or the Earth itself, scientists must use isotopes with much longer half-lives.

One of the most important methods is **uranium-lead dating**. Uranium-238 undergoes a long series of alpha and beta decays, eventually culminating in stable lead-206. The overall half-life for this complex series is driven by the very slow initial decay of $^{238}\text{U}$, which is $4.5 \times 10^9$ years.

By measuring the ratio of $^{238}\text{U}$ to $^{206}\text{Pb}$ trapped inside an ancient rock crystal (such as zircon), geologists can calculate the time elapsed since the rock solidified from magma. If a rock contains equal masses of $^{238}\text{U}$ and $^{206}\text{Pb}$ (accounting for molar mass differences), it indicates that exactly one half-life has passed, meaning the rock is 4.5 billion years old.

## 19.5 Detection and Measurement of Radioactivity

Because human senses cannot detect radioactivity—it is invisible, odorless, and silent—we must rely on specialized instruments to identify its presence and measure its intensity. These detection methods universally rely on the fundamental way radiation interacts with matter: by exciting or ionizing the atoms and molecules it strikes.

### Instruments for Detecting Radiation

When high-energy particles or photons pass through matter, they knock electrons out of their atomic orbitals, creating ion pairs. Detection devices harness this ionizing ability to generate measurable signals.

#### 1. The Geiger-Müller Counter

The Geiger-Müller counter, often simply called a Geiger counter, is the most common instrument for detecting radioactive emissions, particularly beta particles and gamma rays.

It consists of a metal tube filled with an inert gas, typically argon, at a low pressure. A thin wire runs down the center of the tube. A high voltage is applied across the device, making the central wire the anode (positive) and the metal tube the cathode (negative).

```text
       Ionizing Radiation
             |
             V
         [Thin Window]
       +---------------+
       |      (Ar)     | <--- Argon gas-filled metal tube (Cathode, -)
       |               |
       |   +-------+   |
       |   | Anode |   | <--- Central wire (Positive, +)
       |   |  (+)  |   |
       |   +-------+   |
       |  (Ar+)   (e-) | <--- Radiation ionizes Argon into Ar+ and e-
       |               |
       +-------+-------+
               |
      [Amplifier & Counter]

```

When ionizing radiation enters the tube through a thin window, it strikes an argon atom, knocking an electron loose:

$$\text{Ar} \rightarrow \text{Ar}^+ + \text{e}^-$$

The freed electron is strongly attracted to the positively charged central wire. As it accelerates toward the anode, it collides with other argon atoms, ionizing them and releasing more electrons. This creates a "Townsend avalanche"—a cascade of electrons hitting the wire simultaneously, producing a brief electrical pulse. The device counts these pulses, translating them into an audible click or a meter reading.

#### 2. Scintillation Counters

Some substances, known as phosphors, emit flashes of light (scintillations) when struck by ionizing radiation. A scintillation counter uses a phosphor material, such as a sodium iodide (NaI) crystal, coupled with a highly sensitive photoelectric device called a photomultiplier tube.

When radiation strikes the phosphor, it emits photons of light. The photomultiplier tube detects this light, converts it into an electrical current, and amplifies it. Scintillation counters are highly effective for detecting gamma rays, which often pass straight through the low-density gas of a Geiger counter without causing ionization.

#### 3. Radiographic Film and Dosimeters

Radiation was initially discovered by Henri Becquerel because it darkened photographic film. This principle is still used today for personal radiation monitoring. People who work with radiation, such as X-ray technicians and nuclear plant workers, wear film badges or dosimeters.

A traditional film badge contains a small piece of photographic film shielded by light-proof paper. The degree to which the film darkens over a specific period is proportional to the amount of radiation exposure. Modern dosimeters often use thermoluminescent materials (like lithium fluoride) that trap excited electrons. When the dosimeter is later heated, it emits light proportional to the radiation absorbed, providing an accurate record of cumulative exposure.

### Measuring Radioactivity and Radiation Dose

Measuring radioactivity is complex because we must distinguish between the *activity* of the source (how fast it is decaying) and the *dose* of radiation absorbed by a target (how much energy is deposited and the resulting biological impact). Different units are used for each of these concepts.

#### 1. Activity: Rate of Decay

Activity measures the number of nuclear disintegrations occurring per unit of time in a radioactive sample. It tells us how "active" the source is, regardless of the type of radiation it emits.

* **Becquerel (Bq):** The SI unit of activity, defined as one disintegration per second (dps). Because a typical radioactive sample undergoes billions of disintegrations per second, the Becquerel is a very small unit.
* **Curie (Ci):** A historical, but still widely used, unit of activity. It was originally defined as the decay rate of 1 gram of radium-226. One curie is equivalent to exactly $3.7 \times 10^{10}$ disintegrations per second.

$$1 \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}$$

#### 2. Absorbed Dose: Energy Deposited

When radiation strikes biological tissue, it transfers energy. The absorbed dose measures the amount of energy deposited per kilogram of absorbing material.

* **Gray (Gy):** The SI unit of absorbed dose, defined as the absorption of 1 Joule of energy per kilogram of tissue (1 J/kg).
* **Rad (Radiation Absorbed Dose):** A traditional unit of absorbed dose. One gray is equivalent to 100 rads.

$$1 \text{ Gy} = 100 \text{ rad}$$

#### 3. Equivalent Dose: Biological Damage

Not all radiation causes the same amount of biological damage. For example, a 1-rad dose of alpha particles causes roughly 20 times more tissue damage than a 1-rad dose of gamma rays. Alpha particles are massive and highly charged, causing intense, concentrated ionization along their short path, whereas gamma rays spread their energy over a much larger volume.

To account for this, the absorbed dose is multiplied by a **Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE)** factor (also known as a quality factor, Q). The RBE for beta and gamma radiation is approximately 1, while the RBE for alpha radiation is approximately 20.

Multiplying the absorbed dose by the RBE yields the equivalent dose, which provides a true measure of the biological impact.

* **Sievert (Sv):** The SI unit of equivalent dose.

$$\text{Equivalent Dose (Sv)} = \text{Absorbed Dose (Gy)} \times \text{RBE}$$

* **Rem (Roentgen Equivalent for Man):** The traditional unit of equivalent dose.

$$\text{Equivalent Dose (rem)} = \text{Absorbed Dose (rad)} \times \text{RBE}$$

$$1 \text{ Sv} = 100 \text{ rem}$$

**Summary Table of Radiation Units**

| Measurement | Concept | SI Unit | Traditional Unit | Conversion |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Activity** | Rate of nuclear decay | Becquerel (Bq) | Curie (Ci) | 1 Ci = $3.7 \times 10^{10}$ Bq |
| **Absorbed Dose** | Energy deposited in tissue | Gray (Gy) | Rad | 1 Gy = 100 rad |
| **Equivalent Dose** | Biological impact of radiation | Sievert (Sv) | Rem | 1 Sv = 100 rem |

## 19.6 Energy Changes in Nuclear Reactions ($E=mc^2$)

In Chapter 12, we explored thermochemistry and the energy changes associated with ordinary chemical reactions. In chemical reactions, energy is absorbed or released as chemical bonds are broken and formed. The enthalpies of reaction ($\Delta H$) for these processes are typically on the order of hundreds or thousands of kilojoules per mole ($\text{kJ/mol}$).

Nuclear reactions also involve changes in energy, but the magnitude is vastly different. The energy released in a nuclear reaction is typically millions of times greater than the energy released in a standard chemical reaction. This staggering difference arises because nuclear reactions involve actual, measurable changes in mass.

### Mass-Energy Equivalence

In chemical reactions, we rely on the law of conservation of mass: the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products. The mass change associated with the breaking or forming of chemical bonds is so infinitesimally small that it is undetectable.

In nuclear reactions, however, the mass of the products is measurably less than the mass of the reactants. This "missing" mass is converted entirely into energy. The relationship between mass and energy was elegantly defined by Albert Einstein in 1905 with his famous mass-energy equivalence equation:

$$E = mc^2$$

For a physical or chemical change where there is a change in mass ($\Delta m$), the change in energy ($\Delta E$) is expressed as:

$$\Delta E = (\Delta m)c^2$$

* **$\Delta E$** is the change in energy, measured in Joules ($\text{J}$). Note that $1 \text{ J} = 1 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2$.
* **$\Delta m$** is the change in mass (mass of products minus mass of reactants), measured in kilograms ($\text{kg}$).
* **$c$** is the speed of light in a vacuum, approximately $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.

Because the speed of light squared ($c^2$) is such an astronomically large number ($9.00 \times 10^{16} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2$), even a microscopic change in mass results in a colossal release of energy.

### Mass Defect

If we carefully measure the mass of a specific nucleus and compare it to the combined masses of its individual protons and neutrons, we discover a fundamental discrepancy: **an atomic nucleus always weighs slightly less than the sum of its constituent nucleons.**

This difference in mass is called the **mass defect ($\Delta m$)**.

Let us calculate the mass defect for a helium-4 nucleus ($_{2}^{4}\text{He}$, an alpha particle). We must use highly precise atomic masses, typically expressed in atomic mass units ($\text{amu}$):

* Mass of one proton ($_{1}^{1}\text{p}$): $1.00728 \text{ amu}$
* Mass of one neutron ($_{0}^{1}\text{n}$): $1.00866 \text{ amu}$

A helium-4 nucleus contains 2 protons and 2 neutrons. The *calculated* mass of these separated particles is:

$$\text{Calculated Mass} = 2(1.00728 \text{ amu}) + 2(1.00866 \text{ amu}) = 4.03188 \text{ amu}$$

However, precise mass spectrometry shows that the *actual* mass of a helium-4 nucleus is $4.00150 \text{ amu}$.

The mass defect is the difference between the separated nucleons and the intact nucleus:

$$\Delta m = (\text{Mass of separated nucleons}) - (\text{Actual mass of nucleus})$$

$$\Delta m = 4.03188 \text{ amu} - 4.00150 \text{ amu} = 0.03038 \text{ amu}$$

When 2 protons and 2 neutrons bind together to form a helium-4 nucleus, $0.03038 \text{ amu}$ of mass simply disappears. Where does it go? It is converted into the energy that holds the nucleus together.

### Nuclear Binding Energy

The energy equivalent of the mass defect is called the **nuclear binding energy**. It represents the energy required to break a nucleus apart into its individual nucleons. Conversely, it is the exact amount of energy released when those nucleons come together to form the nucleus.

We can calculate the binding energy for a single helium-4 nucleus by substituting its mass defect into Einstein’s equation. First, we must convert the mass defect from $\text{amu}$ to kilograms using the conversion factor $1 \text{ amu} = 1.66054 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$:

$$\Delta m \text{ (in kg)} = (0.03038 \text{ amu}) \times \left( \frac{1.66054 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}}{1 \text{ amu}} \right) = 5.0447 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}$$

Now, apply $\Delta E = (\Delta m)c^2$:

$$\Delta E = (5.0447 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2$$

$$\Delta E = 4.54 \times 10^{-12} \text{ Joules / nucleus}$$

While $10^{-12}$ Joules seems small, this is the energy released for a *single nucleus*. If we scale this up to a macroscopic amount—just one mole of helium-4 ($4.00 \text{ grams}$)—we multiply by Avogadro's number ($6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ nuclei/mol}$):

$$\Delta E \text{ per mole} = (4.54 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J}) \times (6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}) = 2.73 \times 10^{12} \text{ J/mol} = 2.73 \times 10^{9} \text{ kJ/mol}$$

For perspective, the combustion of one mole of methane gas yields about $890 \text{ kJ}$. The formation of one mole of helium from its nucleons releases roughly *three million times* more energy than burning a mole of methane.

#### The MeV Shortcut

In nuclear physics and chemistry, energies are often expressed in electron-volts ($\text{eV}$) or mega-electron-volts ($\text{MeV}$) rather than Joules. A highly useful conversion factor directly relates atomic mass units to mega-electron-volts:

$$1 \text{ amu} = 931.5 \text{ MeV}$$

Using this shortcut, we can quickly find the binding energy of helium-4 without converting to kilograms and Joules:

$$\text{Binding Energy} = (0.03038 \text{ amu}) \times \left( \frac{931.5 \text{ MeV}}{1 \text{ amu}} \right) = 28.3 \text{ MeV}$$

### Binding Energy per Nucleon and Nuclear Stability

While total binding energy tells us how tightly a nucleus is held together overall, it is not a perfect metric for comparing the stability of different elements. Naturally, a massive nucleus like uranium-238 has a higher total binding energy than helium-4 simply because it contains many more nucleons interacting via the strong nuclear force.

To properly compare the stability of different nuclei, we must calculate the **binding energy per nucleon**. This is done by dividing the total binding energy of a nucleus by its mass number ($A$).

$$\text{Binding Energy per Nucleon} = \frac{\text{Total Binding Energy}}{\text{Number of Nucleons } (A)}$$

For helium-4 ($A=4$):

$$\text{Binding Energy per Nucleon} = \frac{28.3 \text{ MeV}}{4} = 7.08 \text{ MeV/nucleon}$$

When we plot the binding energy per nucleon for all stable isotopes against their mass number, a defining curve emerges that dictates the energy dynamics of the entire universe.

2. **Nuclear Fission:** For elements heavier than iron (falling on the right side of the curve), the binding energy per nucleon slowly decreases. ## 19.7 Nuclear Fission and Nuclear Reactors

As established by the binding energy curve in Section 19.6, the most stable atomic nuclei are those with intermediate mass numbers, peaking around iron-56. Very heavy nuclei, such as isotopes of uranium and plutonium, have significantly lower binding energies per nucleon. If such a massive nucleus can be induced to split into two smaller, intermediate-mass fragments, the resulting products will be more stable. This splitting process is called **nuclear fission**, and it is accompanied by an enormous release of energy.

### The Mechanism of Nuclear Fission

While spontaneous fission occurs in nature for a few very heavy synthetic elements, practical nuclear fission is an induced process. It is typically initiated by bombarding a heavy nucleus with a slow-moving neutron (often called a thermal neutron).

The most historically and technologically significant fissionable isotope is uranium-235 ($_{92}^{235}\text{U}$). When a U-235 nucleus absorbs a thermal neutron, it briefly becomes uranium-236. This intermediate state is highly unstable and violently oscillates, stretching like a droplet of liquid until electrostatic repulsion overcomes the strong nuclear force, snapping the nucleus into two distinct fragments.

A typical fission reaction for U-235 is:

$$_{92}^{235}\text{U} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} \rightarrow \left[_{92}^{236}\text{U}\right] \rightarrow \ _{56}^{141}\text{Ba} + \ _{36}^{92}\text{Kr} + 3\ _{0}^{1}\text{n} + \text{Energy}$$

It is important to note that a nucleus does not split exactly the same way every time. The fission of U-235 can produce hundreds of different pairs of product isotopes (such as strontium-90 and xenon-143, or rubidium-90 and cesium-144). However, the overall characteristics of the process remain consistent:

1. **Mass Defect:** The combined mass of the product nuclei and neutrons is strictly less than the mass of the starting U-235 and neutron. This mass defect is converted into kinetic energy and gamma radiation, releasing approximately $200 \text{ MeV}$ per fission event.
2. **Neutron Generation:** Fission produces more neutrons than it consumes. While one neutron is required to initiate the split, typically two or three neutrons are ejected in the process.

### Chain Reactions and Critical Mass

The fact that nuclear fission produces multiple neutrons is the key to harnessing its power. The neutrons released from one fission event can strike neighboring U-235 nuclei, causing them to undergo fission. They, in turn, release more neutrons, which trigger even more fissions. This self-sustaining, geometrically multiplying process is known as a **nuclear chain reaction**.

```text
       Fission Generation 1          Generation 2               Generation 3
                                     
                                  +-- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...
                                  |
              +-- (n) ---> [U-235]--- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...
              |                   |
              |                   +-- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...
(n) ---> [U-235] 
              |                   +-- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...
              |                   |
              +-- (n) ---> [U-235]--- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...
                                  |
                                  +-- (n) ---> [U-235] ---> (n) ...

```

Whether a chain reaction flourishes or dies out depends on the mass and geometry of the fissionable material. Neutrons can escape from the surface of the uranium sample before striking another nucleus.

* **Subcritical Mass:** If the sample is too small, a high percentage of neutrons escape into the surroundings. The chain reaction cannot sustain itself and rapidly dies out.
* **Critical Mass:** This is the exact minimum mass of fissionable material required to sustain a constant chain reaction. At this threshold, exactly one neutron from each fission event triggers exactly one new fission event. The reaction rate remains steady.
* **Supercritical Mass:** If the sample is larger than the critical mass, the rate of neutron loss is minimized. Multiple neutrons from each fission trigger new fissions. The reaction rate increases exponentially, leading to a massive, instantaneous release of energy—the principle behind an atomic bomb.

### Nuclear Reactors

A **nuclear reactor** is an engineering device designed to initiate, sustain, and strictly control a nuclear chain reaction at exactly the critical state, safely extracting the heat generated to produce electricity. The most common type of reactor worldwide is the **Light Water Reactor (LWR)**.

To convert the violent potential of uranium into a steady power source, a reactor utilizes several vital components:

#### 1. The Fuel

Naturally occurring uranium is overwhelmingly U-238 ($99.27\%$), which does not readily undergo fission. The fissionable isotope, U-235, makes up only $0.72\%$. For a light water reactor to operate, the fuel must be **enriched**—processed to increase the concentration of U-235 to roughly $3\%$ to $5\%$. The enriched uranium is usually formed into ceramic pellets of uranium dioxide ($\text{UO}_2$) and stacked inside long zirconium alloy tubes called fuel rods.

#### 2. The Moderator

The neutrons ejected during fission are moving far too quickly to be efficiently captured by other U-235 nuclei. To sustain the chain reaction, these fast neutrons must be slowed down to become "thermal neutrons." This is the job of the **moderator**. In a light water reactor, ordinary water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) surrounds the fuel rods. As fast neutrons collide with the hydrogen atoms in the water, they transfer their kinetic energy and slow down.

#### 3. Control Rods

To keep the reaction strictly at critical mass (and prevent it from becoming supercritical), operators must have a way to control the neutron population. **Control rods** are made of materials that readily absorb neutrons without undergoing fission themselves, such as cadmium, boron, or hafnium.
By raising or lowering the control rods into the reactor core between the fuel rods, operators regulate the reaction rate. Dropping the rods completely into the core absorbs enough neutrons to instantly shut down the chain reaction (a procedure known as a "scram").

#### 4. The Coolant

The immense thermal energy released by fission must be continuously removed from the core. In light water reactors, the water acting as the moderator also acts as the primary **coolant**. The water absorbs the heat and carries it away to a heat exchanger (steam generator).

#### 5. Containment Structure

Because the reactor core contains highly radioactive materials and operates under immense pressure, it is housed in a massive containment structure built of thick steel and high-density reinforced concrete. This structure is designed to prevent the release of radioactive gases or liquids into the environment in the event of an accident.

### Power Generation

The actual generation of electricity in a nuclear plant is thermodynamically similar to a coal or natural gas plant. The only difference is the heat source.

```text
           Nuclear Reactor Core (Heat Source)
           ==================================
           |                                |
           |  [Control Rods]                |
           |      ||||                      |
           |    +------+   Hot Water        |    Steam Generator
           |    | Fuel | ---------------------> +---------------+  Steam     [ Turbine & ]
           |    | Rods |                    |   |  Water boils  | ---------> [ Generator ]
           |    +------+ <--------------------- |  into steam   |            [===========]
           |                 Cold Water     |   +---------------+                  |
           |  (Water Moderator & Coolant)   |           ^                          V
           ==================================           |                 (Cooling Tower /
                    CONTAINMENT BLDG                    v                   Condenser)
                                                  Cold Water Return

```

In a **Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR)**, the primary coolant water flowing through the core is kept under incredibly high pressure (over $150 \text{ atm}$) so it does not boil, even at $300^\circ\text{C}$. This superheated water passes through pipes inside a separate steam generator. The heat transfers to a secondary water loop operating at lower pressure, causing that secondary water to boil into steam. The high-pressure steam spins a massive turbine connected to an electrical generator, producing electricity.

## 19.8 Nuclear Fusion

As established by the binding energy curve in Section 19.6, the quest for nuclear stability takes two different paths depending on the mass of the starting nucleus. While heavy nuclei achieve stability by splitting apart (fission), very light nuclei can achieve an even more dramatic increase in stability by combining together. This process, where two light atomic nuclei merge to form a single, heavier nucleus, is called **nuclear fusion**.

Because the binding energy per nucleon increases so steeply for the lightest elements, nuclear fusion reactions release vast amounts of energy. In fact, gram for gram, the fusion of light isotopes (like hydrogen) yields substantially more energy than the fission of heavy isotopes (like uranium).

### The Power of the Stars

Nuclear fusion is the fundamental energy source of the universe. It is the process that powers the sun and all other active stars. In the core of a star, immense gravitational pressure and unimaginable temperatures create the perfect environment for light nuclei to overcome their natural repulsion and fuse.

For a star like our sun, which is composed primarily of hydrogen, the dominant fusion process is the **proton-proton chain**. This complex, multi-step reaction sequence ultimately converts four hydrogen-1 nuclei (protons) into a single helium-4 nucleus.

1. **Step 1:** Two protons fuse to form deuterium (hydrogen-2), releasing a positron and a neutrino ($\nu$).

$$_{1}^{1}\text{H} + \ _{1}^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow \ _{1}^{2}\text{H} + \ _{+1}^{0}\text{e} + \nu$$

1. **Step 2:** The newly formed deuterium nucleus fuses with another proton to form helium-3, releasing a gamma ray.

$$_{1}^{2}\text{H} + \ _{1}^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow \ _{2}^{3}\text{He} + \ _{0}^{0}\gamma$$

1. **Step 3:** Two helium-3 nuclei (produced by two separate instances of Steps 1 and 2) fuse to form a highly stable helium-4 nucleus, ejecting two protons in the process.

$$_{2}^{3}\text{He} + \ _{2}^{3}\text{He} \rightarrow \ _{2}^{4}\text{He} + 2\ _{1}^{1}\text{H}$$

The net overall reaction for the proton-proton chain reveals the profound mass-to-energy conversion taking place:

$$4\ _{1}^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow \ _{2}^{4}\text{He} + 2\ _{+1}^{0}\text{e} + \text{Energy}$$

Every second, our sun converts approximately 600 million metric tons of hydrogen into helium, converting about 4 million tons of actual mass directly into energy (via $E=mc^2$) to bathe the solar system in light and heat.

### The Coulomb Barrier and Thermonuclear Reactions

If fusion releases so much energy, why do we not see it happening naturally on Earth? The answer lies in the **Coulomb barrier**.

To fuse, two nuclei must come close enough for the incredibly strong, but very short-range, strong nuclear force to bind them together. However, all nuclei are positively charged. As they approach one another, they experience an overwhelming electrostatic repulsion. To overcome this repulsion, the nuclei must collide at extreme velocities.

In physics, the kinetic energy of atoms and molecules is directly proportional to temperature. To achieve the speeds necessary to breach the Coulomb barrier, the fuel must be heated to tens of millions of degrees. Because fusion requires these extraordinary temperatures to initiate, these processes are widely referred to as **thermonuclear reactions**.

At such extreme temperatures, atoms cannot exist in their normal state. The thermal energy is so high that electrons are completely stripped from their nuclei. The resulting state of matter—a superheated, electrically conductive cloud of rapidly moving bare nuclei and free electrons—is called a **plasma**.

### Controlled Nuclear Fusion on Earth

For decades, scientists have sought to harness nuclear fusion as a practical, controllable power source on Earth. The theoretical advantages of a fusion power plant are monumental:

* **Abundant Fuel:** The most promising terrestrial fusion reactions use deuterium, an isotope of hydrogen that can be readily extracted from ordinary seawater.
* **No High-Level Radioactive Waste:** Unlike fission, the primary product of hydrogen fusion is stable, non-toxic helium. It does not produce the long-lived, highly dangerous radioactive waste associated with spent uranium fuel rods.
* **Inherent Safety:** A fusion reactor contains very little fuel at any given instant. If a malfunction occurs, the plasma instantly cools and the fusion reaction simply stops. A runaway meltdown is physically impossible.

The reaction most aggressively pursued for power generation is the **Deuterium-Tritium (D-T) reaction**:

$$_{1}^{2}\text{H} + \ _{1}^{3}\text{H} \rightarrow \ _{2}^{4}\text{He} + \ _{0}^{1}\text{n} + \text{Energy (17.6 MeV)}$$

```text
       Deuterium (H-2)              Tritium (H-3)
      [1 proton, 1 neutron]     [1 proton, 2 neutrons]
                \                     /
                 \                   /
                  \____ Fusion _____/
                        |      |
                  Helium-4     Neutron
                 (Alpha)       (Fast)
                                 |
                          Energy (Heat)

```

#### The Challenge of Confinement

While achieving the roughly 100 million °C required for the D-T reaction is possible, the primary engineering hurdle is **confinement**. No physical material can contain a plasma at 100 million degrees; any physical container would instantly vaporize, cooling the plasma and killing the reaction.

Researchers are currently exploring two primary methods to contain the plasma without touching it:

1. **Magnetic Confinement:** Because a plasma consists of charged particles (ions and electrons), it can be manipulated by magnetic fields. Devices called **tokamaks** use massive, supercooled superconducting electromagnets to generate a powerful, donut-shaped (toroidal) magnetic field. This magnetic "bottle" suspends the plasma in a vacuum chamber, keeping it perfectly isolated from the physical walls of the reactor. The ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) project in France is currently the world's largest magnetic confinement experiment.
2. **Inertial Confinement:** This method relies on speed and precision. A tiny, spherical pellet of frozen deuterium and tritium is placed in a target chamber. Suddenly, it is blasted simultaneously from all sides by the world's most powerful lasers. The outer layer of the pellet vaporizes instantly, creating an inward shockwave that compresses the core to incredibly high densities and temperatures for a fraction of a nanosecond—just long enough for fusion to occur before the pellet blows apart. The National Ignition Facility (NIF) in the United States utilizes this approach.

## 19.9 Biological Effects of Radiation

The interaction of radiation with living tissue is a matter of profound importance in both medical and environmental chemistry. To understand why radiation is potentially harmful, we must compare the energy carried by nuclear emissions to the energies of typical chemical bonds.

Ordinary covalent bonds in biological molecules (such as C–C, C–H, or O–H bonds) have bond enthalpies ranging from roughly 300 to 500 kJ/mol, which equates to about 3 to 5 electron-volts (eV) per bond. In stark contrast, alpha particles, beta particles, and gamma rays carry energies measured in *millions* of electron-volts (MeV). A single nuclear emission possesses more than enough kinetic energy to completely shatter millions of chemical bonds along its path.

### Ionizing Radiation and Free Radicals

Because the energies of nuclear emissions are so vast, they are classified as **ionizing radiation**. When these particles or photons strike biological tissue, they strip electrons away from atoms and molecules, leaving behind highly reactive ions.

Because living tissue is composed of approximately 70% water, the most common and consequential interaction occurs when radiation strikes a water molecule, ejecting an electron:

$$\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{radiation}} \text{H}_2\text{O}^+ + \text{e}^-$$

The resulting water cation ($\text{H}_2\text{O}^+$) is extraordinarily unstable and quickly reacts with a neighboring, neutral water molecule:

$$\text{H}_2\text{O}^+ + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \cdot\text{OH}$$

This reaction produces a hydronium ion ($\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$) and a **hydroxyl radical** ($\cdot\text{OH}$). The dot denotes an unpaired electron. Molecules with unpaired electrons, known as free radicals, are violently reactive. To achieve a stable octet, the hydroxyl radical will ruthlessly tear an electron away from any nearby molecule.

If this nearby molecule is a critical biomolecule—such as an enzyme, a lipid in a cell membrane, or a strand of DNA—the biomolecule is damaged or destroyed. If DNA is damaged and the cell's repair mechanisms fail to fix it properly, the cell may die, or it may mutate and begin replicating uncontrollably, leading to cancer.

### Penetrating Power and Exposure Routes

The biological hazard posed by a specific type of radiation depends heavily on its penetrating power. Because different emissions have vastly different masses and charges, they interact with matter at different rates.

```text
       Penetrating Power of Common Nuclear Emissions

Radiation       Paper      Plastic/Skin      Lead        Concrete
---------       -----      ------------      ----        --------
Alpha (α)   ->   [|] 
                 [|] (Stopped by paper)

Beta (β)    --------->          [|]
                                [|] (Stopped by plastic/skin)

Gamma (γ)   ---------------------------------> [|]
                                               [|] (Stopped by thick lead)

Neutron (n) -----------------------------------------------------> [|]
                                                           (Stopped by concrete/water)

```

1. **Alpha Particles ($\alpha$):** Alpha particles are massive and carry a +2 charge. Because of their high charge, they interact violently with matter, transferring all their energy in a very short distance. Therefore, they have very low penetrating power. External alpha radiation is stopped by a piece of paper or the dead outer layer of human skin; it poses almost no external threat. However, if an alpha emitter is ingested or inhaled (internal exposure), it is the most dangerous form of radiation, depositing massive amounts of destructive energy directly into localized living tissue.
2. **Beta Particles ($\beta$):** Beta particles are much smaller and carry a -1 charge. They can penetrate a few millimeters into living tissue, potentially causing severe "radiation burns" to the skin and damaging eyes. They can be shielded by heavy clothing, thick plastic, or aluminum. Like alpha emitters, they are highly dangerous if ingested.
3. **Gamma Rays ($\gamma$):** Gamma rays are massless, uncharged, high-energy photons. They interact weakly with matter, giving them immense penetrating power. Gamma rays easily pass entirely through the human body, ionizing molecules along their path. They pose a severe *external* hazard and require thick layers of dense material, such as lead or concrete, for adequate shielding.

### Somatic vs. Genetic Effects

The biological effects of radiation are broadly categorized by the type of cells affected:

* **Somatic Effects:** These are effects observed directly in the irradiated organism. They result from damage to somatic (body) cells. Acute somatic effects, caused by massive doses of radiation in a short time, include radiation sickness (nausea, fatigue, hair loss, destruction of white blood cells) and death. Chronic somatic effects, caused by low-level exposure over years, primarily manifest as an increased risk of cancer (such as leukemia or thyroid cancer).
* **Genetic Effects:** These occur when radiation damages the reproductive cells (sperm or eggs). While the irradiated individual may not suffer direct harm from this specific cellular damage, the mutated DNA is passed on to offspring, potentially causing birth defects, genetic diseases, or increased cancer susceptibility in future generations.

### Background Radiation

It is impossible to avoid radiation entirely. Life on Earth evolved in a faintly radioactive environment. This constant, low-level exposure is known as **background radiation**.

Natural sources account for roughly 80% of background radiation. The largest single contributor is **radon gas** (specifically radon-222), a heavy, radioactive noble gas that naturally seeps from uranium-containing soils and rocks into basements and homes. Other natural sources include cosmic rays from space and naturally occurring radioactive isotopes within our own bodies, such as potassium-40 and carbon-14.

The remaining 20% of background radiation comes from artificial sources, predominantly medical procedures like X-rays, CT scans, and diagnostic nuclear medicine. Minor contributions come from consumer products, the nuclear power industry, and fallout from historical atmospheric weapons testing.

Understanding the biological effects of radiation allows scientists and medical professionals to maximize its immense benefits—such as cancer radiotherapy, medical imaging, and clean power generation—while rigidly minimizing the inherent risks to human health.

## Chapter Summary

**Chapter 19: Nuclear Chemistry**

* **19.1 Radioactivity and Nuclear Equations:** Nuclear chemistry focuses on changes within the atomic nucleus. Unstable nuclides undergo spontaneous radioactive decay by emitting alpha particles ($_{2}^{4}\text{He}$), beta particles ($_{-1}^{0}\text{e}$), positrons ($_{+1}^{0}\text{e}$), or gamma rays ($_{0}^{0}\gamma$). Nuclear equations are balanced by conserving both mass number and atomic number.
* **19.2 Patterns of Nuclear Stability:** The stability of a nucleus depends heavily on the ratio of neutrons to protons ($n/p$). Nuclei outside the "band of stability" undergo specific types of decay to adjust this ratio. Magic numbers and the pairing of nucleons (even-even vs. odd-odd) also dictate stability. Elements with $Z > 83$ are inherently unstable.
* **19.3 Nuclear Transmutations and Particle Accelerators:** One element can be artificially converted into another by bombarding its nucleus with other particles. Neutral neutrons easily penetrate nuclei, but positively charged projectiles require particle accelerators (linacs or cyclotrons) to overcome the tremendous electrostatic Coulomb barrier. This is how transuranium elements are synthesized.
* **19.4 Rates of Radioactive Decay and Radiometric Dating:** Radioactive decay follows first-order kinetics with a constant half-life ($t_{1/2}$), independent of temperature or chemical environment. By measuring the ratio of parent to daughter isotopes, techniques like radiocarbon dating ($^{14}\text{C}$) and uranium-lead dating can determine the age of organic and geological artifacts.
* **19.5 Detection and Measurement of Radioactivity:** Radiation is detected by its ionizing ability using devices like Geiger-Müller counters and scintillation counters. Activity is measured in Becquerels (Bq) or Curies (Ci). The biological impact, or equivalent dose, is measured in Sieverts (Sv) or rems, which factors in both the absorbed energy (Gray/rad) and the relative biological effectiveness (RBE) of the specific radiation type.
* **19.6 Energy Changes in Nuclear Reactions ($E=mc^2$):** Nuclear reactions involve a measurable loss of mass (mass defect). This missing mass is converted into the massive nuclear binding energy that holds the nucleus together. Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon, making it the most stable nuclide.
* **19.7 Nuclear Fission and Nuclear Reactors:** Fission is the splitting of a heavy, unstable nucleus (like U-235) into more stable intermediate fragments, releasing neutrons and massive energy. This can trigger a self-sustaining chain reaction. Nuclear reactors carefully control this critical mass reaction using moderators and control rods to generate commercial electricity.
* **19.8 Nuclear Fusion:** Fusion is the combining of light nuclei to form a heavier, more stable nucleus, releasing even more energy per gram than fission. It is the process that powers the stars. While fusion produces no long-lived radioactive waste, achieving controlled thermonuclear fusion on Earth is incredibly difficult due to the temperatures required to overcome the Coulomb barrier and the engineering challenges of plasma confinement.
* **19.9 Biological Effects of Radiation:** High-energy ionizing radiation damages biological tissue primarily by ionizing water, creating highly destructive free radicals (like $\cdot\text{OH}$) that tear apart DNA and proteins. The hazard level depends on the penetrating power of the radiation ($\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$) and whether the exposure is external or internal, leading to potential somatic (cancer, radiation sickness) or genetic consequences.
