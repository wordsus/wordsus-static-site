While metals dominate the periodic table in sheer numbers, the nonmetals—occupying the upper right quadrant—are the foundational building blocks of life and the vast majority of molecular compounds. This chapter explores the diverse chemistry of these elements, driven by their high electronegativities, high ionization energies, and tendency to gain or share electrons. We will investigate the periodic trends governing their reactivity, the structural differences caused by the "first-member anomaly," and the unique properties of key groups, including the noble gases, halogens, oxygen, sulfur, and the pnictogens.

## 20.1 General Concepts: Periodic Trends of Nonmetals

The nonmetals occupy the upper right portion of the periodic table, with the notable exception of hydrogen, which is typically placed at the top of Group 1A but shares many chemical characteristics with the nonmetals. Unlike metals, which are characterized by their tendency to lose electrons, nonmetals are defined by their tendency to either gain electrons to form monatomic and polyatomic anions or to share electrons with other nonmetals to form covalent bonds.

Because the foundational principles of atomic structure, effective nuclear charge ($Z_{eff}$), and basic periodic trends were established in Chapter 6, this section focuses on how those underlying trends manifest in the unique chemical behaviors, bonding patterns, and structural diversities of the nonmetal elements.

### The Defining Properties of Nonmetals

The chemical behavior of nonmetals is largely dictated by their high electronegativities and high ionization energies. As we move across a period from left to right, $Z_{eff}$ increases, pulling the valence electrons closer to the nucleus and making it energetically unfavorable for these atoms to form cations.

The general trend for nonmetallic character—the ability to attract electrons and behave as a nonmetal—can be visualized as follows:

```text
              INCREASING NONMETALLIC CHARACTER 
              Electronegativity Increases
              Ionization Energy Increases
              Atomic Radius Decreases
            ------------------------------------------->
          ^  +------+------+------+------+------+------+
          |  |  3A  |  4A  |  5A  |  6A  |  7A  |  8A  |
  I       |  +------+------+------+------+------+------+
  N       |  |  B   |  C   |  N   |  O   |  F   |  Ne  | Period 2
  C       |  +------+------+------+------+------+------+
  R       |  |      |  Si  |  P   |  S   |  Cl  |  Ar  | Period 3

          |  |      |      |      |      |  At  |  Rn  | Period 6
             +------+------+------+------+------+------+

```

Due to these trends, the most reactive nonmetal is fluorine (top right, excluding noble gases), while elements near the metalloid boundary (such as tellurium and boron) exhibit much weaker nonmetallic character.

### The "First-Member" Anomaly

One of the most critical concepts in the chemistry of nonmetals is the stark difference in behavior between the first member of a group (the Period 2 element) and the heavier members of the same group (Period 3 and below). Carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and fluorine exhibit chemistry that is often radically different from that of silicon, phosphorus, sulfur, and chlorine. This anomaly arises from three primary factors:

**1. Exceptionally Small Atomic Size and High Electronegativity**
The valence electrons of Period 2 elements are in the $n=2$ shell, which is very close to the nucleus and unshielded by any $p$, $d$, or $f$ inner-core electrons. This results in unusually high electronegativities and concentrated partial charges in polar bonds, leading to strong intermolecular forces like hydrogen bonding (unique to N, O, and F).

**2. Ability to Form Strong Pi ($\pi$) Bonds**
Because Period 2 atoms are small, they can approach each other closely enough for their parallel $2p$ orbitals to overlap effectively, forming strong $\pi$ bonds. Consequently, $O_2$ and $N_2$ exist as diatomic molecules with double ($O=O$) and triple ($N\equiv N$) bonds, respectively. Carbon frequently forms double and triple bonds with itself and with N and O.

In contrast, the larger size of Period 3 elements prevents them from approaching closely enough for effective $3p-3p$ lateral overlap. As a result, elements like phosphorus and sulfur prefer to form multiple single ($\sigma$) bonds rather than $\pi$ bonds. Thus, elemental phosphorus exists as $P_4$ tetrahedra rather than $P\equiv P$ molecules, and elemental sulfur exists as $S_8$ rings rather than $S=S$ molecules.

**3. Maximum Coordination Number and the Octet Rule**
Period 2 nonmetals are strictly limited to a maximum of four covalent bonds (an octet) because they only have one $2s$ and three $2p$ orbitals available for bonding. Furthermore, their small atomic radii physically prevent more than four ligands from packing around the central atom without severe steric hindrance.

Period 3 nonmetals and beyond are larger and can accommodate more than four bonded atoms. While historically attributed to the availability of empty $3d$ orbitals for hybridization, modern molecular orbital theory explains expanded octets in molecules like $SF_6$ and $PF_5$ primarily through highly polar, multicenter multi-electron bonds. Regardless of the theoretical model, the empirical result is clear: heavier nonmetals frequently exhibit hypervalency, a feature completely absent in Period 2.

### Oxidation States

The valence electron configurations of nonmetals ($ns^2 np^x$, where $x = 1$ to $5$) allow them to adopt a wide variety of oxidation states depending on the electronegativity of the atoms to which they are bonded.

For any given nonmetal in Groups 4A through 7A, the range of possible oxidation states is generally predictable:

* **Maximum positive oxidation state:** Equals the group number. This occurs when the nonmetal shares all its valence electrons with more electronegative elements (e.g., O or F).
* **Most negative oxidation state:** Equals the group number minus 8. This occurs when the nonmetal completes its octet by gaining or sharing electrons with less electronegative elements (e.g., metals or hydrogen).

Let $G$ be the main group number (4 through 7). The bounds for the oxidation states can be expressed as:

$$ (G - 8) \leq \text{Oxidation State} \leq G $$

**Examples of Oxidation State Ranges:**

* **Nitrogen (Group 5A):** Ranges from $-3$ to $+5$.
* $-3$ in Ammonia ($NH_3$)
* $0$ in Dinitrogen ($N_2$)
* $+1$ in Nitrous oxide ($N_2O$)
* $+5$ in Nitric acid ($HNO_3$)

* **Sulfur (Group 6A):** Ranges from $-2$ to $+6$.
* $-2$ in Hydrogen sulfide ($H_2S$)
* $+4$ in Sulfur dioxide ($SO_2$)
* $+6$ in Sulfur hexafluoride ($SF_6$)

*Note on Fluorine and Oxygen:* Due to their extreme electronegativities, fluorine strictly exhibits an oxidation state of $-1$ in its compounds, and oxygen is almost always $-2$ (with exceptions in peroxides, superoxides, and fluorides).

### Catenation

Catenation is the ability of atoms of the same element to form long chains or rings via covalent bonding. Carbon is the undisputed master of catenation, forming the structural backbone of organic chemistry. However, other nonmetals also exhibit this property to varying degrees.

The tendency to catenate depends strongly on the bond enthalpy of the element-element single bond ($E-E$).

* **C–C bond:** $\sim 348 \text{ kJ/mol}$
* **Si–Si bond:** $\sim 226 \text{ kJ/mol}$
* **S–S bond:** $\sim 266 \text{ kJ/mol}$
* **O–O bond:** $\sim 146 \text{ kJ/mol}$

Because the S–S bond is relatively strong, sulfur exhibits significant catenation, forming $S_8$ rings and long plastic sulfur chains. Conversely, the O–O single bond is remarkably weak due to the repulsion between lone pairs on adjacent small oxygen atoms, making compounds with long oxygen chains (polyoxides) highly unstable. This lone-pair repulsion also explains the relative weakness of the N–N and F–F single bonds compared to their Period 3 counterparts (P–P and Cl–Cl).

## 20.2 The Chemistry of Hydrogen

Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe, comprising approximately 70% of the universe's total mass and 90% of its atoms. Despite its simplicity—consisting of just one proton and one electron in its most common isotope—hydrogen exhibits a rich and unique chemical profile that defies simple categorization within the periodic table.

While conventionally placed at the top of Group 1A due to its $1s^1$ valence electron configuration, hydrogen is distinctly a nonmetal. It does not exhibit the low ionization energy characteristic of alkali metals. Conversely, placing it in Group 7A (the halogens) because it is one electron short of a noble gas configuration ($1s^2$) is also imperfect, as its electron affinity is significantly lower than that of true halogens. Therefore, hydrogen is best understood as a chemical entity unto itself.

### Isotopes of Hydrogen

Hydrogen exists as three distinct isotopes. Because the addition of neutrons significantly changes the relative mass of the atom (doubling or tripling it), the isotopes of hydrogen exhibit more pronounced differences in physical properties—such as reaction rates—than the isotopes of any other element. This phenomenon is known as the kinetic isotope effect.

| Isotope | Symbol | Nucleus | Mass (amu) | Natural Abundance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Protium** | $^1_1H$ | 1 proton, 0 neutrons | 1.0078 | 99.985% |
| **Deuterium** | $^2_1H$ (or $D$) | 1 proton, 1 neutron | 2.0141 | 0.015% |
| **Tritium** | $^3_1H$ (or $T$) | 1 proton, 2 neutrons | 3.0160 | Trace (Radioactive) |

Deuterium is widely used in chemical research, particularly in nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and isotopic labeling to deduce reaction mechanisms. Tritium is a beta emitter with a half-life of 12.3 years and is produced artificially in nuclear reactors.

### Elemental Dihydrogen ($H_2$)

Under standard conditions, elemental hydrogen exists as a colorless, odorless, and tasteless diatomic gas, $H_2$. The $H-H$ bond is a purely nonpolar covalent single bond resulting from the overlap of two $1s$ orbitals.

Because the hydrogen atoms are extremely small, the nuclei can approach each other closely, resulting in an exceptionally strong single bond. The bond dissociation enthalpy of $H_2$ is high:

$$ H_2(g) \rightarrow 2H(g) \quad \Delta H^\circ = 436 \text{ kJ/mol} $$

Due to this high bond energy, $H_2$ is relatively unreactive at room temperature. Most reactions involving molecular hydrogen require high temperatures, high pressures, or a catalyst (such as platinum, palladium, or nickel) to facilitate the breaking of the $H-H$ bond.

### Preparation of Hydrogen

Because elemental hydrogen is light and easily escapes Earth's gravity, very little $H_2$ exists in the atmosphere. It must be prepared from hydrogen-containing compounds, primarily water and hydrocarbons.

**1. Commercial Production (Steam Reforming)**
The primary industrial method for producing hydrogen is the steam reforming of natural gas (methane). The process occurs at high temperatures (700–1000°C) over a nickel catalyst:

$$ CH_4(g) + H_2O(g) \xrightarrow[\text{Ni}]{\Delta} CO(g) + 3H_2(g) $$

To increase the yield of hydrogen, the carbon monoxide by-product is further reacted with steam in the water-gas shift reaction:

$$ CO(g) + H_2O(g) \xrightarrow[\text{Fe/Cu}]{\Delta} CO_2(g) + H_2(g) $$

**2. Laboratory Preparation**
Small quantities of hydrogen are easily generated in the laboratory by the oxidation of an active metal (such as zinc, iron, or magnesium) by a strong acid:

$$ Zn(s) + 2H^+(aq) \rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq) + H_2(g) $$

**3. Electrolysis of Water**
High-purity hydrogen can be produced by passing a direct current through water containing a small amount of an electrolyte (like $H_2SO_4$ or $NaOH$) to increase conductivity.

* **Cathode (Reduction):** $4H_2O(l) + 4e^- \rightarrow 2H_2(g) + 4OH^-(aq)$
* **Anode (Oxidation):** $2H_2O(l) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 4H^+(aq) + 4e^-$
* **Overall Reaction:** $2H_2O(l) \xrightarrow{\text{electricity}} 2H_2(g) + O_2(g)$

### The Hydrides

Hydrogen reacts with almost every element in the periodic table to form binary compounds called hydrides. Depending on the electronegativity of the element bonded to hydrogen, these compounds are classified into three major categories.

```text
Periodic Table of Hydrides

+---+---+                                 +---+---+---+---+---+
| I | 2 |                                 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+
| I | I | M | M | M | M | M | M | M | M | M | C | C | C | C | C |
+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+

+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+---+

I = Ionic (Saline) Hydrides

```

**1. Ionic (Saline) Hydrides**
Formed when hydrogen reacts with highly electropositive s-block metals (Groups 1A and the heavier members of 2A). In these compounds, hydrogen gains an electron to complete its $1s$ orbital, forming the hydride ion ($H^-$), which has an oxidation state of $-1$.

* *Examples:* $LiH$, $NaH$, $CaH_2$
* *Reactivity:* Ionic hydrides are strong bases and powerful reducing agents. They react violently with water to release hydrogen gas:

$$ NaH(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow NaOH(aq) + H_2(g) $$

**2. Covalent (Molecular) Hydrides**
Formed when hydrogen reacts with nonmetals and metalloids (p-block elements). Because these elements are more electronegative than or similar in electronegativity to hydrogen, the bonds are covalent, and hydrogen is assigned an oxidation state of $+1$ (except in cases like boranes and silanes, where it approaches $-1$).

* *Examples:* $H_2O$, $NH_3$, $CH_4$, $HCl$
* *Properties:* These are typically gases or volatile liquids at room temperature. Their physical properties are heavily influenced by intermolecular forces, particularly hydrogen bonding, which is unusually strong in $H_2O$, $NH_3$, and $HF$ due to the high electronegativity of N, O, and F combined with the small size of the hydrogen atom.

**3. Metallic (Interstitial) Hydrides**
Formed when hydrogen gas reacts with transition metals (d-block and f-block). In these materials, hydrogen molecules dissociate into individual atoms and occupy the interstitial holes in the metal crystal lattice.

* *Examples:* $PdH_x$, $TiH_x$
* *Properties:* These hydrides are often non-stoichiometric, meaning the ratio of hydrogen to metal is variable (e.g., $PdH_{0.6}$). The absorption of hydrogen into metals like palladium is reversible, making them subject to ongoing research for hydrogen storage technologies in fuel-cell vehicles.

## 20.3 The Noble Gases (Group 8A)

The noble gases—helium (He), neon (Ne), argon (Ar), krypton (Kr), xenon (Xe), and radon (Rn)—constitute Group 8A of the periodic table. For many decades following their discovery at the end of the 19th century, these elements were referred to as the "inert gases" due to their apparent absolute lack of chemical reactivity. It was not until 1962 that chemists successfully forced a noble gas to form a chemical bond, prompting the transition to the modern name "noble gases," which implies a profound reluctance to react rather than a complete inability to do so.

### Physical Properties and Intermolecular Forces

All noble gases are colorless, odorless, and tasteless monatomic gases under standard conditions. Their lack of reactivity and their physical properties are fundamentally tied to their electron configurations. With the exception of helium ($1s^2$), all noble gases possess a filled valence shell with an $ns^2 np^6$ configuration (a stable octet).

Because they exist as individual atoms rather than molecules, the only intermolecular forces acting between noble gas atoms are London dispersion forces. As discussed in Chapter 10, the strength of dispersion forces depends primarily on polarizability, which increases with the size of the electron cloud.

```text
Trend in Physical Properties of Noble Gases

Element    Atomic Radius (pm)    Boiling Point (K)    Polarizability
--------------------------------------------------------------------
  He           32                      4.2             Lowest
  Ne           71                     27.1               |
  Ar           98                     87.3               |
  Kr          112                    119.7               |
  Xe          131                    165.0               V
  Rn          140                    211.3             Highest

```

Helium has the lowest polarizability and, consequently, the weakest intermolecular forces of any substance. It boasts the lowest boiling point of all elements ($4.2 \text{ K}$) and is the only substance that will not solidify under standard atmospheric pressure, regardless of how close to absolute zero it is cooled. Liquid helium requires elevated pressure ($\sim 25 \text{ atm}$) to force the atoms close enough together to form a solid lattice.

### The Ionization Energy Barrier

The chemical inertness of the noble gases stems from their exceptionally high first ionization energies (IE). Helium has the highest first ionization energy of any element ($2372 \text{ kJ/mol}$), making it energetically prohibitive to form $He^+$ under normal chemical conditions. Furthermore, noble gases have negative or virtually zero electron affinities, meaning they will not accept additional electrons.

However, as we descend Group 8A, the valence electrons are found in shells progressively further from the nucleus, shielded by an increasing number of core electrons. Consequently, the first ionization energy drops significantly from helium down to radon.

By the time we reach xenon, the first ionization energy has decreased to $1170 \text{ kJ/mol}$. In 1962, the British chemist Neil Bartlett realized that this value is remarkably close to the first ionization energy of the diatomic oxygen molecule, $O_2$ ($1180 \text{ kJ/mol}$). Bartlett had previously synthesized an unusual ionic compound, dioxygenyl hexafluoroplatinate ($O_2^+[PtF_6]^-$), by reacting $O_2$ with the powerful oxidizing agent platinum hexafluoride ($PtF_6$). Hypothesizing that $PtF_6$ should also be capable of oxidizing xenon, Bartlett performed the reaction and successfully synthesized the first noble gas compound, a yellow-orange solid originally formulated as $Xe^+[PtF_6]^-$ (later shown to be a more complex mixture of xenon fluoroplatinates).

### The Chemistry of Xenon

Following Bartlett's breakthrough, chemists quickly discovered that xenon could react directly with fluorine gas under specific conditions of temperature and pressure to form three distinct binary fluorides: xenon difluoride ($XeF_2$), xenon tetrafluoride ($XeF_4$), and xenon hexafluoride ($XeF_6$).

The synthesis of these compounds depends on the stoichiometric ratio of the reactants and the reaction conditions:

$$ Xe(g) + F_2(g) \xrightarrow{400^\circ\text{C}, 1\text{ atm}} XeF_2(s) $$

$$ Xe(g) + 2F_2(g) \xrightarrow{600^\circ\text{C}, 6\text{ atm}} XeF_4(s) $$

$$ Xe(g) + 3F_2(g) \xrightarrow{300^\circ\text{C}, 60\text{ atm}} XeF_6(s) $$

**Molecular Geometry of Xenon Fluorides**
Because the central xenon atom has a full octet before bonding, any bonds formed with fluorine result in an expanded valence shell, placing these molecules firmly in the domain of hypervalency. The structures of these compounds are excellently predicted by the Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) model covered in Chapter 8.

* **$XeF_2$:** The central Xe atom has 8 valence electrons plus 2 from the fluorines, giving 10 electrons (5 electron domains). These arrange in a trigonal bipyramidal geometry. To minimize repulsion, the three lone pairs occupy the equatorial positions, resulting in a **linear** molecular geometry.
* **$XeF_4$:** With 12 valence electrons (6 domains) around Xe, the electron-domain geometry is octahedral. The two lone pairs position themselves opposite one another ($180^\circ$ apart) to minimize repulsion, yielding a **square planar** molecular geometry.

```text
        Molecular Geometries of Xenon Fluorides

          F                           F       F
          |                            \     /
      :-- Xe --:                        --Xe--
          |                            /     \
          F                           F       F
    (Lone pairs in                 (Lone pairs above
     equatorial plane)              and below plane)
          
        XeF2                           XeF4
       Linear                      Square Planar

```

**Xenon Oxides**
Xenon does not react directly with oxygen. However, highly reactive xenon-oxygen bonds can be formed via the hydrolysis of xenon fluorides. When $XeF_6$ reacts with water, it undergoes a complete hydrolysis to form xenon trioxide ($XeO_3$):

$$ XeF_6(s) + 3H_2O(l) \rightarrow XeO_3(aq) + 6HF(aq) $$

$XeO_3$ is a colorless, highly explosive solid. Unlike the expanded-octet fluorides, the central xenon atom in $XeO_3$ maintains a formal $+6$ oxidation state and features three double bonds to oxygen and one lone pair, resulting in a trigonal pyramidal shape analogous to ammonia ($NH_3$).

### Other Noble Gas Compounds

While xenon exhibits the most extensive chemistry of the noble gases, a few compounds of other heavy noble gases have been isolated:

* **Krypton:** Krypton has a much higher ionization energy than xenon ($1351 \text{ kJ/mol}$). Consequently, its chemistry is highly restricted. Krypton difluoride ($KrF_2$) can be synthesized by passing an electrical discharge through a mixture of Kr and $F_2$ at cryogenic temperatures. $KrF_2$ is highly unstable and decomposes at room temperature.
* **Radon:** Radon is known to react with fluorine to form radon difluoride ($RnF_2$). However, because all isotopes of radon are highly radioactive with short half-lives (the longest being $Rn-222$ at 3.8 days), studying its chemistry is extremely hazardous and difficult; the intense radiation rapidly degrades the compounds formed.
* **Argon:** In 2000, chemists isolated argon fluorohydride ($HArF$) in a solid argon matrix at $40 \text{ K}$. It decomposes upon warming above $40 \text{ K}$, indicating that the chemical threshold for Group 8A is pushed to its absolute limit at argon. No stable compounds of helium or neon have ever been synthesized.

## 20.4 The Halogens (Group 7A)

The elements of Group 7A—fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), iodine (I), and the highly radioactive, synthetically prepared astatine (At)—are collectively known as the halogens, a term derived from Greek roots meaning "salt formers." Because their valence electron configuration is $ns^2np^5$, they are precisely one electron short of a highly stable noble gas octet. This intense drive to acquire a single electron dominates their chemistry, making the halogens nature’s most potent class of oxidizing agents.

In their elemental forms, the halogens exist as diatomic molecules ($X_2$). They are highly toxic and fiercely reactive, rarely found uncombined in nature. Instead, they are abundantly distributed as halide anions ($X^-$) in minerals and seawater.

### Physical Properties and State Trends

The halogens provide one of the clearest visual demonstrations of how intermolecular forces scale with molecular size. Because the $X_2$ molecules are completely nonpolar, the only attractive forces acting between them are London dispersion forces.

As we move down the group, the principal quantum number ($n$) of the valence shell increases, leading to a larger atomic radius and a more diffuse, easily polarizable electron cloud. This increase in polarizability translates to stronger dispersion forces, which smoothly dictate the physical state of the elements at room temperature (approximately 25°C).

| Element | Molecule | Appearance at 25°C | Melting Point (°C) | Boiling Point (°C) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Fluorine** | $F_2$ | Pale yellow gas | -220 | -188 |
| **Chlorine** | $Cl_2$ | Yellow-green gas | -101 | -34 |
| **Bromine** | $Br_2$ | Red-brown liquid | -7 | 59 |
| **Iodine** | $I_2$ | Purple-black solid | 114 | 184 |

*Note: Bromine is remarkable for being one of only two elements on the periodic table (the other being mercury) that is a liquid at standard temperature and pressure.*

### Chemical Reactivity and Oxidizing Power

Halogens generally react by accepting an electron to form a halide ion ($X^-$) or by sharing an electron to form a single covalent bond. Their standard reduction potentials reflect their exceptional strength as oxidizing agents. Fluorine is the most electronegative element in the periodic table and the strongest elemental oxidizing agent known. It reacts explosively with water and is capable of coaxing even inert elements like xenon into compounds.

The oxidizing strength of the halogens decreases steadily down the group:

$$ F_2 > Cl_2 > Br_2 > I_2 $$

This hierarchy means that a lighter (higher) halogen will thermodynamically spontaneous oxidize the halide ion of any heavier (lower) halogen in a single-displacement reaction. For example, bubbling chlorine gas through a solution of sodium bromide produces aqueous chloride ions and liquid bromine:

$$ Cl_2(g) + 2Br^-(aq) \rightarrow 2Cl^-(aq) + Br_2(l) $$

Conversely, adding iodine to a chloride solution yields no reaction, as $I_2$ is a weaker oxidizing agent than $Cl_2$.

### Hydrogen Halides and Hydrohalic Acids

The halogens react with hydrogen gas to form diatomic hydrogen halides ($HX$):

$$ H_2(g) + X_2(g) \rightarrow 2HX(g) $$

These compounds are all colorless gases at room temperature. When dissolved in water, they form hydrohalic acids (e.g., hydrochloric acid, hydrobromic acid).

The acid strength of the hydrogen halides in aqueous solution provides a fascinating counter-intuitive trend. Given fluorine's extreme electronegativity, one might expect $HF$ to be the strongest acid, as the H-F bond is highly polarized. However, hydrofluoric acid is a **weak acid** in water, while $HCl$, $HBr$, and $HI$ are **strong acids** that dissociate completely.

This behavior is primarily governed by bond enthalpy. The $H-F$ bond is exceptionally short and strong ($567 \text{ kJ/mol}$), meaning the energy required to break the bond outweighs the energy released by hydrating the ions. As atoms get larger down the group, the $H-X$ bond length increases and the bond enthalpy drops significantly (the $H-I$ bond is only $299 \text{ kJ/mol}$), allowing the heavier hydrogen halides to readily donate protons in solution.

### Interhalogen Compounds

Halogens can react with one another to form a diverse class of covalent binary compounds known as interhalogens. These compounds generally follow the formula $XX'_n$, where $X$ is the heavier, less electronegative halogen (the central atom), $X'$ is the lighter, more electronegative halogen (the terminal atoms), and $n = 1, 3, 5, \text{or } 7$.

Because fluorine is the smallest and most electronegative halogen, it serves as the terminal atom in the highest-order interhalogens. The geometry of these molecules is perfectly predicted by the VSEPR theory discussed in Chapter 8.

* **$ClF_3$ (Chlorine trifluoride):** The central Cl has 10 valence electrons (3 bonding pairs, 2 lone pairs). It adopts a **T-shaped** molecular geometry. $ClF_3$ is an incredibly aggressive fluorinating agent, capable of igniting glass and asbestos on contact.
* **$BrF_5$ (Bromine pentafluoride):** The central Br has 12 valence electrons (5 bonding pairs, 1 lone pair), yielding a **square pyramidal** geometry.
* **$IF_7$ (Iodine heptafluoride):** The central I has 14 valence electrons (7 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs), resulting in a **pentagonal bipyramidal** geometry. Only iodine is large enough to physically accommodate seven fluorine atoms around its central core without extreme steric repulsion.

### Oxyacids and Oxyanions of the Halogens

With the exception of fluorine—which is strictly restricted to a $-1$ oxidation state—the halogens can expand their octets and achieve positive oxidation states ($+1, +3, +5, +7$) when bonded to the highly electronegative oxygen atom. This gives rise to a series of oxyacids and their corresponding oxyanions.

Chlorine forms the most complete series of oxyacids:

| Oxidation State | Acid Formula | Acid Name | Anion Formula | Anion Name |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **+1** | $HClO$ | Hypochlorous acid | $ClO^-$ | Hypochlorite |
| **+3** | $HClO_2$ | Chlorous acid | $ClO_2^-$ | Chlorite |
| **+5** | $HClO_3$ | Chloric acid | $ClO_3^-$ | Chlorate |
| **+7** | $HClO_4$ | Perchloric acid | $ClO_4^-$ | Perchlorate |

**Acid Strength Trend in Oxyacids:**
As discussed in Chapter 16, the acid strength of a halogen oxyacid depends on two factors:

1. **Number of oxygen atoms:** Acid strength increases as the number of highly electronegative oxygen atoms bonded to the central halogen increases. The oxygen atoms draw electron density away from the $O-H$ bond, weakening it and stabilizing the resulting conjugate base via resonance. Thus, perchloric acid ($HClO_4$) is one of the strongest known strong acids, while hypochlorous acid ($HClO$) is a weak acid.
2. **Electronegativity of the central halogen:** For a given number of oxygen atoms, acid strength increases with the electronegativity of the central halogen. Therefore, chloric acid ($HClO_3$) is a stronger acid than bromic acid ($HBrO_3$), which in turn is stronger than iodic acid ($HIO_3$).

## 20.5 Oxygen and Its Compounds

Oxygen is the most abundant element in the Earth's crust and oceans by mass, and the second most abundant in the atmosphere. As the first member of Group 6A (the chalcogens), its chemistry is heavily dictated by its small atomic radius and exceptionally high electronegativity—second only to fluorine.

Because oxygen lacks accessible $d$ orbitals, it is strictly limited to an octet of electrons. It achieves this stable configuration either by gaining two electrons to form the oxide ion ($O^{2-}$), or by forming two covalent bonds (either two single bonds or one double bond).

### Allotropes of Oxygen

Oxygen exists in two primary allotropic forms under standard conditions: dioxygen ($O_2$) and ozone ($O_3$). These two molecules exhibit drastically different physical and chemical properties.

#### Dioxygen ($O_2$)

Dioxygen, commonly referred to simply as "oxygen gas," is a colorless, odorless gas that makes up 21% of the Earth's atmosphere by volume. It is vital for cellular respiration and is the universal oxidizing agent in combustion reactions.

While the Lewis structure of $O_2$ depicts a double bond with all electrons paired ($\ddot{O}=\ddot{O}$), this simple model fails to explain a crucial physical property of the molecule: liquid oxygen is pale blue and is physically drawn into a magnetic field. This paramagnetism indicates the presence of unpaired electrons.

As discussed in Chapter 8, Molecular Orbital (MO) theory resolves this discrepancy. The MO diagram for $O_2$ reveals that the highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMOs) are a pair of degenerate $\pi^*_{2p}$ antibonding orbitals. According to Hund's Rule, the two highest-energy electrons occupy these orbitals singly with parallel spins, resulting in a paramagnetic molecule with a formal bond order of 2.

#### Ozone ($O_3$)

Ozone is a pale blue, highly toxic gas with a sharp, pungent odor often noticed near electrical sparks. It is thermodynamically unstable relative to $O_2$ and is formed endothermically when energy (such as a high-voltage electrical discharge or ultraviolet radiation) splits an $O_2$ molecule, allowing an atomic oxygen to react with another $O_2$ molecule:

$$ 3O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2O_3(g) \quad \Delta H^\circ = +284.6 \text{ kJ/mol} $$

In the stratosphere, the ozone layer is critical to life on Earth because it absorbs high-energy UV radiation. At ground level, however, it is a harmful pollutant and a major component of photochemical smog.

Structurally, ozone is a bent molecule. The central oxygen atom is $sp^2$ hybridized with one lone pair, giving an $O-O-O$ bond angle of approximately $117^\circ$. The two $O-O$ bonds are of equal length ($128 \text{ pm}$), intermediate between a standard single bond ($148 \text{ pm}$) and a standard double bond ($121 \text{ pm}$). This is best represented by two equivalent resonance structures:

```text
       ..            ..
      :O:           :O:
      / \           / \
    ..   ..       ..   ..
   :O = O:  <--> :O - O:
    .                 .
       
   Resonance Structures of Ozone

```

Because of its instability, ozone is a much more powerful oxidizing agent than dioxygen.

### Oxidation States and the Oxygen Anions

Oxygen almost universally exhibits an oxidation state of $-2$ in its compounds. However, it can form homonuclear bonds with itself to create two other important anions: the peroxide ion and the superoxide ion.

| Species | Formula | Oxidation State of O | Bond Order | Magnetic Property |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Dioxygen** | $O_2$ | $0$ | 2 | Paramagnetic |
| **Superoxide** | $O_2^-$ | $-1/2$ | 1.5 | Paramagnetic |
| **Peroxide** | $O_2^{2-}$ | $-1$ | 1 | Diamagnetic |

**1. Peroxides ($O_2^{2-}$)**
Peroxides feature an oxygen-oxygen single bond ($O-O$). Because oxygen is so electronegative, the $O-O$ single bond is remarkably weak ($\sim 146 \text{ kJ/mol}$) due to the strong electrostatic repulsion between the dense lone pairs on adjacent atoms. Consequently, peroxides like hydrogen peroxide ($H_2O_2$) and sodium peroxide ($Na_2O_2$) are highly reactive and serve as strong oxidizing agents.

**2. Superoxides ($O_2^{-}$)**
Superoxides are formed when $O_2$ acquires a single additional electron. This electron enters one of the $\pi^*_{2p}$ antibonding orbitals, reducing the bond order to 1.5 and maintaining one unpaired electron, meaning the ion is paramagnetic. Superoxides are typically only formed by the reaction of $O_2$ with the largest, most active alkali metals (potassium, rubidium, and cesium).
Potassium superoxide ($KO_2$) is utilized in closed-system life-support equipment (such as spacecraft and submarines) because it acts as both a carbon dioxide scrubber and an oxygen generator:

$$ 4KO_2(s) + 2CO_2(g) \rightarrow 2K_2CO_3(s) + 3O_2(g) $$

### Classification of Oxides

Because of its high electronegativity, oxygen forms binary compounds (oxides) with almost every element in the periodic table (excluding the lighter noble gases). These oxides are classified based on their acid-base behavior in aqueous environments.

**1. Basic Oxides**
Oxides of metals with low electronegativity (primarily Groups 1A and 2A, and lower oxidation states of transition metals) are ionic solids. When dissolved in water, the oxide ion ($O^{2-}$) reacts completely with water to produce hydroxide ions, forming basic solutions. If insoluble in water, they will readily dissolve in strong acids.

* *Example with water:* $CaO(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2(aq)$
* *Example with acid:* $CuO(s) + 2HNO_3(aq) \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2(aq) + H_2O(l)$

**2. Acidic Oxides**
Oxides of nonmetals (and transition metals in unusually high oxidation states, such as $Mn_2O_7$) are primarily covalent compounds. These are also known as acid anhydrides. They react with water to form acidic solutions or react with bases to form salts and water.

* *Example with water:* $SO_3(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2SO_4(aq)$
* *Example with base:* $CO_2(g) + 2NaOH(aq) \rightarrow Na_2CO_3(aq) + H_2O(l)$

**3. Amphoteric Oxides**
Oxides of elements located near the metal-nonmetal boundary of the periodic table, as well as several transition metals in intermediate oxidation states, can exhibit both basic and acidic properties. These amphoteric oxides will not dissolve in neutral water but will dissolve in both strong acids and strong bases.
Aluminum oxide is a classic example:

* *Acting as a base:* $Al_2O_3(s) + 6HCl(aq) \rightarrow 2AlCl_3(aq) + 3H_2O(l)$
* *Acting as an acid:* $Al_2O_3(s) + 2NaOH(aq) + 3H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2NaAl(OH)_4(aq)$

## 20.6 Sulfur, Selenium, and Tellurium

As we descend Group 6A (the chalcogens) from oxygen to polonium, the elements transition from purely nonmetallic behavior to metalloid and eventually metallic character. While oxygen's chemistry is dominated by its small size, extreme electronegativity, and strict adherence to the octet rule, the heavier chalcogens—sulfur (S), selenium (Se), and tellurium (Te)—exhibit significantly different chemical profiles.

Because S, Se, and Te reside in Period 3 and below, they possess larger atomic radii, lower electronegativities, and the ability to expand their valence shells (hypervalency) to accommodate more than eight electrons, leading to a vast array of compounds with oxidation states ranging from $-2$ to $+6$.

### Allotropes and Solid-State Structures

Unlike oxygen, which exists almost exclusively as the diatomic molecules $O_2$ and $O_3$, the heavier chalcogens are highly prone to catenation—the formation of chains and rings of atoms of the same element. This tendency decreases down the group as atom-atom single bond enthalpies weaken ($S-S > Se-Se > Te-Te$).

**Sulfur Allotropes**
Sulfur possesses more allotropes than any other element, a consequence of the strong $S-S$ single bond ($\sim 266 \text{ kJ/mol}$). At room temperature, the most stable form is **rhombic sulfur** ($S_\alpha$), which consists of distinct, puckered eight-membered rings ($S_8$) arranged in a crystalline lattice.

```text
       S         S
      / \       / \
     S   \     /   S      The puckered "crown" structure
     |    S---S    |      of the S8 molecule.
     S             S
      \           /
       S---------S

```

If rhombic sulfur is heated above 95°C, the crystal packing changes to form **monoclinic sulfur** ($S_\beta$), which still contains $S_8$ rings but arranged in a different crystal lattice.

The melting and boiling behavior of sulfur is uniquely complex:

1. Around 115°C, sulfur melts into a highly mobile, pale yellow liquid consisting of intact $S_8$ rings.
2. As the temperature approaches 160°C, the $S_8$ rings begin to break open and polymerize, forming incredibly long chains of S atoms (up to hundreds of thousands of atoms long). This causes the liquid to turn dark red and become intensely viscous—so thick it cannot be poured from an inverted test tube.
3. If this boiling, viscous liquid is rapidly cooled (quenched) by pouring it into cold water, it forms **plastic sulfur**, an amorphous, rubbery solid composed of tangled sulfur chains. Over time, these chains will slowly break and reform into the thermodynamically stable rhombic $S_8$ rings.

**Selenium and Tellurium Allotropes**
Selenium exhibits several allotropes, broadly categorized as red and gray selenium. The red allotropes consist of $Se_8$ rings, structurally analogous to sulfur. However, the thermodynamically stable form at room temperature is **gray selenium**, which consists of infinite helical chains of Se atoms. Gray selenium is a semiconductor and exhibits *photoconductivity*—its electrical conductivity increases significantly when exposed to light, a property historically exploited in photocopiers and light meters.

Tellurium only exists in one crystalline form, a continuous three-dimensional network structurally similar to gray selenium. It is a silvery-white metalloid with metallic luster, but it is a poor conductor of electricity, firmly classifying it as a semiconductor.

### Oxidation States and Chemical Reactivity

The valence electron configuration of these elements is $ns^2 np^4$. While they can achieve a noble gas configuration by gaining two electrons to form chalcogenide anions ($S^{2-}$, $Se^{2-}$, $Te^{2-}$), their lower electronegativities relative to oxygen mean these anions are only formed with the most electropositive metals (e.g., $Na_2S$, $CaTe$).

When bonding with nonmetals, S, Se, and Te readily share electrons and commonly exhibit positive oxidation states of $+4$ and $+6$, particularly when bonded to highly electronegative elements like oxygen and fluorine.

* **-2 Oxidation State:** Found in hydrogen sulfide ($H_2S$) and metal sulfides ($ZnS$).
* **+4 Oxidation State:** Found in sulfur dioxide ($SO_2$) and the sulfite ion ($SO_3^{2-}$).
* **+6 Oxidation State:** Found in sulfur trioxide ($SO_3$), the sulfate ion ($SO_4^{2-}$), and sulfur hexafluoride ($SF_6$).

### Hydrogen Compounds

The binary compounds of chalcogens with hydrogen have the general formula $H_2E$ (where $E$ = S, Se, or Te).

* **Hydrogen sulfide ($H_2S$):** A highly toxic, colorless gas with the unmistakable odor of rotten eggs. It is a weak diprotic acid in water.
* **Hydrogen selenide ($H_2Se$) and Hydrogen telluride ($H_2Te$):** Also highly toxic gases.

The $H-E$ bond enthalpies decrease dramatically down the group as atomic size increases and orbital overlap becomes less effective. Consequently, the thermal stability of these hydrides decreases, and their acid strength in aqueous solution increases:

$$ \text{Acid Strength: } H_2Te > H_2Se > H_2S > H_2O $$

### Oxides and Oxyacids of Sulfur

Sulfur forms two primary oxides, both of which are acidic anhydrides of immense industrial and environmental importance.

**Sulfur Dioxide ($SO_2$) and Sulfurous Acid ($H_2SO_3$)**
Combustion of elemental sulfur in air yields sulfur dioxide, a colorless, choking gas:

$$ S(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow SO_2(g) $$

The $SO_2$ molecule is bent (bond angle $\sim 119^\circ$) with an $sp^2$ hybridized central sulfur atom carrying one lone pair. When dissolved in water, $SO_2$ acts as an acidic oxide, forming solutions of sulfurous acid ($H_2SO_3$), a weak diprotic acid:

$$ SO_2(g) + H_2O(l) \rightleftharpoons H_2SO_3(aq) $$

**Sulfur Trioxide ($SO_3$) and Sulfuric Acid ($H_2SO_4$)**
Further oxidation of $SO_2$ yields sulfur trioxide. This reaction is thermodynamically favorable but kinetically very slow at room temperature:

$$ 2SO_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2SO_3(g) \quad \Delta H^\circ = -198 \text{ kJ} $$

In the industrial **Contact Process**, this reaction is driven to completion at high temperatures using a vanadium(V) oxide ($V_2O_5$) catalyst. The resulting $SO_3$—a trigonal planar molecule in the gas phase—is then used to produce sulfuric acid.

Sulfuric acid ($H_2SO_4$) is the most heavily produced industrial chemical in the world, utilized in fertilizer manufacturing, oil refining, and metallurgy. Pure sulfuric acid is a dense, viscous liquid characterized by three dominant chemical properties:

1. **Strong Acid:** It is a strong diprotic acid. The first ionization is complete in water, while the second ionization is that of a moderately weak acid ($K_a = 1.2 \times 10^{-2}$).
2. **Powerful Dehydrating Agent:** Concentrated $H_2SO_4$ has an immense affinity for water. It will violently remove the elements of water (H and O) from organic compounds, such as turning sucrose into elemental carbon:

$$ C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}(s) \xrightarrow{H_2SO_4(conc)} 12C(s) + 11H_2O(g) $$

1. **Moderate Oxidizing Agent:** Hot, concentrated sulfuric acid can oxidize nonmetals and unreactive metals like copper:

$$ Cu(s) + 2H_2SO_4(aq) \rightarrow CuSO_4(aq) + SO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) $$

### Halides of Sulfur

Because sulfur can expand its octet, it reacts with halogens to form a variety of compounds, most notably with fluorine. The most important of these is **sulfur hexafluoride ($SF_6$)**.

In $SF_6$, sulfur is in its maximum $+6$ oxidation state. The molecule features an octahedral geometry ($sp^3d^2$ hybridization model) with six highly polar $S-F$ bonds. Despite the polarity of the individual bonds, the molecule's perfect symmetry renders it completely nonpolar overall. The six fluorine atoms form a tight, sterically protective shell around the central sulfur atom, preventing nucleophilic attack. As a result, $SF_6$ is a remarkably unreactive, inert, non-toxic gas, widely used as an electrical insulator in high-voltage transformers and switchgear.

## 20.7 Nitrogen and Phosphorus

Nitrogen and phosphorus are the first two members of Group 5A (the pnictogens), possessing a valence electron configuration of $ns^2np^3$. Like the oxygen-to-sulfur transition in Group 6A, the chemical relationship between nitrogen and phosphorus perfectly illustrates the "first-member anomaly." Nitrogen, a Period 2 element, is governed by its small size, high electronegativity, and profound ability to form strong $p\pi-p\pi$ multiple bonds. Phosphorus, a Period 3 element, has a larger atomic radius, lower electronegativity, and readily utilizes hypervalency while heavily favoring single bonds over multiple bonds.

### Elemental Forms: The $N_2$ Molecule vs. $P_4$ Tetrahedra

**Nitrogen ($N_2$)**
Elemental nitrogen exists as a colorless, odorless diatomic gas, comprising roughly 78% of the Earth's atmosphere by volume. Because nitrogen atoms are small, they can approach each other closely enough for three $2p$ orbitals on each atom to overlap effectively. This results in one sigma ($\sigma$) and two pi ($\pi$) bonds, creating an incredibly strong nitrogen-nitrogen triple bond:

$$ :N\equiv N: $$

The bond dissociation enthalpy of $N_2$ is immense ($941 \text{ kJ/mol}$). This high activation energy barrier makes $N_2$ exceptionally unreactive (kinetically inert) under standard conditions. It is routinely used in chemistry to provide an inert atmosphere, preventing oxidation of sensitive compounds.

**Phosphorus Allotropes**
Because the larger $3p$ orbitals of phosphorus cannot overlap laterally to form stable $\pi$ bonds, elemental phosphorus does not exist as $P\equiv P$ molecules. Instead, phosphorus forms three single bonds per atom to complete its octet, leading to an extensive catenated chemistry with three main solid allotropes:

1. **White Phosphorus:** Consists of discrete $P_4$ molecules. To satisfy the bonding requirements, the four phosphorus atoms arrange themselves at the corners of a tetrahedron.

```text
           P
          /|\
         / | \
        P--+--P
         \ | /
           P
    
    White Phosphorus (P4)

```

In a perfect tetrahedron, the bond angles are $60^\circ$. However, the natural bond angle for $p$-orbitals is $90^\circ$ (or $109.5^\circ$ for $sp^3$ hybridization). This extreme deviation creates massive **ring strain**. Consequently, white phosphorus is highly unstable, fiercely reactive, and spontaneously bursts into flames upon exposure to air. It is stored under water to prevent oxidation.

1. **Red Phosphorus:** Formed by heating white phosphorus in the absence of air. The $P_4$ tetrahedra link together to form polymeric chains, relieving much of the ring strain. It is much less reactive than white phosphorus and is safe to handle (used in the striking surface of matchboxes).
2. **Black Phosphorus:** The most thermodynamically stable form. It has a fluted, layered structure similar to graphite and is an electrical semiconductor.

### Nitrogen Fixation and Ammonia

Because biological systems require nitrogen to synthesize proteins and nucleic acids, the inert $N_2$ molecule must be converted into a biologically usable form—a process known as **nitrogen fixation**.

In nature, this is accomplished by specialized bacteria or by the extreme energy of lightning strikes. Industrially, it is achieved via the **Haber-Bosch process**, one of the most significant chemical reactions of the 20th century. Nitrogen and hydrogen gases are reacted at high pressures ($200 \text{ atm}$) and elevated temperatures ($400^\circ\text{C}$ to $500^\circ\text{C}$) over an iron catalyst to produce ammonia:

$$ N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g) \quad \Delta H^\circ = -92.2 \text{ kJ} $$

Ammonia ($NH_3$) is a colorless, pungent, toxic gas. It is a weak Brønsted-Lowry base in water and serves as the primary precursor for almost all other synthesized nitrogen compounds, including fertilizers, explosives, and plastics.

Another important hydrogen-nitrogen compound is **hydrazine** ($N_2H_4$), which features an $N-N$ single bond. Because the $N-N$ single bond is remarkably weak ($\sim 160 \text{ kJ/mol}$) due to lone-pair repulsion, hydrazine is highly reactive and famously used as rocket fuel.

### Oxides and Oxyacids of Nitrogen

Nitrogen forms an extensive series of oxides, with oxidation states ranging from $+1$ to $+5$. Several of these oxides are odd-electron molecules (radicals), making them paramagnetic and highly reactive.

* **Nitrous oxide ($N_2O$):** Oxidation state $+1$. A linear molecule ($N-N-O$) known as "laughing gas," used as a mild anesthetic and aerosol propellant.
* **Nitric oxide ($NO$):** Oxidation state $+2$. A colorless, paramagnetic gas with one unpaired electron. It plays a critical signaling role in human cardiovascular biology.
* **Nitrogen dioxide ($NO_2$):** Oxidation state $+4$. A toxic, reddish-brown paramagnetic gas responsible for the color of photochemical smog. Because it is a radical, it readily dimerizes to form the colorless, diamagnetic liquid dinitrogen tetroxide ($N_2O_4$):

$$ 2NO_2(g) \rightleftharpoons N_2O_4(l) \quad \Delta H^\circ = -57.2 \text{ kJ} $$

When $NO_2$ dissolves in water, it disproportionates to form **nitric acid** ($HNO_3$) and nitric oxide:

$$ 3NO_2(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2HNO_3(aq) + NO(g) $$

Nitric acid is a strong acid and a powerful oxidizing agent. While hydrochloric acid ($HCl$) cannot dissolve copper, nitric acid does so readily, reducing the nitrate ion to $NO_2$ gas in the process:

$$ Cu(s) + 4H^+(aq) + 2NO_3^-(aq) \rightarrow Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2NO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) $$

### Halides and Oxyacids of Phosphorus

Because phosphorus is in Period 3, it utilizes its empty $3d$ orbitals to expand its octet, a feature absent in nitrogen chemistry.

**Phosphorus Halides**
Phosphorus reacts directly with halogens to form two primary series of halides: trihalides ($PX_3$) and pentahalides ($PX_5$).
For example, with chlorine:

* Limited $Cl_2$: Forms **phosphorus trichloride** ($PCl_3$), a fuming liquid with a trigonal pyramidal geometry (P is $+3$).
* Excess $Cl_2$: Forms **phosphorus pentachloride** ($PCl_5$), a white solid with a trigonal bipyramidal geometry (P is $+5$).

**Phosphorus Oxyacids**
While nitrogen forms nitric acid featuring a planar $NO_3^-$ unit, phosphorus heavily favors tetrahedral oxygen coordination. The two most common oxyacids of phosphorus highlight a crucial structural difference:

```text
      O                   O
      ||                  ||
  H-O-P-O-H           H-O-P-O-H
      |                   |
      O                   H
      |
      H
      
 Phosphoric Acid     Phosphorous Acid
    (H3PO4)             (H3PO3)
 Triprotic Acid      Diprotic Acid

```

* **Phosphoric Acid ($H_3PO_4$):** Phosphorus is in the $+5$ oxidation state. All three hydrogen atoms are bonded to highly electronegative oxygen atoms, making them acidic. Thus, $H_3PO_4$ is a weak triprotic acid.
* **Phosphorous Acid ($H_3PO_3$):** Phosphorus is in the $+3$ oxidation state. Only two hydrogen atoms are bonded to oxygen atoms. The third hydrogen is bonded *directly* to the central phosphorus atom. Because the $P-H$ bond is essentially nonpolar, this hydrogen does not ionize in water. Thus, despite having three hydrogen atoms, $H_3PO_3$ is only a diprotic acid.

## Chapter Summary

The chemistry of the nonmetals is governed by their high electronegativities, high ionization energies, and their tendency to form covalent bonds or anions. As we move across the periodic table, effective nuclear charge dictates a steady increase in nonmetallic character, peaking at fluorine. However, the chemistry of nonmetals is sharply divided by the "first-member anomaly." Period 2 nonmetals (C, N, O, F) are strictly constrained by the octet rule and frequently form strong $\pi$ bonds due to their small atomic radii. In contrast, heavier nonmetals (Period 3 and below) utilize hypervalency, heavily favor single bonds, and exhibit a pronounced tendency toward catenation.

Hydrogen occupies a unique chemical space, forming covalent molecular hydrides with nonmetals, saline hydrides with active metals, and interstitial hydrides with transition metals. The Noble Gases (Group 8A), long thought unreactive due to their filled octets, can be forced to react; xenon forms distinct hypervalent fluorides and oxides. The Halogens (Group 7A) act as powerful oxidizing agents, their reactivity scaling inversely with size. Oxygen dominates its group through extreme electronegativity and the formation of diverse oxides, while heavier chalcogens like sulfur exhibit extensive catenation and varied oxidation states. Finally, the pnictogens showcase the stark contrast between nitrogen's kinetically inert, triple-bonded diatomic state and the hypervalent, singly-bonded polymeric networks of phosphorus.
