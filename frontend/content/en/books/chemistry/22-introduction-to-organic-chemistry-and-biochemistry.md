Life is governed by the same chemical laws that rule inanimate matter, yet it relies almost entirely on the astonishing versatility of a single element: carbon. In this chapter, we transition from inorganic principles into the vast domain of organic chemistry. We will explore how carbon's unique ability to form intricate chains and rings gives rise to a limitless variety of hydrocarbons. From there, we will examine how specific functional groups dictate molecular reactivity. Finally, we will see how these organic building blocks assemble into the magnificent biopolymers—proteins and carbohydrates—that sustain life itself.

## 22.1 General Characteristics of Organic Molecules

Historically, chemistry was divided into two distinct branches: inorganic chemistry, which studied matter from nonliving sources (like minerals), and organic chemistry, which studied matter derived from living organisms. Early chemists believed that organic compounds contained a mystical "vital force" that could only be generated by living things. This concept was dismantled in 1828 when Friedrich Wöhler accidentally synthesized urea, an organic compound found in urine, by heating ammonium cyanate, a purely inorganic salt.

Today, **organic chemistry** is defined as the study of carbon-containing compounds. While organic compounds are the foundation of biochemistry (DNA, proteins, and carbohydrates), they also make up pharmaceuticals, plastics, synthetic fibers, and fuels.

### The Uniqueness of Carbon

Carbon is not the most abundant element in the Earth's crust, nor is it the most abundant in the universe. Yet, millions of organic compounds are known, vastly outnumbering inorganic compounds. This staggering diversity arises from carbon's unique position in the periodic table and its specific bonding characteristics, previously introduced in Chapters 7 and 8.

1. **Tetravalency:** Carbon has an electron configuration of $[He] 2s^2 2p^2$, giving it four valence electrons. To achieve an octet, carbon forms four covalent bonds. Depending on the hybridization of its atomic orbitals ($sp^3$, $sp^2$, or $sp$), carbon can form single, double, or triple bonds with other atoms.
2. **Catenation:** Carbon possesses a remarkable ability to bond with other carbon atoms to form stable, extended chains and rings—a property known as **catenation**. While other elements like silicon can also form chains, Si–Si bonds are relatively weak and highly reactive. The carbon-carbon bond is exceptionally strong and stable under a wide range of conditions.
3. **Bonding with Heteroatoms:** In addition to bonding with itself and hydrogen, carbon frequently forms strong covalent bonds with oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, and the halogens. Atoms other than carbon and hydrogen in an organic molecule are collectively called **heteroatoms**.

### Structural Diversity

Because carbon can bond to itself in continuous chains, organic molecules can range from a simple molecule with one carbon atom (like methane, $CH_4$) to complex polymers containing hundreds of thousands of carbon atoms. The arrangement of a carbon framework can take several forms:

* **Straight (Unbranched) Chains:** Carbon atoms are linked in a continuous sequence.
* **Branched Chains:** One or more carbon atoms branch off from the main, continuous chain.
* **Cyclic Rings:** The carbon chain connects back on itself to form a closed loop.

This structural diversity leads to the phenomenon of **isomerism** (first introduced in Chapter 21 for coordination complexes). In organic chemistry, structural isomers are molecules that share the same molecular formula but have different bonding arrangements.

### Representing Organic Molecules

Due to the complex structures of organic molecules, relying solely on molecular formulas (which only indicate the types and numbers of atoms, e.g., $C_4H_{10}$) is insufficient, as it does not distinguish between isomers. Chemists use several conventions to represent organic structures:

**1. Full Structural Formulas (Lewis Structures)**
These diagrams explicitly show every atom and every covalent bond in the molecule. While extremely clear, they are cumbersome to draw for large molecules.

```text
Full Structural Formula for Butane (C₄H₁₀):

      H   H   H   H
      |   |   |   |
    H-C---C---C---C-H
      |   |   |   |
      H   H   H   H

```

**2. Condensed Structural Formulas**
In condensed formulas, the carbon-hydrogen and carbon-carbon single bonds are omitted. Each carbon atom is written alongside the hydrogen atoms bonded directly to it.

* *Example:* Butane is written as $CH_3-CH_2-CH_2-CH_3$ or simply $CH_3CH_2CH_2CH_3$.
* *Branched Example:* Isobutane (an isomer of butane) is written as $CH_3CH(CH_3)CH_3$, where the parentheses indicate a branching group attached to the preceding carbon.

**3. Skeletal (Line-Angle) Structures**
For speed and clarity, organic chemists heavily rely on skeletal structures. In these representations, the carbon framework is drawn as a continuous, zig-zag line.

* **Rule 1:** Every vertex (point where two lines meet) and every line terminus represents a carbon atom.
* **Rule 2:** Hydrogen atoms bonded to carbons are generally omitted. It is assumed that every carbon has enough hydrogen atoms attached to fulfill its tetravalency (four bonds).
* **Rule 3:** Heteroatoms (O, N, Cl, etc.) and any hydrogens bonded directly to heteroatoms *must* be explicitly drawn.

```text
Skeletal Structure Comparison:

     /\/\         = Butane (4 carbons in a chain)

      /\
     /  \         = Isobutane (branched chain, 4 carbons total)
      | 

```

### Physical Properties of Organic Molecules

The physical properties of organic molecules differ significantly from those of typical inorganic salts. These differences stem primarily from the nature of covalent bonding and intermolecular forces (discussed in Chapter 10).

* **Melting and Boiling Points:** Because organic compounds consist of discrete molecules held together by relatively weak intermolecular forces (London dispersion forces, dipole-dipole interactions, and hydrogen bonds), they generally have much lower melting and boiling points than ionic compounds. Many organic molecules with low molar masses are gases or liquids at room temperature.
* **Solubility:** The solubility of organic compounds follows the "like dissolves like" principle. Hydrocarbons (containing only C and H) are entirely nonpolar and thus insoluble in polar solvents like water. However, they dissolve readily in nonpolar organic solvents like hexane or toluene. If an organic molecule contains a polar functional group (like an $-OH$ group), it may be soluble in water, provided the nonpolar carbon chain is not too long.
* **Conductivity:** Organic compounds do not dissociate into ions in solution (with the exception of organic acids and bases, such as carboxylic acids and amines). Therefore, most aqueous solutions of organic compounds do not conduct electricity; they are nonelectrolytes.
* **Combustibility:** A hallmark chemical property of almost all organic compounds is that they are highly combustible. As established in Chapter 12 (Thermochemistry), the combustion of organic matter in the presence of oxygen is highly exothermic, yielding carbon dioxide and water as products.

## 22.2 Saturated Hydrocarbons: Alkanes and Cycloalkanes

Hydrocarbons are the simplest organic compounds, composed exclusively of carbon and hydrogen atoms. They are broadly categorized based on the types of carbon-carbon bonds they contain. **Saturated hydrocarbons** are those in which all carbon-carbon bonds are single bonds. The term "saturated" indicates that the carbon framework holds the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms. This category is further divided into open-chain alkanes and cyclic cycloalkanes.

### Alkanes: Structure and General Formula

Alkanes are acyclic (non-ring) saturated hydrocarbons. Because every carbon atom in an alkane forms four single covalent bonds, the carbon centers are $sp^3$ hybridized and possess a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of approximately $109.5^\circ$ (as detailed in Chapter 8).

All non-cyclic alkanes fit the general molecular formula **$C_n H_{2n+2}$**, where $n$ is an integer representing the number of carbon atoms.

The simplest alkanes form a homologous series—a group of compounds that differ from adjacent members by a repeating unit, in this case, a methylene group ($-CH_2-$). The names of the first ten unbranched (straight-chain) alkanes serve as the foundation for all organic nomenclature.

| Number of Carbons ($n$) | Name | Molecular Formula | Condensed Structural Formula |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Methane | $CH_4$ | $CH_4$ |
| 2 | Ethane | $C_2H_6$ | $CH_3CH_3$ |
| 3 | Propane | $C_3H_8$ | $CH_3CH_2CH_3$ |
| 4 | Butane | $C_4H_{10}$ | $CH_3CH_2CH_2CH_3$ |
| 5 | Pentane | $C_5H_{12}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_3CH_3$ |
| 6 | Hexane | $C_6H_{14}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_4CH_3$ |
| 7 | Heptane | $C_7H_{16}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_5CH_3$ |
| 8 | Octane | $C_8H_{18}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_6CH_3$ |
| 9 | Nonane | $C_9H_{20}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_7CH_3$ |
| 10 | Decane | $C_{10}H_{22}$ | $CH_3(CH_2)_8CH_3$ |

### Structural Isomerism in Alkanes

As the number of carbon atoms increases beyond three, it becomes possible to arrange the atoms in more than one way, leading to structural (constitutional) isomers. For example, while there is only one way to connect the carbons in propane ($C_3H_8$), butane ($C_4H_{10}$) has two isomers, and pentane ($C_5H_{12}$) has three. As $n$ increases, the number of possible isomers grows exponentially (e.g., decane has 75 isomers).

```text
Structural Isomers of Pentane (C₅H₁₂)

1. n-Pentane (Straight chain)
   CH3-CH2-CH2-CH2-CH3

2. Isopentane (Branched chain)
       CH3
       |
   CH3-CH-CH2-CH3

3. Neopentane (Highly branched)
        CH3
        |
    CH3-C-CH3
        |
        CH3

```

Though isomers share the same molecular formula, their different structures result in different physical properties. For instance, highly branched isomers like neopentane are more compact, leading to weaker London dispersion forces and lower boiling points compared to their straight-chain counterparts.

### IUPAC Nomenclature of Alkanes

To systematically name the vast number of possible organic structures, chemists use rules established by the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC).

When alkanes lose one hydrogen atom, they become substituent groups called **alkyl groups**. They are named by replacing the "-ane" suffix of the parent alkane with "-yl" (e.g., a $-CH_3$ group is a *methyl* group, $-CH_2CH_3$ is an *ethyl* group).

The basic IUPAC rules for naming branched alkanes are:

1. **Find the longest continuous carbon chain:** This determines the parent alkane name. If a molecule has a chain of six carbons, the parent name is *hexane*.
2. **Number the chain:** Number the carbon atoms in the parent chain starting from the end closest to a substituent (branch). This ensures the substituents receive the lowest possible numbers (locants).
3. **Identify and name substituents:** Determine the names of the alkyl groups attached to the parent chain.
4. **Assemble the name:** List substituents alphabetically in front of the parent name. Use prefixes (*di-*, *tri-*, *tetra-*) if multiple identical substituents are present, and separate numbers from words with hyphens and numbers from numbers with commas. Note that prefixes like *di-* and *tri-* are ignored when alphabetizing.

**Example Application:**
Consider the structure of "isopentane" shown in the diagram above.

* The longest continuous chain has 4 carbons $\rightarrow$ parent is **butane**.
* Numbering from the left gives the attached methyl group the locant 2. (Numbering from the right would give it 3, which is higher).
* The substituent is a **methyl** group.
* The systematic IUPAC name is **2-methylbutane**.

### Cycloalkanes

Carbon chains can also join ends to form closed rings. These are known as **cycloalkanes**. Because joining the ends of a chain requires the removal of two hydrogen atoms (one from each terminal carbon), the general formula for a cycloalkane is **$C_n H_{2n}$** (where $n \ge 3$).

Cycloalkanes are named simply by adding the prefix **cyclo-** to the name of the unbranched alkane with the same number of carbons.

```text
Common Cycloalkanes (Skeletal Structures)

    /\            +---+           ___            ___
   /__\           |   |          /   \          /   \
                  +---+          \___/         |     |
                                                \___/
Cyclopropane    Cyclobutane    Cyclopentane   Cyclohexane
   (C₃H₆)         (C₄H₈)         (C₅H₁₀)        (C₆H₁₂)

```

#### Ring Strain and Conformation

The idealized tetrahedral bond angle for $sp^3$ hybridized carbon is $109.5^\circ$. In small rings like cyclopropane, the geometry forces the interior carbon-carbon bond angles to be $60^\circ$. This severe deviation from the ideal tetrahedral angle causes substantial **angle strain** (or ring strain), making cyclopropane highly reactive and unstable compared to open-chain alkanes. Cyclobutane experiences similar, though slightly less severe, strain with internal angles near $90^\circ$.

Cyclohexane ($C_6H_{12}$), however, is exceptionally stable. If cyclohexane were a flat, planar hexagon, its internal angles would be $120^\circ$, resulting in strain. To relieve this, the molecule puckers out of the plane into a three-dimensional **chair conformation**. In this puckered arrangement, every carbon-carbon-carbon bond angle is almost exactly $109.5^\circ$, and all adjacent carbon-hydrogen bonds are perfectly staggered, rendering cyclohexane nearly strain-free.

## 22.3 Unsaturated Hydrocarbons: Alkenes and Alkynes

If a hydrocarbon contains at least one multiple (double or triple) carbon-carbon bond, it is classified as an **unsaturated hydrocarbon**. The term "unsaturated" implies that the molecule contains fewer than the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms for its carbon framework. To achieve saturation (converting multiple bonds to single bonds), additional atoms must be added to the molecule. The two primary classes of unsaturated, acyclic hydrocarbons are alkenes and alkynes.

### Alkenes: Structure and Nomenclature

**Alkenes** (also known as olefins) are hydrocarbons that contain one or more carbon-carbon double bonds ($C=C$). The presence of the double bond alters the geometry and properties of the molecule significantly compared to alkanes.

As discussed in Chapter 8 (Valence Bond Theory), the carbon atoms involved in a double bond are $sp^2$ hybridized. This hybridization results in a trigonal planar geometry around each carbon atom, with bond angles of approximately $120^\circ$. The double bond itself consists of one strong sigma ($\sigma$) bond formed by the head-on overlap of $sp^2$ orbitals, and one weaker pi ($\pi$) bond formed by the side-to-side overlap of unhybridized $p$ orbitals above and below the bonding axis.

The general molecular formula for an acyclic alkene with one double bond is **$C_n H_{2n}$**.

The simplest alkene is ethene ($C_2H_4$), commonly known as ethylene.

```text
Structure of Ethene (C₂H₄):

      H       H
       \     /
        C = C
       /     \
      H       H

```

**Naming Alkenes:**
IUPAC nomenclature for alkenes follows rules similar to those for alkanes, with three key modifications:

1. The parent chain must be the longest continuous carbon chain that *contains the double bond*.
2. The suffix of the parent chain changes from "-ane" to **"-ene"**.
3. The chain is numbered from the end that gives the carbon atoms of the double bond the lowest possible locants (numbers). The position of the double bond is indicated by the lower of the two numbers assigned to the double-bonded carbons.

For example, a four-carbon chain with a double bond between the first and second carbons is **1-butene** ($CH_2=CHCH_2CH_3$), whereas if the double bond is between the second and third carbons, it is **2-butene** ($CH_3CH=CHCH_3$).

### Geometric (Cis-Trans) Isomerism in Alkenes

In alkanes, there is free rotation around carbon-carbon single bonds. However, the $\pi$ bond in an alkene locks the carbon atoms in place; rotating the molecule around the double bond would require breaking the parallel overlap of the $p$ orbitals, which takes a significant amount of energy.

This restricted rotation gives rise to a specific type of stereoisomerism known as **geometric isomerism** or ***cis-trans* isomerism**. If each carbon atom of the double bond is attached to two different groups, the molecule can exist in two distinct spatial arrangements:

* ***Cis* isomer:** The two similar groups (or the two main carbon chain extensions) are on the *same side* of the double bond.
* ***Trans* isomer:** The two similar groups are on *opposite sides* of the double bond.

```text
Geometric Isomers of 2-Butene:

      cis-2-butene                 trans-2-butene

      H₃C       CH₃                H₃C       H
         \     /                      \     /
          C = C                        C = C
         /     \                      /     \
        H       H                    H       CH₃

(Similar groups are on the        (Similar groups are on
 same side of the double bond)     opposite sides)

```

Because *cis* and *trans* isomers have different shapes, they often exhibit different physical properties, such as differing boiling points, melting points, and dipole moments.

### Alkynes: Structure and Nomenclature

**Alkynes** are hydrocarbons that contain one or more carbon-carbon triple bonds ($C \equiv C$).

The carbon atoms in a triple bond are $sp$ hybridized. This creates a linear molecular geometry around the triple bond with bond angles of $180^\circ$. A triple bond is composed of one $\sigma$ bond and two mutually perpendicular $\pi$ bonds. Because of the linear geometry, alkynes do not exhibit *cis-trans* isomerism.

The general molecular formula for an acyclic alkyne with one triple bond is **$C_n H_{2n-2}$**.

The simplest alkyne is ethyne ($C_2H_2$), widely known by its common name, acetylene, which is used as a fuel in high-temperature welding torches.

```text
Structure of Ethyne (C₂H₂):

        H - C ≡ C - H

```

**Naming Alkynes:**
The nomenclature for alkynes is nearly identical to that of alkenes, but the suffix used is **"-yne"**. The main chain must contain the triple bond, and it is numbered to give the triple bond the lowest possible locant. For example, $CH_3-C \equiv C-CH_3$ is 2-butyne.

### Chemical Properties: Addition Reactions

Unlike saturated alkanes, which are relatively unreactive and primarily undergo combustion or free-radical substitution, unsaturated hydrocarbons are chemically reactive. Their reactivity is defined by the electron-rich $\pi$ bonds.

The most characteristic reaction of alkenes and alkynes is the **addition reaction**. In an addition reaction, the relatively weak $\pi$ bond is broken, and its electrons are used to form two new $\sigma$ bonds with incoming atoms. The overall framework of the molecule remains intact, but the degree of saturation increases.

**1. Hydrogenation:** The addition of hydrogen gas ($H_2$) across a double or triple bond in the presence of a metal catalyst (such as Pt, Pd, or Ni) converts unsaturated hydrocarbons into saturated alkanes.

$$CH_2=CH_2 + H_2 \xrightarrow{Pt} CH_3-CH_3$$

**2. Halogenation:** The addition of a halogen molecule (like $Br_2$ or $Cl_2$) across a double bond. This reaction occurs rapidly at room temperature without a catalyst. The addition of bromine is a classic qualitative test for unsaturation; the reddish-brown color of a bromine solution rapidly fades to colorless as it reacts with an alkene or alkyne.

$$CH_2=CH_2 + Br_2 \rightarrow BrCH_2-CH_2Br$$

**3. Hydration:** The addition of water ($H_2O$, effectively $H-$ and $-OH$) across a double bond in the presence of a strong acid catalyst yields an alcohol.

$$CH_2=CH_2 + H_2O \xrightarrow{H^+} CH_3-CH_2OH$$

Because alkynes possess two $\pi$ bonds, they can undergo addition reactions twice—first converting the triple bond to a double bond, and then converting the double bond to a single bond if excess reagent is present.

## 22.4 Aromatic Hydrocarbons

In the early days of organic chemistry, the term "aromatic" was used to describe a class of compounds extracted from plants that possessed distinct, often pleasant, fragrances (such as vanilla or cinnamon). Today, the term has lost its sensory meaning and instead refers to a specific structural and electronic feature: the presence of a highly stable, planar ring of atoms with delocalized $\pi$ electrons.

The fundamental building block of aromatic hydrocarbons (also called arenes) is **benzene**, a cyclic hydrocarbon with the molecular formula $C_6H_6$.

### The Structure of Benzene

The structure of benzene puzzled 19th-century chemists. Its molecular formula, $C_6H_6$, suggests a high degree of unsaturation compared to the corresponding saturated cycloalkane, cyclohexane ($C_6H_{12}$). However, benzene does not behave like a typical alkene or alkyne; it is exceptionally stable and unreactive toward reagents that typically attack $\pi$ bonds.

In 1865, August Kekulé proposed that benzene consists of a hexagonal ring of carbon atoms with alternating single and double bonds, and that the molecule rapidly flips between two identical structures. Today, we understand this phenomenon not as a rapid flipping, but as **resonance** (introduced in Chapter 7).

The actual structure of benzene is a hybrid of the two Kekulé resonance structures.

```text
Kekulé Resonance Structures              Delocalized Model

       H                 H                       H
       |                 |                       |
       C                 C                       C
     /   \\            //  \                   /   \
   H-C     C-H  <--> H-C    C-H      ==>     H-C   O C-H
     \\   /            \   //                  \   /
       C                 C                       C
       |                 |                       |
       H                 H                       H

```

To explain this stability using Valence Bond Theory and Molecular Orbital Theory (Chapter 8), we examine the hybridization of the carbon atoms. Every carbon atom in benzene is $sp^2$ hybridized, possessing a trigonal planar geometry that creates a perfectly flat, hexagonal ring. The unhybridized $p$ orbital on each of the six carbon atoms is oriented perpendicular to the plane of the ring.

Because all six $p$ orbitals are parallel and adjacent, they overlap continuously around the entire ring. This creates a donut-shaped cloud of electron density above and below the plane of the carbon framework. These six $\pi$ electrons are completely **delocalized**—they are not confined between any two specific carbon atoms but are shared equally among all six. This delocalization imparts immense thermodynamic stability to the molecule, a property known as **aromaticity**. To represent this delocalized $\pi$ system in skeletal structures, chemists frequently draw a hexagon with a circle inside.

### Nomenclature of Aromatic Compounds

The nomenclature of benzene derivatives depends on the number and positions of the substituents attached to the aromatic ring.

**1. Monosubstituted Benzenes**
For a benzene ring with a single substituent, the naming is straightforward: the substituent name is simply added as a prefix to "benzene".

* *Examples:* Chlorobenzene, nitrobenzene, ethylbenzene.

However, several monosubstituted benzenes have historical common names that are accepted by IUPAC and are used extensively in organic chemistry.

* **Toluene:** Methylbenzene ($C_6H_5CH_3$)
* **Phenol:** Hydroxybenzene ($C_6H_5OH$)
* **Aniline:** Aminobenzene ($C_6H_5NH_2$)

**2. Disubstituted Benzenes**
When two groups are attached to a benzene ring, their relative positions must be indicated. This can be done using numbers or, more traditionally, using the prefixes *ortho-*, *meta-*, and *para-* (often abbreviated as *o-*, *m-*, and *p-*).

* ***ortho* (1,2-substitution):** The substituents are on adjacent carbon atoms.
* ***meta* (1,3-substitution):** The substituents are separated by one carbon atom.
* ***para* (1,4-substitution):** The substituents are on opposite sides of the ring.

```text
Positional Isomers of Dimethylbenzene (Xylene):

      CH₃               CH₃               CH₃
      |                 |                 |
    /   \             /   \             /   \
  |   O   |-CH₃     |   O   |         |   O   |
    \ _ /             \ _ /             \ _ /
                        |                 |
                       CH₃               CH₃

  ortho-xylene      meta-xylene       para-xylene
 (1,2-dimethyl-    (1,3-dimethyl-    (1,4-dimethyl-
    benzene)          benzene)          benzene)

```

**3. Polysubstituted Benzenes**
If three or more substituents are present, the *ortho*, *meta*, and *para* system cannot be used. Instead, the carbon atoms of the ring are numbered to give the lowest possible combination of locants for the substituents. The substituents are then listed alphabetically. If a common name (like toluene) is used as the parent name, the carbon bearing that parent substituent is designated as C1.

### Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)

Aromaticity is not limited to single rings. **Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)** consist of two or more benzene rings fused together, meaning they share adjacent carbon atoms. Like benzene, these fused systems feature highly delocalized $\pi$ electrons.

* **Naphthalene ($C_{10}H_8$):** The simplest PAH, consisting of two fused benzene rings. It was historically used as the active ingredient in mothballs.
* **Anthracene ($C_{14}H_{10}$):** Three fused rings in a linear arrangement.
* **Phenanthrene ($C_{14}H_{10}$):** Three fused rings in an angular arrangement.

### Chemical Reactivity: Substitution vs. Addition

Because benzene appears structurally similar to alkenes (with its "double bonds"), one might expect it to undergo the rapid addition reactions typical of unsaturated hydrocarbons (Section 22.3). However, adding atoms across the $\pi$ bonds of benzene would destroy the continuous cyclic overlap of $p$ orbitals, thereby destroying the aromatic stability.

Consequently, aromatic compounds highly resist addition reactions. Instead, they primarily undergo **substitution reactions**, in which a hydrogen atom on the aromatic ring is replaced by an electrophile (an electron-seeking atom or group).

$$C_6H_6 + Br_2 \xrightarrow{FeBr_3} C_6H_5Br + HBr$$

In the reaction above, benzene reacts with bromine, but only in the presence of a strong Lewis acid catalyst ($FeBr_3$). Rather than adding bromine across a double bond, one bromine atom substitutes for a hydrogen atom, generating bromobenzene and leaving the stable, delocalized aromatic ring intact.

## 22.5 Functional Groups with Oxygen (Alcohols, Ethers, Carbonyls)

While the carbon-hydrogen framework provides the structural backbone of organic molecules, their chemical reactivity and physical properties are largely determined by **functional groups**. A functional group is a specific atom, or a group of atoms, that exhibits a characteristic chemical behavior regardless of the size or complexity of the rest of the molecule.

Oxygen is one of the most common heteroatoms found in functional groups. Because oxygen is significantly more electronegative than carbon and hydrogen, its presence introduces bond polarity, altering intermolecular forces and creating specific sites for chemical reactions. In organic structures, a generic carbon-containing alkyl group attached to a functional group is frequently represented by the letter **R** (and **R'** if a second, potentially different, alkyl group is present).

```text
General Structures of Oxygen-Containing Functional Groups:

  Alcohol        Ether         Aldehyde        Ketone
   R-O-H        R-O-R'           O               O
                                //              //
                              R-C-H           R-C-R'

 Carboxylic Acid      Ester
       O                O
      //               //
    R-C-O-H          R-C-O-R'

```

### Alcohols ($R-OH$)

An **alcohol** is an organic compound containing a **hydroxyl group** ($-OH$) bonded to an $sp^3$ hybridized carbon atom.

**Nomenclature:** IUPAC rules dictate that the parent chain must contain the carbon bonded to the hydroxyl group. The "-e" ending of the corresponding alkane is replaced by the suffix **"-ol"**. The chain is numbered to give the hydroxyl-bearing carbon the lowest possible locant. For example, $CH_3CH_2OH$ is ethanol, and $CH_3CH(OH)CH_3$ is 2-propanol.

**Classification:** Alcohols are classified by the number of carbon atoms directly bonded to the carbon bearing the $-OH$ group:

* **Primary ($1^\circ$) Alcohol:** The $-OH$ carbon is bonded to one other carbon.
* **Secondary ($2^\circ$) Alcohol:** The $-OH$ carbon is bonded to two other carbons.
* **Tertiary ($3^\circ$) Alcohol:** The $-OH$ carbon is bonded to three other carbons.

```text
Classification of Alcohols:

      H                 R'                R'
      |                 |                 |
    R-C-O-H           R-C-O-H           R-C-O-H
      |                 |                 |
      H                 H                 R''
   Primary          Secondary          Tertiary
 (1° Alcohol)      (2° Alcohol)      (3° Alcohol)

```

**Properties:** The highly polar $O-H$ bond allows alcohol molecules to participate in extensive intermolecular hydrogen bonding. Consequently, alcohols have significantly higher boiling points than alkanes of comparable molecular weight. Furthermore, the ability to hydrogen bond with water makes lower molecular weight alcohols (like methanol, ethanol, and propanol) completely miscible with water. As the nonpolar hydrophobic alkyl chain ($R$) grows longer, however, water solubility rapidly decreases.

### Ethers ($R-O-R'$)

An **ether** consists of an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl or aryl (aromatic) groups.

**Nomenclature:** Simple ethers are often named by stating the two alkyl groups attached to the oxygen in alphabetical order, followed by the word "ether" (e.g., $CH_3-O-CH_2CH_3$ is ethyl methyl ether). The IUPAC system treats the shorter alkyl-oxygen chain as an alkoxy substituent on the longer parent alkane chain (e.g., methoxyethane).

**Properties:** Because the oxygen atom in an ether is bonded to carbons rather than hydrogens, ether molecules cannot hydrogen bond *with each other*. As a result, their boiling points are much lower than those of isomeric alcohols and are closer to those of alkanes of similar mass. However, the oxygen atom still possesses two lone pairs, allowing ethers to act as hydrogen-bond acceptors with water, granting lower ethers moderate aqueous solubility. Chemically, ethers are relatively unreactive, making them excellent, chemically inert solvents for organic reactions.

### The Carbonyl Group

The **carbonyl group** consists of a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom ($C=O$). The carbonyl carbon is $sp^2$ hybridized, resulting in a trigonal planar geometry with bond angles of roughly $120^\circ$. Because oxygen is highly electronegative, the $\pi$ electrons of the double bond are pulled toward the oxygen, creating a strong permanent dipole. The carbonyl carbon is therefore electron-deficient (electrophilic), which heavily dictates the reactivity of the molecules that contain it.

#### Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehydes and ketones are structurally similar, differing only in the placement of the carbonyl group.

* **Aldehydes ($R-CHO$):** The carbonyl group is located at the *end* of the carbon chain. The carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom. In IUPAC nomenclature, the parent alkane "-e" is replaced with **"-al"** (e.g., $CH_3CHO$ is ethanal).
* **Ketones ($R-CO-R'$):** The carbonyl group is located *within* the carbon chain. The carbonyl carbon is bonded to two other carbon atoms. The IUPAC suffix is **"-one"** (e.g., $CH_3COCH_3$ is propanone, commonly known as acetone).

Both aldehydes and ketones experience dipole-dipole interactions, giving them higher boiling points than alkanes but lower than those of alcohols (since they cannot self-hydrogen bond).

#### Carboxylic Acids ($R-COOH$)

When a carbonyl group is directly adjacent to a hydroxyl group, the combined entity is called a **carboxyl group** ($-COOH$). Compounds containing this group are **carboxylic acids**.

**Nomenclature:** The parent chain is named by replacing the "-e" of the corresponding alkane with **"-oic acid"**. For example, $CH_3COOH$ is ethanoic acid (widely known by its common name, acetic acid, the primary organic component of vinegar).

**Properties:** The presence of both a highly polar $C=O$ group and an $O-H$ group allows carboxylic acids to form strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds, often existing as strongly bound dimers in the liquid and gas phases. They exhibit extremely high boiling points.

More importantly, as their name implies, these compounds are acidic. The hydrogen atom of the hydroxyl group can dissociate as a proton ($H^+$) in aqueous solution. This acidity is largely due to the resonance stabilization of the resulting carboxylate anion ($R-COO^-$), where the negative charge is delocalized equally over both oxygen atoms (as discussed in Chapter 16).

#### Esters ($R-COO-R'$)

**Esters** are derivatives of carboxylic acids in which the acidic hydrogen atom has been replaced by an alkyl group. They are typically formed through a condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol (a process called esterification), producing water as a byproduct:

$$R-COOH + R'-OH \rightleftharpoons R-COO-R' + H_2O$$

**Nomenclature:** Esters are named as two words. The first word is the alkyl group from the alcohol ($R'$). The second word is derived from the carboxylic acid, replacing "-oic acid" with **"-oate"**. For instance, the reaction of ethanol and ethanoic acid produces *ethyl ethanoate*.

**Properties:** Esters cannot form hydrogen bonds with each other, leading to lower boiling points than their parent carboxylic acids. Esters are famous in organic chemistry for their highly pleasant, fruity aromas. The scents of bananas, pineapples, apples, and strawberries are all primarily due to specific ester molecules present in the fruit.

## 22.6 Functional Groups with Nitrogen (Amines and Amides)

Nitrogen is another vital heteroatom in organic chemistry, serving as a cornerstone for biological molecules such as amino acids, DNA, and neurotransmitters, as well as many synthetic drugs and dyes. Situated between carbon and oxygen in the periodic table, nitrogen is more electronegative than carbon but less so than oxygen. A neutral nitrogen atom typically forms three covalent bonds and retains one nonbonding pair (lone pair) of electrons, making it electron-rich and dictating much of its chemical behavior.

The two most prominent families of nitrogen-containing organic compounds are amines and amides.

### Amines: Organic Derivatives of Ammonia

**Amines** can be conceptualized as organic derivatives of ammonia ($NH_3$), in which one or more of the hydrogen atoms have been replaced by alkyl or aryl (carbon-containing) groups.

**Classification:**
The classification of amines differs fundamentally from that of alcohols. While alcohols are classified by the carbon *attached* to the hydroxyl group, amines are classified by the number of carbon groups attached directly to the **nitrogen atom** itself:

* **Primary ($1^\circ$) Amine:** One carbon group is attached to the nitrogen ($R-NH_2$).
* **Secondary ($2^\circ$) Amine:** Two carbon groups are attached to the nitrogen ($R_2NH$).
* **Tertiary ($3^\circ$) Amine:** Three carbon groups are attached to the nitrogen ($R_3N$).

```text
Classification of Amines:

       H                 R'                R'
       |                 |                 |
     R-N-H             R-N-H             R-N-R''

 Primary (1°)      Secondary (2°)     Tertiary (3°)
    Amine              Amine              Amine

```

**Nomenclature:**
In simple amines, the alkyl groups attached to the nitrogen are listed alphabetically, followed by the suffix **"-amine"** (e.g., $CH_3NH_2$ is methylamine, and $CH_3-NH-CH_2CH_3$ is ethylmethylamine).

In the IUPAC systematic method, the longest carbon chain attached to the nitrogen forms the parent name, replacing the "-e" of the alkane with **"-amine"**. For secondary and tertiary amines, the additional alkyl groups attached to the nitrogen are treated as substituents. To indicate that these substituents are on the nitrogen atom and not on the carbon chain, the prefix **"$N$-"** is used.

* *Example 1:* $CH_3CH_2CH_2NH_2$ is 1-propanamine.
* *Example 2:* $CH_3CH_2-N(CH_3)_2$ is $N,N$-dimethylethanamine.

**Physical and Chemical Properties:**
Because the $N-H$ bond is polar, primary and secondary amines can form intermolecular hydrogen bonds. However, because nitrogen is less electronegative than oxygen, these hydrogen bonds are weaker than those in alcohols. Consequently, amines generally have boiling points that are higher than those of alkanes but lower than those of alcohols of comparable molecular weight. Tertiary amines lack an $N-H$ bond and cannot hydrogen bond with each other, resulting in significantly lower boiling points.

Many low-molecular-weight amines possess distinct, often highly unpleasant odors, resembling raw fish or decaying organic matter. In fact, the biological compounds responsible for the smell of rotting meat are diamines aptly named *putrescine* and *cadaverine*.

Chemically, the most important feature of amines is the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom. This lone pair allows amines to act as **Brønsted-Lowry bases** (proton acceptors) and Lewis bases (electron-pair donors), much like ammonia. In aqueous solutions, amines establish an equilibrium by accepting a proton from water, producing hydroxide ions:

$$R-NH_2 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons R-NH_3^+ + OH^-$$

Because they are basic, amines react readily with strong acids (like $HCl$) to form water-soluble ammonium salts.

### Amides: Carboxylic Acid Derivatives

**Amides** are compounds in which a carbonyl group ($C=O$) is directly bonded to a nitrogen atom. They are structurally related to carboxylic acids, where the hydroxyl ($-OH$) group has been replaced by an amino group ($-NH_2$, $-NHR$, or $-NR_2$).

```text
Structure of an Amide:

         O
        //
      R-C-N-H
          |
          H
   (Primary Amide)

```

**Nomenclature:**
Amides are named by dropping the "-oic acid" or "-ic acid" ending from the parent carboxylic acid name and adding the suffix **"-amide"**. For example, the amide derived from ethanoic acid ($CH_3COOH$) is **ethanamide** ($CH_3CONH_2$).

If the nitrogen atom has alkyl groups attached (forming secondary or tertiary amides), these substituents are named using the **"$N$-"** locant system, just as with amines. For example, if a methyl group replaces one of the hydrogens on the nitrogen of ethanamide, the name becomes **$N$-methylethanamide**.

**Physical and Chemical Properties:**
Amides behave very differently from amines. The defining characteristic of an amide is the proximity of the nitrogen lone pair to the highly electronegative carbonyl oxygen. Through resonance, the lone pair on the nitrogen is delocalized into the carbonyl $\pi$ system.

```text
Resonance in Amides:

       O                  O⁻
      //                  |
    R-C-N-H₂   <-->     R-C=N⁺-H₂

```

This resonance delocalization has two profound effects:

1. **Loss of Basicity:** Because the nitrogen lone pair is tied up in resonance with the carbonyl group, it is unavailable to accept a proton. Therefore, unlike amines, **amides are not basic**; they are essentially neutral in aqueous solution.
2. **Restricted Rotation:** The partial double-bond character of the $C-N$ bond restricts free rotation, making the amide linkage rigid and planar.

Primary and secondary amides participate in exceptionally strong hydrogen bonding, as the partially negative carbonyl oxygen acts as a powerful hydrogen-bond acceptor, and the partially positive hydrogens on the nitrogen act as strong donors. As a result, amides generally have very high melting and boiling points; with the exception of formamide (methanamide), all simple unsubstituted amides are solids at room temperature.

The amide functional group is of paramount importance in biochemistry. When amino acids link together to form proteins, the bonds that connect them are amide linkages, historically referred to by biologists as **peptide bonds**. The rigidity of this bond and its capacity for hydrogen bonding are the primary forces dictating the three-dimensional structures of proteins.

## 22.7 Introduction to Proteins and Carbohydrates

Biochemistry is the study of the chemical substances and processes that occur in plants, animals, and microorganisms. The structural diversity of carbon, explored throughout this chapter, reaches its pinnacle in biological macromolecules. These macromolecules are primarily polymers—large molecules built from the repetitive linking of smaller subunits called monomers. Two of the most fundamentally important classes of biopolymers are proteins and carbohydrates.

### Proteins: The Machinery of Life

Proteins are structurally complex and functionally diverse macromolecules. They serve as structural components of tissues (collagen, keratin), biological catalysts (enzymes), transporters of molecules (hemoglobin), and chemical messengers (hormones). Despite their vast array of functions, all proteins are polymers constructed from the same set of 20 fundamental building blocks: **amino acids**.

#### Amino Acids and the Peptide Bond

As the name implies, an amino acid contains two of the functional groups discussed earlier in this chapter: an amine group ($-NH_2$) and a carboxylic acid group ($-COOH$). In the amino acids that construct proteins (specifically $\alpha$-amino acids), both of these functional groups are attached to the same central carbon atom, known as the $\alpha$-carbon.

Also attached to the $\alpha$-carbon is a hydrogen atom and a variable side chain, designated as the **R group**.

```text
General Structure of an α-Amino Acid:

         H
         |
    H₂N--C--COOH
         |
         R

```

The 20 standard amino acids differ *only* in the structure of their R group. These side chains can be nonpolar (e.g., a methyl group in alanine), polar and uncharged (e.g., a hydroxyl group in serine), acidic (e.g., a second carboxyl group in aspartic acid), or basic (e.g., an additional amine group in lysine).

Proteins are formed when amino acids link together via a condensation reaction. The carboxyl group of one amino acid reacts with the amine group of the next, releasing a molecule of water. The resulting covalent bond is an amide linkage (as discussed in Section 22.6), but in biochemistry, it is specifically called a **peptide bond**.

```text
Formation of a Peptide Bond:

     H   O           H   O                 H   O   H   O
     |  //           |  //                 |  //   |  //
H₂N--C--C--OH  +  H--N--C--C--OH  ---> H₂N--C--C---N--C--C--OH  +  H₂O
     |               |                     |       |
     R₁              R₂                    R₁      R₂
                                               (Peptide
Amino Acid 1      Amino Acid 2                   Bond)

```

A chain of two amino acids is a dipeptide; a chain of many is a polypeptide. When a polypeptide chain folds into a specific, functional three-dimensional shape, it is recognized as a protein.

#### Levels of Protein Structure

The function of a protein is inextricably linked to its three-dimensional shape, which is determined by four levels of structural organization:

1. **Primary Structure:** The linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain. This sequence is directly dictated by genetic information (DNA).
2. **Secondary Structure:** Localized folding patterns within the chain, primarily stabilized by hydrogen bonding between the $N-H$ and $C=O$ groups of the peptide backbone. The two most common secondary structures are the **$\alpha$-helix** (a coiled spring shape) and the **$\beta$-pleated sheet** (a folded, accordion-like structure).
3. **Tertiary Structure:** The overall three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide chain. This conformation is driven by interactions between the R groups, including hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds (salt bridges), hydrophobic interactions, and covalent disulfide bonds ($-S-S-$) between cysteine amino acids.
4. **Quaternary Structure:** If a functional protein consists of two or more independent polypeptide chains (subunits), the arrangement of these subunits relative to one another is the quaternary structure. Hemoglobin, which contains four subunits, is a classic example.

### Carbohydrates: Energy and Structure

**Carbohydrates** are organic molecules composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a ratio of $1:2:1$. Their general empirical formula, $C_n(H_2O)_m$, led early chemists to mistakenly believe these molecules were "hydrates of carbon."

Chemically, carbohydrates are **polyhydroxy aldehydes** or **polyhydroxy ketones**, or large molecules that break down into these simpler units upon hydrolysis. They serve primarily as short-term energy storage (like glucose and starch) and structural support (like cellulose in plants).

#### Monosaccharides

The simplest carbohydrates are the monosaccharides (simple sugars), which cannot be hydrolyzed into smaller carbohydrate units. Most common monosaccharides contain between three and six carbon atoms.

**Glucose** ($C_6H_{12}O_6$) is the most abundant monosaccharide and the primary fuel source for cellular respiration. In its open-chain form, it is an *aldose*—it contains an aldehyde group at the end of a six-carbon chain, with hydroxyl groups on the remaining carbons. **Fructose**, an isomer of glucose found in fruit, is a *ketose*—it contains a ketone group within its carbon chain.

```text
Fischer Projection of D-Glucose (Open Chain):

      CHO      (Aldehyde group at C1)
      |
    H-C-OH
      |
   HO-C-H      (Note the specific spatial arrangement
      |         of -OH and -H groups)
    H-C-OH
      |
    H-C-OH
      |
      CH₂OH    (Primary alcohol at C6)

```

In aqueous solution, the open-chain forms of five- and six-carbon sugars undergo an internal reaction. A hydroxyl group along the chain reacts with the carbonyl carbon to form a highly stable cyclic structure (a ring). For glucose, this typically forms a six-membered ring.

#### Disaccharides and Polysaccharides

Just as amino acids link to form proteins, monosaccharides link together to form more complex carbohydrates. Two cyclic monosaccharides can join via a condensation reaction, eliminating water and forming an ether-like connection called a **glycosidic bond**.

* **Disaccharides:** Consist of two linked monosaccharides. **Sucrose** (table sugar) is a disaccharide formed by linking one glucose molecule to one fructose molecule. **Lactose** (milk sugar) consists of glucose linked to galactose.
* **Polysaccharides:** Consist of hundreds or thousands of monosaccharide units linked together.

The most important polysaccharides are all polymers of glucose, yet they possess vastly different physical properties due to the specific stereochemistry of their glycosidic bonds:

1. **Starch:** The primary energy storage molecule in plants. It consists of glucose units linked together in a way that causes the polymer chain to coil into a helical structure. This relatively open structure allows enzymes to easily access and hydrolyze the bonds, making starch highly digestible for humans.
2. **Glycogen:** The energy storage molecule in animals, stored primarily in the liver and muscles. It is structurally similar to starch but is highly branched, allowing for rapid release of glucose when energy is needed.
3. **Cellulose:** The primary structural component of plant cell walls. In cellulose, the glucose units are linked in a slightly different geometric orientation ($\beta$-linkages rather than the $\alpha$-linkages found in starch). This causes cellulose to form long, straight, rigid fibers that hydrogen bond tightly to one another. Humans lack the enzyme necessary to break this specific $\beta$-glycosidic bond, which is why cellulose is indigestible to us and acts as dietary fiber.

## Chapter Summary

* **Organic Chemistry** is the study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's ability to form four stable covalent bonds and link together in long chains and rings (catenation) creates immense molecular diversity.
* **Hydrocarbons** contain only carbon and hydrogen.
* **Alkanes** are saturated (only single bonds, $sp^3$ hybridized). They undergo free rotation and exist as continuous chains, branched chains, or rings (cycloalkanes).
* **Alkenes** contain at least one double bond ($sp^2$ hybridized) and can exhibit *cis-trans* geometric isomerism due to restricted rotation.
* **Alkynes** contain at least one triple bond ($sp$ hybridized).

* **Aromatic Hydrocarbons**, such as benzene ($C_6H_6$), possess rings with delocalized $\pi$ electrons. This continuous overlap provides exceptional thermodynamic stability, making them prone to substitution rather than addition reactions.
* **Functional Groups** are specific atom arrangements that dictate a molecule's chemical behavior.
* **Oxygen-containing groups** include alcohols ($-OH$), ethers ($-O-$), aldehydes (terminal $C=O$), ketones (internal $C=O$), carboxylic acids ($-COOH$), and esters ($-COO-$).
* **Nitrogen-containing groups** include amines (basic derivatives of ammonia) and amides (neutral derivatives of carboxylic acids where the $-OH$ is replaced by a nitrogen group).

* **Biochemistry** relies on biological polymers.
* **Proteins** are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide (amide) bonds. Their intricate 3D folding dictates their biological function.
* **Carbohydrates** are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones. Monosaccharides (like glucose) can link via glycosidic bonds to form disaccharides (like sucrose) or complex structural/energy-storage polysaccharides (like starch and cellulose).
