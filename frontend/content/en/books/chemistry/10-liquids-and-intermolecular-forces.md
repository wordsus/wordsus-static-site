In our study of gases, we explored a state of matter where particles are widely separated and move independently. However, as temperature drops, the kinetic energy of molecules decreases until the attractive forces between them take over. This delicate balance pulls molecules together into the condensed phases: liquids and solids. In this chapter, we will investigate these critical intermolecular forces. We will discover how these microscopic attractions dictate the macroscopic properties of liquids, such as surface tension and viscosity, and how they govern the phase transitions that transform ice to water, and water to steam.

## 10.1 A Molecular Comparison of Gases, Liquids, and Solids

In Chapter 9, we explored the physical properties of gases and developed a model to explain their behavior based on the Kinetic-Molecular Theory. The defining characteristic of a gas is that its particles are far apart and moving rapidly. Because of these large distances, the intermolecular forces (the forces of attraction between distinct molecules) are almost completely negligible.

However, as we shift our focus to liquids and solids—collectively referred to as **condensed phases**—we find that particles are packed much more closely together. To understand why a substance exists as a gas, a liquid, or a solid at a given temperature and pressure, we must look at the balance of two opposing physical factors at the molecular level.

### The Competition Between Kinetic and Attractive Energies

The state of matter of any substance is determined by the competition between two antagonistic entities:

1. **Kinetic Energy ($E_{kinetic}$):** This is the energy of motion. In chemistry, the average kinetic energy of a collection of particles is directly proportional to the absolute temperature ($T$). Kinetic energy keeps particles in constant motion, driving them apart and promoting disorder.
2. **Intermolecular Forces of Attraction ($E_{attraction}$):** These are the cohesive forces that draw particles together. They depend on the specific chemical structure and polarity of the molecules, which we will classify in Section 10.2.

The delicate balance between these two energies dictates whether a substance is a gas, a liquid, or a solid:

* **Gases ($E_{kinetic} \gg E_{attraction}$):** At high temperatures or low pressures, the kinetic energy of the molecules vastly exceeds the attractive forces between them. The molecules have enough energy to break entirely free from one another, expanding to fill their container.
* **Liquids ($E_{kinetic} \approx E_{attraction}$):** As temperature decreases, kinetic energy decreases. When the kinetic energy becomes comparable to the attractive forces, the molecules can no longer entirely escape each other's influence. They are drawn close together but still possess enough kinetic energy to move past one another, allowing the substance to flow.
* **Solids ($E_{kinetic} \ll E_{attraction}$):** At low temperatures, the intermolecular forces heavily dominate. The kinetic energy is so low that molecules are pulled into rigid, tightly packed arrays. Motion is restricted to slight vibrations around fixed positions.

### Macroscopic Properties vs. Molecular Behavior

The fundamental molecular differences translate directly into the macroscopic physical properties we observe every day. Table 10.1 summarizes these differences.

**Table 10.1: Macroscopic Properties of the Three States of Matter**

| State | Volume and Shape | Compressibility | Fluidity | Density |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Gas** | Assumes both the volume and shape of its container. | Highly compressible. | Flows readily. | Very low. |
| **Liquid** | Has a definite volume but assumes the shape of the occupied part of its container. | Virtually incompressible. | Flows readily. | High. |
| **Solid** | Has a definite volume and a definite shape. | Virtually incompressible. | Does not flow. | High. |

> **Note on Compressibility:** Both liquids and solids are referred to as condensed phases because their particles are already virtually touching. Therefore, applying pressure to a liquid or solid does little to reduce its volume. A gas, conversely, is mostly empty space, making it highly responsive to pressure changes.

We can conceptualize the distribution and movement of particles in each phase using the simplified diagram below:

```text

 [             o   ]       [   o  o  o  o ]       [ o o o o o o ]
 [  o       o      ]       [  o  o  o  o  ]       [ o o o o o o ]

   particles far apart,      particles close,        particles locked in

```

* **In the Gas Phase:** The lack of significant attractive forces allows molecules to move independently in straight lines until they collide. This independence is why gases expand indefinitely.
* **In the Liquid Phase:** The molecules are touching, meaning the density of a liquid is remarkably similar to that of a solid. However, the lack of a rigid, ordered structure means liquids are still highly disordered fluids. The molecules tumble and slide past each other continually.
* **In the Solid Phase:** Molecules are rigidly held in specific geometric arrangements (crystal lattices, which will be detailed in Chapter 11). The particles cannot undergo translational motion (moving from point A to point B); they can only vibrate. This restriction grants solids their definite shape and structural integrity.

### Changing States

Because the kinetic energy of molecules is proportional to temperature, changing the temperature of a substance alters the balance between $E_{kinetic}$ and $E_{attraction}$.

Heating a solid increases its molecular vibrations until $E_{kinetic}$ is sufficient to overcome the rigid lattice forces, causing the solid to melt into a liquid. Further heating eventually provides enough kinetic energy to overcome the remaining intermolecular attractions entirely, boiling the liquid into a gas. Conversely, cooling a gas forces the molecules to slow down until $E_{attraction}$ can lock them together into a liquid, and eventually, a solid. Furthermore, increasing the pressure forces gas molecules closer together, artificially enhancing the effect of $E_{attraction}$ and promoting condensation.

## 10.2 Types of Intermolecular Forces (Dispersion, Dipole-Dipole, Hydrogen Bonding)

In Section 10.1, we established that the condensed states of matter exist because of attractive forces between molecules. It is crucial to distinguish these **intermolecular forces** (forces *between* molecules) from **intramolecular forces** (forces *within* a molecule, such as covalent or ionic bonds). Intermolecular forces are generally much weaker than intramolecular bonds. For example, it takes 41 kJ of energy to vaporize one mole of water (overcoming intermolecular forces), but 930 kJ to break the O–H covalent bonds within one mole of water vapor (overcoming intramolecular forces).

The three primary types of intermolecular attractions—dispersion forces, dipole-dipole forces, and hydrogen bonding—are collectively known as **van der Waals forces**. The physical properties of a liquid or solid depend heavily on which of these forces are present and how strong they are.

### 1. London Dispersion Forces

You might wonder how nonpolar gases, such as helium or nitrogen, can ever be liquefied. If there are no permanent charge separations in these molecules, what pulls them together? In 1930, physicist Fritz London proposed a solution based on the quantum mechanical behavior of electrons.

Even in a nonpolar molecule or atom, the electrons are in constant motion. At any given instant, the electron distribution may be unsymmetrical, creating a temporary, **instantaneous dipole**. This transient dipole can then influence the electron cloud of a neighboring atom, inducing a similar, aligned dipole. The resulting electrostatic attraction between these temporary dipoles is called the **London dispersion force** (or simply dispersion force).

```text
    Atom A (Helium)           Atom B (Helium)
 
       Symmetrical                Symmetrical
    [   e-  He  e-  ]          [   e-  He  e-  ]   No attraction
 
           ↓                          ↓

      Instantaneous                 Induced
          Dipole                     Dipole
    [   He     e-e- ]  ------> [   He     e-e- ]   Dispersion force
        δ+         δ-              δ+         δ-   pulls them together

```

**Key Characteristics of Dispersion Forces:**

* **Ubiquity:** Dispersion forces operate between **all** molecules, whether they are polar or nonpolar.
* **Polarizability:** The strength of dispersion forces depends heavily on the **polarizability** of the molecule—the ease with which its electron cloud can be distorted. Larger atoms with more electrons (and larger electron clouds) are more polarizable. Therefore, dispersion forces generally increase with molar mass. This explains why, at room temperature, the halogens transition from gases ($F_2$, $Cl_2$) to a liquid ($Br_2$) to a solid ($I_2$).
* **Molecular Shape:** For isomers (molecules with the same chemical formula but different arrangements of atoms), shape plays a critical role. A long, cylindrical molecule like *n*-pentane has a greater surface area for electron interaction than a compact, spherical isomer like neopentane, resulting in stronger dispersion forces and a correspondingly higher boiling point.

### 2. Dipole-Dipole Forces

As discussed in Chapter 8, molecules with an asymmetrical distribution of polar bonds possess a permanent dipole moment. These polar molecules have a permanent partial positive ($\delta^+$) end and a permanent partial negative ($\delta^-$) end.

**Dipole-dipole forces** arise from the electrostatic attraction between the permanent dipole of one molecule and the permanent dipole of a neighboring molecule. The $\delta^+$ end of one molecule is attracted to the $\delta^-$ end of another.

```text
         δ+  δ-          δ+  δ-          δ+  δ-
       (H——Cl) - - - - (H——Cl) - - - - (H——Cl)
             \                       /
              \                     /
               \      δ+  δ-       /
                 - - (H——Cl) - - -

```

Because these dipoles are permanent, dipole-dipole forces are typically stronger than dispersion forces for molecules of similar size and mass. When molecules approach each other in a liquid or solid, they tend to orient themselves to maximize these attractions and minimize repulsions. However, the constant kinetic energy in a liquid causes tumbling, meaning the molecules experience both attractive and repulsive interactions, though the net effect is cohesive attraction.

### 3. Hydrogen Bonding

**Hydrogen bonding** is a special, exceptionally strong category of dipole-dipole interaction. It occurs exclusively in molecules where a hydrogen atom is covalently bonded to a small, highly electronegative atom: specifically, **Nitrogen (N), Oxygen (O), or Fluorine (F)**.

Because N, O, and F are so electronegative, the electron density in the bond is pulled strongly away from the hydrogen atom. Hydrogen, having no inner core electrons to shield its nucleus, is left as a virtually bare proton on the edge of the molecule. This highly concentrated $\delta^+$ charge interacts powerfully with the nonbonding (lone) electron pairs on the N, O, or F atoms of adjacent molecules.

```text
                  δ-
                 /O\
                H   H
               δ+   δ+
                     :
                      :  <-- Hydrogen Bond (interaction with lone pair)
                       :
                        δ-
                       /O\
                      H   H
                     δ+   δ+

```

The strength of hydrogen bonds (typically 15 to 40 kJ/mol) is much greater than regular dipole-dipole or dispersion forces, although still roughly only 5% to 10% the strength of a covalent bond.

The profound impact of hydrogen bonding is evident in boiling points. Despite having a very low molar mass, water ($H_2O$) has an unusually high boiling point of 100°C. If water only experienced dispersion and standard dipole-dipole forces, extrapolating from heavier group 6A hydrides ($H_2S$, $H_2Se$, $H_2Te$) suggests it would boil at roughly -80°C, and life on Earth as we know it would not exist.

## 10.3 Viscosity and Surface Tension

The intermolecular forces discussed in Section 10.2 not only dictate whether a substance is a liquid at room temperature but also govern how that liquid behaves. Two of the most easily observable macroscopic properties that arise directly from the strength of these microscopic interactions are **viscosity** and **surface tension**.

### Viscosity

Some liquids, like water or gasoline, flow quickly and easily. Others, like molasses or heavy motor oil, flow very sluggishly. **Viscosity** is defined as the measure of a liquid’s resistance to flow. The greater a liquid's viscosity, the slower it flows.

Viscosity is determined by the ease with which individual molecules can move past one another. This mobility is governed by two primary factors:

1. **Intermolecular Forces:** Liquids with strong intermolecular forces (such as the extensive hydrogen bonding network in glycerol) hold their molecules tightly together, resisting movement and resulting in high viscosity.
2. **Molecular Size and Shape:** Long, complex, or highly branched molecules can become physically entangled, much like a plate of spaghetti. Long-chain hydrocarbons (like those found in motor oils) have high viscosities primarily because of these entanglements and the strong cumulative London dispersion forces along their lengths.

Viscosity is typically measured in units of poise ($\text{P}$) or centipoise ($\text{cP}$), where $1 \text{ P} = 0.1 \text{ kg}/(\text{m} \cdot \text{s})$. For context, the viscosity of water at $20^\circ\text{C}$ is approximately $1 \text{ cP}$.

**The Effect of Temperature on Viscosity**
Viscosity is highly temperature-dependent. As temperature increases, the average kinetic energy of the molecules ($E_{kinetic}$) increases. This added thermal energy helps overcome the attractive intermolecular forces ($E_{attraction}$) holding the molecules together, allowing them to slip past one another more easily. Consequently, **the viscosity of a liquid decreases as its temperature increases**. This principle is crucial in automotive engineering, where engine oils must be carefully formulated to provide sufficient lubrication (viscosity) at high operating temperatures without becoming too thick to pump in cold weather.

### Surface Tension

If you observe a freshly washed car, you will notice that water beads up into nearly perfect little spheres. This behavior is due to **surface tension**, the energy required to increase the surface area of a liquid by a unit amount (typically expressed in $\text{J}/\text{m}^2$).

To understand surface tension, we must look at the different environments experienced by molecules at the surface of a liquid compared to those in the interior.

```text
    Liquid Surface:    Net inward pull (no liquid molecules above)
                        ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓
                      (O) (O) (O) (O) 
                      ↗↑↖ ↗↑↖ ↗↑↖ ↗↑↖
    Liquid Interior:  (O) (O) (O) (O) 
                      ↙↓↘ ↙↓↘ ↙↓↘ ↙↓↘
                      (O) (O) (O) (O)
                       Pulled equally in all directions

```

* **Interior Molecules:** A molecule deep within the liquid is completely surrounded by other molecules. It experiences intermolecular attractions from all sides, resulting in a net force of zero.
* **Surface Molecules:** A molecule at the surface experiences attractive forces only from the molecules beside and below it. There are no liquid molecules above it to balance the downward pull.

This imbalance results in a net inward force that pulls surface molecules toward the interior, causing the liquid to contract and pack together as closely as possible. Because a sphere possesses the smallest surface-area-to-volume ratio of any geometric shape, freely falling liquids and droplets on non-interacting surfaces naturally assume a spherical shape to minimize their surface energy.

Liquids with strong intermolecular forces, such as water with its robust hydrogen bonds, exhibit high surface tension. This high surface tension creates a "skin" on the water's surface strong enough to support small, dense objects like a steel needle or water strider insects, provided the surface is not broken.

### Cohesive vs. Adhesive Forces and Capillary Action

The behavior of liquids when they come into contact with solid surfaces introduces two distinct types of intermolecular attractions:

* **Cohesive Forces:** Intermolecular forces that bind *similar* molecules to one another (e.g., hydrogen bonding between water molecules).
* **Adhesive Forces:** Intermolecular forces that bind a substance to a *surface* (e.g., forces between water molecules and the silicon dioxide lattice of glass).

The competition between cohesion and adhesion explains the shape of the **meniscus**—the curved surface of a liquid in a narrow tube.

```text
       Water in Glass                 Mercury in Glass
       (Adhesion > Cohesion)          (Cohesion > Adhesion)
    
       |                   |          |      _____      |
       | \               / |          |    /       \    |
       |  \_____________/  |          |   /         \   |
       |                   |          |  |           |  |
       |___________________|          |__|___________|__|
             Concave                        Convex

```

* **Concave Meniscus (Water):** The adhesive forces between the polar water molecules and the polar Si–O bonds in the glass are stronger than the cohesive forces within the water itself. The water literally pulls itself up the walls of the glass.
* **Convex Meniscus (Mercury):** Mercury atoms undergo metallic bonding (very strong cohesive forces), but interact very weakly with glass (weak adhesive forces). The mercury atoms pull away from the glass toward the center of the tube, creating an inverted dome.

**Capillary Action**
When a glass tube with a very small diameter (a capillary tube) is placed in water, the liquid spontaneously rises up the tube. This phenomenon, known as **capillary action**, is driven by the interplay of adhesion and cohesion. Adhesion pulls the leading edge of the water up the walls of the tube to increase contact area, while surface tension (driven by cohesion) acts to minimize the surface area, dragging the rest of the liquid up behind it. The liquid continues to rise until the downward force of gravity on the liquid column exactly balances the upward force of adhesion. Capillary action is a vital physical process, enabling phenomena ranging from the wicking of fluids in paper towels to the transport of water from the roots to the leaves of tall trees.

## 10.4 Phase Changes and Heating Curves

Matter frequently transforms from one state to another in processes called **phase changes** (or phase transitions). These transformations are physical changes, not chemical ones; the composition of the substance remains identical, but the spatial arrangement and mobility of its molecules change drastically due to the breaking or forming of intermolecular forces.

### Types of Phase Changes

There are six primary phase changes, classified by the states involved and the direction of heat flow:

**Endothermic Phase Changes (Require Energy Input)**
To overcome the attractive intermolecular forces holding molecules together, energy must be absorbed from the surroundings.

* **Melting (Fusion):** Solid to Liquid.
* **Vaporization:** Liquid to Gas.
* **Sublimation:** Solid directly to Gas (e.g., dry ice, $CO_2(s)$, turning into carbon dioxide gas).

**Exothermic Phase Changes (Release Energy)**
As molecules slow down and are drawn together by intermolecular forces, energy is released into the surroundings.

* **Freezing:** Liquid to Solid.
* **Condensation:** Gas to Liquid.
* **Deposition:** Gas directly to Solid (e.g., frost forming on a cold window).

### The Thermodynamics of Phase Changes

Every phase change is accompanied by a specific change in enthalpy ($\Delta H$), which represents the heat exchanged at constant pressure.

* **Heat of Fusion ($\Delta H_{fus}$):** The energy required to melt one mole of a solid.
* **Heat of Vaporization ($\Delta H_{vap}$):** The energy required to vaporize one mole of a liquid.
* **Heat of Sublimation ($\Delta H_{sub}$):** The energy required to sublime one mole of a solid.

Because vaporization requires entirely severing all intermolecular interactions so that molecules can behave as a gas, $\Delta H_{vap}$ is consistently much larger than $\Delta H_{fus}$, which only requires breaking enough interactions to allow molecules to flow. Furthermore, according to Hess's Law, the enthalpy of sublimation is the sum of the enthalpies of fusion and vaporization:

$$ \Delta H_{sub} = \Delta H_{fus} + \Delta H_{vap} $$

The exothermic processes have the exact same magnitude of enthalpy change as their endothermic counterparts, but with an opposite (negative) sign. For instance, the heat of condensation is $-\Delta H_{vap}$.

### Heating Curves

If we take a block of ice at $-20^\circ\text{C}$ and add heat to it at a constant rate until it becomes steam at $120^\circ\text{C}$, we can plot its temperature against the amount of heat added. This graph is known as a **heating curve**.

The heating curve for water, shown below, illustrates a fundamental concept in physical chemistry: **temperature remains constant during a phase change.**

```text

  100 -|---------------------------------=======================

       |                                 (Liquid & Gas Coexist)
       |                                 q = n·ΔH_vap
       |                                /

```

### Analyzing the Segments of a Heating Curve

A complete heating curve consists of alternating sloped and horizontal segments. The equations used to calculate the heat ($q$) required for each segment depend on whether the substance is undergoing a temperature change or a phase change.

**1. The Sloped Segments: Heating a Single Phase**
When heat is added to a pure solid, pure liquid, or pure gas, the added thermal energy increases the kinetic energy of the molecules. Consequently, the temperature rises. The amount of heat required is calculated using the substance's specific heat capacity ($C_s$), its mass ($m$), and the change in temperature ($\Delta T$):

$$ q = m \cdot C_s \cdot \Delta T $$

*Note that the specific heat capacity differs depending on the state of matter. For water, $C_{s, solid} = 2.09 \text{ J}/(\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})$, $C_{s, liquid} = 4.18 \text{ J}/(\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})$, and $C_{s, gas} = 1.84 \text{ J}/(\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})$.*

**2. The Horizontal Plateaus: Phase Changes**
When the temperature reaches the melting point ($0^\circ\text{C}$ for water) or boiling point ($100^\circ\text{C}$ for water), the temperature stops rising, creating a plateau on the graph.

Why doesn't the temperature increase even though we are still adding heat? Because all the added thermal energy is now being used to overcome the intermolecular forces ($E_{attraction}$) rather than increasing the average kinetic energy ($E_{kinetic}$) of the molecules. Until all the ice has melted into liquid, or all the liquid has boiled into gas, the temperature will not change.

The heat required for these phase changes is calculated using the number of moles ($n$) and the molar enthalpy of the phase change:

$$ q = n \cdot \Delta H_{fus} \quad \text{(for melting)} $$

$$ q = n \cdot \Delta H_{vap} \quad \text{(for boiling)} $$

*(Note: Sometimes these values are given per gram instead of per mole, in which case the mass $m$ is used instead of $n$.)*

To find the total heat required to take a substance from a solid at a low temperature to a gas at a high temperature, you must calculate the heat for each individual step on the heating curve and sum them together. Skipping steps or combining them into a single equation will result in severe thermodynamic errors.

## 10.5 Vapor Pressure and Boiling Point

If you leave a glass of water uncovered on a table, the water will eventually evaporate completely. However, if you place a lid on that same glass, the water level will drop slightly at first but then remain constant indefinitely. To understand why liquids behave this way, and how this relates to boiling, we must examine the concept of vapor pressure through the lens of dynamic equilibrium and kinetic energy.

### Evaporation and Dynamic Equilibrium

At any given temperature, the molecules in a liquid possess a distribution of kinetic energies. While the *average* kinetic energy is tied to the temperature, individual molecules constantly collide, transferring energy. At any specific moment, a small fraction of molecules near the surface will acquire enough kinetic energy to overcome the intermolecular forces holding them in the liquid phase and escape into the gas phase. This process is called **vaporization** or **evaporation**.

In an open container, these vaporized molecules disperse into the atmosphere, allowing evaporation to continue until the liquid is gone. In a closed container, however, the vaporized molecules are trapped. As the number of gas-phase molecules increases, so does the probability that they will collide with the liquid surface, lose energy, and be recaptured by intermolecular forces—a process called **condensation**.

Initially, the rate of evaporation is much higher than the rate of condensation. As vapor builds up, the condensation rate increases. Eventually, the system reaches a point where the two opposing rates are exactly equal:

$$\text{Rate of vaporization} = \text{Rate of condensation}$$

At this point, the system has reached **dynamic equilibrium**. The total number of molecules in the liquid and gas phases remains constant, even though individual molecules are continually trading places.

```text
       Initial State:             Equilibrium State:
       Rate(vap) > Rate(cond)     Rate(vap) = Rate(cond)
       
       |       ↑       |          |    ↑   ↓   ↑   |  <-- Vapor (Constant Pressure)
       |   ↑       ↑   |          |  ↓   ↑   ↓   ↑ |
       |---------------|          |----------------|
       |               |          |                |
       |    Liquid     |          |     Liquid     |
       |_______________|          |________________|

```

The pressure exerted by the vapor in equilibrium with its liquid phase at a specific temperature is called the **vapor pressure** of the liquid.

### Factors Affecting Vapor Pressure

Vapor pressure is determined by two fundamental factors:

**1. Intermolecular Forces:**
Vapor pressure is highly dependent on the identity of the liquid. Liquids with weak intermolecular forces (like diethyl ether) require very little thermal energy for their molecules to escape. Consequently, a large number of molecules enter the gas phase, resulting in a high vapor pressure. These liquids evaporate rapidly and are termed **volatile**. Conversely, liquids with strong intermolecular forces (like water, due to hydrogen bonding) hold their molecules tightly. Fewer molecules escape, resulting in a low vapor pressure (nonvolatile).

**2. Temperature:**
Vapor pressure always increases non-linearly with temperature. As temperature rises, the entire distribution of molecular kinetic energies shifts to higher values. This dramatically increases the fraction of molecules that possess the minimum energy required to escape the liquid phase.

### The Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

The exponential relationship between vapor pressure ($P$) and absolute temperature ($T$) is described mathematically by the **Clausius-Clapeyron equation**:

$$ \ln P = -\frac{\Delta H_{vap}}{RT} + C $$

Where:

* $P$ is the vapor pressure.
* $\Delta H_{vap}$ is the molar heat of vaporization of the liquid.
* $R$ is the ideal gas constant ($8.314 \text{ J}/(\text{mol}\cdot\text{K})$).
* $T$ is the absolute temperature in Kelvin.
* $C$ is a constant specific to the liquid.

This equation shows that a plot of the natural logarithm of vapor pressure ($\ln P$) versus the inverse of temperature ($1/T$) yields a straight line with a slope equal to $-\Delta H_{vap}/R$.

In practical laboratory settings, it is often more useful to compare a single substance at two different temperature-pressure states. By writing the Clausius-Clapeyron equation for two different states and subtracting them, we can eliminate the constant $C$ and derive the two-point form:

$$ \ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right) = \frac{\Delta H_{vap}}{R} \left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) $$

This equation is exceptionally powerful, allowing chemists to predict the vapor pressure of a liquid at any temperature if its heat of vaporization and one vapor pressure point are known.

### Vapor Pressure and Boiling Point

Evaporation is a surface phenomenon; it only occurs at the interface between the liquid and the gas. **Boiling**, however, is a bulk phenomenon.

If you heat a liquid, its vapor pressure increases. When the temperature becomes high enough that the liquid's vapor pressure exactly equals the external pressure exerted on the liquid's surface (usually atmospheric pressure), bubbles of vapor can physically form *within the interior* of the liquid. These bubbles are sustained because the pressure inside them (the vapor pressure) pushes outward just as hard as the external atmosphere pushes inward. The bubbles rise to the surface and escape.

**The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which its vapor pressure equals the external pressure.**

Because atmospheric pressure fluctuates depending on weather and altitude, the boiling point of a liquid is not a single, fixed temperature. To standardize physical properties, chemists define the **normal boiling point** as the boiling point of a liquid at a standard external pressure of 1 atm (760 torr).

The plain-text graph below illustrates the vapor pressure curves for three different liquids and how they determine the normal boiling points.

```text

  Pressure
   (atm)   
           |    Diethyl          Ethanol        Water
      1.0 -|-----o----------------o-------------o--- (Normal Boiling Points)
           |    /                /             / 
           |   /                /             /
           |  /                /             /
      0.5 -| /                /             /
           |/                /             /
           |                /             /
           |_______________/_____________/________________
                 34.6°C         78.3°C         100.0°C

```

**The Effect of Altitude:**
At higher elevations, the atmospheric pressure is significantly lower than 1 atm. Because the external pressure is lower, a liquid does not need to be heated as much for its vapor pressure to match the external pressure. Consequently, water boils at lower temperatures at high altitudes. For example, in Denver, Colorado (elevation roughly 1,600 meters), atmospheric pressure is about 0.83 atm, and water boils at roughly $95^\circ\text{C}$. This lower boiling temperature is why recipes often require longer cooking times at high altitudes; it is the temperature of the boiling water, not the physical act of boiling, that cooks the food.

## 10.6 Interpreting Phase Diagrams

In the preceding sections, we examined phase changes primarily by varying temperature at a constant atmospheric pressure. However, both temperature and pressure dictate the state of matter of a substance. A **phase diagram** is a graphical representation that summarizes the conditions under which equilibria exist between the different states of matter for a pure substance. It also allows us to predict the phase of a substance at any given temperature and pressure.

### The Anatomy of a Phase Diagram

A standard phase diagram plots pressure ($P$) on the vertical axis (y-axis) against temperature ($T$) on the horizontal axis (x-axis). The diagram is divided into three primary regions, each representing conditions under which a single state (solid, liquid, or gas) is thermodynamically stable.

Separating these regions are lines, or phase boundaries, which represent combinations of pressure and temperature where two phases coexist in dynamic equilibrium.

```text
    Pressure (P)
          |
          |                  Solid-Liquid Line (Melting/Freezing)
          |                 /
          |                /
      P_c -|.............../...................o Critical Point (T_c, P_c)
          |               /                   .
          |    SOLID     /      LIQUID       .
          |             /                   .
          |            /                   .  Liquid-Gas Line 
          |           /                   .   (Vapor Pressure)
          |          /                   .
          |         /                   .
      P_t -|........o Triple Point (T_t, P_t)
          |        / .                 .
          |       /   .               .
          |      /     .    GAS      .
          |     /       ............
          |____/______________________________________
                          Temperature (T)
               Solid-Gas Line
               (Sublimation/Deposition)

```

**1. The Phase Boundaries (The Lines):**

* **The Vapor Pressure Curve (Liquid-Gas Line):** This curve represents the equilibrium between the liquid and gas phases. It shows how the boiling point of the liquid changes with pressure. This line terminates at the critical point.
* **The Melting Curve (Solid-Liquid Line):** This line represents the equilibrium between the solid and liquid phases, showing how the melting point changes as pressure is applied.
* **The Sublimation Curve (Solid-Gas Line):** This curve separates the solid and gas regions. It represents the vapor pressure of the solid at different temperatures, indicating the conditions under which a solid can sublime directly into a gas.

**2. The Triple Point:**
All three phase boundary lines intersect at a single, unique coordinate called the **triple point**. At the specific temperature ($T_t$) and pressure ($P_t$) of the triple point, all three phases of the substance (solid, liquid, and gas) coexist simultaneously in perfect thermodynamic equilibrium. Any change in temperature or pressure will cause at least one phase to disappear. For water, the triple point occurs at $0.0098^\circ\text{C}$ and $0.00603 \text{ atm}$.

**3. The Critical Point and Supercritical Fluids:**
The liquid-gas line does not extend indefinitely. It terminates at the **critical point**, defined by the critical temperature ($T_c$) and critical pressure ($P_c$).

* **Critical Temperature ($T_c$):** The highest temperature at which a liquid can exist. Above $T_c$, the kinetic energy of the molecules is so immense that no amount of pressure can force them to condense into a liquid.
* **Critical Pressure ($P_c$):** The pressure required to bring about liquefaction at the critical temperature.

When a substance is subjected to conditions above its critical temperature and critical pressure, the distinct boundary between liquid and gas vanishes. The molecules are packed closely together like a liquid, but they possess the chaotic, unconstrained motion of a gas. This state of matter is called a **supercritical fluid**. Supercritical fluids possess unique properties, expanding to fill their container like a gas but dissolving substances like a liquid.

### Comparing Water and Carbon Dioxide

Phase diagrams are specific to the chemical identity of the substance. Comparing the phase diagrams of water ($H_2O$) and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) highlights a crucial anomaly in water's physical properties.

**The Slope of the Solid-Liquid Line**
For almost all substances, including $CO_2$, the solid phase is denser than the liquid phase. Increasing the pressure on such a substance forces the molecules closer together, favoring the formation of the denser solid phase. Consequently, as pressure increases, a higher temperature is required to melt the solid. This results in a melting curve with a **positive slope** (leaning to the right).

Water is a glaring exception. Because of its highly ordered, open hexagonal hydrogen-bonded lattice in the solid state, ice is *less dense* than liquid water. Increasing pressure on ice actually collapses the lattice, favoring the formation of the denser liquid phase. Therefore, as pressure increases, the melting point of ice decreases. This gives water's melting curve an unusual **negative slope** (leaning to the left). This anomaly explains why ice skates can glide so smoothly; the high pressure from the thin blade locally lowers the melting point of the ice, creating a thin, lubricating layer of liquid water.

**Normal Sublimation vs. Boiling**
The normal phase transitions we observe daily depend on where standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm) lies relative to the substance's triple point.

* **Water:** The triple point pressure is $0.006 \text{ atm}$, well below 1 atm. Therefore, if we heat ice at 1 atm, we cross the solid-liquid line first (it melts), and then the liquid-gas line (it boils).
* **Carbon Dioxide:** The triple point pressure of $CO_2$ is $5.11 \text{ atm}$. Standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm) is far below the triple point. Therefore, the 1 atm pressure line intersects only the solid-gas curve. When solid $CO_2$ (dry ice) is heated at room pressure, it cannot melt into a liquid; instead, it sublimes directly into a gas. Liquid $CO_2$ can only exist at pressures greater than $5.11 \text{ atm}$.

## Chapter Summary

Chapter 10 explored the properties of condensed phases (liquids and solids) and how these macroscopic physical properties arise from microscopic intermolecular forces.

* **Section 10.1:** The state of matter is dictated by the competition between the kinetic energy of molecules (driving them apart) and intermolecular attractive forces (drawing them together).
* **Section 10.2:** The primary intermolecular forces (van der Waals forces) include London dispersion forces (present in all molecules, dependent on polarizability), dipole-dipole forces (between polar molecules), and hydrogen bonding (a strong dipole-dipole interaction involving H bonded to N, O, or F).
* **Section 10.3:** Macroscopic liquid properties such as viscosity (resistance to flow) and surface tension (the energy required to expand a liquid's surface) are directly proportional to the strength of a liquid's intermolecular forces.
* **Section 10.4:** Phase changes involve overcoming intermolecular forces. Heating curves illustrate that the temperature of a pure substance remains entirely constant during a phase change, with energy quantified by enthalpies of fusion ($\Delta H_{fus}$) and vaporization ($\Delta H_{vap}$).
* **Section 10.5:** Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by a gas in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid. It increases with temperature as described by the Clausius-Clapeyron equation. A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals the external atmospheric pressure.
* **Section 10.6:** Phase diagrams visually map the stable states of a substance across varying temperatures and pressures. They feature phase boundaries, a triple point where all three phases coexist, and a critical point beyond which the substance becomes a supercritical fluid.
